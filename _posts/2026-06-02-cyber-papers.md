## 🇨🇭 Switzerland & Vaud

- **A new popular initiative wants "digital security" written into the federal Constitution.** The association Swiss Digital Pact on Tuesday launched the federal popular initiative _"Pour la sécurité numérique de la Suisse"_ ("For Switzerland's digital security"), arguing the Confederation should be constitutionally obliged to guarantee the country's digital resilience and **sovereignty** (here meaning control over critical data, infrastructure and software rather than depending on foreign providers). A popular initiative is the Swiss direct-democracy instrument that, with 100,000 valid signatures inside 18 months, forces a nationwide vote on a constitutional amendment — so this is the first step of a years-long process, not a law. "Switzerland must remain safe, resilient and master of its digital future," said secretary Massimo Rodella. Why it matters: it would elevate cybersecurity from an administrative task (today handled by the NCSC — the National Cyber Security Centre) to a constitutional duty, and signals growing unease about reliance on US/foreign cloud and software. [single-source] [Le Temps, 2026-06-02](https://www.letemps.ch/articles/une-initiative-pour-ancrer-la-securite-numerique-dans-la-constitution)

## 🛡️ Cybersecurity

- **CVE-2026-8206 — Kirki WordPress plugin: unauthenticated account takeover, CVSS 9.8 (critical; trivially exploitable, no login needed, full account compromise).** The Kirki "Freeform Page Builder / Customizer" plugin, versions 6.0.0–6.0.6, accepts an **arbitrary email address** in its password-reset flow when a username is supplied — so an unauthenticated attacker can have the reset link for _any_ registered user (including an administrator) delivered to the attacker's own inbox, then take the account over. The vector string `AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N` confirms: network-reachable, low complexity, no privileges, no user interaction. Update to a patched 6.0.x release. [NVD, published 2026-06-02](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-8206)
- **CISA adds Oracle WebLogic CVE-2024-21182 to the KEV catalog (active exploitation).** The **KEV** (Known Exploited Vulnerabilities — CISA's authoritative list of bugs confirmed to be exploited in the wild, and a federal patch mandate) gained Oracle WebLogic Server on 1 June. Why it matters: WebLogic sits behind a lot of enterprise middleware, the T3 listener is often reachable, and KEV-listing means this is being used now, not theoretically. Patch and restrict T3/IIOP exposure. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2024-21182); KEV listing via [CISA KEV catalog (dateAdded 2026-06-01)](https://www.cisa.gov/sites/default/files/feeds/known_exploited_vulnerabilities.json)

## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

_All papers below are from today's arXiv announcement batch (RSS `lastBuildDate` Tue, 02 Jun 2026; IDs in the `2606.000xx` range, announce type `new`). Papers already covered in this morning's Overview (`2606.00079`, `.00091`, `.00135`, `.00144`, `.00151`, `.00232`) are excluded._

- **ART: Attention Run-time Termination — `arXiv:2606.00024` (2026-06-02).** Long-context decoding is bottlenecked by the memory bandwidth needed to fetch the **KV-cache** (the stored attention keys and values from earlier tokens, which grow with context length). Most pruning tricks look only at keys, because pulling in values too is expensive — but attention outputs depend on both. ART tracks the *accumulated attention output* live during the GPU kernel and simply stops fetching further KV blocks once they can no longer change the result. Why interesting: it's a values-aware shortcut that gets most of the speed-up of pruning without the usual quality hit, attacking the single biggest cost of long-context inference. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00024)
- **SENSE: semantic retrieval-based speculative decoding — `arXiv:2606.00021` (2026-06-02).** Speculative decoding speeds up generation by having a cheap drafter propose several tokens that the big model verifies in parallel; the *retrieval-based* variant drafts by copying likely continuations from a corpus, which is plug-and-play but brittle because it matches on exact words. SENSE instead retrieves on the model's hidden-state embeddings (meaning, not surface text) and adds a soft-gated verification step. Why interesting: it makes the cheapest, training-free flavour of speculative decoding robust to paraphrase, widening where it actually helps. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00021)
- **FoLoRA: forgetting-aware LoRA — `arXiv:2606.00132` (2026-06-02).** Fine-tuning a foundation model on a narrow task often erodes unrelated skills it learned in pretraining (**catastrophic forgetting**). **LoRA** (Low-Rank Adaptation — the standard cheap fine-tuning method that trains a small additive update instead of all the weights) doesn't address this directly. FoLoRA adds a preservation penalty, derived from a first-order condition expressed as a generalized Rayleigh quotient, that actively regulates the adapt-vs-preserve trade-off *during* training rather than via fixed constraints. Why interesting: it gives a principled dial for "specialize without lobotomizing," relevant to anyone maintaining many task-specific adapters off one base model. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00132)
- **Test-time prompt optimization for VLM reward models — `arXiv:2606.00083` (2026-06-02).** Reinforcement learning needs a reward signal, but in robotics there often isn't one. A recent trend uses a **VLM** (Vision-Language Model — a model that reads images and text together) as a zero-shot judge to score whether a robot did the task; the catch is that a badly worded prompt yields false-positive rewards that poison the policy. This paper uses the small set of expert demonstrations roboticists already collect to optimize that judging prompt at test time. Why interesting: it tackles the weakest link in "let an off-the-shelf VLM grade your robot" — reward noise — using data you already have. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00083)
- **TrustLDM: a trustworthiness benchmark for language diffusion models — `arXiv:2606.00023` (2026-06-02).** **Language diffusion models** (an emerging alternative to standard left-to-right LLMs that generate text by iteratively denoising in any order, which can decode faster) are catching up to autoregressive models on quality — but their flexible decoding may open new failure modes. TrustLDM is the first benchmark to systematically probe their safety, privacy and fairness across architectures. Why interesting: it gets ahead of a young architecture before it's widely deployed, and asks whether speed gains come with hidden trust costs. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00023)
- **World Models: a comprehensive survey — `arXiv:2606.00133` (2026-06-02).** A **world model** is an internal, learned simulator of an environment's dynamics that lets an agent predict and plan "in its head" rather than by trial in the real world — a central idea spanning RL, robotics, self-driving and video generation, but one whose literature is fragmented. This survey imposes a four-axis taxonomy (architecture, training, reasoning mechanism, application). Why interesting: a useful map of one of the hotter routes people are betting on toward more general agents, handy as a single reference rather than a new result. [preprint] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00133)
