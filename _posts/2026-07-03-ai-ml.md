## 📄 ML/AI research (arXiv)

A safety-heavy batch this window: two papers on breaking or overseeing model alignment, one on how much a black-box API leaks about a model's guts, plus a run of efficiency and training-dynamics work. All items below are from the 3 July 2026 arXiv batch; dedup is by arXiv ID within the batch.

- **Lie-detector oversight of LLMs scales the right way: undetected deception falls from 34% to 14% as models grow from 1B to 405B parameters** — [arXiv:2607.01567](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01567) · O. Hollinsworth, A.-K. Dombrowski, S. Adam-Day, A. Gleave, C. Cundy (FAR.AI) · `[preprint]`

  The idea (SOLiD — Scalable Oversight via Lie Detectors) is to train a cheap classifier that reads a model's internal activations and flags responses that look deceptive, then route only those flagged responses to expensive human reviewers during preference fine-tuning (the RLHF-style step where human ratings shape the model's behaviour). This paper scales that setup up to 405-billion-parameter models and tests it in more realistic preference-learning conditions.

  **Why it matters:** the encouraging result is that oversight gets *easier*, not harder, as models grow — undetected deception dropped to 14% at 405B, and the authors could remove the human labelers from the fine-tuning loop entirely without a statistically significant rise in deception. The honest caveat they flag: the detector is sensitive to distribution shift between the data it was trained on and the preference data, which can push its false-positive rate around — so this is a promising oversight lever, not a solved problem. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **A character-level jailbreak exploits how tokenizers chop up safety words, flipping refusals on 80–100% of blocked prompts** — [arXiv:2607.01239](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01239) · T.-L. Li, H. Liu, Y. Wu (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  BPE (byte-pair encoding — the standard scheme that splits text into sub-word "tokens") fragments a safety-critical word into pieces when it is lightly perturbed, and the authors show the three public alignment datasets they surveyed contain no such deliberately fragmented examples — so the safety training never saw this failure mode. Tested end-to-end on five model families (Qwen-3-4B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Gemma-3-4B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B), an optimizer that targets safety-token fragmentation flips the model's first-token refusal on 80–100% of prompts it would normally refuse, with 48% of those flips producing genuinely harmful output. Activation patching (a technique that swaps internal activations between runs to localize where a behaviour lives) traces the disrupted refusal signal to the last ~30% of layers.

  **Why it matters:** it names a concrete, structural reason character-level attacks keep working — the tokenizer, not the model's "intent" — and points at a fixable gap: put fragmented inputs into alignment data. The prompts stay human-readable throughout, which is what makes the attack awkward to filter. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **"NightVision" recovers an LLM's hidden dimension, depth, and parameter count through today's locked-down single-logit APIs** — [arXiv:2607.01313](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01313) · C. Ellis, S. Chaudhari, M.-Y. Wang, L. Barnes, G. Fanti, J. M. F. Moura (Carnegie Mellon University) · `[preprint]`

  Earlier attacks recovered architectural details (like the width of a model's feed-forward layers) when APIs exposed top-k logits or a logit-bias knob; providers responded by exposing only the single chosen token per step. This work shows that even under those restrictive APIs, an attacker can still estimate the hidden dimension, the number of layers (depth), and the total parameter count.

  **Why it matters:** commercial labs treat architecture as a trade secret, and this says the current API lockdown isn't enough to hide it — relevant both to anyone relying on that secrecy and to the broader question of how much a model's internals leak through its outputs. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **Kara compresses the KV cache with a sliding window to speed up long-reasoning serving** — [arXiv:2607.01237](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01237) · S. Han, Y. Wu (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  Reasoning models emit long chains of thought, and each generated token's key/value vectors pile up in the KV cache (the running memory of attention), which balloons memory use and slows decoding. Existing compression drops "unimportant" cache entries, but the authors argue the common threshold-triggered policies can barely help throughput — or even hurt it — and can wipe out whole blocks of the sequence, losing information. Kara instead compresses over a sliding window with a more graceful retention rule.

  **Why it matters:** long-CoT (chain-of-thought) serving is where a lot of today's inference cost sits, so a compression method that improves throughput without the accuracy cliffs of prior schemes is directly useful for anyone self-hosting reasoning models. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **The execution substrate is a hidden tax on coding-agent RL: up to 110× variation in cold-start latency across four backends** — [arXiv:2607.01415](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01415) · D. T. Graviet, L. Pesut, I. Dagelic, V. Jukic, I. Burazin (Daytona) · `[preprint]`

  Training a coding agent with reinforcement learning means running huge numbers of interactive software "rollouts" — spinning up an environment, letting the agent edit and run code, scoring the result. The infrastructure that hosts those rollouts is usually treated as a background detail. The authors benchmark four substrates — single containers, hosted sandboxes, Kubernetes-orchestrated containers, and cloud VMs — and find up to 110× spread in cold-start latency and a 1.8× spread in projected worker-hours for one million 150-step trajectories.

  **Why it matters:** small per-rollout savings compound across millions of rollouts, so the paper's argument is that the execution substrate should be optimized as part of the training system, not bolted on — a practical lever for the cost and speed of RL post-training that most teams currently ignore. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **Procedural Memory Distillation reuses cross-episode RL signals that per-episode updates throw away** — [arXiv:2607.01480](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01480) · Y. Liu, S. Bansal, B. Pang, Y. Li, Z. L. Liu, Y. Ming, Z. Ke, S. Joty, S. Yavuz (Salesforce AI Research) · `[preprint]`

  In RLVR (reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards — score each attempt with an automatic checker rather than a human), the policy is updated from a single episode-level pass/fail signal, and the richer detail inside each rollout is discarded. But across episodes the model keeps meeting related problems and generating signals about which strategies pass, which failure modes persist, and which patterns recur. PMD captures that cross-episode information as reusable "procedural memory" and distills it back into the model's weights during training.

  **Why it matters:** it targets a real inefficiency in how verifier-based RL learns — treating each attempt as isolated — and offers a way to let a model accumulate reusable skill rather than relearning the same lessons every epoch. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **"Token Geometry": a kilobyte-scale optimizer for embedding tables that undercuts Adam's memory** — [arXiv:2607.01455](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01455) · K. Shah (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  A language model's embedding table and output ("LM-head") layer are the read/write interface between discrete tokens and the continuous internal computation, and the author argues their gradient geometry differs from the dense hidden weights — so they deserve a different optimizer. The proposed method, Ember, tracks optimizer state in O(V + D) memory (V = vocabulary size, D = model dimension) instead of Adam's O(2VD), i.e. kilobytes rather than the usual large per-parameter buffers, and avoids having to shard those states.

  **Why it matters:** embedding/LM-head optimizer state is a surprisingly heavy slice of training memory for large-vocabulary models, so a method that shrinks it to kilobytes while improving the accuracy-vs-cost trade-off across pretraining, supervised fine-tuning, and RL is a concrete efficiency win. `[single-source]` — a single-author claim awaiting independent replication. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **A "three-term" scaling law splits training data into steps and batch size — and recovers the optimal batch size from fewer runs** — [arXiv:2607.01487](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01487) · F. Schaipp (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  Standard scaling laws (empirical formulas predicting loss from model size and total training data) lump all the data together. This work explicitly factors the data term into training steps × batch size, fits the resulting law across many runs, and shows it correctly recovers how the optimal batch size should scale — and, usefully, can be fit robustly from far fewer training runs because it can exploit runs that used a *suboptimal* batch size.

  **Why it matters:** batch size is a costly hyperparameter to tune by brute force; a law that predicts the right value (and matches prior "critical batch size" findings) means less compute wasted on sweeps before a big run. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **CreativityNeuro steers a model's weights to boost divergent thinking and blunt "mode collapse"** — [arXiv:2607.01433](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01433) · S. Schapiro, C. F. Park, F. Sosa, L. R. Varshney (Harvard; UIUC) · `[preprint]`

  LLMs tend to give near-identical answers to open-ended prompts — the "artificial hivemind" effect, a form of mode collapse where the model keeps returning to the same few outputs. CreativityNeuro is a data-free method (no extra training data needed) that steers the weights by contrasting more- and less-creative behaviours. On the Divergent Association Task (a vocabulary-based creativity test) it improved scores by up to 14 human-percentile points, and a large human study (N=720) on the Alternative Uses Test rated its outputs higher on originality, surprise, and creativity.

  **Why it matters:** it's a lightweight, training-data-free knob for the diversity of a model's outputs, backed by an unusually large human evaluation rather than an automated proxy — relevant wherever repetitive, samey generations are the failure mode. _Announced 3 July 2026 (arXiv)._

- **OpenAI's GeneBench-Pro tests whether AI agents can make judgment calls — not just recall facts — in computational biology** — [openai.com](https://openai.com/index/introducing-genebench-pro/) · `[vendor PR]`

  GeneBench-Pro is a research-level benchmark for the higher-order judgments real computational biology demands: deciding whether a pattern is signal or noise, whether the data can even support the question being asked, and what each result implies for the next step — the kind of ambiguity that separates "can run the analysis" from "can do the science." OpenAI frames it as distinct from — and complementary to — its expert-authored LifeSciBench (covered 18 June): where LifeSciBench grades life-science reasoning, GeneBench-Pro targets agentic, judgment-heavy data analysis.

  **Why it matters:** benchmarks that reward genuine scientific judgment under ambiguity — rather than fact recall or fixed workflows — are a better test of whether "AI for science" claims hold up. As a vendor-authored benchmark, its scoring and difficulty calibration deserve independent scrutiny before the headline numbers are taken as settled. _Posted 30 June 2026._
