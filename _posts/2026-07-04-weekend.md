## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

A strongly RL-weighted week, and the standout papers share an attitude: stop treating the network as an undifferentiated blob you scale, and start finding the small piece that actually matters.

### Is One Layer Enough? Training a single transformer layer can match full-parameter RL
**[arXiv:2607.01232](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01232)** · Zijian Zhang, Rizhen Hu, Athanasios Glentis et al. (University of Minnesota; Peking University) · `[preprint]`

The authors run a systematic layer-by-layer study of reinforcement-learning post-training (RL — training a model from a reward signal rather than from labelled answers, the step that turns a base model into a reasoner) and find something surprising: training just *one* well-chosen transformer layer recovers most of — and sometimes exceeds — the gains of updating all the model's parameters. They define a "layer contribution" score and show the RL improvement concentrates in a small subset of layers, usually a single mid-stack one, while the layers near the input and output barely help. The pattern holds across seven models (the Qwen2.5 and Qwen3 families), three RL algorithms (GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization, which scores a batch of sampled answers against each other instead of training a separate value network — plus GiGPO and Dr. GRPO), and math, code, and agentic decision-making tasks, with the layer rankings strongly correlated across datasets.
*Why this matters:* If RL only really touches a handful of layers, post-training could become far cheaper and — more interestingly — far more interpretable, because it localizes *where* in the network reasoning behaviour is being installed. It reframes RL from a mysterious global rewrite into a targeted edit. Read alongside this week's daily-brief finding that GRPO's reward design can provably collapse its gradient to rank-2 ([arXiv:2606.29238](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29238)), a picture emerges of RL post-training as a much lower-rank operation than its cost suggests.

### A Hippocampus for Linear Attention: exact memory for what the recurrent state forgets
**[arXiv:2607.02303](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02303)** · Wanyun Cui (Shanghai University of Finance & Economics) · `[preprint]`

Linear-attention and state-space models (SSMs — recurrent alternatives to attention, like Mamba, that run in linear time) compress an entire input into a fixed-size recurrent state. That buys O(1) memory but loses precise recall once many key–value pairs compete for the same slots. Borrowing from neuroscience's Complementary Learning Systems idea, HOLA keeps the usual "compressive" recurrent state but bolts on a small, bounded *exact* key–value cache that stores only the associations with large prediction error, read back through a separate normalization gate for sharp retrieval — a fast lossy memory plus a small precise one, much like a cortex-and-hippocampus split. At 340M parameters trained on 15B tokens, HOLA cuts WikiText perplexity (a measure of how surprised the model is by held-out text; lower is better) from 27.32 to 22.92 — below a full-attention Transformer at 26.88 — and holds up on needle-in-a-haystack recall out to 32k tokens, 16× its training length.
*Why this matters:* It is a clean recipe for giving cheap sub-quadratic models the long-range exact recall they normally sacrifice, and it beats a full Transformer at the same scale — evidence that the efficiency/quality trade-off can be structural rather than fundamental.

### Right in the Right Way: verifiable rewards *and* human demonstrations
**[arXiv:2607.01181](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01181)** · Mehul Damani, Isha Puri, Idan Shenfeld, Jacob Andreas (MIT) · `[preprint]`

RLVR (RL with Verifiable Rewards — training against an automatic checker: does the code pass its tests, is the math answer correct) has become the workhorse of reasoning models, but it is blind to anything un-scorable, like style or diversity, which produces reward hacking (gaming the metric without doing the task) and collapse toward a single output. The authors add an adversarial generator–discriminator: a discriminator learns to tell human-written outputs from model ones and supplies a learned reward for exactly those un-verifiable axes, while the generator is trained to maximize both task correctness and this adversarial signal. Across bug-fixing (much smaller edits at equal correctness), story generation (more human-like and diverse), and a reward-hacking benchmark (misbehaviour nearly eliminated), it keeps RLVR's accuracy while fixing its ugly side effects.
*Why this matters:* A practical bridge between RL's checkable rewards and supervised fine-tuning's demonstrations — you get the accuracy of the checker without the tunnel vision it induces.

### Learning the Supports for a Categorical RL Critic
**[arXiv:2607.01880](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01880)** · Jen-Yen Chang, Takayuki Osa, Tatsuya Harada (University of Tokyo; RIKEN AIP) · `[preprint]`

Modern deep RL often turns value estimation — predicting expected future reward — into a *classification* problem over a histogram of possible returns (the "HL-Gauss" trick), which trains more stably than regressing a single number and has helped RL scale. The catch: you must hand-pick the fixed value range the histogram spans, awkward when RL's targets are non-stationary and stochastic. This paper derives an objective that *learns* the lower and upper bounds of that range jointly with the value function, and proves it is a tighter upper bound on the underlying error than the fixed-range version. Empirically the range adapts stably and matches the hand-tuned baseline across continuous-control tasks while beating it on a subset — with no interval to set.
*Why this matters:* It removes a finicky, task-specific knob from a technique that has quietly become important for scaling RL, and backs the fix with theory rather than just benchmarks.

### Maven: evidence-state rewards for long-context reasoning
**[arXiv:2607.02073](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02073)** · Yuxuan Gao, Pekka Marttinen (Aalto University) · `[preprint]`

When a model reasons over a very long document, standard RL rewards only the final answer — giving no feedback on whether an intermediate step actually improved the evidence the model is holding. Maven gives the model an editable "evidence memory" and defines an answer-conditioned value over its state, then rewards each action by its marginal contribution: *add* actions are credited by how much they improve answer support, *link* actions by evidence synergy, *drop* actions by how much removing a distractor helps. Across Llama and Qwen models on long-context benchmarks (LongBench v2, RULER), it beats outcome-only RL and produces more sufficient, less distractor-laden evidence sets.
*Why this matters:* It reframes long-context reasoning as *stateful evidence navigation* with dense, per-action credit — a promising alternative to the sparse "was the final answer right?" signal that dominates RL today.

### WorldSample: closed-loop real-robot RL with a grounded world model
**[arXiv:2607.02431](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02431)** · Y. Xue, L. Xu, Z. Liu et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

Reinforcement learning on physical robots is throttled by how expensive real trials are — each rollout gives one action–outcome path and takes real seconds of hardware time. WorldSample closes a real-to-synthetic loop: a handful of real rollouts ground a learned world model (a simulator that predicts the next camera image and reward from an action), which then generates many synthetic transitions. The trick is not trusting those blindly — a "Policy-Paced Learning" scheduler filters and paces the synthetic samples to balance useful augmentation against the model's tendency to hallucinate and overestimate value. On contact-rich, precise manipulation tasks it lifts success rate by 28% while cutting training steps by 59%, and even improves the world model's own visual fidelity.
*Why this matters:* Trial-and-error learning has been largely impractical on real robots; generating *grounded* synthetic experience without the usual hallucination collapse is a concrete step toward changing that.

### Conditional Co-Ablation: recovering hidden self-repair backups in transformer circuits
**[arXiv:2607.01940](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01940)** · Zhaoyi Gong, Ziwei Zeng, Chau Yuen, Wee Yong Lim (Nanyang Technological University) · `[preprint]`

Mechanistic interpretability — reverse-engineering the internal "circuits" a model uses — usually scores each component by *ablating* it (zeroing it out) and measuring the damage. But transformers "self-repair": knock out a primary attention head and a dormant backup silently takes over, so both look unimportant in isolation. CoAx recasts this as a recovery task, with a label-free score for how much each remaining unit's importance *grows* once a primary set is removed — exposing the second-order interaction single-unit scores miss. On GPT-2-small's canonical Indirect-Object-Identification circuit it lifts backup-head recovery from 0.33 to 0.91 (ROC-AUC, a 0–1 ranking-quality score), and the method transfers across eight models and into repair-aware pruning.
*Why this matters:* It shows "which components matter" is not a property you can read off one unit at a time — a correction that ripples into attribution, capability removal, and pruning wherever redundancy hides the real circuitry.

### Expander Sparse Autoencoders: parameter-efficient interpretability dictionaries
**[arXiv:2607.01799](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01799)** · Rodrigo Mendoza-Smith (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs — tools that unpack a model's internal activations into a large "dictionary" of human-interpretable features) need a dense decoder whose cost grows with features × model width, which becomes punishing at frontier scale. Expander SAEs constrain the decoder to a sparse *expander-graph* mask — a graph that is sparse but still well-connected — so each feature touches only a few dimensions, turning the expensive step into a cheap gather-and-reduce and learning far fewer values. On several models the method traces a clean storage-versus-fidelity frontier; at its tightest setting on Qwen2.5-3B it uses 293× fewer decoder values than a dense SAE while keeping 84% of the reconstruction quality, with identifiability theory to justify it. In prose, this continues the SAE-efficiency line that June's subspace-aware SAE work opened ([arXiv:2606.06333](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06333)) — a *distinct* paper attacking the same scaling wall from a different angle (graph sparsity rather than learned subspaces).
*Why this matters:* Interpretability tools have to run on the biggest models to be useful on the models we actually worry about; making the dictionaries hundreds of times cheaper to store is exactly the kind of unglamorous scaling fix the field needs.

### Lynx: overlapping KV-cache transfer with decoding via bit-plane speculation
**[arXiv:2607.01831](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01831)** · W. Han, G. M. Yeung, M. Barletta et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

In disaggregated serving — where the "prefill" stage that reads your prompt runs on different machines than the "decode" stage that writes the answer — a long prompt's KV cache (the stored attention keys and values the model reuses so it need not re-read earlier tokens) must be shipped across the network before decoding can even start. Lynx rejects the idea that a KV cache is indivisible: it splits each cache into a high-priority "Anchor" stream (the most significant bits, carrying the coarse attention structure) and a low-priority "Residual" stream, begins decoding speculatively as soon as the Anchor lands, then verifies against full precision once the Residual arrives. It matches aggressive 4-bit KV-cache latency while preserving full-precision accuracy, improving time-to-first-token over an 8-bit baseline by up to 1.43×.
*Why this matters:* It attacks a very real bottleneck in retrieval-augmented and agentic serving — the network transfer of huge caches — by overlapping it with compute, and does so without the accuracy hit that low-bit KV caches usually cost.

### Log_b Quant: quantizing language models in logarithmic space
**[arXiv:2607.01127](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01127)** · J. Bohn, T. Dippold, M. Koubaa et al. (TU Munich) · `[preprint]`

Quantization — storing weights at lower numeric precision (say 4-bit instead of 16-bit) to shrink and speed up a model — usually spaces its allowed values *uniformly*, which wastes precision on the rare large-magnitude weights that dominate a model's error. Log_b Quant instead places the quantization levels *logarithmically*, with an adjustable base tuned to the weights' actual heavy-tailed distribution, so more levels land where the weights actually are. At 4-bit it beats asymmetric linear quantization at tensor-wise granularity (one shared scale per weight matrix — coarse but cheap) with large memory savings.
*Why this matters:* A simple, distribution-aware tweak to the quantization grid that improves the accuracy-vs-size trade-off for private, on-device inference on consumer hardware — the un-sexy end of the stack where a lot of real deployment lives.

### Fast and Accurate Anomaly Detection in Time Series
**[arXiv:2607.02046](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02046)** · E. Mele, M. Cafaro, A. Coluccia, I. Epicoco (University of Salento) · `[preprint]`

Real-world anomaly detection — flagging the rare abnormal point in a stream — is hobbled by extreme class imbalance and, for supervised methods, scarce labels. This work proposes an *unsupervised* algorithm (no labelled anomalies needed) built on the Haar wavelet transform (a simple multi-scale decomposition of a signal) paired with a purpose-designed statistical t-test, giving the decision an explicit theoretical footing rather than a black-box score. Across an unusually large evaluation of 343 datasets it outperforms state-of-the-art unsupervised and self-supervised baselines while staying fast.
*Why this matters:* A lightweight, label-free, statistically grounded detector that beats heavier learned models at scale is attractive exactly where labels don't exist — monitoring, IoT, intrusion detection.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

### Smoking-gun evidence that big black holes are built from smaller ones
**[arXiv:2607.01121](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01121)** · Yin-Jie Li, Yuan-Zhu Wang, Shao-Peng Tang, Yi-Zhong Fan (Purple Mountain Observatory, CAS, Nanjing) · `[preprint]`

Using the full catalogue of 259 binary-black-hole mergers in the new GWTC-5 gravitational-wave dataset, the authors split the population by spin: a low-spin group (black holes born directly from collapsing stars) and a high-spin group. They then compare their masses — and find the high-spin group's mass distribution reproduces, peak for peak up to about 80 solar masses, the *predicted remnant masses* of the low-spin group. The overlap is so tight that the natural reading is that the heavy, fast-spinning holes are literally the leftovers of *earlier* mergers — "hierarchical assembly" in dense star clusters, where black holes sink to the centre and merge again and again. As a bonus, the sharp 54-solar-mass cutoff of the star-collapse population lets them back out the rate of a key nuclear reaction (carbon-12 capturing a helium nucleus to make oxygen-16), matching lab measurements.
*Why this matters:* It suggests the whole observed black-hole population can be explained by ordinary stellar collapse plus repeated mergers — no exotic primordial black holes required — and, elegantly, turns a gravitational-wave catalogue into a nuclear-physics measurement. This is the in-depth companion to the merging-black-hole mass-structure item the daily science brief flagged this week.

### A clean route to "deconfined" quantum criticality between magnetism and superconductivity
**[arXiv:2607.00762](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00762)** · Chuang Chen, Subir Sachdev (Harvard), Zi Yang Meng (University of Hong Kong) · `[preprint]`

*The stakes:* "Deconfined quantum criticality" is the flagship proposal for phase transitions that Landau's 80-year-old symmetry-breaking picture cannot describe — and whether such transitions are truly smooth ("continuous") or secretly jumpy has been fiercely debated for two decades. *The result:* the team simulates, without the notorious fermion "sign problem" that makes such simulations blow up, the transition on a square lattice between a Néel antiferromagnet (an insulating checkerboard of up- and down-spins) and a d-wave superconductor (the pairing pattern of the cuprate high-temperature superconductors) — the two phases that flank the cuprate puzzle. Their trick is a *parton* construction: split the electron into a spin-carrying piece and a charge-carrying piece, both moving in a background magnetic flux and glued together by a fluctuating gauge field (a bookkeeping force that enforces which combinations are physically real). Unlike earlier attempts, they find a genuinely *continuous* transition where both orders vanish together. *The anchor:* think of water turning to ice, except instead of one kind of order appearing, two unrelated orders — magnetism and superconductivity — switch off at the very same knife-edge point, mediated by fractional "half-electron" constituents that exist only near it. *Scope:* genuinely new is the sign-problem-free simulation showing that continuity with the right electronic spectrum; it is still an idealized lattice model, not a real cuprate.
*Why this matters:* It is a rare piece of clean numerical evidence in a foundational debate about what kinds of phase transitions nature actually allows.

### A nickel-vacancy diamond qubit with all-optical control and millisecond memory
**[arXiv:2607.02258](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02258)** · I. M. Morris, T. Alberth, L. Crooks et al. (Michigan State University; University of Leipzig; Element Six) · `[preprint]`

A scalable quantum network needs nodes that can do three things at once: talk to photons efficiently, be controlled coherently, and remember their state for a long time — and the well-studied diamond colour centres each sacrifice one. This paper demonstrates that a *nickel-vacancy* centre (a transition-metal defect in diamond, distinct from the usual nitrogen- and silicon-vacancy centres) can do all three. Its spin-orbit-protected ground state gives it intrinsic robustness, and using laser-only ("all-optical," no microwaves) dynamical decoupling — rapid control pulses that average out slow environmental noise, like continually re-tuning to cancel drift — they stretch its coherence time from 371 nanoseconds to 1.27 milliseconds, and do it at 1.65 kelvin, a temperature reachable with compact closed-cycle fridges rather than exotic cooling.
*Why this matters:* A millisecond of memory is roughly a million times the raw dephasing time — the difference between forgetting a phone number mid-dial and remembering it all day — and getting it in a warm, controllable, photon-friendly defect is exactly what quantum repeaters need. It is one device, though; entanglement and networking are not yet shown.

### Tightest test yet that neutrino mixing is "unitary" — from 8 years of IceCube
**[arXiv:2607.02355](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02355)** · Sharmistha Chattopadhyay, Anil Kumar, Sanjib Kumar Agarwalla (Institute of Physics, Bhubaneswar; UW–Madison) · `[preprint]`

The three known neutrino flavours mix through a 3×3 matrix that the Standard Model requires to be *unitary* — probabilities have to sum to one. Hidden heavy "sterile" neutrinos (hypothetical neutrinos that feel only gravity and are invisible to the weak force) would spoil that, leaking probability out of the 3×3 block. Using eight years of public IceCube DeepCore atmospheric-neutrino data — which spans a wide range of energies and Earth-crossing paths, and so is sensitive to the matter effects that non-unitarity would distort — the authors find no deviation from unitary mixing and set the most stringent bound to date on the leading parameter.
*Why this matters:* Non-unitarity is a generic fingerprint of the heavy new particles many beyond-Standard-Model theories predict (the "seesaw" mechanisms that would explain why neutrinos are so light). Tightening the bound — here, checking the probability books balance to within a few percent, using the Earth itself as the baseline — squeezes whole classes of those theories. It is a null result, but a record-setting one, and notably extracted from public data by an outside group.

### Quantum noncommutativity uniquely determines relative entropy
**[arXiv:2607.01712](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01712)** · Gilad Gour (Technion – Israel Institute of Technology) · `[preprint]`

*The stakes:* relative entropy measures how distinguishable two quantum states are, and it underpins limits on communication, thermodynamics and resource conversion — yet the standard axioms permit an *infinite* family of alternative "divergences" (the Rényi family and more), leaving open why this one particular quantity governs so much. *The result:* Gour proves that a single extra, operationally natural requirement collapses all that freedom — if a distinguishability measure is additive and must respect the betting odds set by the optimal measurement in a binary state-guessing game, it has to be exactly the Umegaki quantum relative entropy. *The anchor:* it is like discovering that among all "reasonable" ways to score a bet, only one stays self-consistent once you allow quantum states that can't be measured simultaneously. Strikingly the rigidity is *purely quantum*: classically the Rényi family survives, but quantum noncommutativity (the fact that measuring A-then-B differs from B-then-A) kills every alternative — and the proof holds single-shot, without needing infinitely many copies.
*Why this matters:* It answers a decades-old "why this entropy and not another?" question with a clean uniqueness theorem — the kind of structural result that tells you a definition wasn't an arbitrary choice.

### The oldest Milky Way stars say the universe is ~13.7 billion years old
**[arXiv:2607.00764](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00764)** · Indranil Banik, Harry Desmond et al. (University of St Andrews; University of Portsmouth) · `[preprint]`

From 247,103 Milky Way stars with high-resolution LAMOST spectra and Gaia distances, the authors isolate a clean sample of 155,600 old, metal-poor stars and reconstruct their age distribution, finding the oldest at 13.73 billion years (give or take ~0.17), stable across quality cuts. Add the ~0.2 billion years for the first stars to form and it matches the 13.6-billion-year age expected from the standard cosmological model calibrated on the cosmic microwave background.
*Why this matters:* This is a *stellar* age measurement, entirely independent of the microwave-background route, and it lands on the standard model — which is bad news for a popular class of fixes to the "Hubble tension" (the persistent ~9% mismatch between the locally-measured and CMB-inferred cosmic expansion rate) that require a *younger*, roughly 12.9-billion-year universe. When two independent clocks agree, the proposals that need them to disagree lose ground.

### Two long-standing matroid conjectures fall in a four-page note
**[arXiv:2607.02208](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02208)** · Matt Larson (Princeton University / Institute for Advanced Study) · `[preprint]`

*Context:* matroids abstract the notion of "linear independence" — a matroid is the shadow of a set of vectors that remembers only which subsets are independent. A celebrated decade of combinatorics (Adiprasito–Huh–Katz and successors) has shown many matroid-derived sequences are "log-concave" — smoothly rising then falling with no sudden dips. In a terse four-page note Larson disproves *two* natural next conjectures at once: White's conjecture (about how a matroid's algebraic ideal is generated) and Mason's conjecture (that the number of "flats" of each rank is log-concave), exhibiting an explicit counterexample with a dip.
*Why this matters:* Negative results, but sharp ones — they mark exactly where the celebrated log-concavity phenomena stop, redrawing the boundary of a very active research program.

### The explicit Gross–Zagier formula behind the "4, 7 cases" of Sylvester's cube-sum conjecture
**[arXiv:2607.01744](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01744)** · Hongbo Yin (Shandong University) · `[preprint]`

*The stakes:* "which whole numbers are a sum of two cubes of fractions?" is a textbook-simple question tied directly to the Birch–Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture, one of the Millennium Prize problems — and each solved case is hard-won. This is the analytic companion to Yin's recent proof that every prime congruent to 4 or 7 mod 9 is a sum of two rational cubes (about two-thirds of Sylvester's 1879 conjecture). Here he proves the explicit Gross–Zagier formula relating the "height" of specially constructed points on an associated elliptic curve to the slope of that curve's L-function at its centre. *The anchor:* the elliptic curve encodes the cube-sum question geometrically, and the Gross–Zagier formula is a bridge — "the L-function's slope equals the size of a specific rational point," so a nonzero slope guarantees a genuine solution exists.
*Why this matters:* It completes the analytic machinery certifying that the constructed solutions are real, closing out the technical half of a concrete, centuries-old number-theory problem.

## 🧬 Biology, biotech, neuroscience

### A chemically defined synthetic cell completes a full cell cycle
**"SpudCell"** · Nathaniel J. Gaut, Christopher Deich, Brock Cash et al.; Katarzyna P. Adamala (University of Minnesota) · `[preprint]`

The team assembled a cell-like system entirely from known, purified parts — 36 enzymes and a ~90,000-base-pair genome split across nine DNA molecules, all inside a lipid membrane — and showed it can grow, replicate its genome, divide, and even undergo selection across roughly five generations. Crucially, unlike earlier "minimal cells" that start by stripping a living organism down, SpudCell is built *bottom-up* from non-living components, so every one of its 150–200 molecule types is specified (a natural cell has millions). The authors do not claim it is alive; the claim is narrower and cleaner — the first from-scratch construct to run a complete cell cycle over multiple generations. ([bioRxiv DOI, posted 2026-07-02](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.07.01.735724); DOI/authorship confirmed via bioRxiv listing plus [Science](https://www.science.org/content/article/lab-created-spudcell-marks-major-step-toward-building-life-scratch) and [Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/for-the-first-time-a-cell-built-from-scratch-grows-and-divides-20260701/) coverage — the bioRxiv landing 403s even via proxy)
*Why this matters:* A fully-defined chassis you can engineer molecule by molecule is a long-sought milestone for both biomanufacturing and origin-of-life research — the biology equivalent of building a system you can actually read the source code of.

### Germline-targeting vaccines elicit broadly neutralizing HIV antibodies in primates
Jon M. Steichen, Patrick J. Madden, Claudia T. Flynn et al. (affiliations not listed in feed)

Two back-to-back *Nature* papers report that a "germline-targeting" immunization regimen — one that first primes the rare naive B cells capable of maturing into the right antibody, then steers them with a designed sequence of booster shots — drove broadly neutralizing antibodies (bnAbs — antibodies that neutralize many divergent viral strains at once) against HIV's vulnerable "apex" site in non-human primates. HIV's extreme diversity has blocked bnAb-based vaccines for decades; showing that a structured priming-and-boosting schedule can *reproducibly* walk the immune system toward those rare antibodies in primates is a major proof of concept. ([Nature, 2026-06-30, paper 1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10837-5) · [paper 2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10838-4))
*Why this matters:* It is the strongest primate evidence yet that rational, staged immunization — not luck — can produce the antibodies an HIV vaccine has always needed.

### COMPASS: a pan-cancer foundation model predicting immunotherapy response
Wanxiang Shen, Intae Moon, Thinh H. Nguyen et al.; Marinka Zitnik (senior author, Harvard)

COMPASS predicts whether a patient will respond to cancer immunotherapy from the tumour's bulk transcriptome (its overall RNA-expression profile — a readout of which genes are active), and — unlike today's biomarkers, which are specific to one drug or cancer type and often fail to transfer — it generalizes across many cancers and treatments. Published in *Nature Medicine*. ([DOI, 2026-07-03](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-026-04502-7))
*Why this matters:* A single, generalizable predictor of who benefits from immunotherapy could sharpen patient selection across the board — sparing non-responders a toxic, expensive, and time-consuming treatment.

### An engineered base editor permanently corrects the familial dysautonomia mutation
S. Yun, A. Chekuri et al.; B. P. Kleinstiver, C. R. R. Alves (affiliations not listed) · `[preprint]`

Familial dysautonomia is a fatal recessive neuropathy caused by a single T-to-C DNA change in the ELP1 gene that mis-splices one exon. The authors engineered a cytosine base editor (a CRISPR tool that chemically rewrites a single DNA letter *without* cutting both strands of the double helix, avoiding the riskier breaks of classic CRISPR) plus a guide RNA to correct the mutation, hitting up to ~70% correction in human cells and restoring normal splicing; a virus-delivered version produced genetic correction in a humanized mouse model. ([bioRxiv, version posted 2026-06-27](https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.11.27.625322))
*Why this matters:* A one-time genetic fix for the root cause of an untreatable disease — and a template for the many other disorders that come down to a single splicing-breaking letter.

### A splice-switching small molecule cuts prion protein by ~70%
B. Liu, B. J. Fair, Z. Kuang et al. (affiliations not listed) · `[preprint]`

Prion diseases are driven by the misfolding of prion protein, so lowering how much of it the body makes is a leading strategy. By chemically modifying the approved spinal-muscular-atrophy drug risdiplam, the authors made "CP3," a small molecule that flips on a normally-skipped "cryptic exon" in the prion gene, routing its RNA message for destruction and cutting the protein by about 70% in cells; co-dosed with a helper it lowered the protein in mouse brains. ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-27](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.26.734742))
*Why this matters:* An orally-available *small molecule* — as opposed to an injected gene therapy or antisense drug — would be a far more deliverable route to treating a uniformly fatal disease.

### Towards a virtual yeast: an AI agent modeling a eukaryotic cell
Liujia Qian, Zizhuo Zhou, Peijie Zhou et al. (affiliations not listed)

A *Nature* paper describing an AI-driven "virtual yeast" built to simulate the behaviours of a eukaryotic cell (a cell with a nucleus, unlike bacteria) — the eukaryotic counterpart to the "virtual cell" efforts gathering pace, aiming to predict how a cell responds to a perturbation before anyone touches a bench. ([DOI, 2026-07-01](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10574-9))
*Why this matters:* A working computational cell would let biologists run experiments in silico first — the same "simulate before you build" shift that reshaped engineering.

### GenPage: end-to-end generative homepage construction at Netflix
Lequn Wang, Jiangwei Pan, Linas Baltrunas · Netflix Technology Blog · 2026-06-29

Netflix describes an autoregressive approach that *generates* a personalized homepage one row at a time, each element conditioned on what's already been placed and on the user's context — moving away from the standard architecture of independently scoring rows and assembling them, toward a single model that builds the whole page. ([Netflix Tech Blog](https://netflixtechblog.com/genpage-towards-end-to-end-generative-homepage-construction-at-netflix-77146fba8a08))
*Why it's useful:* A concrete production example of applying generative, sequence-modeling ideas to whole-page recommendation and layout — a problem most recsys teams still solve with a pipeline of separate rankers.

### "'It's hard to eval' is a product smell"
Hamel Husain · hamel.dev · 2026-06-29

Husain argues that "our product is hard to evaluate" is usually a *product-design* problem, not an evals problem: an output that's hard for you to verify is hard for your users too, so designing for verifiability should come *before* building evals. He walks through three real AI products — including internal data agents — with before/after sketches, e.g. making an agent surface its intermediate queries so both users and evals can check its work. ([hamel.dev](https://hamel.dev/blog/posts/eval-smell/))
*Your read:* Worth the ten minutes if you build with LLMs. The reframing — eval difficulty as a fixable UX decision — is genuinely transferable, and the "show your work so it can be checked" principle applies well beyond AI features.

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. RL post-training is turning out to be a low-rank, localizable operation.** The single loudest signal this week: reinforcement learning may touch far less of a model than its cost implies. "Is One Layer Enough?" localizes most of RL's benefit to a *single* mid-stack layer; the daily-brief GRPO analysis shows its gradient can provably collapse to rank-2; and the categorical-critic and Maven papers both make RL more sample- and compute-efficient by tightening *where* and *how* the reward signal lands. The field is visibly shifting from "RL as an expensive, mysterious global rewrite" to "RL as a targeted, analyzable edit." If that holds, the practical payoff — cheaper post-training — matters less than the conceptual one: RL becomes something you can *inspect*, which pulls it toward the interpretability world rather than away from it.

**2. Efficiency is coming from structure, not scale.** Look at how this week's systems papers win: HOLA grafts a small *exact* memory onto a lossy recurrent state; Expander SAEs wire their dictionary like a sparse-but-connected expander graph; Log_b Quant reshapes the quantization grid to match the weights' actual distribution; Lynx splits a KV cache along *bit-planes* to overlap transfer with compute. None of these buys quality with more parameters or more data — each exploits a structural fact about the problem to beat brute force. It's a healthy counter-current to the scaling narrative, and notably most of it is aimed at the cheap, on-device, private-inference end of the stack.

**3. The foundation-model template is colonizing new data types.** Within a single week: Google's tabfm brings zero-shot, in-context prediction to *tables*; COMPASS brings it to *tumour transcriptomes* for immunotherapy response; and a "virtual yeast" pushes the pattern toward whole *cells*. The recipe — pretrain once on a modality, then generalize zero-shot to new instances of it — is escaping natural language and landing wherever there's enough structured data to pretrain on. The interesting frontier is no longer "can we build a foundation model for X" but "which X's have the data density to make it work," and biology and tabular data both just answered yes.

**4. Two fields are converging on the same wish: fully-specified systems you can read.** SpudCell's appeal is that *every molecule is known* — a cell you could, in principle, read the source of. That is precisely the interpretability community's goal for neural networks, and this week both CoAx (recovering the backup circuits that hide from naive analysis) and Expander SAEs (making the feature dictionaries cheap enough to actually deploy at scale) pushed on it. Synthetic biology building life bottom-up from defined parts, and interpretability taking trained models apart into defined features, are chasing the same thing from opposite directions: a complex system whose behaviour you can trace to components you fully understand.
