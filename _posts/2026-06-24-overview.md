## 🔬 Science

- **Quantum teleportation beats just sending the photon down a lossy line.** Teleportation moves a quantum state from A to B by consuming a pre-shared pair of entangled particles plus a classical message — no quantum particle physically travels the link. A USTC (University of Science and Technology of China) team led by Jian-Wei Pan now reports an experiment in which teleporting a single-photon state across a *lossy* channel transferred it with **higher efficiency than directly transmitting the photon** through the same channel. Why it matters: in any real fibre or free-space link photons get absorbed, and loss is the central obstacle to long-distance quantum communication. Showing that the entanglement-assisted route can outperform brute-force transmission is the kind of concrete advantage that quantum-repeater networks are designed around. ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-23](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03348-7))

- **A hydrogel that swells a sample a billionfold claims to space out individual amino acids for an ordinary microscope.** Expansion microscopy embeds a specimen in a water-absorbing polymer and physically inflates it, so detail that was too fine for light microscopy is pulled apart far enough to see. "Thousandfold expansion microscopy" (1000ExM) pushes this to a four-network interpenetrating hydrogel that expands from ~18× to **>1,000× linearly — a billionfold in volume** — after cleaving the protein backbone and anchoring side chains to the gel. The authors report that protein/peptide structure survives (checked on GFP, nanobodies, the mCLING peptide) and that *computational* analysis indicates adjacent amino-acid residues are resolved — sub-nanometre precision on a conventional fluorescence microscope. Why it matters: if it holds up experimentally, it would put near-atomic protein imaging within reach of standard lab microscopes rather than cryo-electron-microscopy facilities. Caveat: the amino-acid-resolution claim is so far a computational inference, not a direct readout. `[preprint]` ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-05-31](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.31.729018v1); reported by [Nature news, 2026-06-23](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01842-9))

- **Engineering T cells to hand-deliver gene-editing machinery to other cells.** The hardest part of therapeutic genome editing is *delivery* — getting the editing enzymes into exactly the right cells at useful efficiency. This preprint repurposes T cells (the immune system's roving, tissue-infiltrating killers) as the courier: engineered T-lymphoid cells both *produce* editing enzymes and *transfer* them to neighbouring target cells, triggered by a programmable ligand (a designed molecular "go" signal) and working either through direct cell-to-cell contact or at a distance. Why it matters: it borrows the immune cell's natural homing and target-recognition to attack the delivery bottleneck, rather than relying on viral vectors or lipid nanoparticles. Early-stage feasibility, in cells. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-23](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.21.729417v1))

- **First structure of the cell-wall "flippase" MurJ in a single-membrane pathogen.** Bacteria build their protective peptidoglycan wall from a precursor called lipid II, which has to be flipped from the inner to the outer face of the membrane — the job of an enzyme, MurJ, that is essential and present in every wall-making bacterium, and therefore a coveted antibiotic target. MurJ had been solved only in *diderm* (double-membrane, Gram-negative) bacteria; this work reports cryo-structures of MurJ from **monoderm** *Staphylococcus aureus* (the single-membrane species behind MRSA and many hospital infections), whose version diverges sharply in sequence. Why it matters: a structural blueprint of a validated-but-undrugged target in a major pathogen is exactly what structure-based antibiotic design needs. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-23](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.21.733627v1))

- **Mapping four-stranded DNA across many human genomes at once.** G-quadruplexes (G4s) are knot-like four-stranded DNA structures — not the usual double helix — that fold up at G-rich motifs covering ~1% of the genome; they help regulate when genes are transcribed and DNA is copied, but also drive mutations and instability. Until now they had mostly been studied on a *single* reference genome. Using the human **pangenome** (a graph built from many individuals rather than one reference), this preprint catalogues which predicted G4s are present or absent across population groups, measures how strongly natural selection acts on them, and gauges their gene-regulatory potential. Why it matters: G4s are themselves drug targets (notably in cancer), so knowing which are conserved and under selection versus variable across people sharpens both the biology and the targeting. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-23](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733261v1))

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **Six different "offline RL" recipes for distilling reasoning may be doing nearly the same thing to the weights.** Methods like SFT, RFT, DFT, RIFT, Offline GRPO and DPO (variants of supervised and offline reinforcement-learning fine-tuning used to pour a big teacher model's reasoning into a smaller student) are usually compared only on final accuracy. Training all six on *identical* math rollouts from one base model (Qwen3-4B, attention-only LoRA — low-rank adapters that touch only the attention layers), the authors measure the actual weight changes and find SFT, RFT and RIFT produce **near-colinear weight deltas (cosine ≥ 0.97)**. Why interesting: it suggests several methods marketed as mechanistically distinct collapse to essentially one update — a useful sanity check before the field invents a seventh acronym. `[preprint]` (arXiv:2606.23740, submitted 2026-06-24 — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23740))

- **A theory argument that vanilla diffusion models simply can't extrapolate to new combinations.** Compositional generation means taking a model trained on some conditions and asking it for a *combination* it never saw (e.g. "this object" + "that style"). This paper conjectures — with theory-guided generalization arguments plus synthetic and realistic experiments — that for certain well-motivated settings **no inference-time trick** can make a vanilla conditional diffusion model efficiently sample the composed target distribution. Why interesting: compositional generalization is a headline promise of generative models; a clean negative result that bounds what's achievable *without retraining* is more useful than another method that works only on toy cases. `[preprint]` (arXiv:2606.23920, submitted 2026-06-24 — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23920))

- **Distil the *strategy*, not the worked example.** Standard reasoning distillation makes a weak model imitate a strong model's exact solution traces — teaching *what to answer* and encouraging memorisation of instance-specific steps. Strategy-Guided Policy Optimization (SGPO) instead extracts reusable, structured *strategy descriptions* from the strong model and trains on those, aiming to transfer *how to reason*. Why interesting: if it generalises, it targets the long-standing weakness of distilled reasoners — brittleness on problems even slightly off the training distribution. `[preprint]` (arXiv:2606.24064, submitted 2026-06-24 — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24064))

- **Putting the learnable nonlinearity on the wires, in analogue hardware.** Physical neural networks compute directly with device physics for very low power, but they usually force a device's messy nonlinear response to act like a plain scalar weight. Borrowing from Kolmogorov–Arnold networks (KANs — networks that learn a function *on each connection* instead of a single multiply), this work makes each analogue connection a trainable nonlinear element, realised as band-pass filters on field-programmable analogue arrays (reconfigurable analogue chips). The payoff is task-dependent and tracks how *smooth* the target is — working for robot kinematics, continuous control, and solar-panel maximum-power-point tracking. Why interesting: a concrete route to ultra-low-power ML that leans into analogue physics rather than fighting it. `[preprint]` (arXiv:2606.23742, submitted 2026-06-24 — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23742))

- **Rethinking how we certify a network is robust when the "best" certificate is uncomputable.** A robustness certificate guarantees that no small perturbation of a given input can flip the network's prediction — geometrically, the largest distortion-box around the input that's still safe. Most methods maximise that box's *volume*, but recent results show the volume-optimal box is intractable to compute in reasonable time. This paper introduces an alternative "apothem" measure (roughly, the radius of the largest ball that fits inside the box) to get *trustworthy* certifications without chasing the impossible optimum. Why interesting: provable robustness is how safety-critical ML escapes the cat-and-mouse of adversarial examples, and a tractable, honest certificate is worth more than an optimal one you can't compute. `[preprint]` (arXiv:2606.23858, submitted 2026-06-24 — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23858))
