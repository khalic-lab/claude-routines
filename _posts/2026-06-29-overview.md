- **Proteins that grab a chosen drug molecule, designed from scratch by two neural nets talking to each other.** Designing a protein to bind a *small molecule* (a drug-sized chemical, not another protein) is hard because the sequence, the 3-D fold, and the way the ligand sits all have to be optimised at once, and existing deep-learning tools couldn't do it "zero-shot" (no prior example of that exact target). The authors pair LASErMPNN — a graph neural network that proposes a sequence to fit a given backbone-plus-docked-ligand — with a structure predictor that folds the resulting sequence around the ligand; iterating the two against each other ("neural iterative selection–expansion", NISE) converged on accurate binders and beat a comparable loop driven by a hand-built physics energy function. Why it matters: a credible route to designer proteins that sense or sequester specific drugs, metabolites or toxins on demand. [Nature, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10670-w)
- **The brain may store numbers as a high-dimensional "simplex," not a number line.** The textbook picture is a *mental number line* — quantities laid out left-to-right on a single dimension. Recording from single neurons in the human medial temporal lobe (a deep memory-and-recognition region) while people counted dots and did arithmetic, the authors instead find numerosity coded on a **simplex-shaped manifold** — a higher-dimensional shape (think a tetrahedron generalised to many corners) whose geometry lets the same neurons support far more flexible combinations than a line. Dot-arrays and written numerals evoked distinct codes that were nonetheless linked by a shared latent structure within a task. Why it matters: a concrete neural-geometry account of why humans handle numbers so nimbly. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` [bioRxiv, 2026-06-28](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.25.734462)
- **Sleep spindles appear to strengthen "activity-silent" working memory.** Working memory — holding something in mind for seconds — may not need neurons to keep firing; recent theory says it can rest in *latent synaptic states* ("activity-silent" memory) that you can briefly reactivate by perturbing the cortex (a "ping"). If those states live in synapses, sleep should be able to retune them. The authors report that **sleep spindles** — short thalamo-cortical oscillation bursts (~11–16 Hz) already tied to memory consolidation — enhance these pinged-out latent representations, linking a specific sleep rhythm to hidden memory traces. Why it matters: ties the long-debated mechanism of short-term memory to sleep-dependent synaptic recalibration. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` [bioRxiv, 2026-06-28](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.26.734777)
- **Reading neural "word-meaning" codes out of ordinary recorded conversation.** Building models of how individual neurons encode language usually needs carefully controlled lab stimuli, which caps how much and how natural the data can be. This pipeline instead mines *incidental everyday speech* from epilepsy patients with implanted electrodes — automating transcription, who-said-what diarisation, and alignment of words to neural spikes — across 21 patients, 800+ hours and ~5 million words, to estimate per-neuron tuning for word meaning. Why it matters: a path to far larger, more naturalistic datasets for speech brain–computer interfaces, without bespoke experiments. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` [bioRxiv, 2026-06-28](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.23.733980)

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

Today's first arXiv listing (announced 2026-06-29). Bias toward interpretability, efficient inference, and agents.

- **arXiv:2606.27440 (2026-06-29) — PairSAE: opening up protein-structure models with sparse autoencoders.** `[preprint]` Sparse autoencoders (SAEs — networks that decompose a model's internal activations into a few human-interpretable features) work well on transformer text embeddings but break on *pairformer* architectures (the AlphaFold-style blocks that reason over every pair of residues), where the pairwise tensor causes a quadratic blow-up of features. PairSAE first compresses the pairwise tensor into token-wise "interaction roles" via a tensor decomposition (N-mode SVD), then runs an SAE on top; on Boltz-2 activations for protein–ligand complexes it recovers features that line up with known UniProt annotations and even predict binding-affinity values. Why it matters: a first practical way to ask what structural-biology foundation models actually "know." Continues the SAE-interpretability line (a *distinct* paper from the recent SoftSAE/subspace-SAE work, not a follow-up). [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27440)
- **arXiv:2606.27510 (2026-06-29) — "The Curse of Multiple Mediators": activation patching measures more than you think.** `[preprint]` Activation patching is the workhorse of mechanistic interpretability: swap one component's activation from a "clean" to a "corrupted" run and see how much the output moves, attributing causal blame to that part (its *natural indirect effect*, NIE). Re-deriving the math from causal mediation analysis, the authors show the NIE doesn't isolate one component — it also bakes in **interaction effects**: how that component's importance depends on the state of *other* components. They demonstrate on the GPT-2 indirect-object-identification circuit that components whose role is conditional get either hidden or inflated, and prove the contamination grows with how far the patched activation moves. Their reframe: treat the interaction term as a *diagnostic* (it flags prompt-dependent, combinatorial mechanisms) rather than noise. Why it matters: a caution that many published "this head does X" claims may be patching artefacts. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27510)
- **arXiv:2606.27449 (2026-06-29) — Prism Transformer: more attention heads in deeper layers, for free.** `[preprint]` Standard multi-head attention splits the hidden width into equal-size heads at every layer, so each head always gets the same sub-dimension. The author argues this starves early layers — too little room per head to capture rich local structure — and proposes a **progressive head schedule**: few, wide heads early; many, narrow heads deep, building a local-to-global hierarchy. Crucially it's parameter- and FLOP-neutral (same weight matrices, zero extra compute). Across 124M/354M/757M models it consistently lowers validation loss and lifts zero-shot scores on PIQA, HellaSwag, ARC-Easy and WinoGrande (standard commonsense/reasoning benchmarks). Why it matters: a "free" architectural tweak that squeezes more out of the same Transformer budget — if it holds at larger scale. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27449)
- **arXiv:2606.27550 (2026-06-29) — EntMTP: spend speculation effort where the text is predictable.** `[preprint]` Multi-token prediction (MTP — predicting several next tokens at once) underpins *self-speculative decoding*, where a model drafts a few tokens ahead and then verifies them in one pass to run faster. Today's MTP models fix the drafting depth for the whole sequence, which wastes verification compute: low-entropy stretches (predictable text) could safely draft many tokens, while high-entropy points should draft conservatively. EntMTP is a **training-free** scheduler that switches between tree-shaped attention topologies based on local entropy — deeper drafting when the model is confident, shallower when it isn't. Why it matters: cheaper inference speed-ups with no retraining, slotting into existing MTP models. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27550)
- **arXiv:2606.27483 (2026-06-29) — Internalizing the Future: teaching LLM agents to imagine outcomes before acting.** `[preprint]` LLM agents are good at step-by-step decisions but stay *reactive* over long horizons — unlike humans doing "what-if" reasoning, they lack an internal world model to simulate where a plan leads. The proposal trains a single autoregressive model to write out both a predicted future *state rollout* and a plan-conditioned *success estimate* — a text version of a reinforcement-learning Q-value (the expected payoff of an action). The authors flag a "format–capability gap": naively fine-tuning on look-ahead traces yields shallow mimicry, so they add a multi-stage recipe (starting with world-model mid-training) to ground the predictions. Why it matters: a route to agents that plan by foresight rather than reacting one step at a time. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27483)
