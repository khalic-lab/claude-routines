## 🔬 Science

- **HPV vaccination has driven cervical-cancer deaths in England's youngest women to zero.** A *Lancet* analysis of national mortality data from 2001–2024 found no cervical-cancer deaths among women aged 20–24 in England across 2020–2024 — the first such five-year stretch on record — and a 69% drop in mortality for women aged 25–29, both cohorts offered the vaccine at ages 12–18. HPV (human papillomavirus) causes nearly all cervical cancer, and prior data showed falling *incidence*; this is among the first hard evidence the programme is cutting *deaths*, not just diagnoses. The authors (Queen Mary University of London) estimate ~200 deaths prevented so far, and flag a worry: only 76% of girls were vaccinated by age 15 in 2024–25, below the WHO's 90% elimination target. [The Lancet, 2026-06-25](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(26)00918-9/fulltext) · secondary: [Nature Medicine research highlight, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41591-026-00033-3)
- **Base editing in human embryos pins down what NANOG actually does in early development.** Mouse studies long implicated the transcription factor NANOG in early lineage decisions, but testing it in human embryos was blocked by the genotoxicity of nuclease-based ("cut-the-DNA") CRISPR. The team used base editing — a CRISPR variant that chemically rewrites a single DNA letter without cutting the strand — to switch NANOG off, and show it is essential for specifying the epiblast (the cells that go on to form the body). The work also reignites the ethics debate over editing human embryos for research. [Nature, primary paper, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10792-1) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-02027-0)
- **A precision test of the deuteron finds no asymmetry — bad news for theories beyond the Standard Model.** Physicists probed how the nucleus of "heavy hydrogen" (deuterium — a proton plus a neutron) responds to electric fields, searching for a permanent electric dipole moment. Such a moment would mean the particle's charge is lopsided in a way that violates time-reversal symmetry, which the Standard Model forbids at any measurable level — so detecting one would be a direct signature of new physics and could help explain why the universe has more matter than antimatter. The result is consistent with zero, tightening the limits and leaving conventional theory standing. [Phys. Rev. Lett. 136, 241801 (2026)](https://doi.org/10.1103/ns3s-ld4k) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-02036-z)
- **Apes and humans laugh to the same beat, suggesting deep evolutionary roots for vocal control.** A comparative analysis of tickle-induced laughter across humans and great apes found the same rhythmic temporal structure in the bursts, implying the fine motor control underlying laughter — and, by extension, an ingredient of speech — may have begun evolving in a common ancestor ~15 million years ago. A nice "think of laughter as a metronome shared across species" anchor; the claim rests on acoustic-rhythm patterning, not on apes producing anything like language. [Communications Biology, 2026](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42003-026-10499-z) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01994-8)
- **Math explainer: the "positive Grassmannian," the geometry that keeps turning up in physics.** A Grassmannian is the space of all flat subspaces (lines, planes…) through the origin of a higher-dimensional space; the *positive* part restricts to those with positive coordinates, which turns out to encode a surprising web of structure linking combinatorics, particle-scattering amplitudes (the "amplituhedron"), and the math of fluid waves. Quanta's piece is a guided tour of why one object shows up in so many distant fields — the kind of cross-disciplinary "why does this keep appearing?" decode worth the read. [Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-25](https://www.quantamagazine.org/what-is-the-positive-grassmannian-and-why-does-it-show-up-everywhere-20260625/)

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.26474 (submitted 2026-06-25) — RL-induced tool use traced to a single steerable feature.** [preprint] When you fine-tune a model with reinforcement learning to call external tools, *where* in the network that new ability lives has been a black box. The authors use "dedicated feature crosscoders" (a sparse-dictionary method that maps differences between a base and a fine-tuned model) and isolate a compact feature set in Qwen2.5-3B; reconstructing it lifts tool-call correctness by +31 percentage points and even transfers ~+7 pts of tool-calling to the *frozen base model* — a "capability spillover" that hints RL changes can be localized and ported without retraining. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26474)
- **arXiv:2606.26620 (submitted 2026-06-25) — an open suite of sparse autoencoders for the Qwen3 family.** [preprint] Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) decompose a model's tangled internal activations into many individually interpretable "features," but training them is costly and few open ones exist. This release covers Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B across residual-stream, MLP and attention sites, with reconstruction and model-recovery metrics — and a worked example steering the model toward refusal via selected features. A practical infrastructure drop for interpretability researchers rather than a single new finding. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26620)
- **arXiv:2606.26836 (submitted 2026-06-25) — benchmarks may understate model ability by ~82%, if you let multiple models and samples compete.** [preprint] Standard benchmarks report one model, one run. The authors build a "Capability Frontier" — the best score achievable at each cost level if an oracle picks the right model and best-of-N generation per question — across 21 LLMs and 16 benchmarks, and correct for both single-model underestimation and noisy-max overestimation. Correcting for single-model evaluation alone cut the error rate 54%. Why it matters: a lot of "the frontier has plateaued" intuition rests on a measurement convention that hides routing-and-resampling gains. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26836)
- **arXiv:2606.26587 (submitted 2026-06-25) — squeezing LLM activations to 4-bit floats without the usual accuracy cliff.** [preprint] FP4 (4-bit floating-point) quantization of activations is fast on new accelerators but breaks on input-dependent "outlier" values that blow up the per-block scale. SharQ is training-free: it splits each activation into a sparse outlier "backbone" (sent to a sparse FP4 matrix multiply) and a dense residual computed relative to the quantized backbone, sharing one FP4 weight payload. The payoff is cheaper inference memory/bandwidth on commodity hardware without a separate fine-tune. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26587)
- **arXiv:2606.26463 (submitted 2026-06-24) — teaching an RL agent how long to think when the clock keeps running.** [preprint] Standard reinforcement learning lets the world pause while the agent deliberates; reality doesn't. The authors study "variable-delay real-time RL," where the environment advances while you plan, and train a lightweight gating policy on top of a planner to pick a *state-dependent* thinking budget — long deliberation only when it's worth it. It beats fixed-budget and heuristic baselines on real-time Pac-Man, Tetris, Snake, Speed Hex and Speed Go, and transfers to a split setup with agent and environment on separate GPUs. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26463)
- **arXiv:2606.26300 (submitted 2026-06-24) — why rewarding coding agents reliably is the hard part now.** [preprint] A position-and-analysis paper arguing the classic "verifying is easier than solving" intuition has flipped for coding agents: strong models generate plausible solutions easily, but every verifier is only a proxy for human intent, and RL optimization actively widens the proxy-vs-intent gap (reward hacking, signal saturation). It frames verifier quality along three axes — scalability, faithfulness, robustness — argues you can't max all three at once, and dissects four reward constructions (test-based, rubric-based, and others). Useful conceptual map for anyone building RL reward signals for agents. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26300)
