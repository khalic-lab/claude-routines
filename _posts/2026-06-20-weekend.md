## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

A theme runs through several of the reinforcement-learning entries below: **RLVR** (reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards — training on a checker's right/wrong signal, e.g. "is this math answer correct?", rather than on human ratings) has a *credit-assignment* problem. When the only reward arrives at the end of a long reasoning chain, how do you decide which of the hundreds of intermediate tokens deserved the praise or blame? This week's papers attack that — and the related problem of exploring efficiently — from several angles. (A fourth approach, VIMPO's critic-free value-implicit optimization, ran in the 2026-06-19 cyber+papers daily and is not repeated here.)

### GraphPO — letting parallel reasoning attempts share what they discover
**[arXiv:2606.18954](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18954)** · Yuliang Zhan, Xinyu Tang, Jian Li et al. · `[preprint]`
Standard RLVR samples several answers to the same question independently, then rewards the ones that land correctly. The waste is obvious once named: independent attempts re-tread near-identical intermediate steps, burning compute re-exploring the same ground, and a single final-answer reward gives no hint about *which* steps helped. Tree-based methods share common prefixes, but their branches still expand in isolation — two branches that wander into the same reasoning state can't tell each other. GraphPO replaces the tree with a graph: when different reasoning paths converge on a similar intermediate state, they're merged into one node, so information (and the credit signal) flows across attempts instead of being siloed.
*Why this matters:* It's a structural efficiency win for the most expensive part of training reasoning models — the search — and a more faithful picture of reasoning as a graph of states, not a set of independent monologues.

### Direct Advantage Estimation, now for the partially observed world
**[arXiv:2606.20411](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20411)** · (DAE extension) et al. · `[preprint]`
Direct Advantage Estimation (DAE) is a known trick for squeezing more learning out of fewer environment interactions ("sample efficiency"), but it carried two crippling assumptions: the agent had to see the *full* environment state, and it had to model transition probabilities explicitly — prohibitively expensive when observations are high-dimensional (images, say). This paper extends DAE's theory to **POMDPs** (partially observable Markov decision processes — the realistic setting where the agent sees only noisy, incomplete glimpses of the world) with minimal changes, and swaps the costly transition model for a compact *discrete latent dynamics model* (a small learned world-model operating over a handful of abstract states rather than raw pixels).
*Why this matters:* Sample efficiency is the binding constraint for RL in robotics and any setting where real interactions are slow or costly; relaxing the full-observability assumption is what moves a method from benchmark to plausibly-deployable.

### CRAX — a safe-RL benchmark fast enough to actually iterate on
**[arXiv:2606.20376](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20376)** · (CRAX) et al. · `[preprint]`
"Safe RL" studies agents that must maximise reward *subject to* constraints — don't tip the robot over, don't exceed a speed limit — which is how you'd want an autonomous system trained. Progress has been throttled by benchmarks: the realistic 3D-physics ones run on CPU and are agonizingly slow, so researchers can't run enough experiments. CRAX rebuilds constrained-RL environments on **MuJoCo XLA (MJX)** — a physics engine that runs on GPU/TPU via JAX — and reports up to ~100× speedups over CPU baselines, with six environment suites and realistic 3D dynamics.
*Why this matters:* Benchmarks that are 100× faster don't just save time; they change what experiments are *possible*, which is historically how subfields accelerate. A workhorse contribution rather than a conceptual one.

### Quantile of Means — principled exploration without the usual bookkeeping
**[arXiv:2606.20107](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20107)** · (Quantile of Means) et al. · `[preprint]`
To explore well, RL agents need to know what they *don't* know. The theoretically optimal way is count-based uncertainty (track how often you've seen each state), but it's impractical in large or continuous problems, and the popular practical alternative — training an *ensemble* of value estimates and using their disagreement as a proxy for uncertainty — has worked without a theoretical guarantee. This paper builds a quantile-based ensemble method for finite-horizon MDPs that is provably *minimax-optimal* (achieves the best worst-case regret bound, up to constants) while remaining count-free and simple.
*Why this matters:* It puts a theoretical floor under ensemble exploration — the thing practitioners already do — closing a long-standing gap between what works and what's provably good.

### Lie-Algebra Attention — when the token *is* a geometric transformation
**[arXiv:2606.20547](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20547)** · Przemysław Musialski · `[preprint]`
This is the week's most unusual architecture idea. In ordinary attention, a token carries a feature vector and the model *learns* how strongly any two tokens should interact. Here the token is stripped down to a bare element of a **matrix Lie group** — think of it as a pure geometric transformation (a rotation, a rigid pose, an affine warp) with no attached feature payload. The interaction strength between two such tokens isn't learned at all; it's the *closed-form distance* between their relative transformation (computed as g_i⁻¹g_j, the canonical "how do I get from pose i to pose j") measured by the natural norm on the group's Lie algebra (the linearized "velocity space" of the group near the identity). Notably it reaches the full affine groups that earlier geometry-aware attention schemes had to exclude.
*Why this matters:* For data that *is* geometry — robot poses, molecular conformations, camera trajectories — baking the exact relative-geometry invariant into attention, rather than hoping the model learns it, is both more principled and more sample-efficient. A genuinely novel construction, though so far a theoretical/architectural proposal more than a benchmarked system.

### What safety-aligned LLMs actually learn from "mixed compliance" examples
**[arXiv:2606.20508](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20508)** · (mixed compliance study) et al. · `[preprint]`
It's known that showing a model a few in-context examples of it complying with requests can "jailbreak" it (coax it past its safety training). This paper asks a sharper question: do benign examples (harmless request → helpful answer) and harmful examples (harmful request → harmful answer) act the same way? Across four models they find they don't — benign demonstrations sometimes *reduce* and sometimes *increase* harmful compliance depending on the model, so they aren't interchangeable. They further localize where the vulnerability is installed: **preference optimization** (the alignment stage, e.g. DPO/RLHF-style tuning that teaches the model which answers humans prefer) is the critical training phase shaping this behaviour.
*Why this matters:* Alignment is only as good as our understanding of what it actually teaches; pinning the failure to a specific training stage is the kind of mechanistic handle that makes safety tuning improvable rather than mysterious.

### Contagion Networks — when one biased LLM judge infects a whole agent system
**[arXiv:2606.20493](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20493)** · (Contagion Networks) et al. · `[preprint]`
Increasingly, LLMs grade other LLMs ("LLM-as-judge") inside multi-agent systems. This paper formalizes a worry: if one evaluator has a systematic bias (say it over-rewards verbose, structured answers), does that bias *spread* through a network of interacting agents? In a controlled 3-agent setup using DeepSeek-chat with three evaluator-bias profiles, they measure a "Cross-Agent Contagion Matrix" and find biases consistently propagate between agents (contagion coefficients of 0.16–0.35) even when every agent runs the same underlying model. They identify distinct propagation regimes governed by the spectral radius (the largest eigenvalue of the contagion matrix — a measure of whether a perturbation amplifies or dies out across the network).
*Why this matters:* As production systems chain LLM evaluators together, a single mis-calibrated judge could quietly skew the whole pipeline; this gives a vocabulary and a measurable quantity for catching it. Small-scale (3 agents, one model) — a framework and a warning, not yet a field measurement.

### Tri-Info — telling when a robot policy is about to fail, from information theory alone
**[arXiv:2606.19998](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19998)** · (Tri-Info) et al. · `[preprint]`
**VLA models** (vision-language-action models — networks that take camera input and a language instruction and output robot motor commands) are black boxes whose mistakes can break things in the physical world, so knowing *when to distrust* one matters. The authors observe that successful and failed rollouts differ in their information-theoretic signature, and derive three signals — whether the policy's actions stay diverse, stay temporally consistent, and stay coupled to actual state changes — that together flag impending failure. Across six VLA models and three benchmark environments, the signals match the best existing baselines in-domain while generalizing better out-of-domain.
*Why this matters:* Failure detection that's model-agnostic and grounded in information theory (rather than a bespoke classifier per robot) is exactly what's needed before VLA models can be trusted near people.

### Sparsity, Superposition, and Forgetting — a toy world to watch catastrophic forgetting happen
**[arXiv:2606.20431](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20431)** · Jan Wasilewski, Jędrzej Kozal, Michał Woźniak · `[preprint]`
"Catastrophic forgetting" — a network learning task B and clobbering what it knew about task A — is easy to observe and hard to mechanistically explain, because real datasets tangle many causes together. This paper builds a deliberately synthetic world with ground-truth latent features and tunable knobs for *sparsity* (how few features are active at once) and *overlap*, then watches how representations decay. The key lens is **superposition** — the phenomenon where a network packs more features than it has neurons by letting them share directions in activation space — and they fit sparse dynamical equations (via SINDy, a method for discovering governing equations from data) linking forgetting to the degree of superposition and how often a feature was exposed.
*Why this matters:* It turns "forgetting" from a vague pathology into something with measurable drivers, in a setting clean enough to test interpretability claims — a useful methodological scaffold for continual-learning research.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

### Cosmology may have settled the neutrino mass ordering
**[arXiv:2606.18987](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18987)** · Raul Jimenez, Carlos Peña Garay, Fergus Simpson et al. · `[preprint]`
Neutrinos come in three types and we know the three mass states are split into a close pair plus one outlier — but not whether the outlier sits *above* the pair ("normal" ordering) or *below* it ("inverted"). Lab experiments have chased this for two decades. This team combines **DESI DR2** galaxy-clustering data (which limits the *summed* neutrino mass, because heavier neutrinos smooth out cosmic structure) with neutrino-oscillation results and finds the summed mass below 0.064 eV — above the floor the normal ordering requires (~0.059 eV) but below the minimum the inverted ordering allows (~0.099 eV). They report a Bayes factor against the inverted hierarchy exceeding 460 (decisive on conventional scales), robust across four different statistical-prior choices.
*Why this matters:* If it holds, cosmology has answered a fundamental particle-physics question that dedicated detectors couldn't — and it pushes the target for neutrinoless-double-beta-decay searches into a harder, few-meV regime. `[single-source]`

### Is dark energy just the vacuum, reacting to an expanding universe?
**[arXiv:2606.20036](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20036)** · Dong Ha Lee, Carsten van de Bruck, Eleonora Di Valentino et al. · `[preprint]`
DESI's recent hints that dark energy might be *weakening* over time sent theorists hunting for exotic new fields. This paper argues you may not need one. It proposes the acceleration comes from the **QCD vacuum** — the roiling ground state of the strong nuclear force, which is never truly empty — responding to spacetime's expansion, with no new particle and no new propagating degree of freedom. They build the first full cosmological implementation and fit it against CMB data (Planck + ACT + SPT-3G), DESI baryon-acoustic-oscillation measurements, and supernova catalogues, finding it matches the DESI-preferred late-time behaviour as well as the standard "evolving dark energy" parametrization — and reproduces an apparent "phantom crossing" (the dark-energy pressure dipping into a regime that usually triggers theoretical instabilities) without those instabilities.
*Why this matters:* It's a candidate to dissolve the "dark energy crisis" using known physics rather than a fifth force. Provocative and single-team — exactly the kind of claim that needs independent replication. `[single-source]`

### Vine Codes — cheaper quantum error correction that today's chips could actually wire
**[arXiv:2606.20263](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20263)** · Georgia M. Nixon, Campbell K. McLauchlan, Charles C. L. van Rest et al. · `[preprint]`
Fault-tolerant quantum computing needs **error-correcting codes** that bundle many noisy physical qubits into one reliable "logical" qubit. The front-runner, the surface code, only needs neighbour-to-neighbour connections (which superconducting chips can build) but is hugely wasteful — hundreds of physical qubits per logical one. More efficient **qLDPC codes** (quantum low-density parity-check codes, which spread the error-checks sparsely) usually demand long-range wiring real hardware can't deliver. "Vine Codes" are qLDPC codes that live on an ordinary flat square grid using only the native nearest-neighbour gates superconducting qubits already have, plus a few routing qubits. Concrete examples cut qubit counts by up to ~28% versus the surface code at equal protection, and beat it in noise simulations at a realistic 10⁻³ per-gate error rate.
*Why this matters:* The qubit-overhead of error correction is *the* bottleneck between today's machines and useful ones; this narrows the gap between qLDPC's promise and what hardware can physically connect.

### A hard limit on how precisely you can characterize a noisy quantum computer
**[arXiv:2606.20535](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20535)** · Itai Arad, Zhili Chen, Naixu Guo, Patrick Rebentrost · `[preprint]`
A **Lindbladian** is the equation governing a realistic quantum system — one that both evolves coherently *and* leaks information to its environment (decoherence). To calibrate hardware you'd like to learn all its terms, both the clean (Hamiltonian) and the noisy (dissipative) parts, from the outside. The authors give an efficient algorithm to do exactly that — run the evolution briefly, reconstruct the dynamics from random product-state inputs and random measurements, no ancillas needed, with cost growing only logarithmically in qubit count. The sharp result is the *lower bound*: they prove that the "Heisenberg-limited" precision achievable when learning a clean Hamiltonian becomes **information-theoretically impossible** once you must also estimate the noise — a single dephasing qubit already forces worse scaling, even with unlimited cleverness.
*Why this matters:* It's a fundamental statement about measurement itself — the act of characterizing quantum noise is rate-limited *by* that noise — and it bounds how well any calibration scheme can ever do.

### A multi-year proof program connecting geometry to L-functions wraps up
**[arXiv:2606.19158](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19158)** · Ziqi Guo · `[preprint]`
*Context:* Some of the deepest results in number theory are exact bridges between **geometry** (shapes and spaces) and **analysis** (functions like the Riemann zeta and its cousins). The Gross–Zagier formula and the Kudla program are famous examples. This paper completes a three-part program proving such a bridge for **unitary Shimura varieties** — high-dimensional geometric spaces that package arithmetic information. The theorem pins the "modular height" (a number measuring the arithmetic complexity of the variety) exactly to the logarithmic derivative of a **Hecke L-function** (an analytic object encoding deep number-theoretic data). Think of it as a higher-dimensional descendant of the classical Chowla–Selberg formula. The new contribution computes a certain arithmetic degree and matches it to the "holomorphic projection" of an Eisenstein–theta series, closing the proof with the two prior papers.
*Why this matters:* It's concrete forward progress on the arithmetic-geometry side of the Langlands-adjacent web of conjectures — the structural bridges that later become tools for the bigger questions.

### Watching supercurrents flow in threads through a kagome superconductor
**[arXiv:2606.20157](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20157)** · Matthijs Rog, Tycho J. Blom, Reinier Q. Regter et al. · `[preprint]`
The **kagome** metals AV₃Sb₅ (built from a lattice of corner-sharing triangles — the kagome pattern) show a baffling pile of superconducting oddities that simple theory says shouldn't exist, including a zero-magnetic-field "diode" effect (current that flows more easily one way than the other). The missing clue was *spatial*: where does the supercurrent physically go? Using a **SQUID microscope** (a scanning magnetometer sensitive enough to image where currents flow), the authors find the current isn't smooth — it runs through a network of narrow filaments behaving like linked Josephson junctions (weak superconducting links), which naturally reproduces the weird transport. The channels appear right at the superconducting transition and are far weaker in clean, undoped samples, tying the effect to doping, disorder, and the competing charge-order in these materials.
*Why this matters:* It converts an abstract pile of "anomalous" measurements into a concrete physical picture — current in threads — giving experimentalists a real handle on how superconductivity and charge order coexist and compete.

## 🧬 Biology, biotech, neuroscience

- **Single neurons in the human cortex encode the grammatical building blocks of language.** Recording from individual neurons in the frontotemporal cortex of people as they spoke, researchers found distinct cells tracking parts of speech, word-to-word grammatical relationships, and higher-order syntactic structure (phrase boundaries and sequence) — a cellular-resolution map of how the brain assembles a sentence. They paired the recordings with natural-language-processing models to decode what each neuron represented. A landmark look at syntax at single-cell scale, though it rests on recordings from a small number of neurosurgical patients. [Nature, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10691-5)
- **Developing brain neurons routinely shatter their own DNA squeezing through tight spaces — and survive.** Neurons migrating through the forming mouse cerebral and cerebellar cortex sustain massive DNA double-strand breaks from mechanical stress as they push through narrow gaps, yet repair them cleanly (via non-homologous end-joining — a fast DNA-mending pathway) without the nuclear-envelope rupture or cell death that the same damage causes in cancer cells. It reframes a type of DNA damage normally treated as purely pathological as a tolerated, normal feature of brain development. [Nature, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10648-8)
- **A "chromatin trace" method reads out the epigenetic program a neuron ran while forming a memory.** The team built ChromTRAP, a single-cell multi-omics approach (simultaneously profiling gene expression, DNA accessibility, histone marks, and binding of the activity marker FOS) that retrospectively flags the sparse neuronal ensembles activated during fear learning in mice. It showed learning-specific genes are switched on through region- and cell-type-specific *enhancer* remodeling (enhancers = regulatory DNA switches) rather than a generic activity response. A new framework linking experience to layered gene regulation. Preprint, mouse. [bioRxiv, 2026-06-17](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.16.732522) `[preprint]`
- **A delivery platform hits one brain cell type and spares the others by exploiting surface transporters.** ExACT screens small-molecule chemistries whose uptake depends on which membrane transporters a cell expresses, yielding compounds that selectively enter neurons, astrocytes, or brain blood-vessel cells in mice. The authors linked these carriers to antisense oligonucleotides (short DNA-like strands that silence a target gene) and to small drugs without killing their activity, and even installed a "synthetic entry port" into otherwise-inaccessible neurons via gene therapy. A promising precision-pharmacology idea. Preprint, mostly mouse plus human iPSC-derived cells. [bioRxiv, 2026-06-18](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.14.732141) `[preprint]`
- **An AI diffusion model designs cancer-targeting T-cell receptors that worked in a dish.** TCRDiff generates the antigen-binding loops of T-cell receptors (the molecules T cells use to recognize a specific target) conditioned on a given peptide-MHC target, using the same denoising-diffusion approach behind image generators. In-vitro assays confirmed designed receptors specifically recognized the MAGE-A3 tumour epitope. A proof-of-concept for generative immunotherapy design — early in-vitro validation only. [bioRxiv, 2026-06-14](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.10.730756) `[preprint]`

## 🛡️ Cybersecurity research of the week

A tight, themed week: the two most consequential items are both about **AI tooling becoming attacker real estate**, and a third about the ML supply chain's trust assumptions failing open.

- **picklescan can be tricked into passing a malicious model (CVE-2026-53874).** `picklescan` is the scanner many ML pipelines — Hugging Face among them — rely on to vet pickle files (Python's native object-serialization format, which can smuggle executable code) before loading a model. Researchers showed it could be bypassed by nesting `eval` calls under callable objects via `getattr`, so a poisoned model *passes the scan* but still executes attacker code the moment it's unpickled on `torch.load()`. CVSS 9.8 (critical; unauthenticated code execution on model load). Fixed in 1.0.1. *Why it matters:* this is the ML supply-chain threat model exactly — your "scanned, therefore safe" assumption fails open, which is the worst way for a security control to fail. [VulnCheck advisory, 2026-06-17](https://www.vulncheck.com/advisories/picklescan-arbitrary-code-execution-via-obfuscated-eval-call) `[via snippet]` `[single-source]`
- **Langflow path-traversal RCE actively exploited by a nation-state group (CVE-2026-5027).** Langflow, a popular self-hosted AI-agent builder, fails to sanitize the `filename` field on its file-upload endpoint, letting unauthenticated attackers write files anywhere on disk via `../` sequences — escalating to RCE (remote code execution — running attacker code on the server) through cron injection or config overwrite. CVSS 8.8 (high; unauth file-write → root). VulnCheck confirmed in-the-wild exploitation, Censys counted ~7,000 exposed instances, and the activity has been tied to the Iranian state group MuddyWater. *Why it matters:* self-hosted AI tooling is now exploited within days of being exposed to the internet, by serious actors. [CSO Online, 2026-06-19](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4185063/langflow-rce-under-active-attack-months-after-a-patch-was-shipped.html) `[actively-exploited]`
- **Microsoft 365 Copilot had a missing-authentication information-disclosure flaw (CVE-2026-54130).** Microsoft patched a missing-authentication-for-critical-function bug in M365 Copilot that let an unauthorized attacker disclose information over the network. CVSS 9.8 (critical; unauth data disclosure). No confirmed exploitation, but it's another data point that LLM-assistant surfaces inherit classic authorization bugs with an outsized blast radius — Copilot can see a great deal of an organization's tenant data. [Microsoft MSRC, 2026-06](https://msrc.microsoft.com/update-guide/vulnerability/CVE-2026-54130) `[single-source]` `[vendor PR]`

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

Four threads I noticed pulling across this week's reading.

**1. ML's center of gravity this week was the credit-assignment problem in RLVR — and the field is splitting into camps.** GraphPO, the Direct-Advantage-Estimation extension, Quantile-of-Means and REVES (plus VIMPO and the entropy-collapse/test-time-verification work in this week's dailies) are all circling the same realization: a single right-or-wrong reward at the end of a long reasoning chain is too blunt a teaching signal. What's striking is that the proposed fixes are genuinely *different* in kind — derive a per-step value implicitly from the policy itself; share exploration across parallel attempts via a graph; put a theoretical floor under ensemble-based exploration; mine "near-miss" intermediate steps as training data. This is what a subfield looks like just after it agrees on the problem and before it agrees on the answer. My read: expect a consolidation paper within a couple of months that benchmarks these against each other under one protocol — and watch for the boring-but-decisive practical question of which one is *cheapest*, since this week's dailies already flagged that a bigger initial compute budget sometimes matches the clever method.

**2. AI tooling has crossed from research artifact into security liability — and it's inheriting the worst failure modes of ordinary software.** The cyber section reads like a warning shot: a model scanner (picklescan) that fails *open*, a self-hosted agent-builder (Langflow) exploited by a nation-state within days of exposure, and an LLM assistant (Copilot) with a textbook missing-authorization bug. The connective tissue is that AI systems don't get a security pass for being new; they get a *larger blast radius* for the same old bugs, because a poisoned model runs arbitrary code on load and an assistant can see the whole tenant. The uncomfortable corollary to thread 1: the same week the research community is making models better at reasoning, the deployment community is learning that the surrounding plumbing is soft.

**3. In fundamental physics, "known physics" kept reclaiming territory from "new physics."** Two of the week's biggest cosmology claims argue *against* exotic explanations: existing cosmological data (DESI + oscillations) may already settle the neutrino mass ordering without a new experiment, and DESI's tantalizing dark-energy wobble might be the QCD vacuum responding to expansion rather than a fifth force or new field. Both are single-team and need replication — but they sit on the conservative side of a recurring tension in physics: when an anomaly shows up, the exciting reading is "new ingredient," and the often-correct reading is "better accounting of ingredients we already have." Worth holding both in mind as the dark-energy story develops.

**4. Interpretability is quietly retooling around synthetic worlds with known ground truth.** The superposition-and-forgetting toy framework here, and the critical-percolation synthetic-data model in this week's daily, reflect the same methodological bet: instead of probing a messy real model and arguing about what you found, build an analytically tractable artificial setting where the true features are known by construction, so an interpretability claim can be *checked* rather than asserted. It's a maturing instinct — the field borrowing the controlled-experiment discipline that the rest of science takes for granted.
