## 🔭 Physics, chemistry, math & quantum

### Chiral superconductivity in graphene gets a full set of switchable "flavours" {#st-56c3d3a52504}
**[arXiv:2607.06520](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06520)** · Z. Hua, S. Ye, T. Han et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`
Superconductivity in rhombohedral graphene — several carbon sheets stacked in a staircase pattern — is unusual because it grows out of a magnetic "quarter-metal" parent state and appears to be *chiral*: the electron pairs circulate with a built-in handedness that breaks time-reversal symmetry (the physics that normally lets you run a movie of the electrons backwards and see nothing odd). This transport study on hexalayer graphene reports a new superconducting state and shows that its chirality and its spin–valley character can be flipped with several independent "knobs" (magnetic field, carrier density, displacement field). Chiral superconductors are of interest because they can, in principle, host non-Abelian anyons — exotic quasiparticles whose braiding could encode a qubit that is intrinsically protected from noise. The result is an experimental control demonstration, not a claim of anyons; the significance is that a single tunable material now exposes the full symmetry palette people had only seen piecemeal.
*Why it matters:* one of the most-watched candidate platforms for topological quantum computing just became far more controllable in the lab.

### A clean proof that "quantum proofs" really are more powerful than classical ones {#st-f23ea9abb48e}
**[Quanta, 6 July 2026](https://www.quantamagazine.org/researchers-reveal-the-power-of-quantum-proofs-20260706/)** · reporting on Bostanci, Haferkamp, Nirkhe & Zhandry ([arXiv:2511.09551](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.09551), Nov 2025, STOC 2026) and Bostanci, Huang & Vaikuntanathan ([arXiv:2602.09385](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.09385), Feb 2026)
A long-open question in complexity theory: if a hard problem has a short certificate that a fast checker can verify, does it help for that certificate to be a fragile *quantum* state rather than an ordinary string of bits? The two classes are QMA (quantum Merlin–Arthur — verifiable with a quantum proof) and QCMA (verifiable with a classical proof). This week's Quanta explainer decodes a pair of recent results that finally separate them: both construct a "classical oracle" — an idealised black-box subroutine — relative to which QMA is provably strictly larger than QCMA, meaning there are tasks a quantum witness can certify that no classical witness can. The February paper (Vaikuntanathan and colleagues) reaches the same separation more simply using good error-correcting codes. The honest scope: this is a *relativized* separation (true given the oracle), the standard first step toward — but not yet a proof of — an unconditional separation in the real world.
*Why it matters:* it pins down, in a rigorous setting, that quantum information is not just faster to compute with but genuinely more expressive as *evidence* — relevant to how future quantum systems could prove they did what they claim.

### A half-billion-year-old fossil traces where spiders' fangs came from {#st-69ee3673ee09}
**[Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10713-2)** · Y. Liu, L. Lustri, H. Mai, M. Williams et al. (Yunnan University, Kunming; University of Leicester) · published 2026-07-01
Chelicerates — spiders, scorpions, horseshoe crabs and their kin — are defined by their *chelicerae*, the small pincer/fang appendages at the front of the head, and (in aquatic forms) by *book gills*. Where those structures came from has been contested. Using X-ray microtomography to reconstruct the three-dimensional anatomy of *Urokodia*, a Cambrian arthropod, the authors argue the chelicerae evolved from the grasping "great appendages" of megacheirans (an early Cambrian group) and the book gills from their trunk limbs, filling in the morphological transition that intermediate fossils had left unresolved.
*Why it matters:* it strengthens a specific, testable story for the deep origin of one of the most successful animal body plans on Earth.

### How a bacterium sneaks *inside* its host — a clue to how symbioses get started {#st-606224e03933}
**[Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10711-4)** · G. P. Maeda, A. Z. Xue, E. W. Yu et al. (University of Texas at Austin; Max Planck Institute for Chemical Ecology, Jena) · published 2026-07-01
*Buchnera* is the obligate endosymbiont of aphids — a bacterium that lives permanently inside specialised host cells and that the insect cannot survive without. How such non-culturable symbionts first invade and are tolerated by host cells has been a black box. The authors identify a previously uncharacterised *Buchnera* protein, SyeA, that is secreted into the host cytoplasm, resembles the effector proteins that disease-causing bacteria use to manipulate cells, was already present in the ancestral *Buchnera*, and is essential for the symbiont's transmission to the next generation. In other words, an ancient intracellular partnership appears to rely on the same molecular toolkit as bacterial pathogenesis.
*Why it matters:* it blurs the line between "infection" and "partnership," and hints at how the intracellular symbioses that ultimately gave rise to organelles like mitochondria could have first taken hold.

### Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS carries nitrogen and carbon unlike any Solar System comet {#st-8c5fcd9ae7d0}
**[ESO release, 6 July 2026](https://www.eso.org/public/news/eso2608/)** `[new source]` · underlying paper [Nature Astronomy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02921-7) · C. Opitom, J. Manfroid, D. Hutsemékers, E. Jehin, M. M. Knight et al. (University of Edinburgh, Royal Observatory; University of Liège) · published 2026-07-06
3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object — a body that formed around another star and is now passing through the Solar System — after 1I/ʻOumuamua and 2I/Borisov. Using the CN molecule seen in spectra from ESO's Very Large Telescope, the team measured two isotopic ratios: a carbon ratio ¹²C/¹³C = 151 (roughly Solar-System-like) but a nitrogen ratio ¹⁴N/¹⁵N ≈ 363 — much higher (i.e. much *less* of the heavy ¹⁵N) than the ~150 typical of Solar System comets. Because these ratios are set by the temperature and radiation of the environment a body forms in, the anomalous nitrogen points to formation in a cold, distant region of its home system. This is the first nitrogen-isotope measurement for any interstellar object, and it extends — with a different molecule and a different team — an earlier July study that found 3I/ATLAS's *water* is extremely deuterium-rich; both independently paint 3I/ATLAS as chemically exotic relative to home-grown comets.
*Why it matters:* interstellar objects are our only direct physical samples of other planetary systems, and 3I/ATLAS keeps refusing to look like anything from our own.

### Quaoar's shape and density, nailed down by 14 years of stars blinking out {#st-757cf76f2948}
**[arXiv:2607.06450](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06450)** · G. Margoti, F. Braga-Ribas, J. L. Ortiz, B. Sicardy et al. (affiliation not listed) · published 2026-07-07 · `[preprint]`
When a distant body passes in front of a background star (a *stellar occultation*), the star briefly winks out, and timing that blink from several sites on Earth gives the body's size and shape far more precisely than direct imaging can. Combining 28 occultations of the trans-Neptunian object (50000) Quaoar recorded from 2018–2025 with nine earlier events, the authors fit an oblate (slightly squashed) ellipsoid: equatorial radii of ~566 km, a polar radius of ~511 km, an equivalent diameter of 1094 ± 5 km, and a geometric albedo (reflectivity) of ~0.13. The tight shape constraint sharpens estimates of Quaoar's bulk density and confirms it holds essentially no global atmosphere — notable for a world already famous for hosting a ring system beyond where standard theory says rings should survive.
*Why it matters:* precise bulk properties for individual Kuiper-belt worlds are the raw material for testing how the outer Solar System's dwarf planets formed and evolved.

## 🧠 Why it matters

- <a id="st-3d766a2a99b2" class="st-a"></a>**Our Solar System is a biased sample, and this week keeps proving it.** 3I/ATLAS's odd nitrogen and carbon isotopes — the first such measurement for an interstellar visitor — join a broader run of results (JWST's puzzling population of very early, very red galaxies and over-massive black holes, as [Quanta surveyed on 2 July](https://www.quantamagazine.org/astrophysicists-puzzle-over-webbs-new-universe-20260702/)) in which the assumptions we built from local objects fail the moment we sample beyond them. The methodological lesson is the same at comet and cosmological scales: calibrate on more than the one system we happen to live in.
