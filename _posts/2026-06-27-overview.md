## 🔬 Science

- **Marsquake data say the lower Martian crust is a 14-km layer of "melt-depleted" rock — a fossil of how the planet first cooled.** NASA's InSight lander recorded a seismic discontinuity ~24 km down, above the crust–mantle boundary at ~38 km, but no one knew what it was. Combining phase-equilibrium modelling (predicting which minerals are stable at a given pressure and temperature) with the seismic velocities, the authors interpret the boundary as a switch from mafic to **ultramafic** rock (iron/magnesium-rich, silica-poor — the stuff of a planet's deep interior), and read the lowermost ~14 km as a *melt-depleted cumulate*: rock left behind after early melts drained upward, evidence of "transcrustal magmatism" plumbing the young crust. Why it matters: Mars has no plate tectonics to erase its early history, so its crust is a cleaner record of how rocky planets — Earth included — differentiated in their first stretch. [Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02907-5)
- **A galaxy whose light left 11 billion years ago is the best candidate yet for the source of a high-energy cosmic neutrino.** Neutrinos are near-massless particles that barely interact, so they fly straight from violent cosmic engines without being deflected — but that same ghostliness makes pinning down where one *came from* extremely hard. Here a **gravitational lens** (a foreground mass bending and magnifying background light, a natural telescope) brought a compact, fast-star-forming galaxy at "cosmic noon" — the epoch ~11 Gyr ago when star formation peaked — into view as the most plausible counterpart to a detected high-energy neutrino event. Why it matters: it's a step toward tying individual neutrinos to specific galaxies, the goal of "multi-messenger" astronomy, and hints that cosmic-ray engines were already running in the early universe. [Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02904-8)
- **Two erbium ions, too close to tell apart by sight, were controlled individually using colour instead of position — a building block for solid-state quantum registers.** Atomic defects in crystals make good qubits, but packing them densely enough to interact means they sit nanometres apart, far below the diffraction limit (the ~hundreds-of-nanometres floor on how finely light can be focused). The trick: rare-earth **Er³⁺** ions have extremely narrow optical linewidths, so each one absorbs at a slightly different frequency and can be addressed by *tuning the laser colour* rather than aiming it. The team demonstrated coherent two-qubit electron-spin control of an interacting Er³⁺ pair plus a nearby nuclear-spin ancilla (a helper qubit for storage). Why it matters: erbium emits at telecom wavelengths, so this is aimed squarely at quantum repeaters for long-distance quantum networks. [Nature Physics, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03319-y)
- **A femtosecond laser pulse briefly turned an ordinary semiconductor (SnTe) into a topological insulator that doesn't exist at rest.** A topological insulator conducts only on its surface while its interior stays insulating, with the conducting states protected by the material's band structure. "Floquet engineering" — driving a crystal with intense periodic light to reshape its electronic bands — has long been predicted to *create* such states on demand, but never demonstrated in a semiconductor. Tuning the photo-excitation close to SnTe's bandgap, the authors observed light-induced metallic surface states and the band renormalisation that signals genuine Floquet states. Why it matters: it shows exotic phases of matter can be switched on transiently with light, a route to ultrafast, reconfigurable electronic properties. [Nature Physics (paper), 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03341-0); accompanying [News & Views](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03340-1)
- **Mathematicians finally improved on Erdős's 80-year-old probabilistic bound — the original problem that launched the "probabilistic method."** In 1947 Paul Erdős proved certain networks *must exist* not by building one but by showing a randomly chosen network has a nonzero chance of working — randomness as proof. The motivating case was avoiding **monochromatic cliques**: colour every edge of a graph red or blue and you can't escape large all-one-colour clusters (this is Ramsey theory — how much order is forced by sheer size). The probabilistic method now pervades math and CS (primality testing, circuit design, debiasing data), yet that founding bound had barely budged in eight decades. Per Quanta, that's now starting to change, with ETH Zürich's Benny Sudakov among those quoted. Why it matters: progress on the very problem that birthed a foundational technique is a notable event in combinatorics. [Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-26](https://www.quantamagazine.org/after-80-years-mathematicians-give-famed-erdos-method-an-upgrade-20260626/)

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.26161 (2026-06-27) — "Refusal Lives Downstream of Persona in Chat Models"** `[preprint]`. Interpretability/safety: prior work found separate linear "directions" in a model's activations for *refusing* and for *persona*; this shows they're entangled. Steering Llama-3.1-8B toward a compliant persona dropped its refusal rate from 97% to 2% — and refusal could be restored only at late layers, implying refusal is *gated downstream* of where it's computed. Why it matters: safety measures that treat "refusal" as one isolated knob may be undone by an unrelated persona nudge — a concrete jailbreak-relevant finding. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26161)
- **arXiv:2606.27161 (2026-06-27) — "TOPS: First-Principles Visual Token Pruning … for Efficient MLLM Inference"** `[preprint]`. Efficient inference: multimodal LLMs (models that read images plus text) choke on the huge number of visual tokens an image becomes. Framing pruning as picking a "Token Optimal Preservation Set" under three principles (task relevance, information coverage, semantic diversity), this training-free method removed **77.8% of visual tokens from LLaVA-NeXT while keeping 100% of accuracy** (and 100.6% on the 13B model — i.e. no measurable loss). Why it matters: a free ~4–5× cut in the dominant cost of vision-language inference, droppable into existing models. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27161)
- **arXiv:2606.26502 (2026-06-27) — "Humans Disengage, Reasoning Models Persist"** `[preprint]`. A clean dissociation between people and large reasoning models (LRMs — models that "think" in long chains before answering). Both take longer on harder problems. But *within the same problem*, an LRM spends **more** tokens when it gets it wrong (effect size Cohen's d = 1.47–3.13, i.e. large), while humans spend **less** time on trials they fail. The read: humans abandon problems they expect to miss; LRM chains balloon precisely when the model is uncertain — exactly when it tends to be wrong. Why it matters: "thinking longer" is not a reliable confidence signal, with implications for when to trust or cut off a reasoning model's deliberation. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26502)
- **arXiv:2606.26935 (2026-06-27) — "Where Do CoT Training Gains Land in LLM-based Agents?"** `[preprint]`. Chain-of-thought (CoT — making a model write out intermediate steps) is often credited for agent improvements, but verbalized CoT can be unfaithful (post-hoc rationalization of an answer already decided). Comparing "prompt actions" (act with no CoT) against CoT actions across training checkpoints, the authors find CoT training mostly *improves the no-CoT baseline* rather than widening CoT's own advantage. Why it matters: a sobering note for RL-on-reasoning pipelines — the gains may be getting baked into the model's direct policy, not into the visible reasoning we hoped to inspect. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26935)
- **arXiv:2606.26806 (2026-06-27) — "Memory Depth, Not Memory Access"** `[preprint]`. Long-running agents usually get "memory" via retrieval (fetching past facts at query time), but retrieval doesn't decide which experiences should keep *shaping behavior* after the context window is cleared. The authors define "memory depth" — durable goal-conditioned tendencies written into a small parametric store — and test EVAF, a surprise- and valence-gated LoRA (low-rank adapter — a tiny trainable add-on) that consolidates with only 2–3 writes per 200 events. Retrieval wins on shallow fact recall (~0.96); EVAF wins on goal persistence after context unload (~0.81–0.90). Why it matters: separates "remembering a fact" from "staying on-mission," a distinction agent memory systems usually blur. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26806)
- **arXiv:2606.26454 (2026-06-27) — "Data-driven Machine Learning Cannot Reach Symbolic-level Logical Reasoning"** `[preprint]`. A provocative scaling-limit argument: no matter how much data or compute, supervised deep nets can't attain rigorous **syllogistic** reasoning (deductions like "all A are B; all B are C ⟹ all A are C"), for two stated reasons — training data can't distinguish all 24 valid syllogism types, and end-to-end premise→conclusion mapping pits the pattern-recognition and logic sub-tasks against each other. They report that even GPT-5 can hit 100% accuracy on satisfiability yet give *incorrect explanations*, and that surface form (words vs symbols) sways performance. Why it matters: stakes out a concrete claim that some logical competence is out of reach for pure scaling — contestable, but a clear, falsifiable position. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26454)
