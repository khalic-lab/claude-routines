## 🏢 Lab blogs & official releases

- **OpenAI: a reasoning model surfaced 18 new diagnoses in rare-disease cases that had stumped clinicians for years. [vendor PR]** In a study published in *NEJM AI* (the New England Journal of Medicine's AI title), researchers had an OpenAI reasoning model re-analyse **376 previously unsolved** rare-disease cases and it produced credible leads for **18 new diagnoses**. "Rare disease" here means conditions so uncommon that a single specialist may see only a handful in a career, so cases routinely sit undiagnosed for years (the writeup follows one patient, Kyra, through a ~20-year odyssey to a myofibrillar-myopathy diagnosis — a rare inherited muscle-wasting disease). Why it matters: the bottleneck OpenAI names is expert *time*, not knowledge — a model that triages the literature against a messy case history is a plausible force-multiplier for genetics clinics. Caveats are stated honestly in the post: some cohorts (e.g. early psychosis) were tiny, so the "yield" percentages carry wide confidence intervals. Self-published by a vendor, so read the framing accordingly; the underlying study is peer-reviewed. ([OpenAI, 2026-06-18](https://openai.com/index/diagnose-rare-childhood-diseases/))

## 📊 Benchmarks & evaluations

- **Vals AI's new Code Migration Benchmark: porting tools across languages is still genuinely hard.** Vals AI (an independent model-evaluation outfit) released a benchmark for *code migration* — rewriting a working program from one language/runtime into another, the unglamorous task that dominates real enterprise modernisation. On the CLI-migration track (rebuilding command-line tools across five languages) **Claude Fable 5 led at 60.1%**, well clear of **Claude Opus 4.8 (40.1%)** and **GPT-5.5 (36.9%)** — i.e. even the best model gets ~40% of these wrong, so this is far from solved. On **COBOL→Java** (the classic mainframe-modernisation pain point), **GPT-5.5 and Claude Opus 4.7 tied at 70.0%**. ([Vals AI benchmarks, 2026-06-16](https://www.vals.ai/benchmarks)) `[via snippet]`
- **A practical footnote to those numbers:** Fable 5 tops the migration chart but has been withdrawn from all non-US customers since 13 June under the US export-control directive, so for teams outside the US the *usable* leader on these tasks is Claude Opus 4.8. The benchmark ranking and the access reality have diverged. ([Vals AI benchmarks, 2026-06-16](https://www.vals.ai/benchmarks)) `[via snippet]`
- **OpenAI: LifeSciBench — an expert-authored benchmark for real life-science reasoning. [vendor PR]** OpenAI introduced LifeSciBench, billed as "expert-authored, expert-reviewed," that scores models on the kind of judgement calls scientists actually make. The sample task is telling: critique whether a Duchenne muscular dystrophy gene-therapy package supports *accelerated approval* on a **surrogate endpoint** — a stand-in measurement (here, micro-dystrophin protein expression) used because waiting for the real clinical outcome would take years. That's a regulatory-strategy reasoning test, not a fact-recall quiz, which is the point. Vendor-built benchmark — treat absolute scores cautiously until third parties run it. ([OpenAI, 2026-06-17](https://openai.com/index/introducing-life-sci-bench/))
- **Harvey's Legal Agent Benchmark (LAB) results circulated this week.** Harvey's open-source LAB — **1,200+ agent tasks across 24 practice areas, graded against ~75,000 expert-written rubric criteria** — is designed to mirror how work is actually assigned and reviewed at large law firms (an *agent* benchmark: the model must produce a reviewable work product, not answer a single question). It's a useful counterweight to multiple-choice "bar exam" claims. Note the conflict-of-interest to keep in mind: Harvey is itself a legal-AI vendor publishing a benchmark its own product is measured on. ([Harvey, 2026-06-17](https://www.harvey.ai/blog/legal-agent-benchmark-initial-results)) `[via snippet]` `[vendor PR]`

## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

- **Noam Shazeer is leaving Google for OpenAI — the year's second marquee defection.** Shazeer co-authored the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" paper that introduced the **transformer** (the neural-network architecture under essentially every modern LLM) and was a co-lead of Google's Gemini models; he only returned to Google in 2024 from Character.AI under a deal reportedly worth ~$2.7B. He announced the move himself; Sam Altman called him "one of the people I have most wanted to work with since the very beginning of OpenAI," framing it as "10 years in the making." Google said it is "grateful for Noam's meaningful contributions." Why it matters: coming after Andrej Karpathy's move to Anthropic earlier this year, it's a second loss of a foundational researcher from Google to a direct rival, and a status marker in the talent war that increasingly *is* the competition. ([CNBC, 2026-06-18](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/18/google-gemini-co-lead-noam-shazeer-leaves-for-openai.html); [The Information, 2026-06-18](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/star-google-ai-researcher-shazeer-joins-openai); [Benzinga, 2026-06-18](https://www.benzinga.com/markets/tech/26/06/53269428/google-gemini-co-lead-noam-shazeer-joins-openai-sam-altman-says-its-10-years-in-the-making))
- **Yann LeCun: the leading labs face a "big bubble explosion."** In a CNBC interview, Meta's chief AI scientist argued the economics don't close: "the prices… of AI services [are] going up, but the cost of running them is going down — but not nearly fast enough," so the labs are loss-making and "the use for most people is funded by the investors. That can't go on." His conclusion: OpenAI and Anthropic will have to "increase prices… cut costs, or there's going to be a big bubble explosion." He also called Elon Musk's **xAI** "kind of a failure," citing the departure of its founding team and Musk's difficulty recruiting. Read with the conflict in view: LeCun's own startup, **AMI Labs**, raised $1B in March to pursue *world models* instead of LLMs, so a bursting LLM bubble would route attention and money his way. ([CNBC, 2026-06-18](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/18/yann-lecun-elon-musk-xai-failure-ai-labs-bubble-risk.html))
- **Amazon, Nvidia and AMD jointly back world-model startup Odyssey with $310M.** Odyssey ML raised **$310M at a $1.45B valuation**, with the unusual sight of three rival chip/cloud giants — Amazon, Nvidia and AMD — in the same round, alongside CIA-linked fund **IQT** and Google chief scientist **Jeff Dean** (personally). Odyssey builds **world models**: instead of predicting the next word, they aim to simulate the physical world — physics, dynamics, body language, 3D space — which proponents argue is what video and robotics need and what text models can't capture. The 55-person team (London, Zurich, Palo Alto), founded by ex-autonomous-vehicle engineers Oliver Cameron and Jeff Hawke, runs on **AWS Trainium** (Amazon's in-house AI chips). The round is a clean read on the day's theme — LeCun, Hassabis and Fei-Fei Li all argue world models are the next bet, and investors are now writing nine-figure cheques on it. ([Financial Times, via the-decoder, 2026-06-17](https://the-decoder.com/amazon-nvidia-and-amd-bet-310-million-on-ai-startup-building-3d-world-models/)) `[single-source]` — the underlying scoop is FT's ([ft.com](https://www.ft.com/content/1e0365db-a363-4d73-9960-23d25420e9f5)), read here via the-decoder's writeup. Context: Amazon's custom-silicon business (Graviton/Trainium/Nitro) is past a ~$20B annual run rate with large Trainium commitments from AI labs. ([About Amazon](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/aws/aws-trainium-graviton-ai-chips-explained)) `[via snippet]`
- **Amodei and Hassabis pitch a US-led AI "coalition" at the G7.** At the G7 summit, Anthropic's Dario Amodei and DeepMind's Demis Hassabis joined a closed-door session on AI standards — attended by President Trump and other heads of state — to argue for a US-led international coalition to set the rules of the road.
