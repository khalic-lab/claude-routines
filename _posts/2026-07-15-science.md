### A cryogenic strontium tweezer array holds single atoms for two hours {#st-b2ed5e8336b7}
**[arXiv:2607.12988](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12988)** · A. Kumar, L. Festa, A. Grinberg et al. (Max Planck Institute of Quantum Optics; LMU Munich) · `[preprint]` (submitted 14 July 2026)
Neutral-atom quantum computers trap individual atoms in "optical tweezers" — tightly focused laser beams that hold one atom each — and are scaling toward tens of thousands of qubits. The bottleneck is a trade-off: keeping atoms trapped for a long time usually means sealing them inside a cold, cramped enclosure that blocks the optical access the machine needs to actually manipulate them. This group reports a cryogenically-cooled apparatus that sidesteps the trade-off, holding single ⁸⁸Sr (strontium-88) atoms in a tweezer array for vacuum-limited lifetimes of up to **two hours** while preserving full optical access, and without a complex cryogenic shroud. For comparison, room-temperature setups typically lose atoms in tens of seconds; a two-hour lifetime means an atom essentially never leaks away during a computation.
*Why it matters:* Longer trap lifetimes translate directly into more qubits held stably at once, so this is a concrete engineering step toward the large atom arrays that error-corrected quantum computing will need.

### Infinite-layer nickelates may host a mixed "s+id" superconducting state {#st-ed4c19238033}
**[arXiv:2607.12773](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12773)** · Z. Yuan, J. Zhan, X. Wu, S. Li (Northeastern University, Shenyang; Chinese Academy of Sciences) · `[preprint]` (submitted 14 July 2026)
Nickelates — nickel-oxide compounds chemically tuned to mimic the copper-oxide "cuprate" superconductors — are a leading testbed for understanding unconventional superconductivity, where electrons pair up in ways ordinary theory can't explain. Recent tunneling experiments saw the *symmetry* of the electron pairing vary from spot to spot on the same sample, which nobody could explain. Combining first-principles calculations with Eliashberg pairing theory (the standard framework for phonon-mediated superconductivity), the authors argue two mechanisms coexist: magnetic spin fluctuations produce a *d*-wave state on the nickel orbital, while electron–phonon coupling produces an *s*-wave state on a separate "interstitial" orbital, yielding a combined **s+id** state. Crucially the *s* component only appears in a narrow carrier-density window, so local oxygen defects would tune it up or down — a natural explanation for the patchy tunneling data.
*Why it matters:* It offers a testable microscopic story for a puzzling experiment, and a mixed s+id state (if confirmed) would be a rare form of pairing that breaks time-reversal symmetry — of interest well beyond nickelates.

### A fresh, independent estimate of the muon g−2's thorniest term {#st-49853a18a2bc}
**[arXiv:2607.12918](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12918)** · A. S. Miramontes, A. Bashir et al. (University of Valencia; CSIC) · `[preprint]` (submitted 14 July 2026)
The muon's "anomalous magnetic moment" (g−2 — how much a muon's magnetism deviates from the simplest prediction) is one of physics' sharpest tests of the Standard Model, because theory and Fermilab's experiment can be compared to extraordinary precision. The single largest theoretical uncertainty is the **hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP)** — the muon's brief interaction with a cloud of quarks and gluons, which is hard to compute. This paper computes the leading HVP term with a continuum-QCD method (Dyson–Schwinger and Bethe–Salpeter equations) rather than the usual lattice simulations, obtaining aμ^HVP,LO ≈ 709.7×10⁻¹⁰ — in good agreement with recent lattice results — and quantifies a small isospin-breaking correction of 4.5×10⁻¹⁰. What is new is the *independent route*: agreement between two very different calculational frameworks makes the value more trustworthy.
*Why it matters:* Whether the muon g−2 signals new physics hinges on this exact number; a second method landing on the lattice value strengthens the emerging picture that the older tension was a theory-input problem, not a crack in the Standard Model.

## 🧬 Biology, medicine & neuroscience

### A CRISPR enzyme programmed to shred DNA only inside cancer cells {#st-e9b6f23e4cda}
**[Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10738-7)** · J. Zeng, Z. Cheng et al. (Gladstone Institutes; UCSF) · published 8 July 2026
Many cancers are driven by mutations in tumour-suppressor genes such as *TP53* (mutated in 40–50% of cancers) that are considered "undruggable" — the faulty protein has no pocket a drug can grip. Zeng et al., with a companion paper by Scholz et al. ([s41586-026-10466](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-02122-2)), repurpose **Cas12a2**, a bacterial CRISPR nuclease that, once it recognizes a target RNA, goes on to chew up nearby DNA indiscriminately (a "collateral" self-destruct that bacteria use to abort viral infections). By programming Cas12a2 with a guide matching a cancer-specific transcript, the enzyme stays dormant in healthy cells but, in cells expressing the mutant RNA, triggers collateral DNA cleavage and kills them — selectively limiting tumour-cell growth in mouse models. Results are in cell and mouse models, not patients, so this is early-stage.
*Why it matters:* It reframes an "undruggable" mutation as a *targeting signal* rather than a target — turning the presence of a cancer's own faulty RNA into the trigger that destroys it, a genuinely different therapeutic logic.

## 🌌 Astronomy & cosmology

### A star has been found whose orbit could probe the *spin* of the Milky Way's black hole {#st-57004ba460c4}
**[arXiv:2607.12664](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12664)** · GRAVITY Collaboration — K. Abd El Dayem et al. (Observatoire de Paris; Max Planck Institute for Extraterrestrial Physics) · `[preprint]` (submitted 14 July 2026)
For decades, tracking individual stars looping around Sagittarius A* — the 4.3-million-solar-mass black hole at the Galactic centre — has let astronomers weigh it and confirm Einsteinian effects like the gravitational redshift. But those effects scale as (v/c)² (velocity over the speed of light, squared); the black hole's **spin** only shows up one order deeper, at (v/c)³, and no known star orbited fast and close enough to reach it. The GRAVITY team now reports **S301**, a faint (K-band magnitude 19.3) main-sequence star on an 8.7-year orbit with a small enough closest approach that, with continued monitoring, its orbit becomes sensitive to the spin — the "Kerr" (rotating-black-hole) correction to spacetime. This is the *discovery of a suitable probe*, not yet a spin measurement.
*Why it matters:* Measuring a black hole's spin from a star's orbit would be a first, and a stringent new test of general relativity in the strong-field regime right at our Galaxy's heart.

### A model-independent hint of an anomaly in the recent expansion of the Universe {#st-c3e10a642b7d}
**[arXiv:2607.13009](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13009)** · S. Gupta Choudhury, P. Mukherjee, E. Di Valentino, A. A. Sen (Jamia Millia Islamia; Indian Institute of Astrophysics; University of Sheffield) · `[preprint]` (submitted 14 July 2026)
Instead of assuming a specific dark-energy model, the authors reconstruct the Universe's expansion history directly from data — the latest DESI DR2 baryon-acoustic-oscillation measurements (a "standard ruler" imprinted on galaxy clustering) plus the DES Type Ia supernova compilation (standard-candle explosions) — using a flexible spline fit. Compared against the standard Planck ΛCDM (cold dark matter + cosmological constant) prediction, they find a **localized deviation over redshift 0.3 ≲ z ≲ 0.6, peaking at about 3.5σ near z ≈ 0.47**, and show it survives changes of method, dataset and calibration. At 3.5σ this is an *indication*, not a discovery (particle physics reserves "discovery" for 5σ), and mock tests argue it is not a reconstruction artefact.
*Why it matters:* It adds to a run of recent DESI-driven hints that dark energy may not be a constant — the kind of localized feature that, if it holds up, would point to genuinely new cosmological physics.

### The most complex sugar yet seen in interstellar space {#st-3efdb8431fbb}
**[Nature Astronomy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02905-7)** · I. Jiménez-Serra, J. García de la Concepción, H. Cuppen et al. (Center for Astrobiology (CSIC-INTA); University of Extremadura; Radboud University) · published 13 July 2026
Sugars (monosaccharides) are core biomolecules — metabolic fuel and the backbone of DNA and RNA — and how they first arose is a central origin-of-life question, because lab experiments under early-Earth conditions make them only in trace amounts. Astronomers report detecting **erythrulose**, a four-carbon sugar, in a molecular cloud toward the Galactic centre — the largest sugar molecule identified in interstellar space to date, building on earlier detections of simpler two- and three-carbon species. The identification comes from matching the molecule's rotational spectral fingerprints in radio-telescope data. As reported [by Nature News](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-02173-5), it suggests the chemistry that builds sugars can proceed in cold interstellar gas, before planets even form.
*Why it matters:* It pushes the inventory of complex "prebiotic" molecules available in space one rung up the sugar ladder, supporting the idea that some building blocks for life were delivered to young planets ready-made rather than assembled from scratch.
