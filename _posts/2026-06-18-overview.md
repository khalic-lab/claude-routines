## 🔬 Science

- **Reconfigurable 98-qubit trapped-ion quantum computer with all-to-all connectivity.** A new machine holds 98 qubits (the quantum equivalent of bits) in a trapped-ion architecture — atoms suspended in electromagnetic fields — where *any* qubit can be entangled directly with *any* other (all-to-all connectivity). That matters because superconducting chips can usually only couple neighbouring qubits, forcing extra error-prone "swap" operations to connect distant ones; all-to-all wiring removes that overhead and makes more circuits practical at a given error rate. Nature's own News & Views frames it as a step toward useful fault-tolerant machines. ([Nature, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10676-4); [Nature News & Views, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01702-6))

- **Lethal plague struck Lake Baikal hunter-gatherers 5,500 years ago.** Ancient DNA pulled from the teeth of Siberian foragers carries *Yersinia pestis* — the plague bacterium — showing deadly outbreaks in hunter-gatherer communities, not just among the later farming societies usually blamed for spreading it. The teeth act as a sealed archive: dental pulp traps bloodborne pathogens at the time of death, so recovering the genome both dates the infection and lets researchers reconstruct how this early plague lineage differed from the medieval Black Death strain. ([Nature, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10540-5); [Nature news, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01941-7))

- **A brown dwarf sharing the orbital plane of a warm Jupiter and a hot super-Earth.** Astronomers found a distant brown dwarf — a "failed star" too light to sustain hydrogen fusion — on an orbit coplanar (in the same flat plane) with two inner planets around the same star. Coplanarity is a fossil of how the system formed: if a massive, far-out companion lines up with the inner planets rather than tilting their orbits, it constrains how often big outer bodies disrupt the tidy disks that planets are born from. `[single-source]` ([Nature, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10586-5))

- **Prototype differential atom interferometer built for fundamental-physics tests.** An atom interferometer splits a cloud of ultracold atoms so each atom travels two paths at once and then recombines, and the interference pattern measures tiny forces with extreme precision. The "differential" design runs two such interferometers in parallel and compares them, cancelling shared noise (like vibration) — the trick needed to chase faint signals such as gravitational waves or hypothetical dark-matter fields. This is a working prototype, a stepping stone rather than a result. `[single-source]` ([Nature, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10617-1))

- **Signatures of an "imaginary" charge density wave in a kagome metal.** A charge density wave (CDW) is a frozen, periodic ripple in a material's electron density. In a kagome metal — named for its lattice of corner-sharing triangles, a playground for exotic electron behaviour — researchers report microscopic signatures of an *imaginary* CDW, a variant whose order parameter carries a complex phase and can break time-reversal symmetry (the usual assumption that physics looks the same run forwards or backwards). If it holds up, it links the much-studied kagome charge order to unconventional magnetism. ([Nature Physics, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03339-8))

- **Dusty starburst galaxies at "cosmic noon" tied to high-energy neutrinos.** Cosmic noon is the era ~10 billion years ago when star formation across the universe peaked. This work links compact, dust-shrouded starburst galaxies from that epoch to detected astrophysical neutrinos — near-massless particles that fly straight from their sources, unlike light, which dust absorbs. If these galaxies are confirmed neutrino factories, they help answer a long-standing puzzle: where the universe's highest-energy cosmic rays are actually produced. `[single-source]` ([Nature Astronomy, paper, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02884-9))

- **How many *sloppy* shuffles does it take to randomize a deck? (math).** A classic 1990s result says seven *perfect* riffle shuffles thoroughly mix a 52-card deck. Quanta reports new work on the messier, realistic case — imperfect shuffles where cards clump and drop unevenly — bounding how many such shuffles are needed to reach near-randomness. The question sits in the theory of mixing times for Markov chains (random processes with no memory of the past), which underpins everything from MCMC sampling in statistics to randomized algorithms. ([Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-17](https://www.quantamagazine.org/seven-perfect-shuffles-randomize-a-deck-of-cards-but-how-many-sloppy-ones-20260617/))

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **STARE — stopping "entropy collapse" in RL-trained reasoning models.** arXiv 2606.19236 (submitted 2026-06-17) `[preprint]`. GRPO (a popular reinforcement-learning recipe that rewards a model only when its answer is verifiably correct) tends to make a model over-confident mid-training — its output distribution sharpens until it stops exploring, called *policy entropy collapse*. The authors trace this to a token-level credit-assignment mismatch and reweight per-token advantages by *surprisal* (how unexpected each token was) to keep training near a stable "critical" point. Why it matters: entropy collapse is one of the main reasons RL fine-tuning of reasoning LLMs stalls or degrades. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19236))

- **Diffusion-Proof — non-autoregressive diffusion LLMs for formal theorem proving.** arXiv 2606.19315 (submitted 2026-06-17) `[preprint]`. Standard LLMs write proofs one token at a time (autoregressive), so an early mistake compounds over a long proof. Diffusion language models instead generate a whole block of tokens by iteratively denoising them, letting later reasoning revise earlier steps — a better fit for formal proofs that must be globally consistent and machine-checkable. Why it matters: formal proving is a clean testbed for whether diffusion architectures can beat autoregression on long-horizon, error-intolerant reasoning. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19315))

- **Explaining attention heads by synthesizing Python programs (interpretability).** arXiv 2606.19317 (submitted 2026-06-17) `[preprint]`. Interpretability research tries to replace a network's opaque internal computations with human-readable descriptions. Here, for each attention head (the transformer component that decides which earlier words a token "looks at"), they prompt an LLM to write a small Python program that reproduces the head's attention pattern from the raw text. An executable program is a far stronger, testable explanation than a hand-wavy label. Why it matters: a route to auditing what individual model components actually do. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19317))

- **Rubric-Conditioned Self-Distillation — richer reward signals for post-training.** arXiv 2606.19327 (submitted 2026-06-17) `[preprint]`. Two dominant ways to train reasoning models both have flaws: distilling from chain-of-thought (the model's written-out reasoning steps) inherits noisy or wrong rationales, while RL with verifiable rewards crushes all feedback into a single number that hides *which* part of an answer was good or bad. This work conditions training on structured rubrics — fine-grained criteria — so the model gets itemized feedback instead of one scalar. Why it matters: cheaper, more targeted supervision without costly hand-written reasoning traces. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19327))

- **DreamReasoner-8B — making block-diffusion models reason over long chains.** arXiv 2606.19257 (submitted 2026-06-17) `[preprint]`. Block diffusion models decode several tokens in parallel for speed, but the authors find a sharp trade-off: large parallel blocks wreck long chain-of-thought reasoning, while small blocks preserve it (but are slower). Their fix is a *block-size curriculum* — train fine-grained first, then gradually coarsen — to keep reasoning quality while regaining the speed. Why it matters: a concrete recipe for getting the inference-speed benefits of diffusion decoding without sacrificing reasoning, on an open 8-billion-parameter model. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19257))
