## 🔭 Physics, chemistry, math & quantum

### Heat flows in quantized steps through graphene's fractal quantum-Hall states {#st-26d64dc007a7}
**[arXiv:2607.19205](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19205)** · S. Samai, D. Sarkar et al. (Indian Institute of Science; NIMS) · `[preprint]`
In the quantum Hall effect — the regime where a cold two-dimensional electron sheet in a strong
magnetic field carries current losslessly along its edges — electrical conduction comes in exact,
universal quantized steps. Whether *heat* is carried the same universal way in the richer
"Hofstadter" states (the fractal energy spectrum electrons form when a magnetic field and a
periodic moiré lattice act on them together) had never been tested. Using Johnson-noise
thermometry — reading temperature off the faint electrical noise that any warm conductor emits —
the team measured the thermal conductance of quantum-Hall, Chern-insulator, and interaction-driven
symmetry-broken states in a bilayer-graphene/boron-nitride superlattice (moiré wavelength ~14 nm),
and found it quantized in units of the thermal conductance quantum, tracking each state's Chern
number (the integer that labels its topology).
*Why it matters:* it shows these exotic topological states move heat as cleanly and universally as
they move charge, confirming their fundamental nature and giving a probe of edge modes that ordinary
electrical measurements can miss.

### Earth's inner core may be more than 1,000 °C cooler than the textbook value {#st-4bf0adc01f3e}
**[Science, 21 July 2026](https://www.science.org/content/article/earth-s-core-may-be-far-cooler-scientists-thought)** · `[single-source]` `[via snippet]`
Science reports new high-pressure laboratory measurements putting the melting point of iron at
inner-core-boundary conditions at roughly 4,420–5,220 K — more than 1,000 °C below the ~5,500 °C
usually quoted — implying a cooler core than the standard picture. The team squeezed iron between
diamond anvils to near two million atmospheres (comparable to the outer core) and, instead of
laser-heating it, drove it with microsecond electrical pulses for tighter temperature control. A
cooler core is more permissive of a thermally driven geodynamo (the churning liquid-iron engine that
generates Earth's magnetic field): per the report, core cooling alone could have powered the field
for most of Earth's history, until the solid inner core began to freeze a little over half a billion
years ago. I could not reach the primary paper or the article's full text from the sandbox, so this
rests on Science's account via search snippet — treat the exact figures as provisional pending the paper.
*Why it matters:* the core's temperature sets the energy budget for Earth's magnetic shield and the
age of the solid inner core; shaving 1,000 °C off it reshuffles both.

## 🧬 Biology, medicine & neuroscience

### A single waxy molecule, not just bullying, keeps naked mole-rat queens in charge {#st-98426c234dab}
**[Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10772-5)** · M. Khallaf, D. Hart et al. (Max Delbrück Center; Max Planck Institute for Brain Research; University of Pretoria)
Naked mole-rats are one of the very few eusocial mammals: a single queen breeds while every other
female stays infertile, and how the queen enforces that had been unclear. This study names the
signal — isopropyl myristate, a low-volatility ester (a waxy, barely-evaporating oily molecule)
strongly enriched in queens and nearly absent from non-breeders. Olfactory neurons in the nose and
brain detect it; exposure shifts the hormones prolactin and progesterone in subordinates to suppress
their reproduction and provokes avoidance in high-ranking rivals. In behavioural tests, adding the
ester daily to a queenless colony prevented a new queen from emerging, while withdrawing it triggered
aggression and reproductive competition. The same molecule showed up in breeding females of related
*Fukomys* mole-rats, hinting the mechanism is shared across the group.
*Why it matters:* it gives a concrete molecular handle — a chemical the animals can smell — on how a
mammalian society centrally controls who gets to reproduce, rather than chalking it up to social stress alone.

### The largest genetic study of borderline personality disorder finds its first solid signals {#st-2b391e9beae9}
**[Nature Genetics, 20 July 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41588-026-02654-3)** · F. Streit, S. Awasthi et al. (Heidelberg University; Central Institute of Mental Health; Broad Institute)
Borderline personality disorder (BPD) has been understudied genetically and often framed as purely a
product of environment. This genome-wide association study — a scan of the whole genome for common
DNA variants statistically linked to a trait — pooled 12,339 cases against about 1.04 million
controls, with a separate replication sample, and identified 11 independent genomic loci and 9 risk
genes. It reports a SNP-based heritability of 17.3% (the share of variation these common variants can
account for) and polygenic scores that predict about 4.6% of the risk — a real biological signal but
far from an individual-level test. All participants were of European ancestry, a genuine limitation
on how broadly the results generalise, and the signals overlap with those of other psychiatric and
some physical conditions.
*Why it matters:* it is the first robust evidence of a common-variant genetic contribution to a
heavily stigmatised condition, supporting a biological component and shared architecture with other
disorders — without implying any near-term diagnostic use.

## 🌌 Astronomy & cosmology

### Beta Pictoris d: the faintest exoplanet ever directly imaged from the ground {#st-e6cb458c3817}
**[ESO, 15 July 2026](https://www.eso.org/public/news/eso2609/)** · B. Sutlieff, M. Bonse et al. (University of Edinburgh; ESO)
Astronomers have directly imaged a third planet around Beta Pictoris, a young star 63 light-years
away whose disk and planets are a benchmark for how planetary systems form. Direct imaging —
photographing a planet's own light right next to its far brighter host — normally only works for
bright, massive planets, so Beta Pictoris d is a milestone: it is about 100 times fainter than the
famous Beta Pic b and, corrected for distance, the faintest exoplanet yet imaged from Earth. It is a
gas giant of roughly 2.4 Jupiter masses (against ~10 for b and c) on a much wider orbit. The team
spotted it with the Very Large Telescope's ERIS instrument, then found it lurking in archival VLT/SPHERE
images going back 11 years; an independent group led by Aidan Gibbs (University of California)
detected the same planet with JWST.
*Why it matters:* Beta Pictoris becomes only the second system, after HR 8799, with more than two
directly imaged planets — a natural laboratory for comparing worlds born from the same disk, and a
taste of what ESO's Extremely Large Telescope should routinely reach.

### One JWST instrument mode, two atmosphere fingerprints for a hot Jupiter {#st-19432b611cf7}
**[arXiv:2607.18409](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18409)** · A. Savel, E. Kempton et al. (University of Maryland; University of Chicago; Johns Hopkins APL) · `[preprint]`
Reading an exoplanet atmosphere's metallicity (how enriched it is in elements heavier than hydrogen
and helium) and its carbon-to-oxygen ratio — both clues to where in its disk the planet formed —
usually demands several JWST instrument modes stitched across ~1–5 microns. This work shows both can
be pulled from a *single* archival transit of the well-studied hot Jupiter WASP-39 b, taken with one
NIRSpec mode, by importing a technique from ground-based astronomy: phase-resolved cross-correlation
at the detector's native pixel resolution, which matches the full-resolution spectrum against
molecular templates instead of collapsing it into a coarse light curve.
*Why it matters:* if one instrument mode can do the job, JWST can characterise more planet atmospheres
per hour of scarce telescope time — a practical multiplier for the field.
