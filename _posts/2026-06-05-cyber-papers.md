## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

Today's batch (v1 papers announced 2026-06-05; arXiv RSS for all five categories fetched cleanly via curl). Deduplicated against this morning's Overview (which already cited 2606.05263, .05170, .05168, .04402, .05183, and 2606.06179). Six picks below, biased toward RL, efficient inference, and interpretability/safety.

- **`2606.05958` (2026-06-05) — Steering vectors are an adversarial attack surface.** "Activation steering" controls an LLM's behavior at inference by adding a precomputed **steering vector** to its internal activations — no fine-tuning, and people **share** these vectors and the datasets used to build them. The paper shows that's a supply-chain hole: by **poisoning just 4–6% of the tokens** in the steering dataset, an attacker can silently align the resulting vector with an **anti-refusal direction** — so the shared "helpful" vector **jailbreaks** the model (makes it comply with prohibited requests) while still behaving normally on benign prompts, hiding the tampering. *Why it matters:* the growing ecosystem of downloadable steering vectors is unauthenticated code-equivalent — this is the first clear "don't trust a stranger's steering vector" result. — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05958) `[preprint]`
- **`2606.05800` (2026-06-05) — SALT: when more GRPO rollouts *don't* help, and how to fix it.** **RLVR** (reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards — reward only when the answer checks out) commonly uses **GRPO** (Group Relative Policy Optimization: sample several rollouts per prompt, normalize their rewards against the group as the learning signal). The paper identifies a concrete failure: under group normalization the per-rollout gradients **collapse into a low-rank, signed geometry and cancel each other out**, so adding rollouts buys little. **SALT** is a plug-in that reweights rollouts by their gradient geometry to recover the lost signal. *Why it matters:* rollouts are the dominant cost in RL post-training of reasoning models — this is "stop paying for rollouts that silently cancel." — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05800) `[preprint]`
- **`2606.05538` (2026-06-05) — Less is MoE: trimming experts without breaking general ability.** **MoE** (Mixture-of-Experts — only a few of many sub-networks fire per token, so the model is big in parameters but cheap per token) is hard to compress: prior pruning methods looked fine on commonsense tests but **catastrophically failed on broader benchmarks**. The authors trace the loss to *granularity* — important capabilities live in **specific sparse FFN (feed-forward) dimensions inside experts**, not whole experts — and use **Fisher importance** (a curvature-based measure of which weights matter most) to find and keep those tiny critical sets while trimming the rest. *Why it matters:* a route to shrinking MoE deployment cost without the usual "it benchmarks well but got dumber" regression. — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05538) `[preprint]`
- **`2606.06034` (2026-06-05) — Multiplication-only matrix inversion for quantized Gated DeltaNet.** Modern **linear-attention** long-context models (here **Gated DeltaNet**, a state-space-style alternative to softmax attention) process sequences in chunks, and each chunk needs a **triangular matrix inversion** — a sequential, poorly parallel step that bottlenecks **NPUs** (neural accelerators). The authors replace it with a **truncated Neumann-series approximation** (expressing the inverse as a short sum of matrix products) plus structural masking and parallel residual correction — turning inversion into plain **matrix multiplications**, the one operation accelerators are fastest at, while staying accurate enough under quantization. *Why it matters:* removes a real hardware bottleneck for the efficient-attention architectures competing with transformers on long context. — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06034) `[preprint]`
- **`2606.05817` (2026-06-05) — Consistency training along the transformer stack.** Consistency training nudges a model to **behave the same across different contexts**, which helps reduce misalignment. This work adds two **internal** consistency targets — **MLPCT** (matching the post-activation states of the MLP/feed-forward blocks) and **AttCT** (matching per-head attention distributions) — so consistency is enforced *inside* the network, not just at the output. It's tested against four extra attack types: persona in-context-learning attacks, "adversarial frustration," prefill attacks, and conditional misalignment. *Why it matters:* a cheap, training-time lever against several practical jailbreak/misalignment vectors at once. — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05817) `[preprint]`
- **`2606.05988` (2026-06-05) — Compress-Distill: shrink the reasoning trace before you distill it.** Reasoning models emit long **chain-of-thought** (CoT — the model's written-out intermediate reasoning) traces; distilling those into a smaller "student" model is expensive and teaches the student to ramble. The authors **compress ~283k correct teacher traces** (from Qwen3.5-397B-A17B and gpt-oss-120B) down to **8.6–21% of their original length** before distillation. Result: training tokens cut to **12–30%** of raw, **2.0–7.6× faster training**, and student **outputs 3–19× shorter** at inference — with small quality loss. *Why it matters:* makes reasoning-model distillation dramatically cheaper and yields students that think concisely instead of padding. — [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05988) `[preprint]`
