## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

Today's announcement window (papers submitted 12–15 June, announced 16 June). Six picks below, biased toward hybrid architectures, efficient inference, interpretability, and generative-model theory; the five papers already cited in this morning's Overview are excluded.

- **2606.15007 (sub. 2026-06-12) — Nemotron 3 Ultra: open MoE hybrid Mamba-Transformer.** `[preprint]` A 550-billion-total / 55-billion-active-parameter model that mixes two ideas: **Mixture-of-Experts** (MoE — only a small subset of the network's "experts" fire per token, so you pay for a fraction of the parameters at inference) and a **hybrid Mamba-Attention** backbone (Mamba is a state-space-model layer that scales linearly with sequence length, unlike attention's quadratic cost — so long contexts get cheaper). *Why it matters:* it's an open-weights frontier-scale model that targets agentic, long-context workloads at far lower serving cost than a dense transformer of equivalent quality — the kind of release that resets the open-model baseline. Continues NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 Ultra line (the open-weights launch landed 6 June); this is the *distinct* full technical report. ([arXiv:2606.15007](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15007))
- **2606.14990 (sub. 2026-06-12) — Rational Sparse Autoencoder.** `[preprint]` Sparse autoencoders (SAEs — small networks trained to decompose a model's internal activations into a few human-interpretable "features," a workhorse of mechanistic interpretability) are normally locked to fixed nonlinearities like ReLU or JumpReLU. This paper swaps in *rational* (learnable ratio-of-polynomials) activation functions, giving the encoder more expressive shapes for separating features. *Why it matters:* better feature disentanglement means cleaner answers to "what is this model actually computing," which underpins both safety auditing and debugging. Extends the SAE line (a *distinct* paper from the recent iSAE identifiability work, not a continuation). ([arXiv:2606.14990](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14990))
- **2606.15079 (sub. 2026-06-13) — Ling and Ring 2.6: efficient, instant agentic intelligence at trillion-parameter scale.** `[preprint]` A technical report on a model family engineered for the tension every agent product hits: you want trillion-parameter reasoning quality *and* low-latency responses, which usually trade off. The report details the architecture and training choices that aim to deliver both while staying cheap enough to actually deploy. *Why it matters:* concrete engineering data on making very large models fast enough for interactive agent loops, where round-trip latency, not raw benchmark score, often decides usability. ([arXiv:2606.15079](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15079))
- **2606.14970 (sub. 2026-06-12) — Zero-order, parameter-free optimization for LMO-based fine-tuning.** `[preprint]` "Zeroth-order" optimization estimates gradients from function evaluations alone — no backpropagation — which slashes the memory needed to fine-tune a large model (you never store the full activation graph). "Parameter-free" means it sidesteps the brittle learning-rate tuning that usually makes such methods painful. *Why it matters:* memory is the binding constraint on who can fine-tune frontier models; methods that cut it without a quality cliff widen access to customization on modest hardware. ([arXiv:2606.14970](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14970))
- **2606.15034 (sub. 2026-06-13) — OSGuard: a benchmark for safety in computer-use agents.** `[preprint]` Computer-use agents (models that drive a real desktop/browser by clicking and typing) are usually graded only on whether they finish the task. OSGuard measures the thing task-success hides: did the agent take *unsafe* actions along the way — deleting files, leaking data, clicking through a destructive dialog? *Why it matters:* as these agents get deployed with real system access, a benchmark that scores harm-avoidance separately from competence is exactly the missing safety signal regulators and vendors will lean on. ([arXiv:2606.15034](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15034))
- **2606.16610 (sub. 2026-06-15) — Diffusion Flow Matching: dimension-improved KL bounds and Wasserstein guarantees.** `[preprint]` Flow matching is the training recipe behind many modern image/video generators — instead of the noisy step-by-step denoising of classic diffusion, it learns a smooth "flow" that transports noise to data. This is the theory side: tighter convergence bounds (in KL divergence — how far the generated distribution sits from the true one — and Wasserstein distance, a geometry-aware notion of distributional closeness) whose dependence on dimension is improved. *Why it matters:* sharper guarantees tell practitioners how much data and how many steps are genuinely needed, turning a largely empirical method into something with predictable error behavior. ([arXiv:2606.16610](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.16610))
