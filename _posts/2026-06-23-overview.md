## 🔬 Science

- **Interstellar comet 3I/ATLAS has water chemistry unlike any Solar System body.** Isotopic measurements of 3I/ATLAS — only the third confirmed interstellar visitor — find its water is enriched in deuterium (heavy hydrogen) at D/H = (0.98 ± 0.06)%, *more than ten times* the level in known comets, and its carbon-isotope ratios (¹²C/¹³C of 141–191 in CO₂) exceed anything seen locally or in nearby protoplanetary disks. The authors read these extreme fingerprints as evidence the comet formed in an unusually cold, distant region around its parent star. Why it matters: an interstellar object is a direct, free sample of planet-building material from another solar system, and this one says that material can be chemically very different from ours. [Nature, 2026-06-22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10771-6)
- **Physicists report "chiral gravitons" in a fractional quantum Hall system.** In the fractional quantum Hall effect (electrons confined to a 2D sheet under a strong magnetic field split into quasiparticles each carrying a *fraction* of an electron's charge), a recent geometric theory predicted spin-2 collective excitations dubbed "gravitons" — named by analogy to the hypothetical carrier of gravity, not actual gravity. Using circularly polarized resonant inelastic light scattering, the team observed *multiple* such chiral gravitons, which they argue are the long-predicted but never-seen "high-energy partons" (the elementary building-block quasiparticles of these states). Why it matters: it's direct experimental support for treating these exotic quantum fluids through the geometry of their wavefunction, and a first sighting of a predicted excitation. [Nature Physics, 2026-06-22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03338-9)
- **Faster biological aging tracks the global rise in early-onset cancer.** Among 154,169 young adults in the UK Biobank, "biological age" measured by PhenoAge (a blood-biomarker clock that can run ahead of or behind your calendar age) rose across birth cohorts — a 23% standard-deviation increase for those born 1965–1974 versus 1950–1954 — and accelerated aging was associated with early-onset solid cancers (hazard ratio 1.08 per standard deviation, 95% CI 1.03–1.13, i.e. ~8% higher risk per unit of excess aging), driven by lung, gastrointestinal and uterine cancers. Why it matters: it offers a candidate mechanism — generationally accelerating aging — for the genuinely puzzling worldwide increase in cancers among the under-50s. Single cohort, observational, modest effect size; suggestive rather than settled. [Nature Medicine, 2026-06-22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04448-w) [single-source]
- **Galaxies caught having their gas stripped away 11 billion years ago.** ALMA (the Atacama submillimetre array) imaged extended, one-sided gas tails streaming off five galaxies in a still-forming cluster at redshift z = 2.51 — light from when the universe was only ~2.5 billion years old. The tails reach well beyond the galaxies' starlight (seen by JWST) in objects that show no signs of mergers, the signature of *ram-pressure stripping*: a galaxy plunging into a cluster has its gas swept out like wind tearing away smoke as it plows through the hot intracluster gas. Why it matters: it catches *environmental* shutdown of star formation in the act, at the very epoch massive cluster galaxies were being built — direct evidence in a long debate over whether such galaxies quench from within or from their surroundings. [Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-22](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02892-9)
- **A "dark dimension" as a possible bridge between dark energy and dark matter.** A Quanta feature lays out a live theoretical idea: with hints (from the DESI galaxy survey) that dark energy — the unknown thing accelerating cosmic expansion — might be *changing over time*, some theorists ask whether dark matter could be evolving too. One vehicle is the "dark dimension" proposal (Montero, Vafa & Valenzuela, [arXiv:2205.12293](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.12293)): a single extra spatial dimension about a micron across, in which dark matter would be a tower of light gravitational states. Why it matters: it ties two of cosmology's biggest unknowns to one testable structure — but it rests on the DESI time-varying-dark-energy signal, which is itself preliminary and contested. Treat as a hypothesis worth watching, not a result. [Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-22](https://www.quantamagazine.org/a-dark-dimension-could-link-two-of-the-universes-great-unknowns-20260622/) [single-source]

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.23640** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *Learning Process Rewards via Success Visitation Matching for Efficient RL.* Why interesting: most reinforcement-learning tasks give a reward of 0 everywhere and +1 only at success, which makes *credit assignment* — figuring out which earlier action earned the eventual win — slow and hard. This proposes turning that sparse signal dense by training a discriminator to tell apart states visited on previously *successful* versus *failed* trajectories, yielding a per-step "process reward" without hand-designed reward shaping. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23640) [preprint]
- **arXiv:2606.23583** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *Evaluation Awareness Is Not One Capability: Evidence from Open Language Models.* Why interesting: safety benchmarks assume test-time behavior predicts deployment behavior — which breaks if a model can tell it's being tested and adapts. Across eight experiments on 37 open-weight models from seven families, the authors find this "evaluation awareness" is moderate, training-driven (24/37 detect cues), and *fragmented* rather than a single switch, meaning benchmark compliance can be an optimistic upper bound on real-world safety. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23583) [preprint]
- **arXiv:2606.23670** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *Tapered Language Models.* Why interesting: transformers stack identical layers with parameters spread evenly across depth, yet evidence keeps showing later layers mainly *refine* the residual stream (the running vector each layer reads and rewrites) rather than transform it. The paper tapers capacity — fewer parameters in later layers — as a cheap architectural lever to cut compute without hurting quality. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23670) [preprint]
- **arXiv:2606.23687** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *Randomized YaRN Improves Length Generalization for Long-Context Reasoning.* Why interesting: models trained on short text still extrapolate poorly to very long inputs. This combines YaRN (a method that stretches a short-trained model to longer contexts by rescaling its positional encodings, i.e. how it tracks token order) with randomized positional encoding and a length curriculum to push that generalization further. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23687) [preprint]
- **arXiv:2606.23568** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *SVD-Surgeon: Optimal Singular-Value Surgery for Large Language Model Compression.* Why interesting: SVD (singular value decomposition — factoring a weight matrix into ranked components so the least important can be dropped) is a standard way to shrink LLMs, but most methods just choose *what* to keep. SVD-Surgeon, training-free, instead applies the classic Optimal Brain Surgeon pruning framework in the singular-value basis, computing a closed-form correction to the kept components — cheaper deployment without retraining. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23568) [preprint]
- **arXiv:2606.23676** (submitted 2026-06-22) — *Open Problem: Is AdamW Effective Under Heavy-Tailed Noise?* Why interesting: AdamW is the default optimizer for nearly every large model, yet its convergence theory lives in a "finite-variance" world, while real LLM-pretraining gradients are empirically *heavy-tailed* (occasional enormous outlier updates). Sign-based methods like Lion and Muon, and AdaGrad, already have heavy-tailed guarantees; AdamW does not — this paper crisply poses whether it can, a gap worth flagging given how universally AdamW is used. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23676) [preprint]
