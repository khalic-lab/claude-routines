## 🔬 Science

- **How many elementary particles are there, really? Depends entirely on how you count — answers run from 17 to ~995.** Quanta's Natalie Wolchover walks through why a question that sounds settled isn't: the Standard Model (physics' catalogue of fundamental matter and force particles) has ~17 *types*, but counting every distinct state — the three colour-charges each quark carries, particles vs. antiparticles, the polarisations of force carriers — balloons the tally into the hundreds. The piece is a clean tour of what "fundamental" even means and why physicists disagree. ([Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-15](https://www.quantamagazine.org/how-many-elementary-particles-are-there-really-20260615/))
- **Radioactive fallout in Earth's crust dates the last nearby cosmic forge of heavy elements.** A Nature Astronomy paper uses three live radioisotopes — iron-60, plutonium-244 and curium-247 — deposited in terrestrial reservoirs to pin down the timing of the most recent *r-process* event near Earth (the rapid neutron-capture process, in which exploding stars or merging neutron stars build elements heavier than iron, such as gold, platinum and uranium, in seconds). Because each isotope decays on a different clock, their relative abundances act as a stopwatch on when the last such event seeded our neighbourhood. `[single-source]` ([Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02841-6))
- **Brain health can improve at any age — a three-year study of nearly 4,000 adults aged 19 to 94 found measurable gains from minutes of daily training.** Participants doing a few minutes a day of brain-training activities showed improvements across thinking clarity, emotional well-being and sense of purpose, with gains appearing across the whole age range — cutting against the assumption that cognitive sharpening is a young person's game. Worth the usual caveat for this genre: "brain-training" benefits often transfer narrowly to the trained tasks, so watch for the effect sizes and what exactly was measured. `[single-source]` `[via snippet]` ([ScienceDaily, 2026-06-13](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/06/260613034222.htm))

Carries a boxed warning for embryo-fetal toxicity plus anemia/hypoxia cautions. `[via snippet]` ([FDA, 2026-06-12](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/resources-information-approved-drugs/fda-approves-belzutifan-pembrolizumab-adjuvant-treatment-renal-cell-carcinoma); corroborated [Merck, 2026-06-12](https://www.merck.com/news/fda-approves-keytruda-pembrolizumab-and-keytruda-qlex-pembrolizumab-and-berahyaluronidase-alfa-pmph-each-with-welireg-belzutifan-for-adjuvant-treatment-of-certain-patients/))

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

First cs.LG / cs.CL announcements of 2026-06-16 (arXiv RSS via curl). Tag `[preprint]` throughout.

- **`arXiv:2606.14801` (sub. 2026-06-16) — QPILOTS steers a frozen robot policy at inference instead of retraining it.** `[preprint]` Flow-matching/diffusion policies (action generators that produce a move by iteratively "denoising" from noise) are hard to fine-tune with reinforcement learning because pushing the value-estimate (critic) gradient back through many denoising steps is numerically unstable. QPILOTS leaves the policy untouched and nudges each denoising step using the critic evaluated on a *projected* clean action rather than the noisy intermediate one — hitting a 90% average success rate across 50 offline-to-online RL tasks and improving a large frozen vision-language-action model on six manipulation tasks. Why it matters: a cheap, training-free way to make pretrained robot-control models better at deployment. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14801))
- **`arXiv:2606.15070` (sub. 2026-06-16) — ASAG: stop a reasoning model from "overthinking" by reading its own attention.** `[preprint]` Large reasoning models burn extra compute on long chain-of-thought (step-by-step reasoning written out before answering), but more tokens often *hurt* accuracy. ASAG watches the model's attention distribution to detect when it has effectively settled, and halts generation early — no retraining and no reliance on the brittle confidence scores prior methods used. Why it matters: a training-free lever on the cost/accuracy trade-off that's become the central pain point for reasoning models. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15070))
- **`arXiv:2606.14934` (sub. 2026-06-16) — A "solve once, query anywhere" neural surrogate for physics gives a 150,000× speedup.** `[preprint]` Separable Neural Architectures (SNA) combine neural networks with tensor decomposition to approximate solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs — the equations governing heat, fluids, stress and so on). The variational version comes with classical numerical-analysis guarantees (well-posedness, convergence) and sidesteps the "curse of dimensionality" — cost growing exponentially with the number of parameters — by scaling only algebraically. The authors run a 1,000,000-sample Monte Carlo sweep in 102 s on a laptop CPU, ~150,000× faster than a finite-element baseline on an A100 GPU. Why it matters: real-time engineering simulation and inverse design on a laptop, if it generalises beyond the demonstrated cases. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14934))
- **`arXiv:2606.14945` (sub. 2026-06-16) — Giving autonomous-experiment agents a memory cuts token use up to 90%.** `[preprint]` The "autoresearch" loop — an LLM repeatedly editing code to improve a metric — is stateless, rebuilding the full experimental history from scratch every iteration, so cost grows quadratically. Reframing it as a *stateful* ReAct agent (reason-and-act agent) that carries typed persistent state across steps drops token consumption 90% on hyperparameter tuning and 52% on code optimisation at comparable quality. Why it matters: a structural, not incremental, efficiency win for long-running agentic workflows. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14945))
- **`arXiv:2606.14875` (sub. 2026-06-16) — For multi-hop QA, a terse symbolic rewrite beats a prose summary at the same token budget.** `[preprint]` To fit retrieved passages into a small model's context, you compress them. The authors propose "Telegraph English," which rewrites passages into structured entity–relation statements, and show it beats character-deletion, truncation and random-subsampling baselines by 13–20 F1 points on three multi-hop QA benchmarks (MuSiQue, 2WikiMultihopQA, HotpotQA — datasets that require chaining facts across documents), and beats a coherent prose summary on the hardest set. Why it matters: evidence that *how* you compress context, not just how much, decides whether reasoning survives. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14875))
