## 🔬 Science

- **Stem-cell transplant keeps a severe autoimmune disease at bay for 15 years.** Two people with neuromyelitis optica (a rare condition where the immune system attacks the spinal cord and optic nerve, often causing blindness and paralysis) were the first to get an autologous haematopoietic stem-cell transplant — wiping out and rebooting the immune system with the patient's own blood stem cells — and have stayed relapse-free for 15 years. Why it matters: durable, possibly one-shot remission is a far stronger result than the symptom-managing drugs these patients usually take for life, though n=2 over a long follow-up is a proof-of-principle, not a trial. [Nature news, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01925-7) `[single-source]`

- **Transplanting one animal's "organizer cells" into a distant relative reshapes the host embryo — a clue to how animals first arose.** Organizer cells are the embryonic signalling hub (first found in amphibians in the 1920s) that tells surrounding tissue which body axis and structures to build. Researchers moved organizer cells *across phyla* and found they could still instruct embryos of very different animals, implying the genetic toolkit for body-plan patterning is far older and more shared than expected. Why it matters: it pushes the origin of a core developmental program back toward the base of the animal tree. [Nature news, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01910-0) `[single-source]`

- **More cerebellar tissue tracks with a cognitive "reserve" in old age.** The cerebellum (the brain region at the back of the skull, classically tied to movement and balance) is increasingly implicated in thinking too. A new study links greater grey-matter volume in specific cerebellar sub-regions to higher cognitive test scores in older adults — consistent with a "cognitive reserve" that buffers age-related decline. Why it matters: it broadens where in the brain we look for resilience to ageing, beyond the usual cortex-and-hippocampus story. This is a correlation, not a demonstrated cause. [Nature news, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01870-5) `[single-source]`

- **Early studies: leaning on AI tools measurably erodes the skills of doctors and software engineers.** Two strands of research find that practitioners who routinely offload work to AI assistants perform worse when the tool is taken away — deskilling, the loss of a competence you stop practising. Why it matters for this reader: it's a direct, dated data point (not vibes) on the AI-in-the-loop debate that touches anyone shipping software with a coding assistant. Framed as early results across a handful of studies, so treat the effect size as provisional. [Nature news feature, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01947-1) `[single-source]`

- **The sloth's slowness is written in its genome.** Sequencing points to duplicated copies of genes that govern mitochondria — the cell's energy organelles — as a driver of the sloth's famously low metabolic rate. Why it matters: it gives a concrete molecular handle on extreme metabolic thrift, a trait of interest well beyond sloths (e.g. for understanding energy regulation and torpor). [Nature news, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01869-y) `[single-source]`

- **A three-armed "Sashimi-Bot" learns to handle floppy raw fish.** Raw-fish fillets are irregular and deformable, exactly the kind of soft, unpredictable object that defeats rigid factory robots. A three-arm system learned to manipulate a salmon loin without tearing it. Why it matters: dexterous handling of deformable materials is a long-standing robotics bottleneck with payoffs across food processing, surgery, and warehouse picking — a small but concrete step. [Nature news, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01871-4) `[single-source]`

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.20068 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **Process-verified RL for theorem proving via Lean.** RLVR (reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards — training a model on signals a checker can confirm, not on human ratings) usually gives the model just one bit at the end: proof correct or not. Here the Lean proof assistant (software that mechanically checks formal math proofs step by step) is used as a *process oracle*, marking each individual tactic as locally sound or not — dense, fine-grained feedback instead of a single pass/fail. Why interesting: denser verified signal is one of the clearest levers for teaching models to reason in domains where a checker exists (math, code, logic). [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20068)

- **arXiv:2606.20474 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **UltraQuant: 4-bit KV caching for context-heavy agents.** The KV-cache (key-value cache — the stored attention state a transformer reuses so it doesn't recompute the whole prompt each token) is the main memory cost when an agent reuses a long prefix across many short turns. This compresses it to 4 bits and frames the trade-off jointly across task quality, cache residency, and serving throughput rather than accuracy alone. Why interesting: cheaper KV memory directly raises how many concurrent long-context agents a GPU can serve — a deployment-economics lever, not just a benchmark number. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20474)

- **arXiv:2606.20347 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **Critical percolation as a synthetic data model for interpretability.** Interpretability research (reverse-engineering what features a network has learned) leans on toy datasets, but most are too clean to resemble real data's nested, multi-scale structure. This builds toy data from critical percolation clusters — sparse, self-similar fractal patterns from statistical physics that sit right at a phase transition — to give interpretability methods a realistic-but-controlled testbed. Why interesting: better toy worlds make claims about what a model "understood" more trustworthy. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20347)

- **arXiv:2606.19370 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **Human-like driving autonomy from self-play plus a pinch of human data.** Self-play RL (a policy trained by playing against itself in simulation, no human demos) trains driving agents cheaply but tends to invent effective yet *alien* habits that confuse human drivers. Instead of heavy hand-tuned rewards, this keeps a small set of human demonstrations as a gentle regularizer (a soft pull toward human-like behaviour). Why interesting: a lightweight recipe for the recurring problem that pure self-play optimizes the task while ignoring whether humans can predict the agent. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19370)

- **arXiv:2606.19404 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **"Thermodynamic" signatures of reasoning for hallucination detection.** Builds on the idea that an LLM's attention patterns, viewed as a graph, carry a signal about whether the model is reasoning soundly or confabulating. It treats each layer's attention-graph Laplacian as if it were a physical Hamiltonian and reads off thermodynamic-style quantities (free energy, spectral entropy, heat capacity) as a richer fingerprint than the handful of eigenvalues prior work used. Why interesting: hallucination detection that needs only the model's internal attention, no external fact-checker — and a genuinely novel physics-borrowed descriptor. Single-author, framing is exploratory. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19404) `[single-source]`

- **arXiv:2606.20543 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **Spatially speculative decoding accelerates autoregressive image generation.** Autoregressive image models flatten a 2D image into a 1D token sequence and predict one token at a time, throwing away the fact that nearby pixels are correlated. Speculative decoding (a standard inference trick: cheaply guess several future tokens, then verify them in one pass) is adapted here to predict the token to the *right* and the token *below* simultaneously, exploiting 2D locality to cut the per-image step count. Why interesting: a clean way to make image-token models faster without retraining the generator from scratch. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20543)
