## 📄 ML/AI research (arXiv)

A clear through-line in today's batch: the field is auditing its own training and inference
recipes — why reinforcement learning (RL) on language models is fragile, and how to serve long
contexts without paying full attention cost. Eleven papers below, all from the batch announced
Tue 30 Jun (submissions dated 29–30 Jun); deduped by arXiv ID.

- **GRPO's reward design provably collapses its gradient to rank-2** — [arXiv:2606.29238](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29238) · A. Mishra, S. Chakraborty, B. Kapusuzoglu (Capital One) · `[preprint]`

  Derives GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization — the critic-free RL recipe behind DeepSeek-R1-style training, which replaces a learned value network with the average reward of a group of sampled answers) from the policy-gradient theorem and exposes a built-in flaw: when the reward is given only for the final answer, *every* token in a generated answer gets the same credit, so the learning signal collapses to a single number per answer. They prove this makes the gradient increasingly sparse over training and show empirically (via SVD — singular-value decomposition, a way to measure a matrix's effective dimensionality) that the gradient matrix has an effective rank of ~2 on Nemotron-4B/GSM8K regardless of group size.

  **Why it matters:** it gives a precise account of *when* GRPO's appealing simplicity stops working, which is exactly the recipe most open RL pipelines now copy. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **A lot of RL stabilization may be improving the wrong policy** — [arXiv:2606.29526](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29526) · J. Liang, H. Tang, Y. Ma et al. (Tianjin University; Alibaba) · `[preprint]`

  Tackles the "training–inference mismatch" that makes LLM RL unstable: a model is generated with one engine (optimized for speed) but updated with another (optimized for precision), so the same answer gets slightly different probabilities on each side — a hidden off-policy gap that quietly poisons training. The paper's argument is that prior fixes aim at the wrong target: an update that improves the *training* policy doesn't necessarily improve the *inference* policy, which is the one you actually deploy. It reframes the real objective as monotonically improving the inference policy.

  **Why it matters:** if correct, a lot of RL-stabilization effort has been optimizing the wrong quantity. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **EAPO reuses past experience to cut reasoning-RL's sample cost** — [arXiv:2606.30420](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30420) · J. Lu, K. Huang, J. Wu et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  EAPO (Experience-Augmented Policy Optimization) attacks the sample-cost of RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards — RL where correctness is checked by a verifier, e.g. did the math answer match) for reasoning. Instead of reusing past successful answers as fixed reasoning traces (which go stale as the policy changes), it treats a prior RL-optimized policy as an *action-level* experience prior and injects it only at critical decision points during generation. Tested on Qwen-2.5-Math-7B and Qwen-3-8B.

  **Why it matters:** reasoning RL is expensive because it samples from scratch; reusing experience adaptively rather than verbatim is a plausible efficiency lever. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **A clean theory for when speculative-decoding drafts get accepted** — [arXiv:2606.30265](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30265) · A. Sharma (Independent Researcher) · `[preprint]`

  A theory of *when a draft is accepted* in speculative decoding (the standard inference speed-up where a small fast model proposes tokens that a big model then verifies in parallel). Existing theory assumes you want to sample exactly from the big model's distribution; in practice systems use greedy or relaxed acceptance rules and tree-structured candidates, where success is about local ranking and thresholds. The paper characterizes the rejection regions as lower level-sets of the target distribution and derives exact KL-divergence (a measure of how far two probability distributions are apart) certificates and sharp margin bounds for these practical regimes.

  **Why it matters:** speculative decoding is everywhere in production serving, but its acceptance behavior under the rules people actually run has lacked a clean theory. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **KV-cache eviction hurts accuracy by shrinking token "coverage"** — [arXiv:2606.29563](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29563) · S. Roy, M. Zhai, H. Hajimirsadeghi, G. Samei (RBC Borealis) · `[preprint]`

  Diagnoses *why* KV-cache eviction (dropping stored key/value tensors to save memory during long-context inference) hurts accuracy: eviction reduces the *coverage* of unique tokens the model can still attend to, and they show theoretically that lower coverage caps the mutual information available for the next prediction. The fix is an eviction policy that maximizes coverage rather than just keeping high-attention tokens.

  **Why it matters:** KV-cache is the dominant memory cost of long-context serving, and most eviction schemes trade accuracy for memory — this reframes the trade-off around a measurable quantity. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **"Predict, reuse, repair" speeds sparse-attention decoding up to 40%** — [arXiv:2606.30389](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30389) · T. Wang, G. Rattihalli, A. Dhakal et al. (University of Pittsburgh; HPE Labs) · `[preprint]`

  PRR ("predict, reuse, repair") speeds up *dynamic sparse attention* — decoding that attends to only the top-K most relevant blocks of the KV-cache per query. The catch in sparse attention is a serial dependency: you must first *select* which blocks matter, then attend, and that selection step becomes the new bottleneck. PRR predicts the likely blocks (a lightweight running-average predictor), speculatively computes attention over them while selection is still running, then folds in any missed blocks with a FlashAttention-based repair kernel. Reported up to 40% lower per-token decode latency on long-context benchmarks while preserving quality.

  **Why it matters:** a concrete systems win for the long-context decoding that agentic and document workloads depend on. _Submitted 30 Jun._

- **MuonSSM borrows Muon's orthogonalization to stabilize state-space models** — [arXiv:2606.30461](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30461) · T.-K. Nguyen, T. M. Nguyen et al. (NUS AI Institute) · `[preprint]`

  MuonSSM stabilizes state-space models (SSMs — linear-time alternatives to attention, e.g. Mamba, that maintain a running memory instead of looking back at every token). SSMs degrade over long sequences because their memory updates are poorly conditioned. MuonSSM borrows the orthogonalization idea behind the Muon optimizer: it adds a momentum pathway plus a lightweight Newton-Schulz transform (an iterative way to orthogonalize a matrix) on low-rank inputs, keeping updates bounded and well-conditioned without breaking the parallel scan that makes SSMs fast.

  **Why it matters:** long-horizon stability is the recurring weakness of attention-free architectures; conditioning the *geometry* of the memory update, rather than the transition matrix, is a fresh angle. _Submitted 30 Jun._

- **Multi-block diffusion LMs decode several blocks at once** — [arXiv:2606.29215](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29215) · Y. Jin, J. Xu, Y. Liu et al. (Shanghai Jiao Tong University; Huawei) · `[preprint]`

  Multi-Block Diffusion Language Models extend block-diffusion text generation (diffusion LMs generate text by iteratively denoising, and the "block" variant denoises chunks while keeping a KV-cache for efficiency) to decode several consecutive blocks *concurrently* for extra parallelism. The problem they fix is a train/inference gap: block-diffusion models are trained seeing one noisy block at a time, but multi-block inference operates on a window of blocks with mixed noise levels. They post-train with a multi-block teacher-forcing scheme that matches inference.

  **Why it matters:** parallel block decoding is one of the few routes to make diffusion text generation competitive with autoregressive speed. (Continues the diffusion-LM line from June's Diffusion-Proof work — a distinct paper, not the same artifact.) _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **A static sparse-attention schedule beats a learned one — at 5× less compute** — [arXiv:2606.28560](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28560) · C. A. Capps (Independent Researcher) · `[preprint]` · `[single-source]`

  A careful small-scale study (21 models, 60M parameters, one matched recipe) of *sparse attention* where each token attends to a local window plus a set of Fibonacci-spaced offsets, scaled per layer. The headline: a *static* per-layer schedule for that scaling beats both a fixed value and a *learned* per-layer value on perplexity — and learning the schedule costs ~5× more compute while not helping. It also extrapolates to longer contexts where dense attention fails. (Single-author, small-scale; treat as a suggestive controlled study, not a frontier-scale claim.)

  **Why it matters:** a clean, controlled result that "learned is better" isn't automatic — a well-chosen static schedule can dominate, which is cheaper and more predictable. _Submitted 28 Jun._

- **Editing activations gives LLMs Big-Five personality without retraining** — [arXiv:2606.28770](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28770) · D. Courtis, T. Hu (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  A mechanistic-interpretability take on giving LLMs a personality. Instead of prompting or fine-tuning, it finds directions in the model's residual stream (the internal vector the model carries between layers) corresponding to the "OCEAN" Big-Five personality traits, using sparse autoencoders (SAEs — tools that decompose activations into interpretable features) and contrastive analysis, then nudges a trait up with a small additive shift to the hidden states. Reports trait change while preserving general language ability.

  **Why it matters:** steering behavior by editing activations rather than retraining is the same lever used for safety interventions; personality is a clean testbed for whether it works without collateral damage. _Submitted 29 Jun._

- **Mamba's internal "dwell time" predicts human reading times** — [arXiv:2606.29904](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29904) · Y. Yamamoto, S. Isono, Y. Kawahara, S. Yokoi (SOKENDAI; NINJAL; Osaka U.; Tohoku U.; RIKEN) · `[preprint]`

  A cognitive-science bridge: in Mamba (a state-space model), each layer's recurrent update has a dynamically chosen "timestep" Δt that, loosely, sets how long the model dwells on each word. The authors show this per-word timestep significantly predicts *human* reading times on a naturalistic reading dataset — and remains predictive even after controlling for GPT-2 surprisal (the standard "how surprising was this word" predictor).

  **Why it matters:** it suggests Mamba's internal dynamics capture something about real-time human language processing, offering a new, mechanistically transparent lens on reading — and a rare case where an efficiency-driven architecture turns out to have cognitive-modeling value. _Submitted 29 Jun._
