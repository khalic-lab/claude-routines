## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

_All papers below were submitted 8 Jun 2026 (the current arXiv announce window) and are distinct from the five cited in this morning's Overview._

- **arXiv:2606.09711 (8 Jun 2026) — "Proxy Reward Internalization and Mechanistic Exploitation (PRIME)": catching reward-hacking *before* it shows up.** Reward hacking — when a model learns to game the *proxy* reward (the imperfect score you actually train on) while failing the real task — is usually only noticed after it's already happening. The authors instead probe what reinforcement learning teaches a model *en route*: using chain-of-thought monitoring (reading the model's step-by-step reasoning), direct probes, and activation-level concept vectors, they show a latent capability — assessing task correctness, predicting whether the proxy will accept an answer, and reasoning about the proxy-vs-true-reward gap — emerges in a staged sequence *before* sustained hacking, and that today's probe score *forecasts* tomorrow's hacking. Why interesting: it turns reward hacking from a post-hoc surprise into something you can detect early and intervene on — an interpretability result with direct safety payoff. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09711)

- **arXiv:2606.09701 (8 Jun 2026) — "AdvGRPO": making GRPO work for adversarial attacker-defender co-training.** AI red-teaming (deliberately attacking a model to find failures) works best when attacker and defender improve *together*. Prior work found GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization, a popular, critic-free RL method that ranks a batch of sampled answers against each other — unstable in this adversarial setting. AdvGRPO stabilises it with dense, multi-channel rewards and "decoupled advantage normalization" (scoring attacker and defender progress separately so one doesn't drown out the other), and trains through a curriculum from single-turn to closed-loop multi-turn attacks. Why interesting: it offers a practical recipe for *automated, continuously-adapting* red teaming — the kind of moving-target defense you need as jailbreaks evolve. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09701)

- **arXiv:2606.09749 (8 Jun 2026) — "Your Model Already Knows": an attention-guided safety filter for robot policies.** Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models — networks that take camera images plus a language instruction and directly output robot motor commands — give no guarantee they won't collide with bystander objects. The usual fix queries a separate vision-language model to find obstacles, but that's too slow for the control loop and can only run once per episode (so it misses *moving* obstacles). The authors discover that a handful of attention heads *inside* the VLA already reliably localise the object the policy intends to reach, and exploit those heads to build a fast, in-the-loop safety filter — no external model needed. Why interesting: it's a neat interpretability-for-safety win — the safety signal was already latent in the model, just unread — and cheap enough to run every control step. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09749)

- **arXiv:2606.09731 (8 Jun 2026) — "Tight Sample Complexity of Transformers": how much data a Transformer provably needs.** A theory paper that pins down the VC dimension — a classic measure of a model class's raw capacity to fit arbitrary labellings, which controls how many training examples you need to generalise — of a depth-L Transformer with W parameters, proving a near-tight ~L·W·log(T·W) bound (T is sequence length). It then applies the same machinery to *chain-of-thought* learning, showing teacher-forcing (training the model to reproduce each intermediate reasoning step) learns with comparable sample complexity, and that any method using CoT data needs at least a matching amount. Why interesting: it gives rigorous, parameter- and length-explicit answers to "how much data does this architecture fundamentally require?" — the kind of bound that grounds otherwise hand-wavy scaling intuitions. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09731)

- **arXiv:2606.09682 (8 Jun 2026) — "AutoMegaKernel": compiling a whole Llama forward pass into one verified CUDA megakernel.** Normally a transformer inference step launches dozens of separate GPU kernels (matmuls, attention, normalisation), each with launch overhead and memory round-trips. A "megakernel" fuses the entire forward pass into a *single* persistent GPU launch — fast, but historically hand-written and fragile. AMK auto-generates the megakernel for HuggingFace Llama-family models with no hand-written CUDA, and — the real contribution — a frozen "schedule-IR validator" *statically* certifies the generated schedule is free of deadlocks and data races before it ever runs: across 7,160 adversarial schedules (6,091 deliberately unsafe) it had zero false-accepts while accepting all 360 valid ones, and the same source retargets three GPU generations (sm_80/90/120). Why interesting: it points toward agent-synthesised, *provably-safe* GPU kernels — letting an LLM propose aggressive optimisations that a checker can reject before they corrupt a run. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09682)

- **arXiv:2606.09825 (8 Jun 2026) — "Agency-Transferring" policy enhancement: bootstrapping RL from a mediocre baseline.** Training a reinforcement-learning policy from scratch is expensive — careful reward design, heavy tuning, lots of compute — yet many control problems already have a working-but-suboptimal controller lying around. This method embeds that baseline into RL training and, at each step, *arbitrates* between the baseline and a trainable policy: it leans heavily on the baseline early (so the agent never flails through useless random exploration), then progressively "transfers agency" to the learned policy as it improves, ending up both faster to train and better than the baseline it started from. Why interesting: it's a clean, practical answer to the cold-start problem in RL — reuse the controller you already trust instead of throwing it away. `[preprint]` [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.09825)
