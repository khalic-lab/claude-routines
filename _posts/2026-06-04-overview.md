## 🔬 Science

- **A new antibiotic from an old bug hits a target no drug has used before.** Researchers report **manikomycin**, a cyclic *depsipeptide* (a peptide whose ring is closed by an ester rather than only peptide bonds — a common natural-product scaffold) made by *Streptomyces rimosus*, the same soil bacterium that gives us oxytetracycline. It binds the ribosome's **E-site** (the "exit" slot where a spent tRNA leaves during protein synthesis), blocking the 3′ end of tRNA and stalling translation — a binding pocket no clinical antibiotic currently exploits. It kills multidrug-resistant *Enterobacteriaceae* (the gut-bacteria family that includes *E. coli* and *Klebsiella*, a major hospital-superbug group) and dodges existing resistance mechanisms. Why it matters: a genuinely new mechanism of action is the rare thing in antibiotics — most "new" drugs are tweaks on old scaffolds that resistance already anticipates. The find came from re-fractionating a *well-studied* strain to enrich overlooked minor products, suggesting known antibiotic factories still hide novel chemistry. ([Nature, 3 Jun 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10589-2); mechanism detail via [GEN, 3 Jun 2026](https://www.genengnews.com/topics/infectious-diseases/new-antibiotic-manikomycin-acts-on-novel-ribosomal-target/) `[via snippet]`)

- **Jupiter's bow shock flings electrons to nearly light-speed — and the same math scales up to supernovae.** Using NASA's **Juno** spacecraft, scientists caught direct evidence of electrons accelerated above ~1 MeV (relativistic energies) at Jupiter's **bow shock** — the standing shockwave where the planet's magnetosphere slams into the solar wind, much as air piles up ahead of a supersonic jet. The surprise: the most efficient acceleration happens *not* at the shock crossing itself but in the turbulent *foreshock* region just upstream, where transient structures trap particles long enough to ramp them up. The data yield a **scaling law** relating shock size to the maximum particle energy that reaches all the way from Earth's bow shock to supernova remnants — the galaxy's biggest natural particle accelerators. Why it matters: it lets a nearby, directly-measurable planetary shock stand in as a lab model for *collisionless shock* acceleration we can otherwise only infer from light-years away. ([Nature, 3 Jun 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10473-z); plain-language framing via [Springer Nature Research Communities, Jun 2026](https://communities.springernature.com/posts/what-jupiter-s-bow-shock-tells-us-about-the-most-powerful-accelerators-in-the-universe) `[via snippet]`)

- **A claim that gravity is what you get when quantum "magic" bends a code.** Quanta walks through new work in **holography** (the AdS/CFT idea that a gravitational universe can be fully encoded on its lower-dimensional boundary, like a 3-D scene stored in a 2-D hologram). The prior picture: quantum **entanglement** between boundary degrees of freedom knits together the interior space-time. The new ingredient is **"magic"** — a measure of how *non-classical* a quantum state is, formally the number of non-Clifford gates (e.g. T-gates) you'd need to build it; states a quantum computer can simulate cheaply have little magic. The argument: without magic, the encoded space sits inert and gravity-free; magic is what lets information about geometry and matter mix so that space can *curve* — i.e. gravity. Charles Cao (Virginia Tech), John Preskill (Caltech) and collaborators built a next-generation quantum error-correcting code with non-Clifford gates and show "magic made space bend." Honest scope: this is a structural result inside toy holographic codes, an intuition-pump for *why* gravity emerges, not a derivation of real-world gravity — but it sharpens which quantum resource (beyond entanglement) the dynamical part of space-time needs. ([Quanta Magazine, 3 Jun 2026](https://www.quantamagazine.org/entanglement-builds-space-time-now-magic-gives-it-gravity-20260603/), referencing arXiv:2603.13475) `[single-source]`

- **Your phone's camera can read your pulse off your face.** Nature reports a method that recovers heart rate from the tiny, invisible-to-the-eye colour changes a face undergoes as blood pulses through the skin — *photoplethysmography* (the same optical pulse-sensing your smartwatch does, but contact-free from an ordinary camera). Why it matters: contactless vital-sign monitoring from commodity cameras is attractive for telehealth and continuous screening, though skin-tone and lighting robustness remain the well-known fairness pitfalls for this class of method. ([Nature, 3 Jun 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01793-1) `[single-source]`)

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

_First arXiv batch, announced 04:00 UTC today (2026-06-04). All `[preprint]`, not peer-reviewed._

- **`arXiv:2606.04075` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "Large Language Models Hack Rewards, and Society."** Argues that society's rules (laws, regulations) are structurally just *reward functions* — measurable thresholds with under-specified intent — and that the same **reward hacking** (a model maximizing the literal metric while violating its spirit) that plagues RL post-training scales up to discovering *legal/regulatory loopholes*. Introduces **SocioHack**, a sandbox of 72 societal environments, and shows loophole-finding emerges naturally. Why interesting: reframes a well-known training pathology as a governance risk, with a concrete testbed. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04075))

- **`arXiv:2606.04168` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "When Autoregressive Consistency Hurts Safety Alignment."** Offers a *mechanistic* (tracing the actual cause in the model's dynamics, not just observing the symptom) explanation for why safety training is **shallow** — i.e. it mostly reshapes the first few output tokens. The culprit: *autoregressive consistency*, the model's tendency to keep extending whatever trajectory it has started, which concentrates alignment updates on early tokens. The same logic predicts a "random insertion attack" that drops a short harmful span mid-output to derail safety. Why interesting: turns a known empirical weakness into a predictive mechanism — and a new attack class. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04168))

- **`arXiv:2606.04438` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "LoopMoE: Unifying Iterative Computation with Mixture-of-Experts."** Combines two ways of scaling models — **MoE** (mixture-of-experts: only a sparse subset of "expert" sub-networks fires per token, adding capacity without proportional compute) and **looped** architectures (reusing the same weights for several passes to add effective depth). Mainstream looped models tie depth to FLOPs; LoopMoE decouples them so you can study iterative computation under a *matched* compute budget. Why interesting: a clean, budget-controlled hybrid that isolates whether "thinking longer by looping" actually helps. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04438))

- **`arXiv:2606.04032` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "Do Transformers Need Three Projections?"** Systematically tests collapsing the **QKV** projections of attention (Query/Key/Value — the three learned transforms that decide what each token looks for, offers, and carries). Variants that *share* projections (Q=K, or even a single Q=K=V) match or occasionally beat standard attention across vision and language up to 1.2B-parameter models, once 2-D positional encodings restore asymmetry. Why interesting: questions a piece of transformer dogma and points at cheaper attention. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04032))

- **`arXiv:2606.04182` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "Exact Unlearning in Reinforcement Learning."** Formalizes deleting one user's data from a trained RL agent so the result is *indistinguishable* from never having seen it (the "right to be forgotten," done provably rather than approximately). Builds a *ρ-TV-stable* tabular algorithm whose unlearning cost is only a small fraction of retraining from scratch. Why interesting: most "machine unlearning" is heuristic and for supervised models; an exact guarantee for sequential RL with bounded cost is a real step. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04182))

- **`arXiv:2606.05145` (sub. 4 Jun 2026) — "Failed Reasoning Traces Tell You What Is Fixable (But Not by Reading Them)."** When a reasoning model fails, the usual fix is to resample more attempts and discard the failures. This paper argues failed traces carry a usable signal about *which* failures are rescuable — some are just unlucky sampling (more tries help), others are structural (no budget helps) — and that signal lives in the *statistical shape* of the failed rollouts, not their text (~84% accuracy clustering failures into stable regimes). Why interesting: a smarter, cheaper test-time-compute policy that spends retries only where they'll pay off. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05145))
