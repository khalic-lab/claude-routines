## 🔬 Science

- **Neutral-atom quantum computers get "erasure conversion" for logical qubits.** A *Nature Physics* paper demonstrates logical qubits — error-protected qubits built from many physical ones — in arrays of metastable neutral atoms (atoms parked in a long-lived excited state and held in place by laser tweezers), engineered so that the most common hardware errors knock the atom into a separate state that lights up under a probe. That turns an invisible error into an *erasure* — an error whose location you know — and known-location errors are far easier for error-correcting codes to fix, which in past proposals lifted the fault-tolerance threshold several-fold. Why it matters: erasure conversion is one of the few levers that makes a quantum computer's error budget dramatically more forgiving without better hardware, and neutral atoms are a leading scalable platform. ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03309-0)) `[single-source]` — companion *Nature Physics* work the same day on high-fidelity entanglement and multi-qubit mapping in atom arrays points the same direction ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03258-8)).
- **Earth may have brewed much of its own water, not just imported it.** Diamond-anvil experiments (squeezing samples between two gem tips to reach planetary-interior pressures) by Harrison Horn, S.-H. Dan Shim and colleagues show that hydrogen reacting with molten rock during Earth's early "magma ocean" phase produced up to ~1,000× more water than models predicted — enough to challenge the long-favored idea that asteroids delivered most of our oceans. The honest scope: this is a lab-and-model result about what *could* have happened, not a measurement of early Earth; researchers expect the real answer mixes home-grown and delivered water. Why it matters: it reframes where to look for water on rocky exoplanets — possibly baked in from formation rather than requiring a lucky comet bombardment. ([Quanta Magazine, 2026-06-12](https://www.quantamagazine.org/where-did-earth-get-its-oceans-maybe-it-made-them-itself-20260612/)) `[single-source]`
- **Dopamine drug pramipexole cuts anhedonia in a depression trial.** A *Nature Medicine* randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (85 randomized, 82 analyzed) tested add-on pramipexole — a dopamine agonist already used in Parkinson's — in people with mood disorders and high anhedonia (the loss of pleasure/motivation). It hit its primary endpoint: a 4-point greater drop in the SHAPS pleasure-scale score vs placebo over 9 weeks (Hedges' *g* = 0.62, a moderate effect; *P* = 0.006), and brain imaging showed increased reward-related activation in the ventral striatum — i.e. the drug engaged the reward circuitry it was supposed to. Why it matters: anhedonia responds poorly to standard serotonergic antidepressants, so a mechanistically-targeted dopaminergic option with confirmed target engagement is a meaningful direction. ([Nature Medicine, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04465-9)) `[single-source]`
- **Humans still beat the best AI on research-level math.** *Nature* reports on **First Proof**, a project that posed ten previously-unseen research-level mathematics problems to four AI systems and had anonymous specialist mathematicians grade the answers; the top model scored 6/10 and none matched expert problem-solvers. Concrete anchor: unlike contest-style benchmarks where models now excel, these are open-ended proof problems where a wrong-but-confident chain of steps earns nothing. Why it matters: it's a counter-data-point to "AI has solved math," and a reminder that grading rigor — having experts judge proofs, not just check final answers — separates genuine reasoning from pattern-matching. ([Nature, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01888-9)) `[single-source]`
- **A method that sharpens protein structures from cryo-EM images.** *Nature* covers a new technique that boosts image quality in determining protein structures — relevant because cryo-electron microscopy (flash-freezing molecules and imaging them with electrons) is now the workhorse for mapping the 3D shape of proteins, and shape dictates function and drug-binding. Better images mean structures resolvable at finer detail or from harder-to-image proteins. ([Nature, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01858-1)) `[single-source]`

- **`arXiv:2606.13496` (submitted 2026-06-11) — BudCache: caching diffusion steps to a fixed latency budget.** Diffusion image/video models denoise over many steps, and neighboring steps are often nearly identical, so "step caching" reuses computation — but existing methods use fixed thresholds, so the actual speed varies unpredictably per input. BudCache flips it: you set a latency budget and it optimizes which steps to cache to hit that budget while protecting output quality. Why interesting: makes generation cost *controllable* at deployment, which matters for anyone serving diffusion models under a latency SLA. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13496))
- **`arXiv:2606.13277` (submitted 2026-06-11) — ProtoX-AD: anomaly detection that explains itself.** Time-series anomaly detection (spotting abnormal stretches in sensor/log/finance streams) increasingly uses self-supervised classifiers, but they flag *that* something is anomalous without saying *why*. ProtoX-AD attaches prototype-based explanations (matching a flagged segment to learned "this is what normal/abnormal looks like" exemplars) so each alarm comes with a characterization. Why interesting: interpretability is the practical bottleneck for anomaly detection in production — an unexplained alert is hard to act on. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13277))
- **`arXiv:2606.13550` (submitted 2026-06-11) — UMG-RAG: uncertainty-aware hybrid retrieval for long documents.** RAG (retrieval-augmented generation — letting an LLM pull in external text before answering) faces a granularity tradeoff: big retrieved chunks keep context but add noise; small chunks are precise but hard to match to a query. UMG-RAG is training-free and mixes coarse and fine units, using retrieval uncertainty to decide which to trust. Why interesting: long-document QA is where RAG most often fails, and a no-training method that improves it is cheap to adopt. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13550))
- **`arXiv:2606.13608` (submitted 2026-06-11) — AgentBeats: evaluating AI agents through judge agents.** Agent benchmarks are fragmented — each needs custom plumbing and a separate interface for the benchmark vs the agent, which makes fair comparison hard. AgentBeats proposes "agentified" evaluation: judge agents assess participants over standardized protocols (A2A for task handoff, MCP — the Model Context Protocol — for tool access), collapsing two interfaces into one. Why interesting: reproducible, agent-agnostic evaluation is a precondition for trusting agent leaderboards, and this is a concrete proposal rather than yet another fixed benchmark. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13608))
