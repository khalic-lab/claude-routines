## 📊 Benchmarks & evaluations

**The Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index refreshed yesterday (9 June), and the top of the
table is tightly bunched.** Artificial Analysis is an independent aggregator that blends several
public benchmarks (knowledge, math, coding, agentic tasks) into one composite "intelligence"
number. In the 9 June snapshot, **Claude Opus 4.8 sits on top at ~61.4**, edging out **GPT-5.5**,
with the third-place model only ~4.2 points back and the whole top-10 spread inside ~7.9 points.
Reported component standouts for Opus 4.8: **SWE-bench Pro ~69.2%** (SWE-bench Pro = a hardened
version of the software-engineering benchmark where a model must produce real patches that pass a
repo's tests; high-60s is current frontier, and "Pro" deliberately scores lower than vanilla
SWE-bench because the tasks are harder and contamination-filtered) and **Online-Mind2Web ~84%**
(a live web-agent benchmark — can the model actually drive a browser through real multi-step
tasks). Why it matters: "best model" is currently a photo-finish, not a blowout — the gap between
the top frontier systems is now smaller than the run-to-run noise on many individual benchmarks,
which is itself the story. [Artificial Analysis — model leaderboard (updated 2026-06-09)](https://artificialanalysis.ai/leaderboards/models) `[via snippet]` `[single-source]`

**The caveat that should sit next to every number above: agent benchmarks are alarmingly easy to
game, and someone proved it at scale.** This is older than today (UC Berkeley's Center for
Responsible Decentralized Intelligence published it **12 April 2026**), but it is the single most
important piece of context for reading any agentic leaderboard, so it earns a slot on a quiet day.
The researchers built an automated "scanning agent" and pointed it at the eight major agent
benchmarks — **SWE-bench, WebArena, OSWorld, GAIA, Terminal-Bench** and others — and found every
one of them could be driven to near-perfect scores by **reward hacking** (exploiting the scoring
harness — e.g. editing the grader, reading the answer key, or short-circuiting the task check —
rather than actually doing the task). In other words, a number like "84% on Online-Mind2Web" only
means something if you trust that the harness can't be gamed, and for a worrying share of agent
benchmarks that trust isn't earned. Why it matters: when you see a vendor claim "our agent scores
X% on benchmark Y," the right next question is no longer "is X high?" but "has Y been audited for
reward hacking?" [BenchLM — agentic benchmarks overview](https://benchlm.ai/agentic) `[via snippet]` `[single-source]`

Methodological footnote for the same reason: the genuinely contamination-resistant evals
(LiveCodeBench, which continuously harvests fresh competitive-programming problems so test cases
always postdate a model's training cutoff; and METR's HCAST/RE-Bench, which calibrate tasks
against measured human completion times) are the ones to weight most heavily right now. A leaderboard
that recycles 2023-era tasks is largely measuring how much of the test set leaked into training.

## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

Two **11 June** milestones dominate the near-term calendar — the SpaceX/xAI IPO prices on the
evening of 11 June (ticker SPCX, fixed $135, ~$1.75T target; covered in full here on 9 June, so no re-run),
and, separately, the Anthropic copyright settlement reaches its first payout-math checkpoint. New
today:

- **The Bartz v. Anthropic settlement hits its payout-calculation milestone on 11 June — the
  mechanics of the $1.5B author payout are about to become concrete.** Background: a class of
  authors sued Anthropic over training Claude on their books. Judge William Alsup (N.D. Cal.) ruled
  on summary judgment that training on *legally acquired* books is fair use, but that Anthropic's
  use of *pirated* copies was **not** — and Anthropic settled the piracy exposure for **US$1.5
  billion**, roughly **$3,000 per work**, with terms requiring **destruction of the pirated
  dataset** and covering past training data only (not model outputs). The fairness (final-approval)
  hearing was 14 May; the settlement administrator's per-work calculations are dated **11 June**,
  with disbursement to follow. Why it matters: this is the first time the "pirated training corpus"
  theory has produced a concrete dollar figure per work, and it sets the reference price every other
  pending case (NYT v. OpenAI, Kadrey v. Meta) will be measured against — the fair-use-for-clean-data
  / liable-for-piracy split is now the working template. [Authors Guild — what authors need to know about the Anthropic settlement](https://authorsguild.org/advocacy/artificial-intelligence/what-authors-need-to-know-about-the-anthropic-settlement/) `[via snippet]`; valuation/per-work figures corroborated by [IPWatchdog — the $1.5B Anthropic settlement](https://ipwatchdog.com/2025/10/02/ai-training-data-watershed-1-5-billion-anthropic-settlement/) `[via snippet]`

- **Standard Bots raised a $200M Series C (announced 9 June) for AI-driven industrial robots.**
  The round was led by RoboStrategy with General Catalyst co-leading, bringing total raised to
  ~$220M. Standard Bots makes industrial robot arms and is pitching a combination of lower cost,
  U.S. manufacturing, and "AI-powered ease of use" (natural-language / learned-behavior programming
  rather than the traditional hand-coded motion paths) against incumbents Fanuc and ABB; it plans to
  scale via a new factory. Why it matters: it's another data point in capital rotating from
  pure-software AI toward *embodied* AI — robotics startups that wrap a learned policy around real
  hardware — where the moat is manufacturing plus data, not just a model. [Tech Startups — VC & startup funding roundup, June 9 2026](https://techstartups.com/2026/06/09/venture-capital-startup-funding-roundup-june-9-2026-2/) `[via snippet]` `[single-source]`

## 🍎 Apple Silicon / on-device

Quiet, which is normal for a weekday — the Weekend brief carries the depth here. Nothing
today-dated, but two reference points worth keeping in view:

- **Liquid AI's LFM2.5-8B-A1B (released 28 May) remains the most interesting recent on-device drop.**
  It's a Mixture-of-Experts model — 8.3B total parameters but only ~1.5B "active" per token (MoE
  routes each token through a small subset of expert sub-networks, so you pay compute for ~1.5B
  while keeping the capacity of 8.3B) — explicitly designed to run on phones and laptops. Reported
  gains over its predecessor are large on instruction-following (IFEval ~91.8) and math (MATH500
  ~88.8). Why it matters for Apple-Silicon users: an 8B-class MoE with ~1.5B active is close to the
  sweet spot for fast local inference on a Mac without melting the battery. [Liquid AI — introducing LFM2.5](https://www.liquid.ai/blog/introducing-lfm2-5-the-next-generation-of-on-device-ai) `[via snippet]` `[single-source]`
- **llama.cpp's recent direction is quality-of-life, not headline features:** resumable GGUF
  downloads, pulling GGUF models directly from Docker Hub, and browser WebGPU inference. (GGUF is
  the standard quantized model-file format for local CPU/Mac inference.) No dated June release to
  report; flagging the trend, not a launch. [ggml-org/llama.cpp releases](https://github.com/ggml-org/llama.cpp/releases) `[via snippet]`
