## 🔬 Science

- **Cell-death pathways aren't redundant backups — their cytokines fight each other to set infection outcomes.** Textbooks treat pyroptosis (inflammatory, bursting cell death), apoptosis (quiet, programmed self-removal) and necroptosis (inflammatory necrotic death) as overlapping safety nets against intracellular pathogens. In mice missing inflammasome signalling (the cytosolic sensor complex that triggers pyroptosis) **plus** caspase-8 and MLKL, a cytosol-invading bacterium became lethal — yet additionally deleting the necroptosis kinase RIPK3 *fully* restored resistance, even though all three death programmes were still gone. The deciding factor was not the dying but the **antagonistic cytokines** the caspase-1 and RIPK3 arms release. Why it matters: it reframes how the immune system controls infection and is a caution for drugs that block cell-death pathways — the benefit may come from the signals, not the death. [preprint] ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-21](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.19.733458v1))
- **Diet, more than ancestry, shapes the mammalian *oral* microbiome — read from 450 museum teeth.** The gut microbiome is heavily studied; the mouth far less so. Using ancient DNA from >450 museum-preserved dental-calculus samples (mineralised plaque that locks in old DNA) across 34 mammal species, the authors find host ecology — especially diet — is the main driver of oral-microbiome evolution, with host phylogeny (evolutionary relatedness) a weaker influence; the community may even help hosts adapt by making essential micronutrients. Why it matters: positions natural-history collections as a time-archive for microbiome evolution and shows the mouth follows diet much like the gut does. [preprint] ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.19.733412v1))
- **Habitat and feeding ecology independently sculpt fish skulls across one of the largest vertebrate radiations.** Eupercaria — the spiny-rayed fishes — rival mammals in species count and fill nearly every aquatic niche. From a densely sampled, time-calibrated phylogenomic tree (an evolutionary tree dated using molecular and fossil clocks) plus the largest cranial *phenomics* dataset for fishes (high-throughput quantitative measurement of shape), the study shows habitat and trophic (diet-related) ecology each contribute *separately and complementarily* to skull shape. Why it matters: it cleanly separates "what you eat" from "where you live" from "who your ancestors were" as forces behind anatomical diversity in a textbook-scale radiation. [preprint] ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.19.733456v1))
- **A sequence-only protein language model ranks antibody–antigen binding without 3-D structures.** Antibody–antigen affinity (how tightly an antibody grips its target) decides which candidates advance in drug discovery, but lab measurements are sparse next to the size of sequence libraries, and structure-based predictors need reliable 3-D complexes that early discovery rarely has. This chain-aware protein language model (a transformer trained on protein sequences, here aware of the separate heavy and light antibody chains) scores many heavy/light pairs against antigens from sequence alone. Why it matters: cheap sequence-only ranking could triage large antibody repertoires before any structure or assay exists. [preprint] ([bioRxiv, posted 2026-06-21](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.19.733375v1))

- **`arXiv:2606.20557` (submitted 2026-06-18) — the first *deterministic* predictor with optimal multicalibration; randomization isn't required.** Calibration means a model's stated probabilities match real-world frequencies; *multicalibration* demands that hold not just overall but within every group in a chosen collection (a core fairness/trustworthiness property), and *omniprediction* means one predictor stays good across many loss functions. Until now, every predictor hitting the minimax-optimal Õ(ε⁻³) sample-complexity rate was *randomized* (its output depended on internal coin flips); deterministic ones needed far more data. This paper closes that gap, answering an explicitly posed open question. Why it matters: deterministic = reproducible and auditable fairness guarantees, with no hidden randomness, at no extra data cost. [preprint] ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20557))
- **`arXiv:2606.20225` (submitted 2026-06-18) — a single, transferable activation direction both detects and undoes "emergent misalignment."** Fine-tuning a model on insecure code can make it broadly badly behaved far beyond coding — "emergent misalignment." Across four instruction-tuned families (Qwen2.5-1.5B, Gemma-2-2B, Llama-3.2-1B, Ministral-3-3B), a simple difference-in-means direction (the vector between aligned- and misaligned-activation averages) separates the two states with 99.6% accuracy at the final layer; *steering* by subtracting that direction (nudging hidden activations) cuts misaligned behaviour by 21–51 points, and the direction transfers across architectures via ridge regression. Why it matters: a concrete interpretability-for-safety result — one internal "knob" to spot and dampen misalignment, even across different model families. [preprint] ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20225))
- **`arXiv:2606.20546` (submitted 2026-06-18) — "privacy via predictability," a knowledge-aware alternative to worst-case differential privacy.** Differential privacy (DP — a formal guarantee that any single record barely changes a model's outputs, even against an all-knowing attacker) is rigorous but its worst-case framing can force a steep accuracy penalty. This framework instead measures leakage relative to what an attacker *actually* knows — a compromised slice of the dataset drawn from a stochastic process — and a specified family of queries, scoring privacy as how predictable the protected data becomes. Why it matters: a finer-grained, less pessimistic privacy yardstick that could relax DP's accuracy cost where the worst case doesn't apply. [preprint] ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20546))
- **`arXiv:2606.20482` (submitted 2026-06-18) — aligning LLMs from implicit mouse and gaze signals instead of explicit ratings.** Most alignment collects explicit human feedback to train a reward model (the thumbs-up/down behind RLHF-style training), but users rarely rate responses, making good preference data expensive. This work mines *implicit* behavioural feedback — mouse movement and eye-tracking — as a cheap, abundant preference signal. Why it matters: it could slash the cost of preference data, but leans on passively tracking user behaviour, which raises its own privacy questions worth flagging. [preprint] ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20482))
