## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

Two significant moves from earlier this week that this desk had not logged — recorded here for the file, each dated to when it actually happened (not today):

- **SpaceX is buying Cursor maker Anysphere for \$60 billion (all-stock), announced Tue 16 Jun.** Per a securities filing disclosed that day, Elon Musk's SpaceX — fresh off its own record IPO the prior week — will acquire San Francisco AI-coding startup Anysphere, whose Cursor editor is one of the most-used "agentic" coding tools (an AI that can edit, run, and iterate on a codebase, not just autocomplete a line). The deal converts Cursor stock into SpaceX Class A shares at a price set by SpaceX's volume-weighted average over the seven trading days before close, targets a **Q3 2026 close pending regulatory (antitrust) approval**, and carries unusually large break fees — reportedly **\$10 billion** generally and **\$4 billion** if antitrust specifically kills it. It follows an option SpaceX secured in April to either pay ~\$10B for a partnership or buy the company outright for \$60B. Why it matters: it is one of the largest pure-AI acquisitions on record and folds a leading independent coding tool into a Musk company — a consolidation move in the AI-developer-tooling layer, the same layer OpenAI entered by buying Astral (uv/ruff) in March. Confirmed via the filing and quality secondary reporting; not a rumor. — [CBS News, 16 Jun 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/spacex-cursor-60-billion-ai-acquisition/) · [Quartz, 16 Jun 2026](https://qz.com/spacex-acquiring-cursor-anysphere-60-billion-stock-deal-061626) `[via snippet]`

- **FERC ordered six US grid operators to defend or rewrite the rules governing data-center power access, Thu 18 Jun.** The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (the US federal electricity-market regulator) issued tailored "show-cause" orders — a legal demand to justify an existing practice or change it — under Section 206 of the Federal Power Act to the six regional operators that run the bulk of the US grid: **PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE, and NYISO**. The orders compel each to defend, within ~60 days, why current tariffs (the pricing/connection rules) can absorb gigawatt-scale "large loads" like AI data centers without shifting costs onto ordinary ratepayers — or reform them — plus file a reliability plan within ~30 days for securing enough generation. Why it matters: grid-interconnection queues are now the binding constraint on AI build-out in the US, and this is the regulator trying to shorten a multi-year backlog while shielding consumer bills; it reaches ~30 states but pointedly **not Texas**, whose ERCOT grid sits outside federal jurisdiction. — [Engineering News-Record, 18 Jun 2026](https://www.enr.com/articles/63195-ferc-orders-grid-operators-to-rework-data-center-power-rules) · [E&E News/Politico, 18 Jun 2026](https://www.eenews.net/articles/ferc-acts-to-force-us-markets-to-protect-electricity-ratepayers/) `[via snippet]`
