### MaxProof: population-level test-time scaling reaches gold-medal mathematical proof
**[arXiv:2606.13473](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13473)** · Jiacheng Chen, Xinyu Zhang, Shunkai Zhang et al. (23 authors) · `[preprint]`

MaxProof trains a single model (in the MiniMax-M3 family — the same lab whose open weights and sparse-attention report the dailies covered on 12 June; this is a *distinct* paper, the proof-and-test-time-scaling work, not the weights drop) to do three things: generate proofs, *verify* proofs with a deliberately low false-positive rate, and repair proofs given a critique. At inference, the framework runs the one model in four roles — generator, verifier, refiner, ranker — searches over a whole *population* of candidate proofs, and returns one finalist via tournament selection. The reported result is the striking part: 35/42 on IMO 2025 and 36/42 on USAMO 2026, both above the human gold-medal cutoff. The key design choice is "defense-in-depth" verification — stacking checks so a wrong-but-fluent proof is caught rather than accepted, which is exactly the failure mode that makes proof generation harder than contest-style answer-matching.
*Why this matters:* it lands the same week *Nature* reported humans still beating AI on open research-level proofs — so read the two together: machines now clear *graded-competition* proof at gold level, while *open-ended research* proof remains a human edge. The gap between those two claims is the whole story (see 🧠).

### EurekAgent: "agent environment engineering is all you need" for autonomous discovery
**[arXiv:2606.13662](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13662)** · Amy Xin, Jiening Siow, Junjie Wang et al. · `[preprint]`

The thesis is a deliberate inversion of the usual agent-design instinct. As base models get stronger, the authors argue, the bottleneck in automated scientific discovery stops being *the agent's workflow* (the prompt scaffolding, the planning loop) and becomes *the environment* — the resources, constraints, and interfaces the agent acts within. They call this "environment engineering": building execution settings that amplify productive behaviours (open-ended exploration, cheap validation) rather than prescribing the steps. On their discovery tasks the engineered-environment agents reportedly outperform human-designed approaches on an optimisable metric.
*Why this matters:* it reframes where the marginal research effort should go. If true, the lesson for anyone building agentic systems is to invest in the sandbox — the tools, the feedback signal, the failure modes — over hand-tuned agent choreography.

### Recursive Agent Harnesses (RAH): recursion at the harness level, not the model level
**[arXiv:2606.13643](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13643)** · Elias Lumer, Sahil Sen, Kevin Paul et al. · `[preprint]`

Recursive language models (RLMs) showed that having a model call itself recursively helps on very long contexts. RAH pushes the recursive unit up a level: instead of a bare model call, the thing that recurses is a *full agent harness* — with filesystem tools, code execution, and planning. A parent agent writes and runs a script that spawns subagent harnesses in parallel for fine-grained chunks of work, and uses ordinary function calls for small subtasks. Holding the backbone fixed at GPT-5 to match published Codex/RLM baselines, RAH lifts the Codex coding-agent score on Oolong-Synthetic (199 samples, context buckets up to 4M tokens) from 71.75% to 81.36% — a gain the authors attribute to the harness, not the model — and reaches 89.77% with a Claude Sonnet 4.5 backbone.
*Why this matters:* it formalises and measures the "subagents spawning subagents" pattern that production coding agents have drifted into, and shows the win is real and separable from model quality — useful for anyone deciding whether to invest in orchestration vs. a bigger model.

### Mana: dexterous manipulation of articulated tools, reframed as animation
**[arXiv:2606.13677](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13677)** · Zhao-Heng Yin, Guanya Shi, Pieter Abbeel et al. · `[preprint]`

Articulated tools — scissors, pliers, staplers, anything with internal moving joints — are hard for robots because the hand has to coordinate its own fingers *and* the tool's degrees of freedom while making and breaking contact. Mana (Manipulation Animator) borrows from computer animation: a coarse-to-fine pipeline turns procedurally-generated grasp keyframes into full manipulation trajectories via motion planning plus reinforcement learning (RL — learning a control policy from trial-and-error reward). Specifying a tool's functional affordance takes "a few mouse clicks" (<1 minute per tool), and the system achieves zero-shot sim-to-real transfer — trained purely in simulation, deployed on hardware without real-world fine-tuning — across four tools of different scales and joint types.
*Why this matters:* tool use with internal articulation is one of the last big gaps between "robot can pick things up" and "robot can use a workshop." A largely-automatic data pipeline that transfers zero-shot is a credible path past the usual demonstration-collection bottleneck.

### Distribution-agnostic robust trajectory optimization via chance-constrained RL
**[arXiv:2606.13605](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13605)** · Yashdeep Chaudhary, Roberto Armellin, Harry Holt et al. · `[preprint]`

A clean piece of decision-making-under-uncertainty work aimed at spacecraft trajectories. You compute a deterministic nominal path offline, then use RL only to *robustify* it: it learns a structured affine closed-loop correction (a feedforward nudge plus time-varying feedback gains) so the trajectory still satisfies its constraints when the real world deviates. "Chance-constrained" means the constraints are probabilistic — "stay feasible with probability ≥ p" — enforced here by sampling rollouts and reading off upper-tail quantiles, rather than assuming any particular noise distribution (the only requirement is that you can sample it). The flagship test is a 3-D multi-impulse Earth–Mars transfer benchmarked against a recent robust-optimisation reference; the same scaffold then ports to atmospheric pinpoint rocket landing.
*Why this matters:* "compute a nominal plan, then learn just the robustness layer" is a transferable recipe well beyond aerospace — any setting where you trust a classical optimiser for the average case but need learned feedback for the tails.

### A2D2: reward-guided fine-tuning of any-length discrete diffusion
**[arXiv:2606.13565](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13565)** · Sophia Tang, Yuchen Zhu, Molei Tao et al. · `[preprint]`

Discrete diffusion models generate sequences by iteratively unmasking tokens, and recent work extended them to *any-length* output via token insertion. A2D2 supplies the missing piece: principled reward-guided fine-tuning (steering generation toward a reward, e.g. quality or a verifier score) for the any-length setting, by jointly optimising the *insertion* policy, the *unmasking* policy, and a quality-based inference schedule. The technical core is deriving the Radon–Nikodym derivative (the mathematical object relating two probability "paths") for joint insertion-unmasking trajectories, which lets them prove convergence to the intractable reward-tilted target distribution *without needing samples from it*. Empirically it beats fixed-length fine-tuning and inference-time guidance on both reward and accuracy.
*Why this matters:* diffusion language models are the main non-autoregressive challenger to standard left-to-right generation, and "how do you do RLHF-style steering on them" was an open question — this gives a theoretically grounded answer for the flexible-length case.

### Phase transitions in attention: a Bayesian theory of copy-head emergence
**[arXiv:2606.12058](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.12058)** · Itay Lavie, Kirsten Fischer, Andrey Lekov et al. · `[preprint]`

Induction heads — attention circuits that implement "I saw token X followed by Y earlier, so after X again predict Y," the mechanistic basis of in-context learning — are famous for appearing *abruptly* during training. This paper builds a Bayesian theory of feature learning in a single-layer softmax attention network on a copy task, derives a closed-form posterior over the attention matrix, and reduces it to a low-dimensional set of order parameters. That reduction exposes a genuine *phase transition* in the amount of training data: softmax attention shows a sharp *first-order* transition (a discontinuous jump), whereas linear attention shows an initial second-order transition followed by a smooth crossover. Both Bayesian sampling and ordinary Adam training confirm the picture.
*Why this matters:* it's a first-principles account of *why* the copy subcircuit emerges suddenly rather than gradually — turning an empirical curiosity about training dynamics into something derivable, and giving interpretability a rare closed-form handle on emergence.

### MaskWAM: object-centric world-action models via mask prompting
**[arXiv:2606.13515](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13515)** · Hanyang Yu, Haitao Lin, Jingbo Zhang et al. · `[preprint]`

World-Action Models (WAMs) control robots by *predicting future video* and acting toward it, but they hit two spatial bottlenecks: text instructions are ambiguous in cluttered scenes ("grab the cup" — which cup?), and raw RGB predictions get distracted by task-irrelevant background. MaskWAM makes the model object-centric by using segmentation masks both as inputs (a first-frame mask of the target object as a precise spatial anchor) and as prediction targets (forecasting future masks gives clean, background-suppressing supervision), fused through a Mixture-of-Transformers. Because WAMs are inherently vision-driven, a mask points far more sharply than a sentence — the model beats baselines on LIBERO, RoboTwin, and real-world tasks, especially when language is ambiguous.
*Why this matters:* it's a concrete fix for the "which object do you mean" problem that dogs language-conditioned manipulation, and the future-mask supervision improves even ordinary text-conditioned WAMs — a cheap add-on, not just a new architecture.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

### Sphere packings in higher dimension — an exposition of Klartag's breakthrough
**[arXiv:2606.13313](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13313)** · Guillaume Aubrun (1 author) · `[preprint]`

*Context first:* the sphere-packing problem asks how densely you can fill *n*-dimensional space with equal non-overlapping balls. In high dimensions this is brutally hard, and the best general lower bounds had crept up only by constant factors for decades. Earlier in 2026 Boaz Klartag proved a bound of δ ≳ c·n²·2⁻ⁿ for lattice packings — a *quadratic-in-n* improvement over the long-standing ~n·2⁻ⁿ regime, the kind of jump that doesn't happen often in this corner of geometry. This paper is a clean exposition (a "Bourbaki-style" write-up) of *how* Klartag did it. The proof uses the probabilistic method twice: once in the standard way (averaging over a random lattice), and once in a genuinely new way — studying the stochastic evolution of an ellipsoid that is constrained to keep no nonzero lattice points in its interior.
*Why this matters:* this is the most significant unconditional progress on high-dimensional packing density in a generation, and the second, ellipsoid-flow idea is the reusable novelty. An accessible exposition is exactly what lets the rest of the field build on it. *(Anchor: think of inflating a balloon inside a fixed crystal lattice and watching how large it can grow before it's forced to touch a lattice point — Klartag turns that "how big can the empty region get" question into a tractable random process.)*

### A counterexample to the Elekes–Rónyai problem via split primes
**[arXiv:2606.13619](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13619)** · Cosmin Pohoata (1 author) · `[preprint]`

*Context:* the Elekes–Rónyai problem in arithmetic combinatorics concerns polynomials *f(x,y)*: roughly, unless *f* has special "degenerate" structure (essentially additive, *g(x)+h(y)*, or multiplicative, *g(x)·h(y)*), the set of values *f* takes on a grid *A×A* should be *large* — close to |A|². Pohoata exhibits arbitrarily large sets *A* of reals for which the specific polynomial *x + y + (x−y)²* — which is neither additive nor multiplicative — takes only ≤ |A|^{2−c} values for an absolute constant c>0. That is a genuine counterexample: a non-degenerate polynomial with anomalously few values. The construction leans on number-theoretic input ("split primes").
*Why this matters:* it sharpens the boundary of a foundational expander/non-expander dichotomy in combinatorics, showing the clean "non-degenerate ⇒ many values" intuition has real exceptions and the true classification is subtler than hoped.

### Direct detection of cool molecular gas in a galaxy at z = 7.31
**[arXiv:2606.13393](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13393)** · Karin Cescon, Jacqueline A. Hodge, Leindert A. Boogaard et al. (24 authors) · `[preprint]`

Using deep VLA and ALMA observations, the team detected low-J CO emission (the CO(3–2) line — a standard tracer of cold molecular gas, the raw fuel for star formation) in REBELS-25, a massive star-forming galaxy seen just ~700 million years after the Big Bang. This is the highest-redshift detection of a *low-J* CO transition to date, and it directly confirms a very massive gas reservoir — molecular mass ~10¹¹ solar masses, implying an extreme gas fraction of ~95%. They cross-check the mass with radiative-transfer modelling that properly accounts for the bright cosmic-microwave-background (CMB) at that epoch, which otherwise washes out the faint lines, and recover a consistent figure independent of the usual conversion-factor assumptions.
*Why this matters:* it shows the cold-gas fuel tank of an early-universe galaxy can be measured *directly* rather than inferred, and that the workhorse [C II] tracer still works in the Epoch of Reionization — a methodological green light for next-generation facilities (SKA, ngVLA) to map gas in the first billion years.

### A review of resolving the Hubble tension via late-universe physics
**[arXiv:2606.12980](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.12980)** · Xuan-Dong Jia, Xin-Yi Dai, Yu-Peng Yang et al. · `[preprint]`

*Context:* the "Hubble tension" is the stubborn ~5σ disagreement between the expansion rate H₀ inferred from the early universe (CMB + the ΛCDM standard model) and the larger value measured locally (distance-ladder methods). This review argues the evidence increasingly points to *new low-redshift physics* rather than measurement error: combining DESI baryon-acoustic-oscillation data with uncalibrated Type Ia supernovae yields an H₀ significantly above the early-universe ΛCDM prediction — and crucially does so *without* relying on the local distance ladder, removing the usual "it's a calibration systematic" rebuttal. The authors survey the late-universe model space and conclude known systematics are too small to bridge the gap.
*Why this matters:* it consolidates a shift in emphasis — if the tension survives without the distance ladder, the most-blamed culprit (Cepheid/supernova calibration) is off the hook, and the pressure moves onto dark-energy or late-time-physics modifications of the standard model.

### Are primordial black holes a natural dark-matter candidate?
**[arXiv:2606.12775](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.12775)** · Stefano Profumo (1 author) · `[preprint]`

*Context:* primordial black holes (PBHs) — black holes formed in the very early universe, not from collapsing stars — in the "asteroid-mass window" (10¹⁷–10²² grams) can account for *all* dark matter without violating any current observational bound, yet are often waved away as "fine-tuned" (requiring implausibly precise initial conditions). Profumo tests that dismissal rigorously, applying three complementary fine-tuning measures uniformly across three non-inflationary PBH mechanisms, six inflationary ones, and seven particle-dark-matter benchmarks. The verdict: several PBH mechanisms (e.g. biased-domain-wall formation) are *no more* fine-tuned than mainstream particle candidates like off-resonance WIMPs or freeze-in dark matter; only single-field ultra-slow-roll inflationary collapse is genuinely severely tuned, and for a specific double-exponential-sensitivity reason.
*Why this matters:* it removes a rhetorical objection that has kept PBH dark matter on the fringe, showing "fine-tuned" was conflating the worst mechanism with the whole landscape — relevant as the asteroid-mass window remains observationally open.

### A first search for high-frequency gravitational waves via geomagnetic conversion
**[arXiv:2606.13642](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13642)** · Hongliang Tian, Lei Wu, Xiaolong Yang et al. · `[preprint]`

*Context:* LIGO-class detectors are tuned to gravitational waves at tens-to-thousands of Hz; *high-frequency* gravitational waves (above ~10 kHz, here probing 1 GHz–1 THz) would be a signature of exotic sources and new physics, but there's no standard detector for them. This work exploits the inverse Gertsenshtein effect — a gravitational wave passing through a magnetic field can convert into faint electromagnetic radiation — using Earth's own magnetic field as the converter and radio telescopes (VLA, ALMA) as the readout. No signal was found, but the resulting upper limits on characteristic strain improve existing bounds by up to three orders of magnitude.
*Why this matters:* it's a clever, cheap repurposing of existing radio observatories to open a band of the gravitational-wave spectrum nobody can otherwise reach, and the three-orders-of-magnitude improvement maps out previously-uncharted parameter space ahead of the SKA.

### Quantum Logic Codes: constant-depth complete transversal Clifford instruction sets
**[arXiv:2606.13521](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13521)** · Adam Holmes (1 author) · `[preprint]`

*Context:* in quantum error correction, *transversal* gates — logical operations applied bit-by-bit across the physical qubits, with no qubit interacting with another in the same code block — are the gold standard because they don't spread errors. The catch is they're limited in what gate sets they can realise cheaply. This paper proves universal lower bounds on the circuit depth needed to generate a full logical Clifford algebra (the Clifford group is the workhorse non-universal gate set: Hadamard, phase, CNOT-like gates), and constructs new transversal gates including a *depth-one* logical phase gate in the rotated surface code. The headline is a high-rate code family — encoding √n logical qubits in n physical ones — with a provably constant-depth *complete* 2-local transversal logical Clifford instruction set, tileable to utility scale and concatenatable for higher distance.
*Why this matters:* the cost of logical operations, not just the cost of storing a qubit, is what will gate practical fault-tolerant quantum computing. A high-rate code with a constant-depth complete Clifford "instruction set architecture" is a concrete answer to "how do you actually compute, cheaply, once your qubits are error-protected." *(Anchor: think of it as designing a CPU instruction set, but where every instruction is engineered so a hardware fault can't cascade across the register.)*

### Semi-device-independent certification of nonlocality without entanglement
**[arXiv:2606.13667](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13667)** · Hanwool Lee, Joonwoo Bae (2 authors) · `[preprint]`

*Context:* "nonlocality without entanglement" (NLWE) is the counterintuitive fact that *unentangled* (separable) quantum states can still encode global structure that no local, measure-each-part-separately strategy can fully extract — you need a joint measurement on the whole system. This paper studies *maximum-confidence discrimination* (a state-identification strategy that maximises the chance your guess is right given a detector click) and shows global measurements beat separable ones, establishing NLWE in the language of confidence. The flip side is the useful part: verifying the achievable confidence *certifies* that a genuinely global measurement happened — a "semi-device-independent" certificate, meaning you trust very little about the device internals. Crucially it works with imperfect (non-unit-efficiency) detectors, because it only uses the events that *were* detected.
*Why this matters:* it turns an abstract foundational phenomenon into something demonstrable on today's imperfect hardware, and gives a certification primitive — proof that a device did a real joint quantum measurement — that's useful for quantum-device verification.

## 📊 Data science / applied ML

A strong week for *deployed* (not just benchmarked) ML, courtesy of the industry-authored arXiv papers in the 06-11 batch.

- **Multi-agent RL for dispatch, running in production at DoorDash** — **[arXiv:2606.13604](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13604)** · Haochen Wu, Yi Hou, Shiguang Xie · `[preprint]`. A genuinely deployed system: instead of replacing the combinatorial assignment optimizer that matches couriers to orders, a store-level policy learned from logged marketplace data picks a discrete *multiplier* that shifts the optimizer's tradeoff between delivery quality and batching efficiency. The reward is *delayed operational feedback* — delivery speed, courier utilization, merchant congestion — which is what makes this an honest RL-from-real-world-feedback problem rather than a simulator exercise. _Why it matters:_ it's a template for adding learned control on top of an existing classical optimizer without ripping it out — the realistic path to ML in a high-stakes ops system.
- **Graphical causal reasoning for root-cause analysis in cloud networks** — **[arXiv:2606.13532](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13532)** · Fabien Chraim, Dominik Janzing, John Evans (AWS) · `[preprint]`. Builds a causal graph from binary time-series alarms using bivariate Granger causality and conditional-independence tests, with a spatiotemporal grouping strategy and an automation ontology to cut dimensionality, then assigns edge-specific probabilities as a function of time lag for interpretable root-cause inference. _Why it matters:_ rule-based RCA doesn't scale to large networks; causal-discovery RCA that's *interpretable* (you can see why it blamed a given component) is what on-call engineers will actually trust.
- **Online shift detection and conformal adaptation for deployed safety classifiers** — **[arXiv:2606.11949](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.11949)** · Jun Wen Leong · `[preprint]`. A monitoring layer that uses calibrated sequential statistics to detect when a deployed safety classifier has drifted out of distribution, then a conformal-abstention layer (conformal prediction gives distribution-free error guarantees) adapts thresholds to hold a target 10% error rate. In a pre-registered 800-cell factorial study it caught 86.6% of shifts (mean latency ~40 steps) across synthetic onsets, real temporal jailbreaks, and GCG adversarial attacks. _Why it matters:_ content-safety classifiers silently degrade as attackers and inputs evolve; a statistically principled drift alarm with a guaranteed-error fallback is exactly the production hygiene most deployed classifiers lack.

## 🛡️ Cybersecurity research of the week

- **LiteLLM unauthenticated RCE — CVE-2026-42271 chains with CVE-2026-48710 `[ongoing since 2026-06-08]`.** This week's development on the LiteLLM flaw (the KEV addition itself was logged 9 June): security research detailed how the command-injection bug — two Model Context Protocol (MCP) preview endpoints (`/mcp-rest/test/connection`, `/mcp-rest/test/tools/list`) accept a full stdio-transport server config, so any authenticated low-privilege API key can set the `command` field to an arbitrary binary and have the proxy spawn it — *chains* with a Starlette host-header bypass (CVE-2026-48710) to become **unauthenticated** RCE (remote code execution — run arbitrary code on the host with no login). CVSS 8.7; CISA confirmed active exploitation. LiteLLM is an open-source "AI gateway" that fronts many LLM APIs in an OpenAI-compatible shape, so it sits in front of production model traffic. Fix: LiteLLM ≥ 1.83.7 and Starlette ≥ 1.0.1, or block those two POST routes at the reverse proxy. _Why it matters:_ the AI-gateway layer is becoming critical infrastructure, and "MCP preview endpoint takes a command field" is the kind of feature-as-vulnerability we'll see more of as MCP tooling proliferates. ([The Hacker News, 2026-06-09](https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/litellm-flaw-cve-2026-42271-exploited.html); [Help Net Security, 2026-06-09](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/06/09/litellm-vulnerability-under-active-attack-cisa-warns-cve-2026-42271/); [Horizon3.ai attack research](https://horizon3.ai/attack-research/vulnerabilities/cve-2026-42271-chained-with-cve-2026-48710/))
- **npm supply-chain compromise: `@redhat-cloud-services` namespace, "Miasma" payload.** Researchers reported (around 1–2 June, surfaced this window) at least 32 packages published under the `@redhat-cloud-services` npm scope were compromised, with the attacker bypassing code review entirely to push a payload named **Miasma**. Separately, an AI-*generated* malicious package (`mouse5212-super-formatter`) was found exfiltrating local files to a GitHub repo via a hardcoded token. _Why it matters:_ a trusted-vendor namespace being pushed to without code review is the worst-case supply-chain pattern (downstream installs inherit the trust), and the AI-generated-package finding signals that automated malware authorship has reached the open registries. ([Unit 42 — npm threat landscape](https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/monitoring-npm-supply-chain-attacks/)) `[single-source]`

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

Four things connected across the week's material — this is the one section where I'll editorialize.

1. **Machines cleared *competition* proof at gold level the same week humans held the line on *research* proof — and that's not a contradiction, it's the map.** MaxProof's 35/42 IMO and 36/42 USAMO (above the gold cutoff) and *Nature*'s "First Proof" result (top model 6/10 on open research problems, no model matching experts) are measuring different things. Competition proofs have a verifiable target and a finite bag of techniques; MaxProof's whole edge is a *low-false-positive verifier* plus population search — engineering around verification. Open research proof has no oracle to grade against and rewards genuinely new structure. The honest read: we've largely solved "search the space of known techniques under a good verifier," and barely touched "invent the technique." Terry Tao's vision (covered in the dailies) of AI-as-collaborator that shatters a problem into thousands of Lean-checkable subcases is the *bridge* between the two — and it's telling that verification, not generation, is the load-bearing idea in both MaxProof and the Tao program.

2. **Speculative decoding had a breakout week — three independent advances.** llama.cpp merged EAGLE3; Xiaomi shipped block-diffusion speculative decoding inside MiMo's weights; and the dailies' `arXiv:2606.13426` brought block verification to continuous diffusion. The pattern: the field has accepted that raw model quality is no longer the only frontier — *how fast you can verify a draft* is now a first-class research target, from the kernel level (llama.cpp) to the algorithm level (diffusion block verification) to the product level (in-weights DFlash). For local inference especially, this is where the next 2× lives.

3. **"Engineer the environment / harness, not the agent" is consolidating into a thesis.** EurekAgent argues the bottleneck for autonomous discovery has shifted from agent workflows to environment design; Recursive Agent Harnesses shows a measurable win that's attributable to the *harness* with the model held fixed; and the DoorDash and AWS papers both add *learned control on top of an existing optimizer/system* rather than replacing it. Across research and production, the lesson rhymes: with strong base models, the leverage is in the scaffolding — the tools, feedback signals, and execution environment — not in cleverer prompting of the model itself.

4. **The AI stack is now its own attack surface and its own benchmark-buster, simultaneously.** In one week: an AI gateway (LiteLLM) became an actively-exploited unauthenticated-RCE path *through its MCP tooling*; AI-generated malware showed up in npm; and a Nature Medicine study found general LLMs beating specialized clinical AI on benchmarks. The connective tissue is that AI infrastructure has matured enough to be worth attacking and to disrupt incumbents — and the same MCP/agent plumbing that makes models useful (LiteLLM's tool endpoints, agent harnesses spawning processes) is exactly what's expanding the security perimeter. Convenience and exposure are, as usual, the same feature viewed from two sides.
