## 🔬 Science

- **China's electric-vehicle boom averted an estimated ~262,000 premature deaths through cleaner air.** A modelling study finds that swapping fossil-fuel cars for EVs measurably lowered urban particulate matter (PM — airborne soot and dust fine enough to lodge deep in the lungs) and nitrogen oxides across Chinese cities. Context: outdoor air pollution kills ~4 million people a year worldwide, roughly a quarter of them in China, so this is one of the first large real-world signals — not just a forecast — that mass EV adoption pays off in lives, even before counting CO₂. Caveat: not every pollutant fell (tyre/brake particulates and power-plant emissions persist). ([Nature news, 2026-06-05](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01781-5))
- **Cash handouts to expecting parents raised infants' birth weight.** A randomized trial of unconditional cash transfers (money given with no strings attached) found a measurable increase in newborn birth weight — a strong predictor of later health. Why it matters: it's causal evidence (randomized, not just correlational) that an economic intervention shifts a hard biological outcome, informing the long-running debate over whether direct cash beats in-kind aid for maternal health. ([Nature news, 2026-06-04](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01765-5))
- **Magnetic fields of hot giant exoplanets look just like the Solar System's.** Astronomers measured the magnetic field strengths of "hot Jupiters" (gas giants orbiting scorchingly close to their stars) and found them consistent with the same dynamo scaling that sets field strengths on Jupiter and Earth. Why it matters: a planet's magnetic field shields its atmosphere from being stripped by stellar wind, so this constrains which exoplanets can hold onto an atmosphere — and suggests the physics of planetary dynamos is universal rather than peculiar to our system. ([Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-02](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02870-1))
- **A puzzling "long-period radio transient" turns out to be a white-dwarf binary whose orbit *is* the clock.** Long-period radio transients are sources that emit bright, regular radio bursts every few minutes-to-hours — too slow for ordinary pulsars, and their nature has been contested. The source ASKAP J174508.9−505149 shows radio bursts *and* X-ray emission both ticking at ~1.3 hours, matching the spectroscopic orbital period of a white dwarf (a dead, Earth-sized stellar core) pulling gas from a low-mass red-dwarf companion. The takeaway: at least some of these mystery transients are accreting white-dwarf binaries, not exotic magnetars — the orbital motion itself paces the bursts. ([Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-01](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02882-x); preprint [arXiv:2606.04232](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04232) `[preprint]` [via snippet])

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

_No new arXiv announcement today (weekend). The following are from the most recent batch — submitted Thu 2026-06-04 — and survive yesterday's dedup. All `[preprint]`._

- **arXiv:2606.06470 (2026-06-04) — PC Layer: Polynomial Weight Preconditioning for LLM pre-training.** Reshapes the singular-value spectrum (the "stretch factors" of a weight matrix) with a low-degree polynomial so training stays numerically stable; crucially the preconditioner can be merged back into the weights afterward, so there is *zero* inference overhead. Why interesting: most training-stability tricks cost you at deployment — this one is free at inference and helped both AdamW and the newer Muon optimizer in Llama-1B pretraining. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06470))
- **arXiv:2606.06492 (2026-06-04) — Code2LoRA: hypernetwork-generated adapters for evolving codebases.** A hypernetwork (a network that outputs another network's weights) emits a repository-specific LoRA adapter (LoRA — a tiny low-rank weight patch that specializes a model cheaply), injecting project knowledge with no extra prompt tokens at inference. Why interesting: today you either stuff repo context into the prompt (slow, token-hungry) or fine-tune per repo (expensive, goes stale); this generates the adapter on the fly as the codebase changes. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06492))
- **arXiv:2606.06428 (2026-06-04) — RL elicits in-context translation of unseen languages.** Instead of memorizing specific low-resource languages, a model is trained with reinforcement learning to *use* grammar references and examples supplied in its context window — acquiring the meta-skill of "translate from the rulebook you're handed." Why interesting: it targets genuinely zero-resource languages (no training corpus at all) and aims for transfer rather than per-language overfitting — relevant for the long tail of the world's ~7,000 languages. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06428))
- **arXiv:2606.06473 (2026-06-04) — MLEvolve: a self-evolving agent for automated ML algorithm discovery.** An LLM multi-agent framework that extends tree search to a "Progressive MCGS" (Monte Carlo Graph Search — a search that lets sibling branches share information instead of exploring in isolation) so a coding agent can iteratively invent and refine ML algorithms over long horizons. Why interesting: tackles the known failure modes of autonomous ML-engineering agents — memoryless search and branches that can't learn from each other. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06473))
- **arXiv:2606.06440 (2026-06-04) — Causal atlases from entropic inference (stat.ML).** Standard causal discovery fits a *single* "best" directed acyclic graph (DAG — a causes-arrows diagram with no loops), but real data often support several equally-plausible chains of causation. This builds an *atlas* of multiple data-faithful causal maps via entropic inference (picking distributions that assume the least beyond what the data force). Why interesting: it surfaces causal ambiguity instead of hiding it behind one confident-but-arbitrary graph — useful anywhere a wrong causal arrow is costly. ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06440))
