## 🔬 Science

- **First precise base editing of human embryos posted as a preprint — praise and alarm in equal measure.** A Columbia University team led by developmental biologist Dieter Egli reports using *base editing* (a CRISPR variant that rewrites a single DNA letter without cutting both strands of the double helix, so it's far less prone to scrambling the genome than classic CRISPR) to correct genes governing LDL cholesterol ("bad" cholesterol) and hemoglobin in human embryos — the first time the technique has been applied to embryos. The work was posted to bioRxiv on June 1 and is **not yet peer-reviewed**; reviewers call it an impressive step toward repairing disease mutations before birth, while critics warn it normalizes germline editing and note the method still produces *mosaic* embryos (only some cells get the edit). Why it matters: editing an embryo changes every cell of the resulting person and is heritable — the bright line bioethics has tried to hold since the 2018 He Jiankui scandal. `[preprint]` `[disputed]` ([Nature, 2026-06-05](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01827-8); [Science, 2026-06-05](https://www.science.org/content/article/first-us-team-gene-edit-human-embryos-revealed) [via snippet])

- **Graphene's superconductivity traced to electrons living on opposite faces of the crystal.** In rhombohedral graphene (a few-layer stack of carbon sheets in a specific ABC ordering), superconductivity — current flowing with zero resistance — turns out to emerge from an oddly "split" normal state: electron-like and hole-like carriers sit on *opposite outer surfaces* of the flake, with almost nothing in the bulk. Think of the slab as a sandwich where the two crusts carry the current and the filling is inert. Why it matters: graphene superconductivity is tunable purely by an electric field (no chemical doping), making it a clean lab platform to probe how superconductivity arises — and the dual-surface picture is a new ingredient for theories of these "moiré"-free systems. The peer-reviewed paper appears in Nature Physics, accompanied by a News & Views ("Superconductivity on two surfaces"). ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-05](https://www.nature.com/nphys.rss); [arXiv:2507.18598](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.18598) [preprint] [via snippet])

- **A 1960s claim that memory is "edible" still won't replicate.** In the 1960s, psychologist James McConnell claimed planarian flatworms trained to react to light retained the memory after being cut in half — and, more startlingly, that feeding ground-up trained worms to naïve worms *transferred* the learning, hinting memory might be stored in RNA molecules. A Harvard group led by Sam Gershman re-ran the experiments with McConnell's original protocols and worms sourced from the same Michigan lake, and found that modern planarians simply **don't learn** the task at all — so the original effect can be neither confirmed nor mechanistically explained. Why it matters: it's a clean case study in how a charismatic, paradigm-shaping result can fail the replication test decades on, and a reminder that "memory transfer" remains firmly unproven. ([Quanta, 2026-06-05](https://www.quantamagazine.org/are-memories-transferable-or-edible-20260605/))

- **Astrobiologists themselves are unconvinced by 2025's "we may have found life" headlines.** A survey published in Nature Astronomy quantifies how the field reacted to last year's two big claims — a possible *biosignature* (a chemical fingerprint plausibly produced only by life) on exoplanet K2-18 b in April 2025, and the "Cheyava Falls" rock features found by NASA's Perseverance rover on Mars in September 2025. Only **6.6%** of surveyed astrobiologists agreed life had *probably* been found at K2-18 b (≈two-thirds disagreed); the Mars rock fared a bit better at **15.1%** agreement. Why it matters: it puts hard numbers on expert skepticism, a useful antidote to breathless coverage and a baseline for how the community will weigh the next claim. ([Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-05](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02876-9))

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.06468 (2026-06-04)** — *Goedel-Architect* hits state-of-the-art automated theorem proving. An agentic system writes Lean 4 proofs (Lean is a *proof assistant* — software that mechanically checks every step of a mathematical proof) by first drafting a "blueprint" — a dependency graph of lemmas building to the theorem — then closing each lemma node in parallel. On the open DeepSeek-V4-Flash backbone it reaches **100% on MiniF2F-test** (a standard Olympiad-style proving benchmark) and **88.8% on PutnamBench**, and solves 4/6 of IMO 2025 — at up to 500× lower cost than comparable open pipelines. Why it matters: formal, machine-checked math at this hit-rate and price brings "AI mathematician collaborators" closer to practical use. `[preprint]` ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06468))

- **arXiv:2606.06467 (2026-06-04)** — *Cross-Layer Sparse Attention (CLSA)* makes long-context decoding cheaper without the usual quality hit. Long-context models spend most of their compute scanning the *KV-cache* (the stored keys/values of all previous tokens) to decide what to attend to. CLSA, built on KV-sharing architectures (YOCO), computes the top-k token selection *once* and reuses that routing index across layers, keeping token-level precision while killing repeated routing overhead — up to **7.6× faster decoding and 17.1× higher throughput at 128K context**. Why it matters: reasoning models that emit long chains of thought are bottlenecked exactly here, so this is a direct lever on inference cost. `[preprint]` ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06467))

- **arXiv:2606.06479 (2026-06-04)** — *Supervised Memory Training (SMT)* trains recurrent nets without backprop-through-time. RNNs normally learn long-range dependencies via *BPTT* — unrolling the network across the whole sequence — which is slow (sequential) and suffers vanishing/exploding gradients. SMT instead reframes RNN training as plain supervised learning on one-step memory-transition labels (a Transformer encoder, trained to keep only past info useful for predicting the future, supplies the targets), giving a stable constant-length gradient path between any two tokens and *time-parallel* training. It reportedly beats BPTT on language and pixel-sequence modeling. Why it matters: if it scales, it revives recurrent models as a parallel-trainable alternative to Transformers for very long sequences. `[preprint]` ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06479))

- **arXiv:2606.06475 (2026-06-04)** — *RREDCoT* improves how RL credits reasoning steps. Reasoning models trained with RL (e.g. GRPO — a popular policy-optimization method) only get a reward *after* the full chain-of-thought finishes, a high-variance "delayed reward" problem. RREDCoT redistributes that final reward back onto the individual reasoning *segments* that mattered — using the model itself to estimate which steps were pivotal, with no extra generation passes. Why it matters: cheaper, lower-variance credit assignment could make RL fine-tuning of reasoning models more sample-efficient and stable. `[preprint]` ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06475))

- **arXiv:2606.06474 (2026-06-04)** — *SARDI* turns a diffusion language model's "discarded guesses" into a retrieval signal. Discrete *diffusion language models* generate text by denoising a whole response in parallel, committing confident tokens and dropping unconfident ones each step. SARDI notices the dropped low-confidence tokens still surface relevant entities *early*, and uses them to fire off retrieval (RAG — retrieval-augmented generation, fetching external evidence) before the answer is locked in. Training-free and retriever-agnostic, it beats training-free baselines on five multi-hop QA sets at up to **8× throughput**. Why it matters: a free quality boost for the diffusion-LM family, which is being pushed as a faster alternative to left-to-right generation. `[preprint]` ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06474))
