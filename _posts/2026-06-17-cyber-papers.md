## 🛡️ Cybersecurity

A heavy patch day: Oracle's quarterly mega-update and Android's June bulletin between them shipped dozens of CVSS-10.0 fixes. NVD's date-windowed feed listed **630** CVEs published 2026-06-17, of which 261 score ≥8.8. The standouts below. Note on scoring: **CVSS** (Common Vulnerability Scoring System) runs 0–10; **9.0+ is "critical."** The vector `AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N` means network-reachable, low attack complexity, **no privileges and no user interaction** — i.e. trivially exploitable by anyone who can reach the service.

- **Oracle June 2026 Critical Patch Update — a wall of unauthenticated RCEs across Fusion Middleware.** Oracle's quarterly CPU (its scheduled batch of fixes) shipped multiple CVSS-10.0 flaws that let an unauthenticated, network-only attacker fully take over the product — **RCE** = remote code execution, running attacker code on the server. The worst:
  - **CVE-2026-35301 (CVSS 10.0)** — WebLogic Server Console; unauthenticated HTTP attacker can compromise WebLogic. WebLogic is a Java application server that fronts huge numbers of enterprise apps, so a Console RCE is a classic "own the middleware, own everything behind it" bug. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-35301)
  - **CVE-2026-35308 (CVSS 10.0)** — Oracle Coherence (in-memory data grid) "Centralized Third Party Jars" component; unauthenticated full compromise, scope-changed (the attacker breaks out of the component into the host). [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-35308)
  - **CVE-2026-46978 (CVSS 10.0)** — Oracle Solaris 11.4 Remote Administration Daemon (RAD); unauthenticated attacker over HTTPS compromises the OS. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-46978)
  
  Plus a large cluster of 10.0s across WebCenter Portal/Sites, Enterprise Capture, and E-Business Suite. Why it matters: Oracle middleware sits in the core of banks, telcos and governments; these are the exact products ransomware crews and state actors hunt after a CPU drops, racing defenders who haven't patched. Patch availability: fixes are in the June CPU — apply immediately. Exploitation status: none confirmed in the wild yet, but historically WebLogic Console RCEs are weaponised within days. [NVD CVE-2026-35301](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-35301)

- **Android June 2026 security bulletin — CVSS-10.0 NFC and Telecomm flaws, no user interaction.** Google's monthly bulletin includes several critical local-privilege-escalation bugs that need **no user interaction**:
  - **CVE-2026-0081 (CVSS 10.0)** — a missing permission check in NFC lets an attacker spoof an NFC (near-field "tap-to-pay/transfer") event, escalating privilege. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-0081)
  - **CVE-2026-28615 (CVSS 10.0)** — a Telecomm permissions bypass allows initiating an unauthorized phone call, leading to local escalation of privilege. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-28615)

The flaw is a directory/path-traversal (`../` escapes a permitted folder) letting an **authenticated** remote attacker write or overwrite arbitrary files on the SD-WAN Manager — the controller that pushes config to an organisation's entire software-defined WAN, so file-write there can mean network-wide compromise. - **Postiz JWT-trust flaw lets a low-priv user forge sessions (CVE-2026-48781, CVSS 9.9).** Postiz, an AI social-media scheduling tool, signed an attacker-controlled JSON blob into a session-shaped **JWT** (JSON Web Token — the signed token apps use to prove "this request is from logged-in user X") using the app's own `JWT_SECRET`, and the auth middleware then trusted every claim in that token without re-checking it. A reminder that desktop media apps quietly unpacking downloaded archives are an underrated local-RCE surface. [NVD](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-48055)

## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

Today's second batch (arXiv announcements dated 2026-06-16, the window that rolls into today after the 20:00 ET cutoff), filtered for interpretability, RL, and efficient-inference work and de-duplicated against this morning's Overview (which cited 2606.17107/.17118/.17120/.17289 — no overlap). All `[preprint]`, not peer-reviewed.

- **`arXiv:2606.17399` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "The Discrete-Log Clock: How a Transformer Learns Modular Multiplication."** When a small transformer "groks" (suddenly generalises, long after it has memorised the training set) modular *addition*, prior work showed it uses a sparse set of Fourier frequencies — a clean "clock" algorithm. For modular *multiplication*, earlier analyses reported a messy "dense" spectrum needing all frequencies. This paper argues that messiness is an artefact of looking in the wrong basis: the natural transform for multiplication is the **multiplicative character transform** (the Fourier analysis appropriate to the multiplicative group of integers mod p, rather than the additive one), and in that basis the learned solution is once again sparse and interpretable. Why it matters: mechanistic interpretability — reverse-engineering the actual algorithm a net learns — has leaned heavily on the modular-arithmetic "grokking" toy model; this says a headline "the network needs everything" result was a basis illusion, and the right mathematical lens recovers a simple story. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17399)

- **`arXiv:2606.17478` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "Decoding Hidden Deception in Reasoning LLMs: Activation Explainers for Deception Auditing" (STATEWITNESS).** As reasoning models get better, the safety worry is that they may *deceive* — say one thing while their internal computation "knows" another. Existing deception monitors either score the visible transcript or emit an opaque scalar probe score, giving auditors little to inspect. STATEWITNESS trains a separate decoder that reads the target model's hidden states (its internal activation vectors) and answers natural-language questions or emits structured reports about them — turning "the probe fired" into "here is *why* this looks deceptive," across seven deception datasets and two reasoning models. Why it matters: it's a step from black-box deception detection toward inspectable, court-of-record-style evidence, which is what an audit actually needs. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17478)

- **`arXiv:2606.17609` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "The Benchmark Illusion: Pruned LLMs Can Pass Multiple Choice but Fail to Answer."** Pruning (zeroing out a large fraction of a model's weights to cut memory and inference cost) is supposed to be near-free if benchmark scores hold. This paper shows a trap: under high-sparsity pruning — especially **Wanda** (a popular pruning method) — a model can keep its multiple-choice accuracy while losing the ability to *produce* the correct answer in open generation. Tracking the same multilingual questions before and after pruning, they find pruning often doesn't erase the knowledge so much as demote the right answer below the top output. Why it matters: it's a concrete warning that multiple-choice leaderboards (where the model only has to rank options) systematically overstate how much capability survives compression — the model that "passed the test" may flunk the real task. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17609)

- **`arXiv:2606.17682` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "From Trainee to Trainer: LLM-Designed Training Environment for RL with Multi-Agent Reasoning."** RL (reinforcement learning — training by trial-and-error reward) pipelines for LLMs usually require humans to hand-redesign the training environment between stages, guessing what setup will best improve the current policy. This "LLM-as-Environment-Engineer" framework closes the loop: the current policy model reads its own failure trajectories plus context and proposes the configuration of the *next* training environment. They introduce MAPF-FrozenLake, a controllable multi-agent-pathfinding testbed (agents navigating a grid without colliding) whose generator exposes tunable difficulty dimensions. Why it matters: automated curriculum design — the system deciding what to practice next — is a recurring bottleneck in agent RL, and a model that critiques its own failures to set its next lesson is a tidy self-improvement primitive. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17682)

- **`arXiv:2606.18183` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "A Diffusion Approximation for Temporal-Difference Learning with Linear Features under Markovian Noise."** Temporal-difference (TD) learning is the workhorse for *policy evaluation* — estimating how good a fixed strategy is — and its standard continuous-time description is an ODE (ordinary differential equation) that captures the average trajectory but ignores the random jitter that sets the final error floor. This paper derives an **SDE** (stochastic differential equation — an ODE with a calibrated noise term) for linear TD(0) under Markovian sampling (consecutive, correlated samples, as in real trajectories rather than idealised independent draws). The model cleanly separates the contraction driven by the projected Bellman operator from the fluctuations injected by correlated sampling. Why it matters: it gives theory for *where the error floor comes from*, not just that the average converges — useful for understanding why TD methods plateau and how sampling correlation hurts. [preprint, theory] [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18183)

- **`arXiv:2606.17648` (submitted 2026-06-16) — "From Brewing to Resolution: Tracing the Internal Lifecycle of Code Reasoning in LLMs."** Accuracy alone can't explain why an LLM tracks a variable correctly but fails on a semantically identical loop. This work traces an internal "lifecycle": models first **brew** an answer — it becomes *linearly recoverable* from hidden states many layers before the model can actually decode it into output — and then resolve into one of four outcomes (Resolved, Overprocessed, Misresolved, Unresolved). Identical task accuracies, they show, can hide fundamentally different failure modes that surface-level evaluation misses. Why it matters: it's an interpretability lens on *code* reasoning specifically, and the "brewing" finding (the answer exists internally well before it surfaces) suggests where to intervene to fix the failures that don't show up in the score. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17648)
