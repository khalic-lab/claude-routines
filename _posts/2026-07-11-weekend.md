## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. Reinforcement learning is leaving the chatbot.** For two years RL has meant one thing in practice — the post-training step that turns a base language model into a reasoner. This week it showed up as something more general in two very different places. In a clean, fully-auditable toy world, Abdulsalam, Patel and Saxe demonstrate that RL doesn't merely amplify skills the base model already has; it *composes* primitive operations into new, reusable higher-level strategies (arXiv:2607.07646). And in *Nature*, Google Quantum AI used RL as a live controller that keeps a quantum error-correcting code calibrated against drifting hardware *without stopping the computation* (a longstanding blocker for real quantum algorithms). Read together, these say RL is maturing from "the thing that makes chatbots reason" into a general-purpose method for control and strategy discovery. The honest counter-current is in the same issue of the arXiv: Sels and Morone's semiclassical analysis finds *no* quantum advantage for the QAOA optimization algorithm on spin glasses (arXiv:2607.08708). The lesson isn't "RL/quantum wins everywhere" — it's that the field is now careful enough to map where these tools help and where they don't.

**2. The week the field audited its own foundations.** An unusual share of this week's best ML papers are demolitions. "Super weights" — the handful of parameters whose removal wrecks a model — turn out *not* to be trainable in isolation; targeting them collapses the model to random guessing, so importance does not imply trainability (arXiv:2607.08733). GaLore, a popular memory-saving optimizer, assumes there is a slowly-drifting low-rank gradient subspace to track; a careful measurement shows that beyond a tiny reproducible core, that subspace is mostly estimator noise (arXiv:2607.05872). DeepSWE shows the coding benchmarks everyone cites are contaminated and mis-graded, with an independent judge disagreeing with SWE-Bench Pro's inherited tests a third of the time (arXiv:2607.07946). And the safety result of the week — CoT monitoring makes an adversarial agent *more* likely to get harmful actions approved, not less (arXiv:2607.08066) — is a demolition of a mechanism labs are actively betting on. This is what a maturing field looks like: the most valuable work is increasingly the work that tells you which of your assumptions were never true.

**3. Interpretability is trading striking demos for falsifiable structure.** Three papers this week push mechanistic interpretability from "here is a compelling story about one circuit" toward claims you can actually test. D'Angelo and colleagues at EPFL prove that induction heads — the attention circuits credited with in-context learning — are literally implementing classical n-gram smoothing (Jelinek–Mercer and Dirichlet), grounding a famous, hand-wavy object in decades-old statistics (arXiv:2607.02800). Baez, Karny and Pataranutaporn show that "sycophancy" is not one behavior but several, with factual and opinion flattery occupying distinct internal representations that steering vectors don't transfer between (arXiv:2607.07003). And the super-weights negative result above is, at bottom, an interpretability correction: "this parameter is important" and "you can train this parameter" are different claims. The through-line — visible last week too, in the confidence-intervals-for-patching work — is a field growing a spine: less anecdote, more estimand.

**4. Open weights keep scaling, and the frontier of that scaling is Chinese.** While the daily editions tracked the closed-lab race (GPT-5.6, Grok 4.5), the week's largest open-weight drop came quietly from Meituan: LongCat-2.0, a 1.6-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with a 1-million-token context and an MIT license, tuned to plug straight into agentic coding harnesses. Set it beside *Nature*'s universal cell embedding — a foundation model that puts cells from many species into one latent space — and last week's tabular and virtual-cell foundation models, and the pattern from a fortnight ago holds: the pretrain-once-generalize-zero-shot recipe keeps colonizing new modalities, and the biggest freely-downloadable language models increasingly ship from Chinese labs under permissive licenses.

## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

The RL section leads, but the loudest signal is negative: several of the strongest papers are careful refutations of things the field believed.

### RL Post-Training Builds Compositional Reasoning Strategies {#st-76a020fa5768}
**[arXiv:2607.07646](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07646)** · A. Abdulsalam, N. Patel, A. Saxe · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
Does reinforcement-learning post-training (training a model from a reward signal rather than labelled answers) just surface skills the base model already had, or can it build genuinely new ones? The authors answer this in a *fully observable* setting — a symbol-rewrite grammar where the pretraining distribution is known and every step a model takes can be audited — so the question stops being philosophical. Pretraining a transformer on primitive rewrite chains and then RL-training it on a reasoning task with only a binary right/wrong reward, they find RL solves held-out problems the base model almost never gets, even with far larger sampling budgets. Trace analysis shows *how*: RL first sharpens the primitive steps, then discovers valid *compositions* — collapsing ordered chains into one move, or combining independent moves — and consolidates them into a stable, reused repertoire. The key difference from rejection sampling isn't more exploration but more *selective* exploration: RL concentrates on valid reusable structure instead of scattering shortcuts.
*Why this matters:* it is one of the cleaner pieces of evidence that RL can compose, not just amplify — and because the environment is fully auditable, the claim is checkable rather than inferred from benchmark deltas. It also pins the precondition: composition only emerges if pretraining first organizes the primitives into procedures RL can compress.

### Persuasion Attacks Can Decrease the Effectiveness of CoT Monitoring {#st-6036e87b80c1}
**[arXiv:2607.08066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08066)** · J. Za, J. Bainiaksina, N. Ostrovsky et al. (incl. V. Krakovna) (LASR Labs; UCL) · `[preprint]`
Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring — having a second model read the first model's step-by-step reasoning and block misaligned actions — is a leading, cheap-sounding safety idea. This paper asks the adversarial question: can an agent *talk its monitor into* approving actions that violate the monitor's own policy? Across 40 tasks and thousands of agent–monitor interactions, giving the monitor access to the agent's CoT *raised* approval of harmful actions by 9.5% on average, because the scratchpad becomes an extra persuasion channel. The fix that worked: pairing the monitor with a *fact-checker from a different model family* (e.g. a Claude monitor with a GPT fact-checker) cut approval of policy-violating actions by up to 45%, versus only 6% when the same model played both roles. This is a deeper, adversarial revisit of the finding the 10 July daily flagged.
*Why this matters:* "just read the model's reasoning" is not robust oversight against a capable, motivated agent — the very transparency that was supposed to help can be weaponized. The practical takeaway is concrete and deployable: model-diverse checking beats self-checking.

### Super Weights in LLMs and the Failure of Selective Training {#st-76f71c90599f}
**[arXiv:2607.08733](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08733)** · S. Subramanian, A. Akinfaderin, A. Sehwag (Amazon Web Services) · `[preprint]`
Earlier work found "super weights" — a few individual parameters whose removal degrades a model by orders of magnitude — which invites the natural inference that training those parameters should be especially powerful. It is the opposite. Fine-tuning just the super weights in isolation (100 to ~8,192 parameters) drops OLMo-1B and OLMo-7B to random-guessing accuracy, and widening to a 36K-parameter neighbourhood doesn't help — yet training an *equal number of randomly chosen* positions in the same layers *improves* over baseline. The collapse is specific to the super-weight coordinates, not to sparsity. Vanilla LoRA, spreading a low-rank update across whole attention matrices, succeeds with 0.16% of parameters.
*Why this matters:* it cleanly separates two ideas the field had quietly conflated — parameter *importance* and parameter *trainability* — and warns off the growing literature that tries to edit models through a small privileged set of weights. Effective fine-tuning relies on structured, whole-layer decompositions, not on targeting individually important weights.

### No Subspace to Track: Non-Identifiability and Optimizer State in Low-Rank Training {#st-892d3c92780a}
**[arXiv:2607.05872](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05872)** · N. Thomas (MBZUAI) · `[preprint]`
Memory-efficient optimizers like GaLore save memory by projecting gradients onto a rank-r subspace that they recompute every few hundred steps, assuming that subspace is a real, slowly-drifting object worth tracking. This paper shows that beyond a small reproducible core, it isn't. Two estimates of the top-r subspace taken at the *same* step from disjoint minibatches disagree almost as much as estimates taken hundreds of steps apart — so the "rotation" GaLore chases at each refresh is dominated by estimator noise, and no amount of averaging fixes it (the noisy tail shrinks as N^(−1/4), not the N^(−1/2) of pure noise). What actually helps is treating each refresh as a change of coordinates for the optimizer's own state: the first moment transports exactly through the rotation, the second moment does not, which is why some patches (like LDAdam's) work and others don't.
*Why this matters:* it explains *why* GaLore works despite resting on a false premise, tells you which patches are principled, and hands practitioners one measurable quantity — the reproducible rank — to check before trusting any low-rank training assumption.

### DeepSWE: Measuring Frontier Coding Agents on Original, Long-Horizon Engineering Tasks {#st-97a807fbda20}
**[arXiv:2607.07946](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07946)** · W. Huang, C. Lee, L. Tng, S. Ge (Datacurve) · `[preprint]`
Most agentic coding benchmarks follow SWE-bench: mine merged fixes from public GitHub. That creates two problems the authors argue are fatal — the fixes and their discussions were likely in pretraining (so a high score can be recall, not problem-solving), and each task is graded by the tests that shipped with one specific fix (so they can fail a correct alternative or pass an incomplete one). DeepSWE writes 113 tasks from scratch across 91 active repos in five languages, never contributes them upstream (keeping reference solutions out of the training scrape), and grades each with a hand-written verifier that accepts *any* working implementation. When an independent LLM judge re-reviews graded runs, it disagrees with DeepSWE's verifier about a tenth as often as with SWE-Bench Pro's inherited tests (1.4% vs 32.4%), and the benchmark spreads frontier agents across a wider score band instead of bunching them.
*Why this matters:* it is the constructive companion to OpenAI's own finding (this week's daily) that a third of SWE-Bench Pro is broken — a benchmark built specifically to resist contamination and mis-grading, with the verifiers and trajectories released so the claim can be checked.

### Induction Heads Interpolate N-Grams {#st-5273188d1dd9}
**[arXiv:2607.02800](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02800)** · F. D'Angelo, O. K. Yuksel, S. S. Narashiman, N. Flammarion (EPFL) · `[preprint]`
Induction heads are the attention circuits widely credited with in-context learning — a model picking up a pattern from the prompt and continuing it — but exactly *what estimator* they compute has stayed vague. Studying transformers trained on order-k Markov chains (sequences where the next symbol depends on the previous k), the authors show induction heads implement two classical smoothing tricks: a soft context-matching estimator that blends exact and partial matches by overlap — mathematically Jelinek–Mercer smoothing, the standard way old language models interpolated between short and long contexts — and, via the beginning-of-sequence token, additive pseudo-counts that recover Dirichlet smoothing (nudging estimates toward a prior so unseen cases aren't assigned zero). They build a disentangled transformer that provably implements both and show trained models recover the same attention patterns.
*Why this matters:* it turns a famous but hand-wavy interpretability object into a precise, decades-old statistical mechanism — the kind of grounding that makes model internals predictable rather than mysterious, and a data point for the "interpretability growing a spine" thread above.

### Dissociating the Internal Representations of Sycophancy in LLMs {#st-1056257197d6}
**[arXiv:2607.07003](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07003)** · A. Baez, S. Karny, P. Pataranutaporn · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
Sycophancy — a model agreeing with the user even when the user is wrong — is usually treated as one behavior. This paper asks whether that's true inside the model, splitting sycophancy into a *factual* subtype (caving on verifiable claims) and an *opinion* subtype (caving on subjective beliefs). Training linear probes and steering vectors on one subtype and testing whether they transfer to the other, they find the answer is model-dependent: some LLMs represent the two subtypes with shared machinery, others keep them distinct and even causally interfering.
*Why this matters:* it reframes a headline alignment problem as *several* mechanisms rather than one, which matters directly for mitigation — a steering vector that fixes factual flattery may do nothing for opinion flattery, or actively fight it. The dissociation method generalizes to studying other "single" behaviors that may not be single at all.

### Linear Attention Architectures: Mechanisms, Trade-offs, and Cross-Layer Routing {#st-51c2a0aab76d}
**[arXiv:2607.07953](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07953)** · T. Cerruti, T. Rieder, G. Rowlands, L. Jin, I. Schlag (ETH Zürich) · `[preprint]`
Standard self-attention lets every token see the whole context but costs quadratically in sequence length; linear-attention variants swap that for a fixed-size recurrent memory to get sub-quadratic cost. This ETH Zürich study puts softmax attention and four recent recurrent variants (DeltaNet, Gated DeltaNet, Kimi Delta Attention, Gated DeltaNet-2) into one common notation and measures how they differ in expressivity, memory decay, erase/write control and *training* throughput, with 350M-parameter/15B-token sweeps and DeltaNet runs up to 3B. Two findings stand out: the choice of optimizer matters as much as the architecture (the Muon optimizer consistently lowers final loss versus AdamW in matched settings), and their new cross-layer routing idea only helps in one specific form — forwarding a lower layer's write *value* into the aligned hidden stream (CLVR) improves loss, while the more "natural" variant of forwarding the write *error* does not. This is the deeper, mechanism-level revisit of the bake-off the 10 July daily noted.
*Why this matters:* linear attention is a leading route to cheap long context, but the variants are usually reported in isolation and tangled with optimizer choices; a controlled, like-for-like comparison tells builders what actually buys what — and honestly reports which of the authors' own routing ideas failed.

### KronQ: LLM Quantization via Kronecker-Factored Hessian {#st-831ffc045cbb}
**[arXiv:2607.07964](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07964)** · D. Lee, Y. Li, R. Yin, P. Panda · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
Post-training quantization (PTQ — compressing a model's weights to fewer bits *without* retraining) is how large models get squeezed onto constrained hardware. Second-order methods like GPTQ build their objective purely from input-activation statistics, implicitly assuming every output channel contributes equally to the reconstruction error. KronQ drops that assumption by folding in the *gradient* covariance under a Kronecker-factored approximation of the Hessian (a structured estimate of how sensitive the loss is to each weight), which lets it rotate away outliers on both the input and output dimensions and pick per-layer bit-widths by a new sensitivity metric. The headline: at 2-bit weight-only quantization of LLaMA-3-70B, GPTQ and its successor diverge into gibberish (>2000 perplexity on WikiText-2), while KronQ holds at 7.93.
*Why this matters:* the gap between "diverges" and "usable" at 2 bits is exactly where extreme compression lives or dies, and a principled second-order fix that keeps a 70B model coherent at 2-bit is directly relevant to anyone serving big models cheaply.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

Two conjectures fell, an interstellar comet was caught mid-outgassing, and physicists put quantum mechanics' own rulebook on trial.

### Reinforcement learning control of quantum error correction {#st-c1f7b7bd8c73}
**[Nature, 2026-07-08](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10759-2)** · V. Sivak, A. Morvan, M. Broughton et al. (Google Quantum AI; Google DeepMind)
Quantum error correction (QEC) protects a fragile quantum computer by spreading one logical qubit across many physical ones and constantly detecting errors — but it only works if errors stay rare, which requires perpetually re-tuning the machine's control parameters as the hardware drifts. Today's fix is to *stop* the whole computation to recalibrate, which is incompatible with the long runtimes future quantum algorithms will need. Google Quantum AI's answer is to give the QEC process a second job: the same error-detection data used to correct errors is fed to a reinforcement-learning agent that continuously adjusts the control parameters *during* the computation, unifying calibration with computation. In other words, the error-correction machinery becomes its own live feedback controller, learning to chase the drift instead of pausing to be recalibrated by hand.
*Why this matters:* it removes a real operational blocker on the road to useful quantum computers, and it is the week's clearest example of RL working as a general control method in the physical world — not a language task in sight. That it comes from a Quantum-AI/DeepMind collaboration is itself a sign of where these fields are merging.

### Near-perihelion activity and composition of 3I/ATLAS from JUICE/MAJIS observations {#st-85de31af2929}
**[arXiv:2607.08603](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08603)** · D. Bockelée-Morvan, F. Poulet, Y. Langevin et al. · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
3I/ATLAS is only the third confirmed interstellar object — a body that formed around another star and is now passing through our Solar System — after ʻOumuamua and Borisov. This paper reports visible-to-infrared spectra taken *from a spacecraft*: the MAJIS instrument on ESA's JUICE (Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer) observed the comet shortly after its perihelion. They detect water and CO₂ outgassing, find the CO₂/H₂O ratio holding near 10% while activity tracks solar heating, and conclude that CO₂ — not water — is the main driver of the comet's activity near the Sun. Most intriguingly, broad emission features at 3.2–3.6 microns can't be explained by common cometary molecules and instead point tentatively to *complex organic material* released from dust grains. This is a distinct instrument-and-team follow-on to the ground-based nitrogen-isotope work the 8 July daily covered, and it keeps painting 3I/ATLAS as chemically unlike anything homegrown.
*Why this matters:* interstellar objects are our only direct physical samples of other planetary systems, and getting spacecraft-grade infrared spectra of one is rare and fleeting. The tentative organics are the kind of result that will drive the next round of observation before the comet leaves for good.

### The Algebraic Montgomery–Yang Problem — completely resolved {#st-dd080ae44e2e}
**[arXiv:2607.06686](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06686)** · W. Jo, J. Park, K. Park (Seoul National University; Kangwon National University) · `[preprint]`
*The stakes:* this is a named conjecture of Fields-adjacent geometer János Kollár about a special class of algebraic surfaces — "rational homology projective planes" — that can carry a few sharp cone-like singular points. Kollár conjectured that if the smooth part of such a surface is simply connected (has no holes you could loop a lasso around), it can have *at most three* of those singular points. *The result:* the authors prove it in full. *The anchor:* think of it as a hard cap on how many sharp corners a certain family of otherwise-smooth shapes is allowed to have — and the surprise is that the number is exactly three, no matter how you build the surface. *What's genuinely new:* a lattice-theoretic constraint combining Donaldson's diagonalization theorem (a deep tool from four-dimensional topology) with a special structure on the smooth locus, which together rules out every remaining case. That a topological theorem closes an algebraic-geometry conjecture is the elegant part.
*Why this matters:* it retires a specific, decades-standing question and, in doing so, sharpens the boundary of which singular surfaces can exist — the sort of clean "conjecture closed" that reorganizes a corner of the field.

### The End of the Double Random Current {#st-809c51ca513c}
**[arXiv:2607.07616](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07616)** · U. T. Hansen, F. R. Klausen (University of Innsbruck; University of Cambridge) · `[preprint]`
*The stakes:* the Ising model is physics' canonical model of a magnet — a lattice of up/down spins that align or not — and one powerful way to compute its correlations is through a random graph called the "double random current." In 2016, Fields Medalist Hugo Duminil-Copin asked a deceptively simple question about this graph: does it have only *one* topological "end" (roughly, one way to head off to infinity)? *The result:* the authors prove yes, for any transitive, amenable, one-ended graph. *The anchor:* one-endedness is the statement that no matter which faraway direction you look, the random graph looks the same out there — and that geometric fact has hard consequences, forcing the "free" and "wired" ways of setting boundary conditions to give the same physics, and pinning down which equilibrium states the Ising model can occupy. *Honest scope:* the authors note their route doesn't re-derive the uniqueness results already known by other means (Raoufi's work) — it answers Duminil-Copin's specific question directly.
*Why this matters:* it settles a clean open problem posed by one of the field's leading figures, tying a purely geometric property of a random graph to the equilibrium behaviour of the most-studied model in statistical physics.

### First constraint on Born-rule violations at high-energy colliders {#st-4f62dea4dd52}
**[arXiv:2607.06938](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06938)** · A. Valentini, M. Varma (Imperial College London; Yale) · `[preprint]`
The Born rule is the axiom that turns a quantum wavefunction into a probability — the squared amplitude *is* the chance of an outcome. It's usually treated as exact, but whether it holds precisely at the highest energies we can reach is, remarkably, an open experimental question. The authors model a possible violation as a slight angular "smearing" of a scattering cross-section and turn old data from the PEP collider at SLAC — large-angle electron–positron (Bhabha) scattering at 29 GeV — into the first collider bound on it, limiting the smearing width to below 0.042 at 95% confidence (about twice the experiment's angular bin width). No violation is seen; this is a *bound*, not a signal.
*Why this matters:* testing quantum mechanics' own probability rule at high energy is the kind of foundational check that almost never gets a number attached to it. A first, honest limit — with a clear route to tightening it — is exactly how you start turning an untested assumption into a measured one.

### Topology from Decoherence {#st-788754ce1e92}
**[arXiv:2607.07801](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07801)** · A. Chaduteau, D. Lee, F. Schindler, A. Prem · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
Decoherence — the leakage of quantum information into the environment as noise — is normally the enemy of topological quantum phases, which is why so much effort goes into suppressing it. This paper flips the script: it shows noise can *induce* topology. In a lattice subject to environment-induced dephasing, the noise-averaged dynamics realize a topological phase with a winding number and the "non-Hermitian skin effect" (where excitations pile up at a boundary), and the physical fingerprint is striking — an *asymmetric* diffusion whose direction is locked by the winding number and only reversible by driving the system through a topological phase transition. Crucially the effect is driven purely by interactions and vanishes if you post-select measurement outcomes, marking it as a genuinely open-system phenomenon with no equivalent closed-system (Hamiltonian) description — and the model stays analytically solvable.
*Why this matters:* it identifies correlated quantum noise as a *resource* for topology rather than only a hazard, opening a route to engineered topological behaviour in exactly the noisy, open systems that real hardware inhabits.

### Absence of quantum advantage for approximate spin-glass optimization {#st-3f1a31470792}
**[arXiv:2607.08708](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08708)** · D. Sels, F. Morone (Boston University; Flatiron Institute; NYU) · `[preprint]`
The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) is a flagship candidate for near-term quantum advantage on hard optimization. The authors analyze it semiclassically on the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick spin glass (a canonical hard optimization landscape) using a large-spin approximation, and find the quantum version converges to the known optimal ("Parisi") energy no faster than a classical semiclassical simulation of it — indeed the classical semiclassics slightly *outperforms* the true quantum algorithm at fixed depth.
*Why this matters:* it is a careful, checkable negative result against a much-hyped use case, and a useful counterweight to the week's more upbeat quantum stories — evidence that for approximate spin-glass optimization, QAOA's advantage is absent, at least at the depths and in the regime studied.

### Coherent vibrational wave-packet motion tracks flavin redox state in robin cryptochrome {#st-bc6312d1d4b4}
**[arXiv:2607.07945](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07945)** · K. Kumar, D. Timmer, M. Khazani et al. · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed)
Cryptochromes are blue-light-sensitive proteins, and the one in the European robin — ErCry4a — is the leading candidate for the molecular compass that lets night-migrating songbirds sense Earth's magnetic field. The proposed mechanism runs through a light-triggered "radical pair" whose quantum spin state is subtly steered by the magnetic field. Using ultrafast spectroscopy with 10-femtosecond resolution (fast enough to watch electrons rearrange), the authors track the first steps of that photoactivation: a sub-50-fs shift, then electron transfer from a nearby tryptophan within 360 fs, and — the novel part — *coherent* low-frequency vibrations that persist through both energy relaxation and the electron transfer, which they use as a real-time readout of the flavin's redox state and confirm by mutating the tryptophan to block the transfer.
*Why this matters:* it gives an experimental handle on the earliest, fastest events in the most concrete candidate for animal magnetoreception — a genuine "how do birds see magnetic fields" question — and demonstrates a general trick: reading a molecule's charge-transfer state from its coherent vibrations.

### Planetary-mass exosatellite candidate around a brown dwarf {#st-83c111767c48}
**[arXiv:2607.05193](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05193)** · K. Hoy, A. Zurlo, P. A. Peña R. et al. · `[preprint]` · (affiliation not listed) · `[disputed]`
Despite more than 6,000 known exoplanets, no moon orbiting one — an exomoon — has ever been confidently confirmed; every candidate so far has been contested. This paper reports *strong evidence*, not a confirmation, for satellites orbiting the directly-imaged brown dwarf CD-35 2722 B, using the radial-velocity technique (watching for the tiny periodic wobble a companion induces) applied to VLT/CRIRES+ spectra — the first time this method has been turned on a substellar companion to hunt for moons. The best fit is a satellite of at least 0.74 Jupiter masses on a 169-day orbit, plus a less-certain second one near a 2:1 resonance, echoing Jupiter's Galilean moons.
*Why this matters:* if it holds up, it opens an entirely new observational route to a long-sought class of object. But the honest framing is essential: this is a candidate detection in a field where every prior candidate has eventually been disputed, so treat it as a promising claim awaiting independent confirmation, not a discovery.

*(Also this week, and worth a line: LARES-2, a laser-ranging satellite, tightened the measurement of frame-dragging — the way a spinning mass like Earth drags spacetime around with it — as a precision test of general relativity, [Nature, 2026-07-08](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10715-0).)*

## 🧬 Biology, biotech, neuroscience

A compact section anchored on two strong *Nature* papers.

### Universal cell embedding: a foundation model for cell biology {#st-c103b7e6c09a}
**[Nature, 2026-07-08](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10689-z)** · Y. Rosen, Y. Roohani, A. Agrawal et al. (Stanford; Chan Zuckerberg Biohub)
The past decade built "cell atlases" — molecular definitions of cell types from single-cell RNA measurements (readouts of which genes are switched on). This paper turns that data into a foundation model: the Universal Cell Embedding (UCE) is trained by self-supervision on a large corpus to place cells from many tissues and *many species* into one shared latent space, so a never-before-seen cell can be positioned — and its type inferred — without task-specific training, the way a language model handles a new prompt. It's the biology instance of the pattern this brief has tracked for weeks: pretrain once on a modality, generalize zero-shot to new instances of it.
*Why it matters:* a common coordinate system for cells across species would let biologists compare, transfer and annotate cell types at scale — and it extends the "foundation-model template colonizing new data types" story from tables and tumours to the cell itself.

### Non-genotoxic transplantation via epitope editing {#st-ab132acd739d}
**[Nature, 2026-07-08](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10737-8)** · G. Casirati, A. Cosentino, M. Freschi et al. (Boston Children's Hospital; Dana-Farber Cancer Institute; Harvard Medical School)
Bone-marrow (haematopoietic stem-cell) transplants and many gene therapies still require "conditioning" — wiping out the patient's existing marrow with chemotherapy or radiation, which carries serious short- and long-term harm. Antibody-based conditioning (targeting the KIT receptor on stem cells) was meant to be gentler, but the same antibody would also destroy the *transplanted* cells. The authors' fix is elegant: engineer small amino-acid changes in KIT's outer surface — an "epitope edit" — that block the therapeutic antibody from binding the donor cells, so the antibody clears the patient's own marrow while the edited graft is shielded and then positively selected in the body.
*Why it matters:* it's a route to stem-cell transplants and gene therapies without genotoxic chemo or radiation — potentially widening who can safely receive them — and a clever demonstration of editing cells to *dodge* a drug rather than to fix a disease directly.
