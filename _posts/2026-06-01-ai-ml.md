## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

The day's two real stories both sit here, and both are governance, not money.

**Florida became the first U.S. state to sue OpenAI — and named Sam Altman
personally.** Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier filed an 83-page civil
complaint in Florida state (circuit) court on Monday, June 1, alleging OpenAI
"knowingly released and aggressively marketed" ChatGPT while concealing serious
safety risks — a consumer-protection / deceptive-trade-practices theory (the suit
frames it as "profit over public safety" rather than copyright, the usual AI-suit
hook). The complaint alleges ChatGPT contributed to self-harm, suicide, violence,
and addiction, "particularly among children and teenagers," and references two
shootings whose alleged perpetrators queried ChatGPT while planning. Critically,
the suit seeks to hold **Altman personally liable** for harm to Florida residents,
citing "reckless and willful conduct" and "utter disregard for the risk to human
life" — an unusual move that pierces the usual corporate shield and is the reason
this filing is more than another docket entry. Uthmeier said he expects other
states to follow, and that his office's separate **April criminal investigation**
into the 2025 Florida State University shooting (where prosecutors reviewed the
gunman's chat logs) continues. _Why it matters:_ state AGs wield consumer-protection
statutes that don't require proving copyright infringement or a federal hook, and
naming the CEO personally raises the stakes for every frontier lab's
safety-disclosure posture — this is a template other states can copy cheaply.

**The EU stood up the two expert bodies that will actually enforce the AI Act on
frontier models.** The European Commission announced on June 1 the appointment of a
**Scientific Panel of 60 independent experts** and a **174-member Advisory Forum**,
both serving two-year terms, to advise the Commission's AI Office and national
authorities. The Scientific Panel (established under **Article 68** of the AI Act)
is the technical muscle: its mandate is general-purpose AI models — "GPAI" means the
big foundation models like GPT/Gemini/Claude that aren't built for one task —
covering systemic-risk assessment, model classification (deciding which models
cross the compute/capability thresholds that trigger the strictest obligations),
**evaluation methodologies** (how you actually test a frontier model for dangerous
capabilities), and cross-border market surveillance. The Advisory Forum is the
broader stakeholder body (civil society, academia, industry). _Why it matters:_ the
AI Act's GPAI rules have been law since August 2025 and the Act becomes fully
applicable on **August 2, 2026** — but rules need people to apply them. Until now
the AI Office had the legal authority to classify a model as carrying "systemic
risk" without the independent technical bench to defensibly do it; this panel is
that bench. Expect its first real test to be whether it labels specific named
frontier models as systemic-risk, which flips on extra evaluation, reporting, and
incident-disclosure duties. [[European Commission, 2026-06-01](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/ai-act-enforcement-gets-independent-expert-support)] `[via snippet]` · [[Brussels Times, 2026-06-01](https://www.brusselstimes.com/eu-affairs/2165748/eu-launches-expert-bodies-to-guide-ai-act-enforcement/)] `[via snippet]`

## 🍎 Apple Silicon / on-device

The local-inference toolchain kept its daily cadence, and today's run has one item
that's more than routine maintenance.

**`llama.cpp` shipped same-day support for LG's EXAONE 4.5 vision-language model
(builds b9452–b9460, 2026-06-01).** `llama.cpp` is the C/C++ engine most people use
to run LLMs locally on a Mac, PC, or phone without the cloud; it ships dozens of
tagged builds a week. What's genuinely new since this brief last touched it (b9442,
2026-05-31):

- **EXAONE 4.5 multimodal support** (b9453) — the runtime now handles LG's 33B open
  VLM, including its vision projection layer, so the model's image+text reasoning
  runs locally (an official GGUF port already exists on the Hub). _Why it matters:_
  same-day local support for a fresh-ish open VLM is the fast path to running it
  on-device without cloud dependence.
- **Quantized KV-cache under tensor parallelism** (b9455). The KV-cache is the
  running memory of past tokens that lets a model attend to long context;
  "quantizing" it (storing it in fewer bits) cuts the VRAM that cache eats.
  Tensor parallelism splits one model across multiple GPUs. Combining the two means
  long-context inference on multi-GPU rigs now fits in less memory — relevant to
  anyone running large open weights across more than one card.
- **`llama_context` max-output limiting / `n_outputs_per_seq`** (b9460) — lets you
  cap how many output sequences a context reserves VRAM for, reclaiming memory that
  was previously over-allocated.
- **Metal GLU kernels now support f16 and f32** (b9459) — Metal is Apple's GPU API;
  GLU ("gated linear unit") is a common building block in modern transformer MLPs.
  Supporting both 16- and 32-bit precision on Apple Silicon improves memory
  bandwidth without forcing a precision choice. Directly relevant to M-series Macs.
- **Vulkan integer-op gains** (b9452) — reported 57% faster on Q3_K and 78% on Q6_K
  quantization formats on Intel BMG GPUs, via block-loading. (Vulkan = the
  cross-vendor GPU backend; Q3_K/Q6_K are 3-bit/6-bit quantization schemes.)

_Why it matters overall:_ none of these is headline-grade alone, but the EXAONE 4.5
support is a concrete new capability, and the rest is the steady maintenance that
keeps recent open weights runnable on consumer and Apple hardware.
