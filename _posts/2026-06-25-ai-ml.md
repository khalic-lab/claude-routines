## 🏢 Lab blogs & official releases

- **OpenAI and Broadcom unveil "Jalapeño," OpenAI's first custom inference chip — a purpose-built ASIC, not a repurposed GPU.** OpenAI announced its first "Intelligence Processor," an accelerator co-designed with Broadcom (NASDAQ: AVGO) and aimed specifically at *inference* — running already-trained models to serve answers, as opposed to *training* them. The pitch is architectural: an ASIC (application-specific integrated circuit — a chip hard-wired for one job rather than general-purpose like a GPU) tuned to the actual bottlenecks of large-language-model serving — chiefly **data movement** (shuttling the model's weights and the running context in and out of memory, which dominates inference cost more than raw arithmetic does) and memory-vs-compute balance. OpenAI says it designed the chip in **nine months**, using its own models to accelerate parts of the design work, and that early internal testing shows **performance-per-watt "substantially better" than current state-of-the-art hardware** — but **no benchmark numbers, memory configuration, or clock figures were disclosed**, so treat the performance claim as a directional vendor statement, not a measured result. Deployment is slated to begin "by the end of 2026" at **gigawatt scale** with data-center partners, across multiple chip generations. Why it matters: this is OpenAI extending its stack from products and models down to silicon, reducing its dependence on Nvidia for the serving side — the same vertical-integration playbook Google (TPU) and Amazon (Trainium/Inferentia) already run. `[vendor PR]` — [OpenAI, 2026-06-24](https://openai.com/index/openai-broadcom-jalapeno-inference-chip/); secondary: [Tom's Hardware, 2026-06-24](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/broadcom-and-openai-unveil-custom-built-jalapeno-inference-processor-openais-first-chip-is-a-massive-reticle-sized-asic-built-in-an-ultra-fast-nine-month-development-cycle), [TechCrunch, 2026-06-24](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/24/openai-unveils-its-first-custom-chip-built-by-broadcom/)
- **OpenAI publishes a data drop on Codex agent usage — useful numbers, but it's a measure of OpenAI's own product, not "work" in general.** A new OpenAI write-up reports how usage of Codex (its coding/agent tool) shifted over the past year from short chatbot-style exchanges toward long, delegated tasks. The headline figures: by May 2026, **80.6%** of sampled individual users had made at least one Codex request the company estimates would take a human **more than 30 minutes**, and **25.6%** made at least one estimated to exceed **eight hours**; the share crossing the one-hour mark rose to **70.2%** between December 2025 and May 2026. Internally, OpenAI says Codex now accounts for **99.8% of weekly output tokens** generated inside the company, and that its heaviest daily users (99th percentile) routinely orchestrate **60+ hours of agent runtime per day** spread across parallel agents — and that adoption grew fastest among *non-developers* (e.g. over a quarter of Codex work done by business-function staff was coding-related). Read this as honest internal telemetry on one tool, not an economy-wide finding: every number is self-reported, self-selected to OpenAI's own users and staff, and the "human-time-equivalent" estimates are OpenAI's own. Still, the directional signal — agents being handed longer-horizon, multi-step jobs — is the real trend the rest of the field is also chasing. `[vendor PR]` `[single-source]` — [OpenAI research, 2026-06-25](https://openai.com/index/how-agents-are-transforming-work/)

## 🚀 New models, datasets, & open weights

- **Alibaba's Qwen team open-weights "Qwen-AgentWorld" — language models that *simulate* environments for agents, under Apache 2.0.** Two models shipped: **Qwen-AgentWorld-35B-A3B** and a larger **397B-A17B** (the `A` figure is the *active* parameter count per token — these are Mixture-of-Experts models, MoE, where only a small slice of the total weights fires for any given token, so a 397B model runs at roughly the cost of a 17B one). The idea is a **language world model**: instead of acting in an environment, the model *predicts what the environment would do next* given an action — a learned simulator of state transitions, built from 10M+ real interaction trajectories across 7 task domains. Why a developer should care: a good world model lets you train agents against a cheap, controllable *simulated* environment (thousands of synthetic worlds) rather than the slow, costly real thing — the team reports this yields larger gains for agentic reinforcement learning than training in the real environment alone, and that world-model pretraining also works as a "warm-up" that lifts downstream agent performance. Open weights + an accompanying benchmark (AgentWorldBench, below) make this one of the more substantive open releases of the week. `[preprint]` — [arXiv:2606.24597, submitted 2026-06-23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597); weights: [huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen-AgentWorld-35B-A3B](https://hf.co/Qwen/Qwen-AgentWorld-35B-A3B)

## 📊 Benchmarks & evaluations

- **Qwen-AgentWorld ships its own benchmark — read the "outperforms frontier models" claim with care.** Alongside the models, the team released **AgentWorldBench**, built from the real-world interactions of 5 frontier models across 9 established agent benchmarks, to score how *faithfully* a world model predicts the next environment state. Qwen reports its models "significantly outperform existing frontier models" on it. The methodological caveat is the standard one for vendor-released benchmarks: the team that built the model also defined the benchmark and chose what "fidelity" means, so the result is a strong internal signal but not yet independently replicated — the meaningful test is whether a third party (or a neutral leaderboard) reproduces the gap on held-out environments. As always, "beats frontier models on X" tells you little until you know what X measures; here X is *simulation fidelity*, not end-task success. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` — [arXiv:2606.24597, 2026-06-23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597)

## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

- **Qualcomm to acquire Modular in a ~$3.9B all-stock deal — buying the software layer that frees AI from any one chip vendor.** Qualcomm (NASDAQ: QCOM) agreed to acquire **Modular**, the startup founded in 2022 by **Chris Lattner** (creator of the LLVM compiler infrastructure and the Swift language) and Tim Davis. Modular builds the **Mojo** programming language and the **MAX** inference platform — software that lets a single AI model run efficiently across CPUs, GPUs, NPUs and custom ASICs from different vendors *without rewriting it for each one* (a "silicon-agnostic compute layer"). The deal is **all-stock, valued at roughly $3.9B** per CNBC (Qualcomm's own release omits the figure), bringing Modular's ~150-person team — both founders included — into Qualcomm; it is expected to close in the **second half of 2026**, subject to regulatory approval. Strategic read: Qualcomm, historically a mobile/edge silicon company, is buying credibility in the data-center software stack — CEO Cristiano Amon framed it as betting on "developer-friendly, horizontal platforms" as agentic AI pushes the industry toward "disaggregated, multi-vendor architectures." In plain terms, it's a bet that *portability* (run anywhere, not locked to Nvidia's CUDA) becomes a moat. — [Qualcomm investor release, 2026-06-24](https://investor.qualcomm.com/news-events/press-releases/news-details/2026/Qualcomm-to-Acquire-Modular/default.aspx); deal value: [CNBC, 2026-06-24](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/24/qualcomm-ai-chip-modular-software.html)
- **Abu Dhabi's MGX raises ~$50B to fund AI deals — one of the largest pools of capital ever aimed purely at AI.** State-backed investment firm **MGX** (which already holds stakes in OpenAI, Anthropic and xAI) raised close to **$50 billion** from regional sovereign wealth funds plus global pension funds and institutional investors, aiming to reach **$100B+ in assets** and deploy up to **$10B a year** into AI infrastructure and companies. Why it matters for the broader picture: the AI buildout is increasingly financed by a handful of mega-pools of sovereign and institutional money — the same Q1-2026 data that showed global startup funding hitting a record $300B also showed that stripping out the five largest deals would cut total value by ~73%. Capital is abundant at the very top of the stack and scarce everywhere below it; a $50B AI-only vehicle deepens that concentration. — [Bloomberg, 2026-06-23](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/abu-dhabi-s-mgx-raises-about-50-billion-to-accelerate-ai-deals); [Semafor, 2026-06-23](https://www.semafor.com/article/06/23/2026/abu-dhabis-mgx-secures-50b-for-ai-investments)
