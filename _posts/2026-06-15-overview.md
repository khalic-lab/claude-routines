## 🔬 Science

- **Iron Age Scots whittled a woman's bones into tools after removing her brain.** Re-analysis of two ~2,000-year-old skeletons from Loch Borralie, north-west Scotland, finds an adult woman's four long bones (femur, ulna, both humeri) were snapped and shaved to sharp points after death, with cut-marks inside the skull suggesting the brain was removed — likely a funerary rite, not violence. Why it matters: post-mortem reshaping of a relative's bones into objects is a striking, rarely-documented Iron Age mortuary practice, hinting that the line between "the dead" and "everyday tools" was culturally porous. [Nature, 15 Jun 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01833-w); primary study in *Antiquity* via [University of York](https://www.york.ac.uk/news-and-events/news/2026/research/iron-age-brain-removal-bone-tools/) `[via snippet]`.
- **Big cohort study: switching from cigarettes to vapes carries higher lung-cancer risk than quitting outright.** A large longitudinal study reports that smokers who took up e-cigarettes had higher lung-cancer risk than those who stopped nicotine entirely — i.e. vaping is a harm-reduction step down from smoking, but not equivalent to cessation. Why it matters: it sharpens a contested public-health message (vapes less harmful than cigarettes, yet not "safe"), relevant as regulators weigh e-cigarettes as quit aids. `[single-source]` [Nature, 14 Jun 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01834-9).
- **First complete connectome — wiring diagram of every neuron — of a fruit fly's brain *and* nerve cord.** A Harvard/Princeton-led team mapped all ~160,000 neurons of the adult *Drosophila* central nervous system, extending for the first time in any adult animal through the ventral nerve cord (the insect equivalent of the spinal cord). The surprise: many behaviours like walking are run by local circuits in the cord, not dictated top-down by the brain. Why it matters: a body-wide neural map (now open-source) lets researchers trace how brain and body co-produce movement — a reference scaffold for systems neuroscience. [phys.org, 10 Jun 2026](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-publish-connectome-fruit-fly-brain.html) (published in *Nature*) `[via snippet]`.
- **Theory: a collapsing massive star might dodge the black-hole singularity and become a "gravastar."** Goethe University Frankfurt physicists propose (in *Physical Review D*) that gravitational collapse could ignite a tiny dark-energy-filled "mini universe" inside the dying star whose outward push halts the collapse — yielding a *gravastar*, an exotic object with neither a singularity (the infinite-density point general relativity predicts at a black hole's centre) nor an event horizon (the no-return boundary). Why it matters: singularities are where known physics breaks down; singularity-free alternatives like gravastars are a live attempt to describe collapsed stars without that breakdown — though they remain theoretical and unobserved. `[preprint]` `[single-source]` [ANI / Physical Review D, 14 Jun 2026](https://aninews.in/news/tech/science/a-dying-star-can-create-a-new-universe-instead-of-a-black-hole-study20260614231043/) `[via snippet]`.
- **Millipedes likely walked on land ~460 million years ago — beating vertebrates ashore by 80+ million years.** Combining genomes of 82 living species with 29 fossils, a *Current Biology* study (Virginia Tech) dates the millipede common ancestor ~35 million years before the oldest known millipede fossil, placing arthropod land-living well ahead of the first four-limbed vertebrates. Why it matters: it pushes back a key milestone in the greening/colonisation of land and shows how molecular clocks can fill gaps the fossil record leaves blank. [phys.org, 13 Jun 2026](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-evolutionary-history-millipede-dating-million.html) `[via snippet]`.

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

First cs.LG / cs.CL submissions announced 04:00 UTC today (2026-06-15). All `[preprint]`.

- **arXiv:2606.13705 (15 Jun)** — *Can Editing 1 Neuron Fix Repetition Loops in LLMs?* Traces a reproducible Gemma 4 failure (collapsing into verbatim loops on long enumerations, ~95% of the time) to a handful of MLP neurons — or, in the Mixture-of-Experts model (a network that routes each token to a few specialist sub-networks), a few routed experts — and removes the loop with a static weight edit as small as flipping one neuron's sign, without hurting benchmark scores. Why interesting: a concrete demonstration that a specific generation pathology can be localized to a few parameters and surgically deleted — though the authors note it can't supply a fact the model never learned. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13705)
- **arXiv:2606.13753 (15 Jun)** — *The Weight Norm Sets the Grokking Timescale: A Causal Delay Law.* Grokking is the puzzling delayed onset of generalization — a network keeps memorizing the training data, then "gets it" long after. By clamping the weight norm during training (intervening, not just observing) the authors show it *causally* controls the delay, which follows a clean exponential law across four settings (R²=0.996). Why interesting: turns a much-debated correlation into a controlled causal knob, and a final LayerNorm makes the dependence vanish — a clean handle on a confusing phenomenon. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13753)
- **arXiv:2606.13740 (15 Jun)** — *Efficient On-Device Diffusion LLM Inference with Mobile NPU.* "llada.cpp," billed as the first framework tuned for running diffusion LLMs (which generate text by denoising many tokens in parallel rather than one at a time) on a phone's NPU (neural processing unit — the dedicated matrix-math chip). Reports 17×–42× lower latency than a CPU baseline with quality preserved. Why interesting: pushes parallel-decoding LLMs toward practical on-device use, where latency and battery, not raw accuracy, decide what ships. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13740)
- **arXiv:2606.13767 (15 Jun)** — *Beyond LoRA: Is Sparsity-Induced Adaptation Better?* LoRA (low-rank adaptation — fine-tuning a big model by training a small added factor instead of all weights) gets a sparsity twist: training a single low-rank factor plus a structured-sparse column subspace, with information-theoretic generalization bounds. Across 11 methods, 10 models and 14 datasets it stays competitive while cutting training time up to 10% and peak GPU memory up to 15%. Why interesting: cheaper parameter-efficient fine-tuning with theory attached, not just an empirical trick. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13767)
- **arXiv:2606.13754 (15 Jun)** — *D2H-AD: Hyperdimensional Computing for Anomaly Detection.* Anomaly detection (flagging the rare "this doesn't belong" cases — fraud, faults, intrusions) via hyperdimensional computing — a brain-inspired scheme that encodes data as very long, distributed binary vectors. Reports up to +5.4% ROC-AUC over scoring in the raw feature space and beats five standard baselines, with a small memory footprint aimed at TinyML/edge devices. Why interesting: a lightweight, interpretable detector that runs on binary operations — attractive where GPUs and labels are scarce. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13754)
- **arXiv:2606.13685 (15 Jun)** — *The Coin Flip Judge? Reliability and Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation.* "LLM-as-a-judge" — using one model to grade another's outputs, now a default shortcut in ML evaluation — is stress-tested for reliability and bias. Why interesting: much of the field's reported progress rests on these automated judges; quantifying when they behave like a coin flip is a prerequisite for trusting leaderboard claims. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13685)
