## 🔬 Science

- **Two ancient whole-genome duplications gave vertebrates the genetic raw material to build complex brains.** Analysing single-cell brain transcriptomes (gene-activity readouts from individual cells) across human, mouse, lizard, lamprey and amphioxus, an Oxford-led team finds that *ohnologues* — duplicate gene copies left over from two whole-genome duplications in the vertebrate ancestor ~520 million years ago — were repeatedly repurposed to define new neuron types. The intuition: when a gene gets copied wholesale, the spare copy is free to specialise without breaking the original's job, and the team shows this same trick kept generating new cell types for hundreds of millions of years (e.g. in the cerebellum's grey matter), not just in early vertebrates. Why it matters: it offers a concrete genetic mechanism for *how* brains became more elaborate, rather than just *that* they did. ([Nature, 2026-06-10](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10629-x); plain-language writeup [Univ. of Oxford, 2026-06-09](https://www.ox.ac.uk/news/2026-06-09-ancient-genome-duplications-laid-the-foundations-of-complex-brains) [via snippet])

- **Does Titan have a hidden ocean? A reanalysis of Cassini data reignites the fight.** `[disputed]` Saturn's moon Titan was long thought to hide a global liquid-water ocean under its icy crust — a prime spot to hunt for life. The contested measurement is the *tidal Love number k₂*, essentially how much the moon flexes under Saturn's gravity: a big flex implies a sloshing ocean, a small flex implies a more rigid interior. The new Cassini reanalysis (Goossens et al.) reports strong tidal *dissipation* — roughly 3–4 terawatts of energy bleeding away, a low tidal quality factor Q≈5 — which its authors read as **inconsistent** with a simple subsurface ocean. A companion comment (Durante et al.) disputes the result on technical grounds — tidal model, data weighting, media calibration — and the original team stands by its numbers. Both sides published side by side, so treat "Titan has no ocean" as an open argument, not a settled finding. ([Nature Astronomy measurement, 2026-06-11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02895-6); [Reply/comment, 2026-06-11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02896-5); related [Nature: "Titan's strong tidal dissipation precludes a subsurface ocean", 2026](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-09818-x) [via snippet])

- **Perturb-DBiT runs CRISPR screens that keep a tissue's spatial map intact — reading total RNA, not just genes.** `[preprint→published]` A CRISPR screen knocks out many genes at once and asks which ones change a cell's behaviour, but the usual method dissolves the tissue into a cell soup, losing all positional information. Perturb-DBiT co-sequences, on the *same* tissue slice, both the spatial whole-transcriptome (total RNA — including non-coding RNAs like lncRNAs, microRNAs and tRNAs, not just protein-coding messages) and the guide RNAs that mark which gene was edited where. In a metastatic-cancer model the authors applied 80,000+ guides and tied specific perturbations to tumour migration and immune infiltration *in situ*. Why it matters: it lets you ask "which gene edit changed this exact spot in the tumour, and how?" — keeping the architecture that bulk screens throw away. ([Nature Biotechnology research article, 2026-06-11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03127-y); [News & Views, 2026-06-11](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03126-z))

- **High-fidelity entanglement plus coherent multi-qubit "mapping" demonstrated in a neutral-atom array.** `[single-source]` Neutral-atom arrays — individual atoms held in optical tweezers — are a leading platform for scaling up quantum computers because the atoms are identical and can be shuffled around. This experiment reports high-fidelity entanglement (linking qubits' states so tightly that measuring one instantly constrains the other) together with *coherent* mapping of quantum information across multiple qubits — i.e. moving that information around the array without scrambling it, a prerequisite for the qubit-shuttling tricks these machines rely on to run error-corrected circuits. ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-12](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03258-8))
