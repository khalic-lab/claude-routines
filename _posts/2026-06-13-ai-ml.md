## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

- **A Munich court rules Google is directly liable for what its AI Overviews say — the EU's
  first real test of who owns an AI answer.** The **Regional Court of Munich**
  (*Landgericht München I*, case **26 O 869/26**) issued a temporary injunction barring Google
  from repeating false claims its **AI Overviews** (the AI-generated summary box atop Google
  search results) had made about two Munich publishers — the feature had tied them to scams and
  "subscription traps," mixing in facts about genuinely shady firms and inventing links that
  appeared in none of the cited sources. The legal hinge: the long-standing shield that treats a
  search engine as a neutral *conduit* for third-party content **does not apply**, because an AI
  Overview "evaluates, combines, rewrites and structures" information into a **new statement in
  Google's own words** — making Google a *direct* infringer (Störer/direct publisher), not a mere
  host. The court also rejected Google's defence that users could click through to verify, holding
  that the ability to fact-check a claim doesn't excuse making it. _Why it matters:_ this is the
  cleanest articulation yet of a principle every generative-search and chatbot vendor has been
  dreading — that **synthesizing an answer is authoring it**, with the defamation and liability
  exposure that authorship carries. It's a German interim injunction, not an EU-wide final
  judgment, but it points directly at how European courts may treat AI-generated output going
  forward. Triangulated. `[disputed]` (Google can appeal; characterizations are from secondary
  coverage of the order)
