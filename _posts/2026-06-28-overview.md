## 🔬 Science

- **A "radio-quiet" neutron star turns out to be a faint radio pulsar — and that may explain a long-standing census gap.** Central compact objects (CCOs — young neutron stars sitting at the hearts of supernova remnants) are bright in X-rays but had evaded radio telescopes for decades, leading astronomers to assume they are intrinsically radio-silent with unusually weak magnetic fields. A new study detects radio pulses from the textbook CCO 1E 1207.4–5209 at exactly its 0.4-second X-ray spin period, showing it *is* a (very faint) radio pulsar. Why it matters: the team argues the faintness is real, not just bad luck of beam geometry, which would explain why so many supernova remnants seem to lack a pulsar — and warns that once such a remnant fades, the object would be mistaken for an ordinary billion-year-old pulsar. ([Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02899-2))

- **An acoustic version of the Sagnac effect measures the rotation of a fermionic superfluid.** The Sagnac effect — how a rotating ring shifts two counter-propagating waves out of phase — is the principle behind laser gyroscopes; here a group builds a *sonic* analogue, sending sound waves (phonons) around a cloud of paired fermionic atoms to read out its angular momentum. The cloud is tuned across the BEC–BCS crossover (the continuous interpolation between tightly bound molecule-like pairs and the loosely correlated Cooper pairs of conventional superconductivity), and the interferometer is sensitive to the *internal* structure of those pairs. Why it matters: it's a clean new probe of how quantum-correlated pairs carry rotation, the same physics that underlies superconductivity and neutron-star interiors. ([Nature Physics, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03349-6))

- **A blood test plus machine learning predicts who will respond to cancer immunotherapy.** Immune-checkpoint inhibitors (drugs that release the brakes on T cells) only help a minority of patients, and there's no good way to know in advance who. Researchers profiled 4,336 plasma samples from 1,714 patients across five tumour types and 16 cohorts in Europe and North America, then trained a multimodal model on 154 blood metabolites plus clinical variables. Five metabolites together with age, BMI and kidney function predicted 12-month progression-free survival, with an AUC of 0.88 in training and 0.73 in validation (AUC 1.0 is perfect, 0.5 is a coin flip; 0.73 on held-out patients is useful-but-not-decisive). Why it matters: a cheap, non-invasive readout could help triage who gets these expensive, sometimes toxic drugs — though the modest validation score means it isn't a clinical decision tool yet. ([Nature Medicine, 2026-06-25](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04481-9))

- **IBM unveils a "nanostack" chip with ~100 billion transistors on a fingernail-sized die.** The prototype stacks transistors vertically in two layers (rather than only shrinking them sideways), which IBM frames as a sub-1nm-class — it brands it 0.7nm — node, roughly doubling the density of its 2021 state of the art. IBM claims up to ~50% more performance and ~70% better energy efficiency than its 2nm chips, and a path to production around 2031. Why it matters: lateral scaling (the engine of Moore's Law — transistor counts doubling every ~2 years) has been stalling, and going vertical is the main bet for keeping it alive for another decade. Caveat: this is an IBM R&D announcement, not yet a manufacturable product, and the headline numbers are the vendor's own. `[vendor PR]` ([Science, 2026-06-26](https://www.science.org/content/article/engineers-cram-100-billion-transistors-microchip) [via snippet]; [MIT Technology Review, 2026-06-25](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/06/25/1139696/ibm-unveils-sub1nm-chip/) [via snippet])

- **Mathematicians log the first exponential improvement to an 80-year-old Erdős bound.** In 1947 Paul Erdős introduced the "probabilistic method" — proving an object exists by showing a random construction produces it with nonzero probability — and used it to lower-bound Ramsey numbers (how large a network must be before any red/blue colouring of its connections is forced to contain a single-colour clique of a given size). For decades nobody beat the *base* of Erdős's exponential bound. The new preprint proves, for the off-diagonal regime r(ℓ, Cℓ) where one forbidden clique is a fixed constant C>1 times the size of the other, a bound of the form (p_C^{-1/2} + ε)^ℓ — i.e. it nudges the exponential rate strictly upward, the first such exponential improvement over Erdős. Why it matters: the probabilistic method is everywhere (primality testing, circuit design, de-biasing data), yet its original target had barely moved in 80 years; ETH Zürich's Benny Sudakov is among those whose follow-up work builds on the new random-graph model. `[preprint]` ([arXiv 2507.12926, submitted 2025-07-17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.12926); plain-language account: [Quanta, 2026-06-26](https://www.quantamagazine.org/after-80-years-mathematicians-give-famed-erdos-method-an-upgrade-20260626/))
