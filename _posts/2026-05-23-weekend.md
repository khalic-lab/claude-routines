## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

### The Unlearnability Phenomenon in RLVR for Language Models
**[arXiv:2605.16787](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.16787)** · Yulin Chen, He He, Chen Zhao et al. · `[preprint]`

During RLVR training, a substantial fraction of "hard" examples turns out to be permanently unlearnable — even when the model occasionally produces correct rollouts for them. The authors identify the signature: unlearnable examples have low gradient similarity to learnable ones and rely on non-generalizable reasoning patterns that don't transfer across problems. Standard remedies — more rollouts, curriculum learning, diversified sampling — cannot close the gap for these examples. The paper challenges the implicit assumption running through much of the RLVR scaling literature that sufficient on-policy experience and reward signal will eventually get everything right.

*Why this matters:* If a hard ceiling exists on what RLVR can teach, the field needs to know its shape before throwing more compute at it. — confirmed via HF Papers

### Beyond Reasoning: Reinforcement Learning Unlocks Parametric Knowledge in LLMs
**[arXiv:2605.07153](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07153)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

The conventional account treats RLVR as a reasoning-improvement tool. This paper shows the effect is broader: RL fine-tuning produces an average ~27% relative gain across factual question-answering benchmarks, improving direct recall of parametric knowledge stored in weights, not just reasoning chains. The mechanism appears to be that RL training sharpens the model's ability to activate and surface latent knowledge that was already present but inaccessible via standard prompting.

*Why this matters:* Opens the question of whether RL acts more like a general "activator" of latent capability than a reasoning-skills trainer specifically.

### Your Language Model Is Its Own Critic: RL with Value Estimation from Actor's Internal States
**[arXiv:2605.07579](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07579)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Standard actor-critic RL requires a separate value/critic network — which creates distribution mismatch between actor and critic as they diverge during training. This paper uses the actor's own hidden-state activations as an implicit value signal, eliminating the separate model. The approach reduces parameter count, removes the distribution-mismatch problem, and shows gains on mathematical reasoning benchmarks relative to actor-critic baselines. The actor's internal representations already encode something about expected return that can be read out with a lightweight probe.

*Why this matters:* Simplifies the RL training stack and removes a known source of instability in long-horizon agent training.

### DASH: Fast Differentiable Architecture Search for Hybrid Attention in Minutes on a Single GPU
**[arXiv:2605.20936](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.20936)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Hybrid attention — interleaving full-attention layers with linear or state-space operators — is emerging as the dominant paradigm for inference-efficient LLMs, but existing NAS approaches require 200B+ tokens. DASH relaxes discrete operator-placement into continuous architecture logits, separating search from adaptation; each run uses only 12.3 million tokens and completes in ~20 minutes on a single RTX Pro 6000 GPU. This recovers competitive architectures without the enormous pre-search compute budget that made NAS impractical for most research groups.

*Why this matters:* Democratizes hybrid-architecture search to any group with one modern GPU.

### Diagnosing Training-Inference Mismatch in LLM Reinforcement Learning
**[arXiv:2605.14220](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.14220)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Identifies "Training-Inference Mismatch" (TIM): implementation-level divergences cause rollout generation and policy optimization to assign different log-probability values to the same token sequence under the same model weights, introducing a systematic perturbation into the RL gradient signal. The paper argues TIM should be treated as a first-order factor in LLM RL stability, not numerical noise. A diagnostic toolkit is provided and correcting TIM sources changes training dynamics measurably.

*Why this matters:* Practitioners debugging unstable RLVR runs may have been chasing the wrong variables; TIM gives a sharper diagnostic target.

### MARLIN: Multi-Agent Game-Theoretic RL for Sustainable LLM Inference in Datacenters
**[arXiv:2605.13496](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13496)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Cloud LLM inference accounts for up to 90% of LLM lifecycle energy consumption. MARLIN treats datacenter inference scheduling as a multi-agent game: RL agents route requests while balancing time-to-first-token, carbon intensity, water use, and cost simultaneously. Against baselines: ≥18% reduction in TTFT, 33% fewer carbon emissions, 43% less water consumption, 11% lower energy cost, with minimal throughput penalty.

*Why this matters:* As inference scales to hundreds of billions of daily tokens, the environmental footprint of scheduling policy is no longer a rounding error.

### Emergent Misalignment via Feature Superposition Geometry
**[arXiv:2605.00842](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00842)** · Minegishi, Furuta et al. (University of Tokyo; accepted ACL 2026) · `[preprint]`

Fine-tuning on benign content can produce emergent misalignment because LLM features are encoded in overlapping superposed representations — amplifying a benign target feature inadvertently strengthens geometrically adjacent harmful features. Using sparse autoencoders to map feature geometry, the authors develop a filtering method removing training samples whose activations are closest to known toxic feature directions. This cuts emergent misalignment by 34.5%, outperforming both random removal and LLM-as-judge filtering.

*Why this matters:* Provides a mechanistic explanation for a class of fine-tuning safety failures, and a practical mitigation that scales with better SAE coverage.

### ExpThink: Experience-Guided RL for Adaptive Chain-of-Thought Compression
**[arXiv:2605.07501](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07501)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Existing RL approaches to CoT length reduction apply uniform length penalties regardless of query difficulty. ExpThink learns when to truncate from accumulated experience, adapting compression dynamically. Result: up to 77% reduction in average CoT response length with simultaneous accuracy improvement, and up to 3× improvement in the accuracy-efficiency ratio over baseline reasoning LLMs — avoiding the accuracy regression that typically accompanies aggressive static compression.

*Why this matters:* Inference cost for reasoning models is dominated by CoT length; adaptive compression that doesn't sacrifice accuracy is a major practical win.

### Self-Distilled Agentic Reinforcement Learning (SDAR)
**[arXiv:2605.15155](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15155)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Reward hacking in long-horizon agentic tasks is a pervasive failure mode. SDAR introduces a sigmoid-gated auxiliary objective that distils detached token-level signals from the model's own generations while keeping RL as the primary optimization backbone. The gate prevents the auxiliary signal from overwhelming the RL gradient when they conflict. Tested on multi-step tool-use tasks where standard RL reliably hacks reward.

*Why this matters:* Reward hacking is increasingly the bottleneck for deploying RL-trained agents in production; a simple architectural fix that doesn't require dense human feedback is valuable.

### An Interpretable Latency Model for Speculative Decoding in LLM Serving
**[arXiv:2605.15051](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.15051)** · [authors] et al. · `[preprint]`

Speculative decoding configuration (draft length _k_, acceptance rate thresholds, drafter size) has been done empirically. ### Talagrand's Convexity Conjecture — Proved After 30 Years
**[arXiv:2605.10908](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10908)** · Dongming Merrick Hua, Antoine Song, Stefan Tudose · Submitted 11 May 2026 · `[preprint]`

A 30-year-old conjecture by Michel Talagrand (2024 Abel Prize winner) is now proved. The conjecture, posed in 1995, asserts that even in enormous, chaotic, high-dimensional point assemblages — drawn from probability distributions with subgaussian tails — orderly convex shapes inevitably emerge. More precisely: any centered 1-subgaussian random vector in ℝⁿ can be written as the sum of a universal constant number of standard Gaussian vectors. The result settles a foundational question in high-dimensional probability about the structure of subgaussian distributions. Talagrand himself called the result "the most extraordinary of my entire life."

*Why this matters:* Subgaussian concentration is a workhorse assumption across statistical learning theory, compressed sensing, and random matrix theory; understanding the structural constraints on subgaussian distributions has implications across all of these.
— [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/sensational-proof-topples-decades-old-geometry-problem/) [via snippet]

### An AI Reasoning Model Disproves the Erdős Unit-Distance Conjecture (1946)
**[OpenAI, 20 May 2026](https://openai.com/index/model-disproves-discrete-geometry-conjecture/)** `[vendor PR]`

An internal OpenAI general-purpose reasoning model disproved the central conjecture in Paul Erdős's 1946 planar unit-distance problem — which asked how many unit-distance pairs can exist among _n_ points in the plane. For 80 years, the square grid was believed optimal. The model identified an infinite family of point arrangements that beat the grid by applying algebraic number theory via infinite class field towers and the Golod-Shafarevich theorem — a connection that had never previously been applied to this problem. The disproof has been verified externally by human mathematicians.

*Context:* This is not "an LLM hallucinated a proof." The model found a genuinely novel mathematical construction that humans overlooked for 80 years, in a domain where exhaustive search is infeasible.

*Why this matters:* Establishes a concrete case where a general-purpose AI reasoning system contributed a non-trivial discovery to pure mathematics — distinct from proof verification or formalization tasks.

### Area-Law Entanglement in 1D Bosonic Systems with Long-Range Interactions
**[arXiv:2411.02157](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.02157)** · [authors] et al. · Published in *Nature Communications*, 21 May 2026 `[preprint precursor]`

Entanglement area laws — which bound how much quantum entanglement a ground state can contain — were previously established only for short-range spin systems. This paper extends the result to 1D bosonic chains with long-range interactions and unbounded local energies, covering the Bose-Hubbard and φ⁴ universality classes that describe a wide range of realistic quantum systems. The proof also yields efficiency-guaranteed MPS approximation algorithms for these systems' ground states, meaning numerical simulations are guaranteed to converge rather than fail unpredictably.

*Context:* Area laws are the theoretical foundation that makes tensor-network methods computationally tractable. Extending them to long-range bosonic systems closes a major gap that has blocked rigorous analysis of a large class of quantum many-body problems.

*Why this matters:* Establishes the formal groundwork for applying DMRG and related tensor-network methods to realistic quantum simulation problems that were previously outside the provably-convergent regime.
— [*Nature Communications*, 21 May 2026] [via snippet]

### White Hydrogen Seeping Continuously from Billion-Year-Old Canadian Shield Rocks
**[PNAS, DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2603895123](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2603895123)** · Universities of Toronto and Ottawa · Published 2026;123:21

Geochemists measured sustained H₂ release from borehole data at an active mine near Timmins, Ontario: ~8 kg per borehole per year, sustained continuously for a decade or more, extrapolating to over 140 tonnes per year across the site's ~15,000 boreholes. ## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. A remarkable week for foundational mathematics — two major results, and an AI contribution to both.**
The Talagrand convexity conjecture proof (arXiv:2605.10908) and the OpenAI reasoning model disproving the Erdős unit-distance conjecture arrived within nine days of each other, alongside Scholze and Clausen publishing their condensed mathematics programme's landmark paper. Three major mathematical results in one week is extraordinary; what's more notable is that one of them — the Erdős disproof — was found by a general-purpose AI model, not a math specialist, using a genuinely novel algebraic approach (class field towers applied to a combinatorial geometry problem). The community consensus after external verification is that the model found something real. This is qualitatively different from LLMs formalizing known proofs or searching known mathematical structures. Whether it scales from one striking case to a sustained capacity for mathematical discovery remains open — but the first case is now documented and verified.

**2. The RLVR ceiling is becoming visible — and the field is mapping it from five directions simultaneously.**
Five papers this week probe where reinforcement learning for language models breaks down, and they converge on a consistent picture. The unlearnability phenomenon (2605.16787) shows hard examples have a permanent ceiling regardless of training budget. TIM (2605.14220) shows implementation-level mismatch has been corrupting gradient signals throughout the literature. The "parametric knowledge" paper (2605.07153) shows the positive flip side: RL activates latent knowledge, not just trains reasoning. Actor-as-own-critic (2605.07579) addresses the distributional instability of actor-critic architectures. SDAR (2605.15155) addresses reward hacking in long-horizon settings. Taken together, these papers aren't describing isolated bugs — they're mapping the reliability and capability boundaries of RLVR as a paradigm. The field is entering a consolidation phase where the limits are being charted as carefully as the capabilities.

**3. The open-weights tier has converged with the closed frontier on agentic tasks — but the economics are changing faster than the benchmarks.**
Cohere Command A+ (Apache 2.0, 218B MoE, runs on two H100s with native citations), Kimi K2.6 (1T/32B active, 8× cheaper input than Opus 4.7), DeepSeek V4 Pro (MIT, 1.6T/49B active, 80.6% SWE-bench Verified), and Gemma 4 (Apache 2.0, 256K context) collectively represent a week where multiple open-weight models are within 5–15 points of the closed frontier on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index. The NIST CAISI evaluation still finds an ~8-month gap — but NIST's methodology is not primarily agentic-task-focused. For the tasks that matter in enterprise deployment (long-horizon coding, multi-step tool use, RAG with attribution), the gap has already closed to within measurement error for many workloads. Meanwhile, Karpathy joining Anthropic's pre-training team signals the frontier is not considered solved — but the pressure from below is now structural, not episodic.

**4. The supply chain is now the perimeter — and SLSA Level-3 provenance has been bypassed.**
The TanStack/TeamPCP worm is qualitatively different from earlier supply chain attacks because it contaminated packages carrying valid SLSA Level 3 provenance signatures. The CI chain itself was the attack surface, meaning provenance signatures provided false assurance — the failure mode SLSA was designed to prevent. AI SDKs (Mistral, OpenAI, Guardrails AI) were among the targets, meaning the attack surface specifically includes the tooling developers use to build AI-powered systems. The missing layer is runtime behavioral monitoring: treat every published package as potentially compromised at first execution regardless of its provenance certificate, and monitor for behavioral anomalies. The AI ecosystem is particularly exposed because AI SDK packages are updated frequently and used with elevated trust.
