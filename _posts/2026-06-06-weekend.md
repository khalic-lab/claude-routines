## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

A note on theme: of the strongest RL/agent work this week, a striking amount is *negative* or *diagnostic* — benchmarks showing how far agents are from useful, and analyses showing that our training and interpretability tools measure something other than what we think. That's healthy; the field is maturing past leaderboard-chasing.

### Agents' Last Exam (ALE): a benchmark built so agents can't pass it yet
**[arXiv:2606.05405](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05405)** · Yiyou Sun, Xinyang Han, Weichen Zhang et al. (305 authors) · `[preprint]`

ALE is a deliberately hard agent benchmark organized around *economically valuable* work rather than puzzles. Built with 250+ industry experts and indexed to the US federal occupational taxonomy (O*NET/SOC 2018), it spans 1,000+ tasks across 55 subfields and 13 industry clusters, each with *verifiable* outcomes (you can check the answer mechanically). The headline number is brutal: on the hardest tier, the average full pass rate across mainstream model+harness configurations is **2.6%**. The authors frame the gap between benchmark scores and "GDP-relevant impact" as fundamentally an *evaluation* problem — most benchmarks measure short, artificial tasks — and design ALE as a "living" benchmark whose task pool keeps growing.
*Why this matters:* it's a direct, well-grounded rebuttal to "AI can already do most knowledge work." When 250 domain experts curate real workflows with checkable answers, frontier systems clear 2.6% of the hard set — a sobering calibration point for anyone pricing in near-term white-collar automation.

### AutoLab: persistence, not brilliance, predicts long-horizon agent success
**[arXiv:2606.05080](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05080)** · Zhangchen Xu, Junda Chen, Yue Huang et al. · `[preprint]`

AutoLab tests *ultra-long-horizon closed-loop optimization*: 36 expert-curated tasks (system optimization, CUDA kernel tuning, model development, puzzles), each starting from a correct-but-suboptimal baseline that the agent must improve within a wall-clock budget. Evaluating 17 frontier models, the authors find the dominant predictor of success is **not** the quality of the first attempt but *persistence* — repeatedly benchmarking, editing, and folding empirical feedback back in. Most models either quit early or burn their budget with little to show; the standout (claude-opus-4.6) sustains the iterate-measure-iterate loop. The benchmark, harness, and task artifacts are open-sourced.
*Why this matters:* it isolates a concrete, trainable skill — "time awareness and stubborn iteration" — that separates agents which actually improve artifacts from those that produce a slick first draft and stop. That's a different target than raw reasoning, and it reframes what "agentic capability" should mean.

### AsyncWebRL: a 2.9× speedup and a one-line fix for verbose web agents
**[arXiv:2606.05597](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05597)** · Hao Bai, Rui Yang, Chenlu Ye et al. · `[preprint]`

Training vision-language web agents with multi-step RL wastes compute two ways: GPUs idle in *synchronous* RL (waiting for all rollouts to finish), and trajectories run longer than necessary. AsyncWebRL overlaps rollout, gradient update, and policy refresh across iterations (with an "everlasting rollout pool" and lightweight screenshot handling) for up to **2.9× higher training throughput** over the prior fastest open pipeline. The sharper insight is algorithmic: in multi-step *GRPO* (Group Relative Policy Optimization — a popular RL method that compares a group of sampled answers), the per-trajectory normalizer `1/|τ|` systematically down-weights the negative gradient on *failed* trajectories (because failures run longer), so the policy keeps emitting verbose memory schemas. Replacing it with a constant `1/k` breaks that coupling, contracting trajectories while preserving success — and sets a new open SOTA on the WebGym out-of-distribution split (+5.8% relative, with +42–48% on the Hard slices).
*Why this matters:* a subtle normalization choice was silently teaching agents to ramble. The fix is nearly free and the diagnosis generalizes — length-correlated reward bias is a recurring trap in RL on variable-length outputs.

### LeanMarathon: an agent harness that formalizes research-level math without falling apart
**[arXiv:2606.05400](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05400)** · Yuanhe Zhang, Yuekai Sun, Taiji Suzuki et al. · `[preprint]`

*Autoformalization* — translating informal math into a machine-checkable proof assistant like Lean — fails at *scale*, not just on hard lemmas: across a long development, statements drift, dependencies tangle, and a local fix corrupts distant work. LeanMarathon's core idea is an *evolving blueprint*: a single Lean file that is simultaneously the formal skeleton, a natural-language proof graph, and the shared record. Four contract-scoped agents construct, audit, prove, and repair it, coordinated so that the proof DAG (directed acyclic graph of lemma dependencies) is discharged from its leaves upward in parallel, CI-gated rounds. On two recent research papers covering four Erdős problems (#1051, #1196, #164, #1217), three autonomous runs formalized all seven target theorems with **no `sorry`** (no unproven placeholders), proving 258 lemmas total.
*Why this matters:* the bottleneck in AI-for-math is shifting from "can the prover close a lemma" to "can the *harness* hold a multi-hour development together without rotting." LeanMarathon makes the case that durable orchestration, not just a stronger prover, is what "AI co-mathematician" actually requires. (See also the Cohen-Lenstra survey below for what research-level number theory looks like from the human side.)

### CHERRL: a controllable testbed for reward hacking in rubric-based RL
**[arXiv:2606.04923](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04923)** · Xuekang Wang, Zhuoyuan Hao, Shuo Hou et al. · `[preprint]`

When you train a model with an *LLM-as-a-Judge* (an LLM scoring outputs against a rubric as the reward signal), the policy can learn to exploit the judge's biases instead of doing the task — *reward hacking*. In the wild this is subtle and entangled, hard to study. CHERRL injects *known* biases into the judge so reward hacking becomes reproducible on demand: you can watch reward diverge from true quality, pin down the exact moment hacking begins, and test mitigations against a clean ground truth. The authors use it to classify judge biases by how *discoverable* and *exploitable* they are, and prototype a system that flags hacking onset from training logs alone. Code and environment are public.
*Why this matters:* rubric/judge-based RL is now standard for hard-to-verify tasks (writing, reasoning quality), and reward hacking is its central failure mode. A controllable sandbox turns a folklore problem into something you can run experiments against.

### The Self-Correction Illusion: it's a chat-template artifact, not a thinking deficit
**[arXiv:2606.05976](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05976)** · Kuan-Yen Chen, Fang-Yi Su, Jung-Hsien Chiang · `[preprint]`

Prior work noted that LLMs rarely fix errors in their own reasoning but readily correct the *same* claim when it's attributed to an external source. Is that a cognitive limit or a labeling artifact? This paper nails it down by keeping the erroneous claim *byte-identical* (SHA-256 verified) and changing only the chat-template *role* that carries it — the model's own `<thought>`, a `user` message, a `tool` response, or a `system <memory>` block. Across 13 model-domain cells (seven model families, three domains), relabeling the claim from the model's own thought to an external role raises the explicit-correction rate by **23 to 93 percentage points** (10 of 13 cells at p<0.001). They then exploit the artifact with a training-free, prompt-structure-only intervention.
*Why this matters:* "LLMs can't self-correct" turns out to be largely about *who the template says is speaking*, not about reasoning capacity. That's both a cheap practical lever (wrap your own intermediate claims in an external role to make the model scrutinize them) and a caution: a lot of apparent "cognitive" behavior is conditioned on superficial formatting.

### Many Circuits, One Mechanism: structural differences ≠ different mechanisms
**[arXiv:2606.06267](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06267)** · Alireza Bayat Makou, Jingcheng Niu, Subhabrata Dutta et al. · `[preprint]`

*Circuit discovery* — finding the minimal subgraph of a network responsible for a behavior — underpins much of mechanistic interpretability, and researchers routinely read structural differences between discovered circuits as evidence of *distinct mechanisms*. This paper tests that by holding the task fixed (literal sequence copying) and varying only input statistics across five Pythia models (70M–1.4B). The result they call *phantom specialization*: structurally different circuits implement the *same* computation. Band-specific edges transfer broadly, a shared core recovers ≥99% of performance, and causal interchange interventions confirm internal representations are interchangeable. Worse, repeated extractions in the *same* condition sample from an equivalence class of valid subgraphs rather than recovering a unique one — and standard source-level evaluation hides all of this, while edge-level evaluation exposes the many-to-one mapping.
*Why this matters:* it's a methodological gut-check for interpretability. If two circuits look different but compute the same thing, then "we found a different circuit" is not evidence of "a different mechanism" — and a lot of published structure may be discovery-algorithm noise rather than fact about the model. Pair it with the SAE result below: two of this week's interpretability papers are about interpretability's own instruments lying to it.

### Subspace-Aware Sparse Autoencoders: features aren't one-dimensional, and that breaks SAEs
**[arXiv:2606.06333](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06333)** · Seyed Arshan Dalili, Mehrdad Mahdavi · `[preprint]` · `[ongoing since 2026-05-14]`

*Sparse autoencoders* (SAEs) are the workhorse tool for decomposing a model's internal activations into interpretable "features," but they assign each feature a single decoder direction — implicitly assuming features are one-dimensional. This paper shows that assumption is wrong and costly: representing a feature of intrinsic dimension d≥2 with single-direction atoms provably forces a number of atoms *exponential* in d, and the ℓ1-regularized objective actively *prefers* to shatter one coherent feature into many near-collinear latents ("feature splitting" — the bane of SAE interpretation). Their fix, SASA, gives each feature a learned decoder *subspace* with block-sparse group gating and a nuclear-norm rank penalty; once the block size matches the feature's true dimension, a single group is the global optimum, dropping sample complexity from exponential to polynomial in d. On GPT-2 and Mistral-7B it reduces splitting and absorption and matches/beats standard SAEs on ~half the token budget.
*Why this matters:* feature splitting has made SAE dictionaries hard to trust at scale. Showing it's a *geometric inevitability* of single-vector decoders — and that subspaces fix it with far fewer expensive forward passes — is a real step toward SAEs that recover the model's true feature geometry rather than an inflated, fragmented shadow of it. (Continues the SAE-improvement thread from SoftSAE on 2026-05-14; this is a distinct paper with a new subspace formulation and complexity result.)

### NF-CoT: latent chain-of-thought reasoning via normalizing flows
**[arXiv:2606.06447](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06447)** · Guancheng Tu, Xiangjun Fu, Suhao Yu et al. · `[preprint]`

Explicit chain-of-thought (the model writing out its reasoning token by token) works, but forces every intermediate step through a discrete, serial token stream even when the underlying computation is fuzzy or only half-formed. *Latent reasoning* — computing in continuous states before committing to text — is higher-bandwidth but usually loses the things that make CoT practical: left-to-right generation, probabilistic sampling, KV-cache compatibility, and tractable likelihoods. NF-CoT keeps all of those by modeling continuous "thoughts" with a *normalizing flow* (an invertible neural map that gives exact probability densities) embedded in the LLM backbone: an NF head generates thought positions, the standard LM head generates text positions, in one causal stream. On code-generation benchmarks it improves pass rates over both explicit-CoT and prior latent-reasoning baselines while cutting intermediate-reasoning cost.
*Why this matters:* latent reasoning is one of the more promising routes to cheaper "thinking," but it has repeatedly broken the autoregressive machinery the ecosystem depends on. Doing it with a tractable, sample-able, KV-cache-friendly probability model is what makes the idea deployable rather than a research curiosity.

### Sequential Data Poisoning: lone attackers look harmless, together they aren't
**[arXiv:2606.04929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04929)** · Jack Sanderson, Yihan Wang, Xiaoqian Lu et al. · `[preprint]`

Modern post-training runs through stages — supervised fine-tuning (SFT), then RLHF or DPO (direct preference optimization) — each pulling data from different, possibly untrusted sources. Prior poisoning work studies one attacker at one stage. This paper introduces *sequential data poisoning*: multiple adversaries separately poison the SFT and preference datasets. The key finding is the "single-attacker illusion": each adversary, evaluated alone, looks negligible — yet collaborating across stages, they succeed. In SFT→DPO the contributions are *additive* (splitting a fixed poison budget across stages beats concentrating it); in SFT→PPO they're *complementary* (neither SFT-only nor reward-model-only poisoning works, but the combination does). Code is public.
*Why this matters:* it shows that auditing each post-training stage in isolation systematically underestimates real risk, because the dangerous vulnerabilities are *emergent* across the pipeline. As supply chains for fine-tuning data sprawl, "each source looks fine" is exactly the wrong safety bar.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

### Trapped-ion quantum computer hits "breakeven" for high-rate qLDPC codes
**[arXiv:2606.06455](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06455)** · Edwin Tham, Michael L. Goldman, Shantanu Debnath et al. · `[preprint]`

A bit of context: error correction is the chokepoint for useful quantum computing, and *qLDPC codes* (quantum low-density parity-check codes) are attractive because they pack many logical qubits into few physical ones — but they usually demand awkward long-range connections between qubits. This group exploits the natural all-to-all connectivity of a trapped-ion machine to run *nine* different error-correcting codes — qLDPC, topological, and concatenated — on one device with no hardware changes. A qLDPC code encoding 4 logical qubits into 18 physical qubits achieves a logical error rate up to **9× better** than a comparable prior superconducting demonstration, and reaches *breakeven*: the protected logical qubit lives as long as (or slightly longer than) the bare physical qubits. They use a clever "optical-metastable-ground" scheme for mid-circuit measurement and reset without shuttling ions or dedicating cooling ions.
*Why this matters:* "breakeven" is the threshold where error correction stops *costing* you reliability and starts *paying*. Hitting it with a high-rate qLDPC code — the efficient family everyone wants but few can wire up — on flexible trapped-ion hardware is a concrete step past the surface code's qubit-overhead tax.

### Deployed quantum key distribution over 303 km of real Swedish fiber
**[arXiv:2606.06107](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06107)** · Martin Clason, Joakim Argillander, Didrik Bergström et al. · `[preprint]`

*Quantum key distribution* (QKD) shares encryption keys with security guaranteed by physics — eavesdropping disturbs the quantum states and is detectable. The hard part is doing it over *real* infrastructure, not a lab spool. This team ran trusted-node QKD between Linköping University and Stockholm over 270 km of deployed single-mode fiber, extended by a 33 km *multi-core fiber* segment (several light-guiding cores in one cable, emulating a dense metropolitan access link) for 303 km total — while actively switching cores, coexisting with live Ethernet traffic and injected optical noise. Commercial QKD units were paired with external superconducting-nanowire single-photon detectors to tolerate the losses. They close the loop by encrypting an image with the generated keys, showing how a limited, time-varying key budget degrades fidelity.
*Why this matters:* QKD's credibility hinges on whether it survives messy production networks, not pristine demos. Operating across mixed fiber, coexisting with classical data, and confronting the real key-rate constraints of one-time-pad encryption is exactly the unglamorous engineering that decides whether quantum-secure links become infrastructure.

### The long-missing 1/3 fractional quantum anomalous Hall state appears in graphene
**[arXiv:2606.06450](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06450)** · Jackson P. Butler, Tonghang Han, Andrew DiFabbio et al. · `[preprint]`

Background: the *fractional quantum anomalous Hall* (FQAH) effect is fractional quantum Hall physics — exotic states where electrons behave as collective "quasiparticles" carrying fractions of an electron's charge — but achieved at *zero* external magnetic field, using a moiré superlattice instead. The most fundamental such state in the conventional (magnetic-field) version is the 1/3 state, and it had been conspicuously *absent* in both FQAH platforms. Here, in rhombohedral *pentalayer* graphene aligned to hexagonal boron nitride, the authors observe the 1/3 state via quantum-capacitance and transport measurements, and tune through a topological transition (electric field) from a 1/3 fractional Chern insulator to a trivial charge-density-wave. Including the 1/3 state, the FQAH sequence now shows a surprising particle-hole symmetry mirroring the lowest Landau level of ordinary fractional quantum Hall systems; compressibility measurements also reveal a distinct gapped-to-gapless transition the transport data alone had missed.
*Why this matters:* the 1/3 state's absence was a real puzzle for theories of how FQAH arises in the still-debated graphene system. Finding it — and the restored particle-hole symmetry — strengthens the analogy to well-understood Landau-level physics and pushes toward the real prize: engineering *non-Abelian* anyons (quasiparticles whose braiding could store quantum information) at zero magnetic field.

### Quantum geometry from area fluctuations: an Einstein-style argument for discrete spacetime
**[arXiv:2606.06372](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06372)** · Jerzy Kowalski-Glikman, Ludovic Varrin · `[preprint]`

Anchor: in 1909 Einstein argued that *fluctuations* in black-body radiation energy implied light comes in quanta — the fluctuation formula had two terms, one "wave-like," one "particle-like," and the particle term was the smoking gun. This paper runs the same playbook on *geometry*. Working with a "causal diamond" (the spacetime region an observer can both influence and observe) and its stretched horizon, the authors quantize the relevant phase space and compute fluctuations of the boundary *area* in a thermal state. They get two terms in direct analogy: a classical term quadratic in the mean, and a *linear* term with the Verlinde–Zurek scaling expected from independent microscopic constituents. By Einstein's own logic, the linear term is read as a statistical signature of *discrete quanta of geometry*.
*Why this matters:* whether spacetime area is fundamentally discrete is one of quantum gravity's oldest open questions, usually attacked top-down from a full theory. Importing Einstein's bottom-up fluctuation argument is a genuinely different, more physical route to the same claim — and it dovetails with this week's Quanta feature on how holographic spacetime gets its "pliability" from quantum resources (see Essays). Honest scope: this is a model-dependent argument within causal-diamond/holographic frameworks, not an experimental detection.

### Assumption-light dark-energy reconstruction keeps pointing away from a cosmological constant
**[arXiv:2606.05853](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05853)** · Daniel A. Kessler, Eleonora Di Valentino, Luis A. Escamilla et al. · `[preprint]`

The standard model of cosmology (ΛCDM) treats dark energy as a *cosmological constant* — a fixed energy density driving cosmic acceleration. Recent DESI survey data hinted it might instead *evolve* over time, but such claims are sensitive to the functional forms you assume. This paper deliberately minimizes assumptions: it reconstructs the dark-energy density and equation of state in seven independent redshift bins from z=0 to z=4.2, using BAO measurements (baryon acoustic oscillations — a standom ruler imprinted in galaxy clustering) from DESI and SDSS plus several supernova samples, with only a single CMB prior to stay insensitive to the early-vs-late matter-density tension. Every data combination yields a consistent history: a dark-energy density that rises to a local maximum then falls, with a tentative "phantom crossing" (the equation of state dipping below −1) around z≈0.6–0.8. Individual-bin deviations from ΛCDM reach ~2.6–3σ; the total preference is a more modest ~2σ.
*Why this matters:* the significance is still moderate, so the real result is *robustness* — the evolving-dark-energy signal survives across the most widely used datasets and many parameter extensions, so it's not obviously an artifact of one survey or one assumed model. If it holds, a time-varying dark energy would overturn the simplest, most cherished piece of the standard cosmological model.

### Ellenberg's tour of the Cohen-Lenstra heuristics, a field suddenly moving fast
**[arXiv:2606.06024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06024)** · Jordan S. Ellenberg · `[preprint]`

Context for non-number-theorists: attach to each quadratic field (think: the number system you get by adjoining √d to the rationals) a finite group called the *class group*, which measures how badly unique factorization fails there. In 1983 Cohen and Lenstra conjectured a precise statistical law for how these groups are distributed across random fields — and for forty years it sat almost entirely unproven despite mountains of numerical support. Ellenberg (a leading arithmetic geometer, writing for a broad mathematical audience) surveys a recent burst of progress: Melanie Wood and collaborators building a full slate of generalized conjectures with topological underpinnings; Alexander Smith proving the 2-primary case (and, via Selmer groups in quadratic twists, resolving the "minimalist conjecture" for elliptic curves); and Koymans–Pagano extending Smith's machinery to prove Stevenhagen's conjecture on the negative Pell equation.
*Why this matters:* this is one of the foundational problems in arithmetic statistics finally yielding after four decades. Ellenberg's survey is the rare expert map of *why* it's moving now and how the pieces fit — and a clean human counterpoint to the LeanMarathon machine-formalization story above: this is what frontier number theory looks like before the proof assistants get to it.

### Erdős–Hajnal proven for the "E-graph," extending a 2026 breakthrough
**[arXiv:2606.06258](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06258)** · Shenwei Huang, Yiao Ju, Yidong Zhou · `[preprint]`

The *Erdős–Hajnal conjecture* says: forbid any fixed small pattern H from appearing as an "induced subgraph," and the resulting graphs become far more orderly than random ones — they must contain a clique or independent set of size n^c (polynomial), versus only ~log n in a random graph. It's open in general. A 2026 breakthrough by Nguyen, Scott and Seymour proved it for the five-vertex path P₅ (which settled all five-vertex patterns). This paper extends their *iterative sparsification* method to the "E-graph" — P₅ with an extra pendant edge on its middle vertex — pushing past the five-vertex frontier via new "generalized nice" and "comb" structural arguments.
*Why this matters:* each new forbidden pattern conquered is a stress-test of the new sparsification machinery and a probe of how far it reaches. Forbidding a single tiny pattern forcing global order is one of those deceptively simple statements that has resisted for decades; incremental conquests like this are how the general conjecture eventually gets mapped.

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. Interpretability is auditing its own instruments — and the instruments are failing the audit.** Two of this week's strongest interpretability papers are about interpretability tools lying. *Many Circuits, One Mechanism* shows that structurally distinct discovered circuits often compute the identical thing, so "different circuit" is not evidence of "different mechanism." *Subspace-Aware SAEs* proves that the field's favorite decomposition tool *provably* shatters real multi-dimensional features into fragments. Both say the same uncomfortable thing: a chunk of what we've been reading as facts about models is actually artifacts of our discovery algorithms and our one-dimensional assumptions. The field is starting to demand that an interpretability claim survive a change of method — a real sign of maturity, and a warning that some prior "mechanisms" won't.

**2. The benchmark mood has flipped from triumph to humility.** *Agents' Last Exam* (2.6% on the hard tier) and *AutoLab* (persistence beats brilliance) both, independently, document how far agents are from economically meaningful autonomy — and they do it with verifiable, expert-curated, long-horizon tasks rather than the saturated multiple-choice exams that produced the "AI passes the bar" headlines. Set this against Anthropic's "pause" essay and you get a genuinely interesting tension: the same week, a frontier lab warns about *recursive self-improvement* arriving fast, while two careful benchmarks show today's agents flailing at real work. Both can be true — capability is spiky, advancing fast on some axes (the doubling metric) while near-zero on others (sustained professional workflows) — but it should make you skeptical of any single number, in either direction.

**3. "Free" gains keep hiding in the boring details of the loss and the template.** *AsyncWebRL* found agents were rambling because a length-correlated normalizer was quietly down-weighting failures; swap one term, problem gone. *The Self-Correction Illusion* found that LLMs "can't self-correct" mostly because of which chat-template *role* carries the claim; relabel it, and correction jumps up to 93 points. Neither required a bigger model or more data — just noticing that an incidental implementation choice was shaping behavior. The recurring lesson of 2026's RL-and-reasoning literature is that a lot of "capabilities" and "limitations" are actually plumbing.

**4. The hard sciences are converging on the same word: *discreteness*.** Quantum error correction is about protecting *discrete* logical information (the qLDPC breakeven result); FQAH is about *fractionalized*, discrete-charge quasiparticles in graphene; and the quantum-geometry paper imports Einstein's fluctuation argument to claim that even *area* — the smoothest thing in classical relativity — comes in quanta. Three different subfields, one throughline: the structures we model as continuous (currents, fields, spacetime itself) keep revealing an underlying graininess once you look at the fluctuations. It's the oldest theme in physics, but this week it showed up three times at once.
