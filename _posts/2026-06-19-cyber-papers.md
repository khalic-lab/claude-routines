- **JetBrains Hub — authentication bypass scoring a perfect CVSS 10.0, plus a privilege-escalation and an account-takeover flaw.** Hub is JetBrains' central identity/SSO service that sits behind YouTrack, TeamCity and other JetBrains tools — compromise it and you inherit access to everything wired into it. **CVE-2026-50242 (CVSS 10.0, critical)** is an authentication bypass *via direct database access* yielding administrative access — the maximum score reflects a scope change (the attacker breaks out of the vulnerable component into others) with full confidentiality/integrity/availability loss. Alongside it, **CVE-2026-56142 (9.9)** lets an attacker escalate privilege by attaching authentication details to accounts, and **CVE-2026-56141 (9.8)** is an account takeover via *predictable restore codes* — i.e. the password-recovery codes weren't random enough to resist guessing. All are fixed in Hub 2026.1.13757 and the back-ported 2025.x/2024.x builds; no public exploitation noted yet, but a 10.0 on an identity hub is patch-now. [NVD: CVE-2026-50242](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-50242); [JetBrains security bulletin](https://www.jetbrains.com/privacy-security/issues-fixed/)

- **pgAdmin 4 ships *two* critical flaws — one of them an AI-Assistant prompt-injection that turns poisoned database rows into arbitrary SQL.** pgAdmin is the standard web admin console for PostgreSQL. **CVE-2026-12046 (CVSS 9.5)** is the classic-shaped one: two state-changing endpoints in the SQL Editor were the only routes missing the login-required decorator, and both reach a `pickle.loads` sink — a Python deserialization gadget (rebuilding objects from attacker-controlled bytes, a well-known path to remote code execution) reachable *without authentication*. The more novel **CVE-2026-12045 (CVSS 9.4)** is an AI-era bug: the AI Assistant's `execute_sql_query` tool runs LLM-generated SQL, and an attacker who can influence the database *content the assistant reads* can steer it into executing arbitrary SQL with the pgAdmin user's database role — indirect prompt injection (hostile instructions smuggled in through data the model ingests, not the user's prompt) escaping the intended read-only sandbox. Why it matters: 12046 is a textbook unauth-RCE to patch immediately; 12045 is an early, concrete example of an LLM tool's "read-only" guardrail being bypassed through data poisoning — exactly the failure mode this reader ships code against. Both fixed in pgAdmin 4.16.0. [NVD: CVE-2026-12046](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-12046); [NVD: CVE-2026-12045](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-12045)

## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

Drawn from today's arXiv announcement (2026-06-19, 04:00 UTC batch across cs.LG / cs.AI / cs.CL / cs.CV / stat.ML), excluding the six IDs this morning's Overview already covered. All `[preprint]`, not yet peer-reviewed.

- **arXiv:2606.19544 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **"LLM-as-a-Judge" reliability is largely an illusion: the standard metric overstates judge skill, and rankings swing wildly.** Using a model to grade other models' outputs has become the default evaluation method, but it's usually validated by exact-match agreement — a metric that doesn't correct for the agreement you'd get by chance. The largest audit to date (21 judges from nine providers, ~541,000 individual judgments) finds that switching to Cohen's kappa (a chance-corrected agreement score) deflates measured agreement by 33–41 percentage points; judge rankings shift by up to 14 positions depending on which benchmark you use; and two production judges combine very high test–retest consistency (>0.95) with severe position bias (>0.10 — they favour whichever answer is shown first). Why interesting: if you trust an LLM judge to gate your model releases, this is a direct, dated warning that the validation is shakier than the headline numbers suggest. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19544)

- **arXiv:2606.20008 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **VIMPO: get the dense credit assignment of actor-critic RL without training a separate critic.** In RL for LLM reasoning, GRPO-style methods (group-relative policy optimization — rank a batch of sampled answers against each other, no value network) are simple but hand the *same* trajectory-level score to every token, so the model can't tell which step actually mattered. Actor-critic methods give per-token signal but need a learned value function that's its own source of training instability. VIMPO derives a value function *implicitly* from the optimality conditions of KL-regularized RL (writing it as a recurrence in policy-vs-reference log-ratios, anchored by "no reward remains at the trajectory's end"), getting denser credit assignment with no extra critic to train. Why interesting: critic-free-yet-dense is a sweet spot for the RLVR (RL from verifiable rewards) pipelines now driving reasoning models — cheaper and more stable at once. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20008)

- **arXiv:2606.19919 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **ADaPT: stop punishing long-but-correct reasoning when you train a model to be efficient.** Large reasoning models pay for their long chain-of-thought (the step-by-step "thinking" tokens) in compute, so people add efficiency rewards to shorten it — but those rewards are applied across the whole sequence, implicitly penalising a long answer even when it was *right*. ADaPT decouples the two signals at the token level: it inserts a single "mode-selection" token that chooses fast vs slow thinking, and applies the efficiency reward *only to that token*, so length pressure never bleeds into the correctness of the actual reasoning. Why interesting: a clean fix for a real tension — making reasoning models cheaper to run without quietly degrading the hard problems they're worth running on. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19919)

- **arXiv:2606.20419 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **Cutting vision-language hallucination with a one-time weight edit — no retraining, no inference cost.** Vision-language models (VLMs — models that take an image and produce text) often confidently describe objects that aren't in the picture. QK Product Steering edits the query-key product (the operator that produces the raw, pre-softmax attention scores) by suppressing a handful of dominant singular modes in selected middle layers, then folds the change back into the query weights via a closed-form update — leaving keys untouched so it stays compatible with grouped-query attention (a memory-saving scheme where several query heads share one key/value head). It's data-free, training-free and adds zero latency. Why interesting: most hallucination fixes cost extra inference or fine-tuning; a static weight edit that just *bakes in* the mitigation is unusually deployment-friendly. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20419)

- **arXiv:2606.20097 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **HydraHead: hybridize fast and slow attention at the *head* level, not the layer level.** Full attention is accurate but scales quadratically with sequence length (the long-context bottleneck); linear attention is cheap but weaker, and the usual fix — alternating full and linear *layers* — has proven hard to tune. HydraHead's interpretability analysis finds that whole layers behave similarly but individual attention heads *within* a layer specialise differently, so the head is the natural unit at which to mix the two attention types. Why interesting: it reframes the efficient-attention design space around head-level specialisation — a concrete, evidence-driven knob for building long-context models that stay fast without the layer-mixing fragility. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20097)

- **arXiv:2606.20560 · 2026-06-19** `[preprint]` — **"How transparent is DiffusionGemma?" — auditing whether diffusion language models are harder to interpret than ordinary ones.** Autoregressive models (predict one token at a time, left to right) expose readable intermediate states; diffusion language models instead refine the whole output over many denoising steps in a continuous latent space, which *looks* far more opaque — the paper measures an apparent 28.6× more hidden serial computation between interpretable states than autoregressive Gemma 4. It splits "transparency" into variable transparency (can we read intermediate snapshots?) and algorithmic transparency (can we reconstruct *how* the answer was reached?), and argues the opacity is partly recoverable by mapping those latent snapshots. Why interesting: diffusion LMs are a rising alternative architecture, and whether we can still interpret and audit them is a live safety question — this is an early, concrete attempt to quantify it. (Continues the diffusion-LM line from this week's Diffusion-Proof work — a *distinct* paper on a different question.) [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20560)
