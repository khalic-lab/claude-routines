## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

A strong week for *reinforcement learning for language models* — not new base models, but better ways to train, stabilise and serve them. Four of the ten papers below are RL methods or systems; the rest span efficient inference, interpretability and architecture. All are arXiv preprints from the 2026-06-25 submission batch unless noted.

### OPID: On-Policy Skill Distillation for Agentic Reinforcement Learning
**[arXiv:2606.26790](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26790)** · Shuo Yang, Jinyang Wu, Zhengxi Lu et al. · `[preprint]`

When you train a language *agent* — a model that takes many steps, calls tools, and only finds out at the very end whether it succeeded — outcome-based RL gives a single reward for the whole episode. That tells the model almost nothing about which of its dozens of intermediate decisions were good. OPID extracts "skills" from the agent's *own* completed runs: episode-level skills capture overall workflows and failure-avoidance habits, step-level skills capture good moves at critical moments. It then re-scores each candidate response with and without the skill hint injected, and turns the resulting shift in token probabilities into a dense, per-token bonus layered on top of the sparse final reward. On ALFWorld, WebShop and search-based question-answering it beats outcome-only RL and prior skill-distillation methods on both success rate and sample efficiency.
*Why this matters:* it's a way to give agents fine-grained "learn from your own past moves" feedback without bolting on an external memory store — the credit-assignment problem (figuring out which action earned the reward) is the central difficulty in agent RL, and this attacks it cheaply.

### GEOALIGN: Geometric Rollout Curation for Robust LLM Reinforcement Learning
**[arXiv:2606.26917](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26917)** · Ting Zhou, Zhenqing Ling, Yiyang Zhao et al. · `[preprint]`

RL fine-tuning of language models is notoriously jittery: training oscillates and sometimes collapses. The authors trace one cause to "directional inconsistency" — within a single batch, a handful of high-reward samples can pull the model in a direction that sharply conflicts with the batch majority, producing noisy updates. GEOALIGN is a lightweight add-on that builds preference pairs within each prompt, learns a small projector over hidden states to line up reward-ordered directions, and flags rollouts whose update direction strays too far from the batch consensus — swapping them for steadier alternatives. It is forward-pass only, adds negligible overhead, and improves both dialogue alignment and math reasoning while damping the training wobble, beating PF-PPO, PAR, PODS and Seed-GRPO (variants of PPO/GRPO, the two dominant RL-for-LLM optimisers).
*Why this matters:* a cheap reliability filter that makes RL post-training less of a coin-flip — the kind of unglamorous robustness work that decides whether a training run finishes.

### RolloutPipe: Overlapping Pipelined Rollout and Training in Disaggregated On-Policy LLM RL
**[arXiv:2606.26997](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26997)** · Rongjian Chen, Jianmin Hu, Kejiang Ye et al. · `[preprint]`

Modern RL-with-verifiable-rewards (RLVR — the model is scored against a checkable answer) splits the work across two GPU pools: one *generates* model outputs (rollout), one *trains* on them. Synchronous setups waste the trainer GPUs sitting idle until the whole rollout batch finishes; asynchronous ones train on stale data and drift off-policy. RolloutPipe pipelines at the granularity of complete sample groups: the moment a group is fully generated it's dispatched to the trainer, and an admission policy prioritises exactly the groups needed to assemble the next training batch — so training starts before rollout finishes while staying genuinely on-policy (training only on data from the current model). On Qwen3-1.7B across reasoning and science benchmarks it cuts rollout-to-train-end time by 31–42% and trainer idle time by 37–76% versus the Slime system.
*Why this matters:* RL-for-reasoning is now a dominant and expensive training regime; a 30–40% wall-clock cut on the loop is real money and real iteration speed.

### VLM-PBRS: Automating Potential-Based Reward Shaping with Vision-Language-Model Guidance
**[arXiv:2606.27180](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27180)** · Henrik Müller, Daniel Kudenko · `[preprint]`

RL agents struggle when rewards are sparse (you only get points at the goal), and hand-crafted intermediate rewards invite "reward hacking" — the agent games the proxy instead of doing the task. Potential-based reward shaping (PBRS) is the textbook fix: it provably can't change the optimal policy, but it needs a human to define a "potential function" measuring progress. This paper learns that function automatically from a vision-language model's preferences over pairs of images, removing the expert-design bottleneck. To keep costs down it queries small, cheap VLMs; their labels are noisier, but still accelerate learning, with gains tracking label accuracy. Validated on Meta-World and Franka Kitchen robotic-manipulation tasks.
*Why this matters:* it lets a general-purpose vision model supply the "what counts as progress" signal for robot learning — automating one of the most labour-intensive parts of setting up an RL problem.

### Cascaded Multi-Granularity Pruning for On-Device LLM Inference in Industrial IoT
**[arXiv:2606.26861](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26861)** · Jinghan Wang, Yanjun Chen, Wei Zhang et al. · `[preprint]`

Running LLMs on edge hardware (sensors, controllers) demands aggressive compression, but one-shot structured pruning — deleting whole structural pieces of the network — collapses accuracy at high compression. The authors prune coarse-to-fine: whole layers first, then attention heads, then feed-forward channels, with a lightweight low-rank recovery step between stages to re-estimate what still matters. They also formalise a checkable "Structural Independence Assumption" predicting *when* per-component pruning is safe: classic multi-head-attention + GELU designs satisfy it, but the GQA + SwiGLU designs (grouped-query attention and the gated activation used in most current LLMs) violate it. On bearing-fault-diagnosis models they reach 13.8× compression at 83.8% accuracy where the assumption holds — cutting latency up to 67% and memory 62% on real hardware — while documenting a ~74-point accuracy collapse where it doesn't.
*Why this matters:* a practical edge-compression recipe, plus a genuinely useful warning that the *newest* model architectures are the ones that resist standard pruning — exactly the trap an unwary practitioner would fall into.

### HyperDFlash: MHC-Aligned Block Speculative Decoding with Gated Residual Reduction
**[arXiv:2606.26744](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26744)** · Luxi Lin, Shuang Peng, Rui Ma et al. · `[preprint]`

Speculative decoding speeds up text generation by having a small "drafter" propose several tokens that the big model then verifies in a single pass — if the guesses are good, you get multiple tokens for the price of one forward step. This paper targets DeepSeek-V4's new multi-hyper-connection (MHC) architecture, whose native multi-token-prediction drafting degrades at later positions and whose multi-path residual stream breaks conventional drafters. The fixes: condition the drafter on "pre-collapse" residual states so it stays aligned with the model's real prediction path, and swap a heavy linear compressor for a tiny gated residual reducer (three orders of magnitude fewer parameters) inherited from the model's own hyper-connection head, trained with a distillation loss on the output head. Across math, code and chat it beats both the native baseline and a naive adaptation, accepting longer drafts and decoding faster.
*Why this matters:* keeps the standard fast-inference trick working on the latest frontier-model architecture, where off-the-shelf methods stumble — inference efficiency has to chase architecture changes, and this is that chase in action.

### Beyond the Hard Budget: Sparsity Regularizers for More Interpretable Top-k Sparse Autoencoders
**[arXiv:2606.27321](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27321)** · Nathanaël Jacquier, Maria Vakalopoulou, Mahdi S. Hosseini · `[preprint]`

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are the workhorse of mechanistic interpretability: they decompose a model's tangled internal activations into many simpler features, each ideally meaning one thing (monosemantic). The popular Top-k SAE enforces sparsity by keeping only the *k* most-active features per input — but *k* is fixed regardless of how complex the input is, and the model overfits to the training *k*. The authors add two soft sparsity penalties applied *before* the Top-k cut (an L1 penalty on the unselected units, and a scale-invariant L1/L2-ratio penalty), restricted to units active somewhere in the batch. Across two datasets and three vision foundation models, both penalties improve feature monosemanticity at no cost to reconstruction, and the ratio version makes results robust to whatever *k* you pick at inference. This continues a busy month for SAE design — it is a *distinct* line from the Rational SAE (learnable encoder nonlinearities) the dailies covered on 2026-06-16, but aimed at the same disentanglement goal.
*Why this matters:* it shows the two main SAE philosophies — a hard cutoff vs. a soft penalty — actually combine, yielding cleaner features and removing a brittle hyperparameter from interpretability pipelines.

### From Weights to Features: SAE-Guided Activation Regularization for LLM Continual Learning
**[arXiv:2606.26629](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26629)** · Evan Ning, Wei Xue, Dong Lou et al. · `[preprint]`

To stop a model forgetting old skills when it learns new tasks ("catastrophic forgetting"), the classic fix — Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) — protects the individual weights deemed important to old tasks. The authors argue this fails for LLMs because their weights are *polysemantic*: a single weight participates in many unrelated computations, so per-weight importance is too blunt to isolate what to protect. Instead they regularise in *activation* space, using a pretrained SAE as a dictionary of single-meaning features (showing EWC is a special case of their loss). They build a compact feature mask from current-task data alone — needing no stored examples of past tasks — and on the TRACE and MedCL benchmarks beat weight-space methods. Tellingly, task-relevant information is linearly separable in SAE-feature space but indistinguishable from chance in raw weight space.
*Why this matters:* it reframes catastrophic forgetting as a *feature*-level rather than weight-level problem — and turns interpretability tools from a diagnostic into an active training intervention, a satisfying loop between two normally separate subfields.

### Nemotron-TwoTower: Diffusion Language Modeling with Pretrained Autoregressive Context
**[arXiv:2606.26493](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26493)** · Fitsum Reda, John Kamalu, Roger Waleffe et al. (NVIDIA) · `[preprint]`

Diffusion language models generate text by iteratively refining a noisy block — potentially many tokens in parallel — instead of one token at a time, but existing ones force a single network to both understand the context and do the denoising. TwoTower splits the roles: a *frozen* autoregressive "context tower" reads the clean tokens causally (left-to-right, as a normal LLM does), while a trainable diffusion "denoiser tower" with bidirectional attention refines the noisy block by cross-attending to that context. Built on the open-weight 30B hybrid Mamba-Transformer-MoE Nemotron-3-Nano (Mamba is a state-space model; MoE is mixture-of-experts, where only a subset of the network fires per token) and trained on ~2.1T tokens, it keeps 98.7% of the autoregressive baseline's quality while delivering 2.42× higher generation throughput — with code and weights released.
*Why this matters:* a practical, openly released route to making diffusion-based text generation competitive in quality while running much faster, reusing a pretrained autoregressive model rather than training from scratch.

### AnomNOVIC: Prompt-Free Open-Vocabulary Anomaly Recognition for Robot–Object Interaction
**[arXiv:2606.26829](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26829)** · Philipp Allgeuer, Jan-Gerrit Habekost, Stefan Wermter · `[preprint]`

Robots in the real world constantly meet objects nobody told them about, so they need detectors that work without a predefined list of class names ("open-vocabulary"). AnomNOVIC is a two-stage pipeline: a masked autoencoder trained for anomaly detection proposes generic, object-agnostic bounding boxes around anything salient, then a fast prompt-free classifier (NOVIC) labels those regions with no candidate list required. On a tabletop humanoid-robot setup it reaches 47.1% average precision fully prompt-free (59.0% if class candidates are supplied) and up to 82.6% accuracy on an in-the-wild 48-object test set, beating open-vocabulary baselines like YOLO-World-v2, OWLv2 and YOLOE.
*Why this matters:* spotting and naming genuinely unfamiliar objects on the fly is a prerequisite for open-world robot autonomy — you can't pre-enumerate every object a robot will ever see.

## 🔭 Fundamental science papers of the week

A genuinely loaded week in mathematics — two Erdős problems and a 32-year-old scheduling conjecture — alongside a kagome-magnetism result, two quantum-information papers, and two astrophysics population studies.

### Two Erdős problems claimed in two days
**[arXiv:2606.25849](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25849)** (Problem 1061) and **[arXiv:2606.24872](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24872)** (Problem 768) · Eric Li (sole author, both) · `[preprint]`

Paul Erdős left behind a famous published list of problems that has driven number theory for decades; "resolved" status moves one off the open ledger. Eric Li claims two in two days. **Problem 1061** asks about pairs $(a,b)$ where the sum-of-divisors function satisfies $\sigma(a)+\sigma(b)=\sigma(a+b)$ — $\sigma(n)$ just adds up all divisors of $n$ (e.g. $\sigma(6)=1+2+3+6=12$). Erdős wondered whether the count of such "coincidences" up to $x$ grows merely linearly; Li proves the opposite extreme — it grows faster than $x(\log x)^R$ for *every* fixed $R$ — by reducing the divisor identity to six-prime linear systems on a quadric surface and counting prime points with a higher-dimensional analytic tool. **Problem 768** concerns integers $n$ where every prime $p$ dividing $n$ has a divisor $d>1$ with $d\equiv 1 \pmod p$; Li pins down the *exact* leading constant in the rate at which such integers thin out.
*Context:* the surprise in Problem 1061 is that an additive coincidence between multiplicative quantities, which you'd expect to be rare, is in fact *super-abundant*. **Honest scope:** these are very recent single-author v1 preprints, so they're best read as *claimed* resolutions pending community verification — but two named Erdős problems falling in one week from one author is a notable event.
*Why this matters:* the Erdős list is a public scoreboard for elementary and analytic number theory; clearing two entries at once is the mathematical equivalent of a record-breaking week.

### Proof of the Density-Threshold Conjecture for Pinwheel Scheduling
**[arXiv:2606.27104](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27104)** · Akitoshi Kawamura · `[preprint]`

In pinwheel scheduling, each task $i$ has a period $a_i$ and you must perpetually pick one task per day so that task $i$ runs at least once every $a_i$ days. The obvious necessary condition is that the "density" $\sum 1/a_i \le 1$. In 1993 Chan and Chin conjectured that *any* instance with density $\le 5/6$ is schedulable; Kawamura now proves exactly this. The proof generalises the problem to fractional (non-integer) periods, which collapses an infinite scheduling question into a finite computer search, and throws off two bonuses: a clean proof for two-period instances and a 4/3-approximation for the related "bamboo-garden-trimming" problem.
*Context:* picture one watchman who must wind several clocks, each within its own deadline; the 5/6 density is the precise line for "how full can the schedule be and still always work." That bound was already known to be *tight* (instances just above it are unschedulable); prior work only proved weaker bounds, so this closes the gap — at the cost of computer-assisted case checking.
*Why this matters:* a textbook open problem in real-time scheduling theory (the math behind satellite tasking, sensor polling, maintenance rotations) finally settled, with an elegant relaxation idea.

### NMR Evidence for a Loop-Current State with Broken C₆ Symmetry in Kagome CsV₃Sb₅
**[arXiv:2606.25251](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25251)** · X. Y. Feng, Z. Zhao, J. Dou et al. · `[preprint]`

CsV₃Sb₅ is a "kagome metal" — its vanadium atoms sit on a kagome lattice (a pattern of corner-sharing triangles) that hosts both a charge-density wave (a periodic modulation of electron density) and unconventional superconductivity. Using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and nuclear quadrupole resonance on high-quality single crystals, the team sees the NMR linewidth broaden below ~45 K in a way that *doesn't* depend on applied field — the fingerprint of a static *internal* magnetic field from circulating orbital currents, rather than ordinary spin magnetism. They read this as microscopic evidence for a long-sought "loop-current" state that breaks time-reversal symmetry and further lowers the lattice symmetry from six-fold (C₆) to two-fold (C₂).
*Context:* whether kagome superconductors host genuine loop currents — first proposed decades ago for cuprate superconductors — is a central, contested question; a field-independent local probe is a clean way to argue for one.
*Why this matters:* it constrains theories linking the strange charge order in these materials to their superconductivity, a frontier of condensed-matter physics.

### A 0.651-Approximation to Quantum Max Cut via Rydberg Atoms
**[arXiv:2606.27224](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27224)** · Tomás Crosta, Matthieu Saubanere, Felix Huber · `[preprint]`

Quantum Max Cut is the quantum analogue of the classic graph-partitioning benchmark and is QMA-complete — the quantum counterpart of NP-complete, i.e. a canonical hard problem. The authors build a hybrid algorithm that lets a Rydberg-atom array (neutral atoms excited to high-energy states, a leading quantum-hardware platform) run its natural analog dynamics, then combines that with semidefinite programming and randomized rounding to reach a *conditional* approximation ratio of 0.651, beating the best previous purely-classical ratio of 0.614.
*Context:* this is the quantum cousin of the celebrated Goemans–Williamson 0.878 guarantee for classical Max-Cut — a provable "you'll always get at least this fraction of optimal." The advantage survives even if the Rydberg annealer only reaches 89% of the true ground-state energy, hinting at robustness to real hardware noise. **Honest scope:** it genuinely improves the state-of-the-art ratio, but the bound is conditional and the quantum step is an analog heuristic, not a clean quantum-over-classical separation.
*Why this matters:* a concrete, hardware-anchored case where a near-term quantum platform provably helps on a hard problem.

### Quantum-Limited Subdiffraction Telescopy Requires Genuine Multi-Telescope Interference
**[arXiv:2606.27276](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27276)** · Yujie Zhang, Yunkai Wang, Wilson Wu et al. · `[preprint]`

Stellar interferometry combines light from separate telescopes to resolve detail finer than any single dish — but conventionally it uses only *pairwise* correlations between telescope pairs. The authors prove a no-go: for an $N$-telescope array, pairwise measurements can reach the ultimate quantum precision limit only up to *second-order* image moments (roughly, a source's overall size). Pinning down finer structure at the quantum limit *demands* genuine all-at-once multi-telescope interference. They then construct "array-SPADE" measurements (a generalisation of spatial-mode demultiplexing, which sorts light into spatial patterns) that hit optimal scaling, embeddable in future quantum-network architectures.
*Context:* two telescopes can tell you a star's width; reading its finer shape at the physical limit needs every telescope to interfere together — pairwise correlations leave information on the table. **Honest scope:** a theory result plus a proposed scheme, not a built instrument.
*Why this matters:* it sets the ground rules for any future quantum-enhanced long-baseline telescope, where quantum memories might one day link dishes across continents.

### A Stellar Role Reversal: Structure in Merging Binary-Black-Hole Mass Distributions
**[arXiv:2606.26262](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26262)** · Gina Chen, Katelyn Breivik, Lieke A. C. van Son · `[preprint]`

Using both an analytic framework and binary-population synthesis (simulating how pairs of stars evolve), the authors show how one black-hole formation channel — *stable mass transfer*, where one star donates matter to its companion without a chaotic common-envelope phase — imprints distinctive features on the gravitational-wave black-hole mass distribution. Under conservative mass transfer it splits into two observationally distinct subpopulations: a high-mass, near-equal-mass-ratio group produced by "mass-ratio reversal" (the originally lighter star ends up heavier — the role reversal), and a low-mass non-reversed group. The stability criteria carve complementary regions in the mass–mass-ratio plane, predicting *separated peaks* that current and near-future detectors may resolve.
*Why this matters:* it connects messy individual binary-star physics to concrete, testable bumps in the LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA population data — a way to read formation histories straight off the observed mass function.

### Debiasing the Fast-Radio-Burst Population with the CHIME/FRB Selection Function
**[arXiv:2606.26334](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26334)** · Kyle McGregor, Jason W. T. Hessels, Victoria M. Kaspi et al. · `[preprint]`

Fast radio bursts (FRBs) are millisecond flashes of radio emission from cosmological distances whose origins remain debated. Any telescope's catalogue is distorted by what it can and can't detect, so the team injected 587,367 synthetic bursts into the live CHIME/FRB search pipeline to learn its selection function, then used that to recover the *intrinsic* distributions of burst brightness, scattering timescale, width and dispersion measure (a proxy for the gas the signal traversed). The headline result is the first well-constrained intrinsic scattering-time distribution, showing a slight downturn at long scattering times, cross-checked against the higher-frequency ASKAP survey using simulation-based inference.
*Why this matters:* it's the most carefully de-biased FRB population study to date, and the scattering distribution constrains where these bursts live — bearing on their use as cosmological probes of the universe's diffuse matter.

### Re-dosing AAV gene therapy restores more hearing — despite the antibodies that usually block it
**[Nature Medicine, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04505-4)** · Fan, Zhang, Gao et al.

Four children with inherited deafness from mutations in the *OTOF* (otoferlin) gene, who had each already received one dose of adeno-associated-virus (AAV) gene therapy, were given a *second* dose. The catch the field has long faced: a first AAV dose provokes neutralizing antibodies that are expected to neutralise any second dose. Here re-administration was safe and produced further hearing gains despite those pre-existing antibodies.
*Why this matters:* re-dosing AAV therapies is a barrier across the *entire* gene-therapy field, not just hearing — showing it can work safely in humans has implications well beyond deafness. **Scope:** very small (n=4), single-arm, no control group — a proof of principle, not an efficacy verdict.

### An antibody that clears amyloid already deposited in the heart — sustained over two-plus years
**[Nature Medicine, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04487-3)** · Kahr, aus dem Siepen, Lairez et al.

Cliramitug is a monoclonal antibody (a lab-made immune protein) that targets misfolded transthyretin (TTR), the protein that builds up as amyloid deposits and stiffens the heart in TTR amyloidosis. In this open-label extension, over a median 29.3 months of follow-up the antibody kept reducing cardiac amyloid burden with favourable safety and improvements in structural, functional and biomarker measures.
*Why this matters:* most existing drugs are "silencers" that only stop *new* TTR from being made; an antibody that actively *removes* deposits already in the heart points to reversing damage, not just halting it. **Scope:** open-label extension — no blinding or control arm in this phase.

### Immersive VR plus timed nerve stimulation doubled upper-limb recovery after stroke
**[Nature Medicine, 2026-06-26](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04486-4)** · Aurucci, Djordjevic, Cimolato et al.

A randomized feasibility trial in people with chronic stroke paired immersive virtual reality with a synchronized sensory neurostimulation protocol — nerve stimulation timed to the VR experience. The combined platform outperformed conventional rehabilitation, producing upper-limb motor gains, a restored sense of the affected limb, and better tactile acuity, while logging objective movement data throughout.
*Why this matters:* lasting arm impairment affects a large share of stroke survivors and standard rehab plateaus; pairing VR with precisely-timed stimulation is a promising multimodal route. **Scope:** randomized but explicitly a small, early feasibility study. ([Secondary coverage: NeuroscienceNews, 2026-06-26](https://neurosciencenews.com/).)

### Exercise cuts tau pathology through neuronal Complex I — and a single gene reproduces the effect
**[bioRxiv, 2026-06-22](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.06.16.732673)** · Medina, Quinones, Liu et al. · `[preprint]`

The team built a spatial metabolomic atlas of long-term exercise across six mouse organs, and the brain showed the most region-specific metabolic remodelling. In PS19 mice (a model of tau pathology, the tangled protein implicated in Alzheimer's), exercise cut over 70% of observed hippocampal tau and restored the mitochondrial metabolome — converging on mitochondrial Complex I (the first enzyme of the cell's energy-production chain). Strikingly, expressing the yeast NADH-dehydrogenase gene *Ndi1* in neurons — *without any exercise* — reproduced the core anti-tau and metabolic benefits, implicating Complex I as a *sufficient* mechanism.
*Why this matters:* it offers a concrete molecular route by which exercise may slow tau disease, and a gene/drug target to mimic that benefit. **Scope:** mouse-only preprint, not peer-reviewed.

### A human intracranial map of consciousness returning from anesthesia
**[bioRxiv, 2026-06-22](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.06.17.732807)** · Chen, Zhang, Deng et al. · `[preprint]`

Using rare recordings from electrodes placed *inside* the human brain, the team tracked the transition out of propofol general anesthesia all the way to full wakefulness. They found anesthesia isn't a single signature but an organised low-frequency regime (slow waves, alpha/beta rhythms, broad synchronisation). As the drug wore off this regime dissolved while neural excitability and signal complexity rose, and the actual return of conscious behaviour coincided with a sharp transformation in high-frequency "gamma" activity.
*Why this matters:* it directly probes *how* consciousness re-emerges from an unconscious state — central to both basic neuroscience and to better anesthesia-depth monitoring. **Scope:** preprint, opportunistic clinical recordings.

### Losing smell sharpens vision — and the fly brain shows how
**[bioRxiv, 2026-06-21](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.06.16.732444)** · Coban, Harris, Guer et al. · `[preprint]`

Compromising the sense of smell in both larval and adult fruit flies *improved* their visual associative learning. Combining behaviour, functional imaging and comparative connectomics (wiring-diagram comparison), the authors pinned the mechanism: in larvae, synaptic inputs were structurally reweighted so the learning circuit received a larger share of visual input; in adults, cross-modal inhibition was reduced (disinhibition). Both routes boosted visual responses in the brain's higher-order learning centre.
*Why this matters:* a rare synapse-level account of cross-modal compensation — how losing one sense can measurably sharpen another — in a system where you can actually trace the wiring. **Scope:** invertebrate preprint.

### Hybridisation and herbivory may explain why Amazonia is so tree-diverse
**[bioRxiv, 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.06.17.732868)** · Schley, Twyford, Endara et al. · `[preprint]`

Sequencing 461 genomes from the Amazonian tree genus *Inga*, the authors found regional tree communities form "syngameons" — networks of closely related species linked by ongoing gene flow. Cross-referencing herbivore-abundance data across the tropical Americas, they show that turnover in insect-herbivore communities tracks repeated interspecies transfer of chemical-defence gene clusters, kept in circulation by balancing selection. Hybridisation — long assumed rare and evolutionarily unproductive in rainforest trees — thus lets long-lived trees swap defence genes fast enough to keep pace with rapidly evolving insects.
*Why this matters:* a fresh explanation for the most tree-diverse place on Earth, recasting hybridisation as an *engine* of diversification rather than a dead end. **Scope:** preprint.

### A blood-protein signature in the highest-genetic-risk Alzheimer's group — and a semaglutide hint
**[medRxiv, 2026-06-22](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.02.14.26346321)** · Dammer, Afshar, Bian, Seyfried et al. · `[preprint]`

The plasma proteomes of 413 people carrying two copies of the *APOE* ε4 Alzheimer's-risk allele were compared to 2,764 ε3/ε3 controls across ages 20–90. The ε4/ε4 group showed altered metabolic, GLP-1/IGF, mitochondrial and synaptic protein pathways already in young adulthood — decades before any disease. The GLP-1 receptor agonist semaglutide (the active ingredient in Ozempic/Wegovy) showed apparent reversal of some of these changes at pre-clinical and clinical stages.
*Why this matters:* it points to very early, *blood-detectable* biology in the highest-risk group and a possible prevention rationale for GLP-1 drugs in Alzheimer's. **Scope:** the semaglutide signal is associational and exploratory, not a trial result. `[single-source]`

## 🛡️ Cybersecurity research of the week

A standout week for *AI-targeted* threat research — the first malware family built to manipulate the defender's AI assistant, and a new AI-agent supply-chain angle — alongside solid state-actor and cloud work. (Acronyms: **C2** = command-and-control server; **RCE** = remote code execution; **TTP** = tactics/techniques/procedures; **IAB** = initial-access broker; **MFA** = multi-factor authentication; **KEV** = CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue; **SSRF** = server-side request forgery.)

### macOS.Gaslight — a backdoor that prompt-injects the analyst, not the sandbox — SentinelOne Labs (2026-06-23)
A new Rust macOS implant and infostealer, assessed as North-Korea-aligned, embeds roughly 38 fabricated "system-failure" messages designed to make an AI-assisted triage agent doubt its own session and abandon the analysis. It uses Telegram's Bot API for C2 and AES-GCM-encrypted payloads over certificate-pinned TLS to defeat proxy interception. ([sentinelone.com/labs](https://www.sentinelone.com/labs/macos-gaslight-rust-backdoor-turns-prompt-injection-on-the-analyst-not-the-sandbox/))
*Significance:* one of the first in-the-wild malware families explicitly engineered to defeat *AI-driven* security tooling via prompt injection — the adversary is now targeting the defender's LLM, which is a meaningful shift in the threat model. No CVE; it's a technique, not a single bug.

### STOCKSTAY — Turla builds a redundant backdoor ecosystem against Ukraine — Google Threat Intelligence / Mandiant (2026-06-25)
A multi-component .NET backdoor with WebSocket C2, attributed to Russia's FSB-linked Turla and sharing code with the older KAZUAR framework, delivered via phishing with malicious RDP-config files, fake-app installers and HTA files exploiting a known WinRAR path-traversal flaw (CVE-2025-8088). ([cloud.google.com](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/stockstay-turla-intelligence-gathering))
*Significance:* Google assesses that Turla is deliberately building *parallel, redundant* toolsets so it can retain access even after individual tools get discovered and burned — a state-actor resilience strategy aimed at Ukrainian government and military targets.

### Universal cloud bucket-hijacking across AWS, Azure and Google Cloud — Unit 42 (2026-06-22)
Research abusing the *global uniqueness* of storage-bucket names: an attacker who can delete a target's bucket can recreate one with the same name in their *own* account, silently rerouting the victim's ongoing data and log streams to the attacker. ([unit42.paloaltonetworks.com](https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/cloud-bucket-hijacking-risks/))
*Significance:* a hard-to-detect path to continuous exfiltration of exactly the logs and audit data you'd rely on to notice a breach. No real-world abuse seen yet, and the cloud providers have applied mitigations — but it's an architecture-level lesson about trusting global names.

### Large-scale credential attacks on edge security devices — Unit 42 (2026-06-26)
An active, internet-wide campaign password-spraying Fortinet, Sophos and MSSQL endpoints: spray for a foothold, pull device config files containing stored credentials, crack them offline, then log back in as admin for durable persistence — a self-reinforcing harvesting loop. An initial-access broker on a Russian forum claimed the campaign (unverified). ([unit42.paloaltonetworks.com](https://unit42.paloaltonetworks.com/large-scale-credential-attacks/))
*Significance:* the appliances meant to *protect* the network perimeter are themselves the target, and the loop feeds itself. Mitigations are the usual but unglamorous ones: phishing-resistant MFA, never exposing management interfaces, and rotating credentials.

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. The week's centre of gravity moved from models to the RL training loop — and to the agents it produces.** Almost nothing this week was a new base model; nearly everything was about *training by reward*. Four of the ten papers are RL methods or systems (OPID's dense credit assignment, GEOALIGN's stability filter, RolloutPipe's pipelined throughput, VLM-PBRS's automated reward signal), and the two headline open releases — Ornith-1.0's self-optimising scaffold and Qwen-AgentWorld's environment *simulator* — are both squarely in the agentic-RL frame. Read together they sketch a maturing engineering discipline: the field has stopped treating RL post-training as a dark art and started attacking its concrete failure modes — sparse rewards, jittery updates, idle GPUs, and the absence of a ground-truth answer — one named bottleneck at a time. The frontier this quarter looks less like "bigger model" and more like "better loop."

**2. Interpretability is quietly becoming a *tool*, not just a microscope.** Two SAE papers landed this week, but the more interesting shift is what they're *for*. The Top-k-regularizer work refines the microscope itself; the continual-learning paper uses SAE features to actively *prevent catastrophic forgetting* — turning an analysis instrument into a training intervention. After a month thick with SAE variants (this week's two are distinct from June's Rational SAE), the subfield seems to be crossing from "can we read the features?" to "can we steer training with them?" That's the more consequential question, and the answer is starting to be yes.

**3. AI is now both the weapon and the battlefield in security.** The single most novel security result of the week, macOS.Gaslight, doesn't attack a sandbox or a parser — it attacks the *analyst's AI assistant*, gaslighting an LLM triage agent into giving up. Pair it with Unit 42's malicious-AI-agent-skill research and Willison's two prompt-injection essays, and a coherent picture forms: as AI moves into both offence and defence, prompt injection stops being a chatbot curiosity and becomes a core security primitive — one that, as Willison notes, is simultaneously getting *harder* to pull off at the frontier and *more fundamental* in its underlying cause. Defenders should assume their own AI tooling is now in scope.

**4. "Trust your evaluation" became a theme without anyone planning it.** Apple's "nine judges, two votes" result and Eugene Yan's eval-design patterns arrived independently in the same week and point the same way: the measurement layer is where a lot of current ML quietly goes wrong. Correlated judge errors inflate false confidence; sloppy eval construction can't tell a useful capability from a dangerous one. As more of the stack is itself made of models judging models, the discipline of *evaluating the evaluators* is becoming load-bearing — and this week two serious shops said so out loud.
