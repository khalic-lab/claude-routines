## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

_All papers below were submitted **2026-06-24** (verified via the arXiv Atom API), the day's second window, and exclude the five arXiv IDs already cited in this morning's Overview._

- **`arXiv:2606.26071` (submitted 2026-06-24) — Model Forensics: deciding whether bad behavior means the model is actually misaligned.** Why interesting: most safety work *detects* concerning actions, but a concerning action can come from benign confusion rather than malign intent — so detection alone doesn't prove misalignment. The authors propose a forensic protocol: read the model's chain-of-thought (CoT — its step-by-step "reasoning" text) to form hypotheses about what drove an action, then edit the prompt/environment to test those hypotheses, iterating. They're explicit that CoT isn't always faithful (the text may not reflect the real computation), so it's a lead-generator, not proof. Why it matters: it pushes alignment evaluation from "did it do something scary?" toward "*why* did it do that?" — the question that actually determines whether you can trust a deployment. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26071)
- **`arXiv:2606.26050` (submitted 2026-06-24) — "Natural ungrokking": a model learns a grammar rule, then silently *un*learns it mid-training.** Why interesting: grokking (a model abruptly generalising late in training) is famous; this is the mirror image. A small language model learns the pronoun-gender rule partway through pretraining (resolving "Sue cried because…" → "she", scoring 0.94 on held-out probes by step 925), then by step 3,500 scores near zero on the very same probes — *even though the evidence is still in the training data*, and with no visible bump in the loss curve. The reversal is predictable from one statistic: how often the data stream shows the rule "winning." Why it matters: it's a concrete demonstration that loss going down can hide capabilities being quietly destroyed, and that *what's in the corpus and how often* — not just model size — decides which rules survive. A caution for anyone reading training curves as a health check. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26050)
- **`arXiv:2606.26021` (submitted 2026-06-24) — Tabular foundation models leak their in-context data through attention (AMIA).** Why interesting: tabular foundation models (general-purpose models for spreadsheet-style data) are often assumed privacy-safe because they're pre-trained on *synthetic* data. But they work by in-context learning — you hand them sensitive labelled rows at inference time — and the authors show the attention mechanism leaks enough to mount a Membership Inference Attack (MIA — telling whether a specific record was in the model's context). Their attack, AMIA, needs no shadow models (no expensive surrogate-training step) and exploits how transformer attention concentrates on specific records, beating classical confidence-based attacks. Why it matters: it punctures a "synthetic pre-training = private" assumption right as these models move toward real clinical/financial tables — and ties directly into the membership-inference threat model security teams already track. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26021)
- **`arXiv:2606.26057` (submitted 2026-06-24) — The Unfireable Safety Kernel: putting an agent's guardrails where the agent can't reach them.** Why interesting: today's agent controls — system prompts, output filters, guardrail libraries — all live *inside* the agent's own runtime, which means a clever input (prompt injection) can reach and disable them. The paper names this class "escapable AI systems" and argues real control needs an authorization mechanism with four properties: process separation (runs in a different process), pre-action enforcement on a structurally unavoidable path (the agent *must* go through it to act), fail-closed (deny by default if anything breaks), and externalized signed evidence (a tamper-proof audit trail verifiable from outside). Why it matters: it imports a classic operating-system / reference-monitor security principle into AI-agent design — stop asking the agent to police itself and put the gate somewhere its inputs can't touch. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26057)
- **`arXiv:2606.26002` (submitted 2026-06-24) — HiReLC: hierarchical reinforcement learning that auto-compresses neural networks.** Why interesting: shrinking a model for cheaper deployment means jointly choosing, per layer, how aggressively to *prune* (delete weights) and *quantize* (store them in fewer bits) — a huge combinatorial search usually done by hand or crude heuristics. HiReLC splits the search across two RL levels: low-level agents pick per-block configs (bitwidth, prune-ratio, quantization type), while high-level agents allocate a global compression budget via ensemble voting guided by Fisher-information sensitivity (a measure of which weights matter most to the loss). A surrogate-model loop keeps the RL from being ruinously expensive to evaluate. Why it matters: it's a cleaner, automated lever on the accuracy-vs-size trade-off that governs whether a model fits on a phone or an edge box. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26002)
- **`arXiv:2606.25821` (submitted 2026-06-24) — SARA: making Mixture-of-Experts models share expertise across languages.** Why interesting: sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE — a model that routes each token to a few specialised sub-networks instead of activating the whole thing, buying scale cheaply) tends to route low-resource-language tokens to *different* experts than high-resource ones, so the hard-won capabilities of, say, English don't transfer to under-served languages. SARA (Semantically Anchored Routing Alignment) explicitly aligns the routing so high-resource languages act as "anchors" whose experts get reused for low-resource inputs. Why it matters: it's a targeted fix for a structural fairness/efficiency gap in the MoE architecture now dominant in frontier models — better multilingual coverage without retraining from scratch. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25821)
