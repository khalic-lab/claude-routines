## 📄 ML / AI papers of the week

### LiteResearcher: A Scalable Agentic RL Training Framework for Deep Research Agent
**[arXiv:2604.17931](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.17931)** · Authors TBC (affiliation not confirmed) · `[preprint]`

The fundamental obstacle to training deep research agents with online RL has been environment instability — real-world web search is noisy, non-deterministic, and quickly destabilises training beyond a few hundred steps. LiteResearcher solves this by constructing a lightweight deterministic local environment that mirrors real-world search dynamics, enabling stable training for over 700 RL steps with monotonic improvement curves. The resulting model — LiteResearcher-4B — achieves 71.3% on GAIA and 78.0% on Xbench-DS, matching or outperforming commercial systems including Tongyi DeepResearch (70.9%) and Claude 4.5 Sonnet (71.2%), trained at essentially zero marginal compute cost. The framework also introduces a memory mechanism that summarises prior tool interactions within a 64K context window, combating the context explosion that defeats most multi-step research agents on long browsing chains. The full training framework, data synthesis pipelines, and local environment infrastructure are being open-sourced.

*Why this matters:* A 4B model trained locally on synthetic data matching a frontier commercial research agent is the clearest demonstration yet that deep-research capability is primarily a training methodology problem, not a model-scale problem. The cost-per-query differential has now collapsed to near zero.

### When Errors Can Be Beneficial: A Categorization of Imperfect Rewards for Policy Gradient
**[arXiv:2604.25872](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25872)** · Shuning Shang, Hubert Strauss, Stanley Wei, Sanjeev Arora, Noam Razin et al. · `[preprint]`

This paper reframes the conventional assumption that reward model errors are uniformly harmful in policy gradient training. The authors develop a taxonomy distinguishing harmful errors (which misguide the gradient), benign errors (which have negligible directional effect), and — most strikingly — *beneficial* errors that prevent the policy from stalling around outputs with mediocre ground-truth reward. The key mechanism: certain reward noise smooths the optimization landscape in ways that exact rewards do not, acting as an implicit regulariser that keeps exploration alive. The team also develops new reward model evaluation metrics for RLHF that account for this harm/benefit distinction, and show these metrics correlate better with downstream language model performance post-RLHF than standard ranking accuracy.

*Why this matters:* Has direct practical implications for how reward models are evaluated and filtered in RLHF pipelines — not all reward noise is noise that needs removing.

### Policy Improvement Reinforcement Learning (PIRL)
**[arXiv:2604.00860](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00860)** · Authors TBC · `[preprint]`

PIRL targets reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) as a post-training paradigm for improving LLM reasoning. The core contribution is a framework that maximises *cumulative policy improvement* across training iterations rather than treating each update step as an isolated optimisation. This addresses a well-documented pathology in RLVR training: strong early gains that plateau or reverse in later iterations. By explicitly tracking the improvement trajectory and incorporating it into the objective function, PIRL sustains gains across longer training runs. Evaluated on math and coding reasoning benchmarks, PIRL shows measurable improvement over standard GRPO and PPO post-training baselines.

*Why this matters:* RLVR is now the dominant post-training regime for reasoning models; understanding and fixing the training collapse pattern is operationally important.

### Does RL Expand the Capability Boundary of LLM Agents? A Pass@(k,T) Analysis
**[arXiv:2604.14877](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14877)** · Zhai et al. · `[preprint]`

A methodological contribution to how we evaluate RL-trained agents. The paper introduces Pass@(k,T), a two-dimensional metric that independently varies sampling budget *k* (how many attempts the agent gets) and interaction-depth budget *T* (how deep each attempt goes). This cleanly separates two very different effects of RL training: **capability expansion** (the agent can now solve problems it could not solve with any amount of sampling before training) versus **efficiency improvement** (it solves the same problems more reliably within fewer steps). Applied to several RL-trained LLM agents, the analysis finds the boundary between these effects is blurrier than commonly claimed — many reported capability gains are actually efficiency gains in disguise.

*Why this matters:* Most reported RL-for-agents results conflate capability and efficiency gains; this metric provides a principled way to tell them apart, which matters enormously for understanding when RL actually expands what agents can do vs. making them faster at what they already could.

### AI Safety Training Can be Clinically Harmful
**[arXiv:2604.23445](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23445)** · Suhas BN, Andrew M. Sherrill, Rosa I. Arriaga, Chris W. Wiese, Saeed Abdullah · `[preprint]`

Large language models are being deployed as mental health support agents at scale — yet only 16% of LLM-based chatbot interventions have undergone rigorous clinical efficacy testing, and prior simulation studies have documented psychological deterioration in over a third of cases. This paper tests four models on 250 Prolonged Exposure (PE) therapy scenarios and 146 CBT cognitive restructuring exercises, scored by a three-judge LLM panel. All models scored near-perfectly on surface-level acknowledgment (~0.91–1.00); however, therapeutic appropriateness collapsed to 0.22–0.33 at highest severity for three of four models, with protocol fidelity reaching zero for two. The mechanism is specific: RLHF safety training causes models to interrupt imaginal exposure exercises, insert crisis resources into controlled therapeutic contexts, and refuse to challenge distorted cognitions that mention self-harm — exactly the behaviours that evidence-based PE and CBT protocols require. Safety training is optimised for general harm avoidance, not for the inverted logic of deliberate therapeutic discomfort.

*Why this matters:* Demonstrates a fundamental conflict between "safe for general deployment" and "safe in clinical context." The failure mode is not edge-case jailbreaks — it's core protocol compliance collapsing at the exact moments it matters most. This is a public health concern, not just an AI research curiosity.

### Adaptive Test-Time Compute Allocation for Reasoning LLMs via Constrained Policy Optimization
**[arXiv:2604.14853](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14853)** · Authors TBC · `[preprint]`

Extended chain-of-thought is only economically viable if you can control how much inference compute you actually spend. This paper formalises the test-time compute allocation problem — which inputs deserve more compute under a finite total budget — as a constrained optimisation problem and solves it with Lagrangian relaxation that enables exact budget targeting via binary search. The key advantage over prior soft-penalty approaches: you can specify a hard budget ("this batch can use at most X total tokens") and get a provably optimal allocation across inputs of varying difficulty. Empirically, this outperforms uniform allocation and heuristic difficulty-based allocation across math and coding benchmarks, with the gap widening as budget constraints tighten.

*Why this matters:* Test-time compute scaling only becomes operationally useful when it is controllable. This makes it deployable in real inference infrastructure.

### Mamba-3: Improved Sequence Modeling using State Space Principles
**[arXiv:2603.15569](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15569)** · Aakash Lahoti, Kevin Y. Li, Berlin Chen, Caitlin Wang, Aviv Bick, J. Zico Kolter, Tri Dao, Albert Gu · `[preprint]` _(Published at ICLR 2026)_

State space models (SSMs) like Mamba have long had two hard limitations: failure at state-tracking tasks (parity, arithmetic, modular counting) that Transformers handle well, and hardware-inefficient decoding in practice despite theoretical linear complexity. Mamba-3 addresses both with three principled changes derived from the SSM mathematical framework: trapezoidal discretisation (replacing the Euler method used in Mamba 1–2, giving a more accurate recurrence), a Multi-Input Multi-Output (MIMO) formulation to increase arithmetic intensity and improve GPU utilisation during decoding, and a theoretical connection between complex-valued SSMs and Data-Dependent Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE), bridging the SSM and Transformer worlds formally. The result: Mamba-3 matches Transformers on standard state-tracking benchmarks (parity, arithmetic) for the first time, while retaining SSM inference-time memory advantages. Presented at ICLR 2026.

*Why this matters:* The inability to do arithmetic was the principal objection to deploying SSMs in production reasoning workloads. Mamba-3 removes that objection.

### Attention to Mamba: A Recipe for Cross-Architecture Distillation
**[arXiv:2604.14191](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14191)** · Abhinav Moudgil, Ningyuan Huang, Eeshan Gunesh Dhekane, Pau Rodríguez, Luca Zappella, Federico Danieli · `[preprint]`

The SSM research community wants to benefit from the enormous investment in pretrained Transformer weights — but direct Transformer-to-Mamba distillation is a very large representational jump. This paper proposes a two-stage recipe: first distil the Transformer into a *linearised attention* variant (which shares mathematical structure with SSMs), then distil that intermediate model into a Mamba architecture. The intermediate linearised attention step acts as a representational bridge, making the final SSM distillation tractable. Evaluated across several model scales, the two-stage recipe consistently outperforms direct distillation, and is competitive with training SSMs from scratch while using a fraction of the compute.

*Why this matters:* Opens a realistic path to Mamba-class inference efficiency without discarding the years of pretraining investment already embodied in existing Transformer checkpoints.

### Efficient Mixture-of-Experts LLM Inference with Apple Silicon NPUs
**[arXiv:2604.18788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18788)** · Authors TBC · `[preprint]`

MoE models have an awkward hardware property: expert routing creates variable per-token compute paths, which is unfriendly to batched GPU execution. This paper proposes a framework to execute MoE inference on Apple's Neural Engine (ANE), the NPU in Apple Silicon chips, whose memory architecture is better suited to routing irregularity than a standard GPU. Results: 1.32x–5.55x reduction in prefill latency, 1.81x–7.37x reduction in energy consumption, and 1.78x–5.54x reduction in CPU-cycle usage versus CPU/GPU execution baselines, with negligible accuracy loss. The framework works on currently shipping Apple Silicon generations (M3, M4, M4 Max).

*Why this matters:* MoE is now the dominant architecture for capable open-weight models (Gemma 4, Qwen 3.5, GLM-5.1, Llama 4 Scout). Efficient ANE execution makes these models viable for on-device inference at full quality.

### Text-Guided Multimodal Unified Industrial Anomaly Detection
**[arXiv:2604.22899](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.22899)** · Zewen Li, Shuo Ye, Zitong Yu, Weicheng Xie, Linlin Shen · `[preprint]`

Industrial anomaly detection typically fuses RGB images with 3D point-cloud data, but existing unsupervised methods suffer from ambiguous cross-modal alignment caused by missing high-level semantic guidance and insufficient geometric modelling for RGB-to-3D feature mapping. This paper introduces a unified framework that addresses both gaps: a Geometry-Aware Cross-Modal Mapper handles the RGB-to-3D geometric alignment, while an Object-Conditioned Textual Feature Adaptor injects object-class text semantics to guide cross-modal fusion. The method is fully unsupervised and achieves state-of-the-art on both MVTec 3D-AD and Eyecandies benchmarks.

*Why this matters:* Industrial inspection and quality control are high-value deployment targets for vision AI; fusing RGB + depth + text is the direction the field is heading, and this represents a clean architecture for doing it.

### The Mathematics of Rejecting Infinity
_[Quanta Magazine, Gregory Barber, 2026-04-29](https://www.quantamagazine.org/what-can-we-gain-by-losing-infinity-20260429/)_ · T2

Ultrafinitism — the mathematical philosophy that rejects the actually infinite, claiming numbers must terminate at some physical bound — has long been treated as fringe. This Quanta feature covers a conference convened at Columbia University bringing together physicists, logicians, mathematicians, and philosophers to seriously reconsider the foundations. Mathematician Doron Zeilberger's program holds that standard set theory's axiom of infinity is a contingent choice, not a necessity, and that finite replacements can reproduce all the mathematical results that matter in practice. The surprising take-away for working mathematicians: ultrafinite methods are generating new results in combinatorics and complexity theory even for researchers who don't accept the philosophical program.

*Context:* Set theory has treated the infinite as foundational since Cantor (1870s); ultrafinitism asks whether this assumption is load-bearing or simply habitual. This article does not announce a breakthrough — it is a state-of-the-field feature on a movement gaining real traction in foundations circles.

*Worth reading in full:* Yes — accessible to a technical but non-specialist reader, and the stakes are explained without dumbing down.

### A Treasure Trove of Cambrian Fossils Rewrites the Story of Early Life
_[Quanta Magazine, 2026-05-01](https://www.quantamagazine.org/a-treasure-trove-of-cambrian-fossils-rewrites-the-story-of-early-life-20260501/)_ · T2 · Underlying peer-reviewed paper in review `[single-source primary]`

Palaeontologists in Hunan Province, southern China, have unearthed what is being called the Huayuan biota: 8,681 specimens spanning 153 species, making it one of the best-preserved Cambrian Lagerstätten (exceptional preservation sites) ever discovered — comparable in quality to the Burgess Shale. Half of the species are new to science. A striking finding is how closely many specimens resemble Burgess Shale fauna (~12,000 km distant, separated by multiple ocean basins), implying that Cambrian marine ecosystems were globally connected by ocean currents far earlier and more completely than previously understood. Soft-tissue preservation is sufficient for direct morphological comparison across sites.

*Context:* The Cambrian explosion (~541–485 Ma) produced most animal body plans; each major new Lagerstätte rewrites our picture of which body plans appeared when and how early life dispersed globally.

### Physicists Discover the Most Complex Forms of Ice Yet
_[Quanta Magazine, Shalma Wegsman, 2026-04-27](https://www.quantamagazine.org/)_ · T2 `[primary paper pending confirmation — single-source]`

New simulations combined with experimental work have detected ice phases of structural complexity well beyond anything previously catalogued — far more intricate than the roughly 20 ice polymorphs known before this work. The study suggests many more forms exist and have simply never been looked for systematically. The significance is partly crystallographic (water's phase diagram is far richer than expected) and partly methodological (detection required novel simulation-experimental pipelines that can now be applied to other molecular systems). Relevant to planetary science: exotic ice phases are predicted to exist in the mantles of ice-giant planets and icy moons.

*Context:* Water's phase diagram has been studied intensely for a century; the finding that many forms were missed implies the search methodology was the binding constraint, not the physics.

### A New Type of Neuroplasticity Rewires the Brain After a Single Experience
_[Quanta Magazine, Yasemin Saplakoglu, 2026-04-24](https://www.quantamagazine.org/a-new-type-of-neuroplasticity-rewires-the-brain-after-a-single-experience-20260424/)_ · T2 · Underlying Nature paper `[single-source secondary]`

Researchers describe an extended form of behavioural timescale synaptic plasticity (BTSP) — a form of plasticity operating over seconds-long timescales rather than the millisecond window required by classical Hebbian spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP). The new work shows a single behavioural experience can remodel synaptic weights durably and broadly across extended neural circuits, happening faster and more broadly than any previously characterised form of long-term potentiation. The mechanism appears to rely on dendritic calcium spikes as the instructive signal, allowing information from a whole episode of experience to be integrated rather than just spike-to-spike coincidences.

*Context:* Standard LTP (Hebbian, requiring repeated millisecond-scale stimulation) has dominated synaptic plasticity theory for decades. BTSP provides a mechanistic bridge between single events and lasting memories — operationally, it is how one-shot learning could work at the circuit level.

*Note:* Publication date of April 24 is one day before the strict coverage window; the Quanta article fell within it.

### NeuroAI and Beyond: Bridging Advances in Neuroscience and Artificial Intelligence
**[arXiv:2604.18637](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18637)** · Anthony Zador et al. (31 authors) · `[preprint]`

Based on an NSF workshop convened August 2025, this 31-author roadmap identifies three fundamental capability gaps in current AI that neuroscience can address: inability to interact with the physical world; brittle learning requiring massive data; and unsustainable energy and data requirements. Against each gap it maps a corresponding set of neuroscience principles: co-design of body and controller for physical interaction; multi-scale learning with neuromodulatory control for data efficiency; and sparse event-driven computation for energy efficiency. The roadmap is organised at near, mid, and long-term horizons, and argues that realising the programme requires new training regimes for researchers that span both fields, plus dedicated hardware infrastructure.

*Why this matters:* NeuroAI has been discussed for a decade largely at the level of analogies. This is the first coordinated multi-institutional roadmap with specific near-term technical proposals rather than high-level aspirations.

## 🍎 Apple Silicon / local inference ecosystem

### llama.cpp: Significant April releases

Three technical additions this month are each worth noting separately.

**Hadamard rotation for KV cache quantisation.** Walsh-Hadamard matrix rotation applied to Q/K/V activations before KV cache quantisation, with inverse rotation applied at retrieval. By spreading information more evenly across activation dimensions before quantising, the approach makes Q4_0 quantisation viable for the KV cache — previously too lossy for production use. Practical result: 4× reduction in KV cache VRAM versus FP16. For a 70B model on a 32GB M-series machine this meaningfully extends what fits in memory.

**Q1_0: 1-bit quantisation format** (build b8682, April 6). A new format targeting models trained explicitly for binary weights, such as the "Bonsai" model family. 1-bit weights with standard activations; not a general-purpose quantisation path, but relevant for the emerging binary-weight model ecosystem.

**Gemma 4 day-one support.** Vision and MoE architecture support was in llama.cpp on the day Gemma 4 launched (April 2), with chat template and tool-call parser improvements throughout the month.

→ [Fazm blog, llama.cpp April 2026](https://fazm.ai/blog/llama-cpp-release-april-2026)

### MLX / Ollama: The MLX backend shift

**mlx-lm 0.31.3** released April 22, 2026 (most recent in window). The bigger ecosystem shift is Ollama 0.19 (March 31), which adopted MLX as the default backend for Apple Silicon Macs with 32GB+ unified memory, replacing the Metal/llama.cpp path. Reported speed impact: Qwen 3.5 35B-A3B runs at ~70–80 tok/s on M4 Max 32GB under MLX, vs ~45 tok/s under llama.cpp Metal — a ~65% improvement. Ollama 0.20.4 added a specific M5 performance optimisation (MLX NAX integration in the changelog).

MLX's advantage is architectural: unified memory means tensors live in a single address space shared by CPU and GPU — no copies, no staging buffers — which translates directly to tokens per second for memory-bandwidth-bound LLM workloads.

## 🛡️ Cybersecurity research of the week

### CVE-2026-31431 "Copy Fail" — Nine-Year-Old Linux Kernel Flaw Grants Deterministic Local Root

Disclosed April 29–30 by Xint Code and confirmed within 24 hours by Microsoft Security, CERT-EU, AlmaLinux, Sysdig, and The Register, Copy Fail is a logic flaw in the `authencesn` cryptographic template within the `AF_ALG` userspace crypto socket interface — the kernel's AEAD socket abstraction.

**Mechanism:** The bug allows any unprivileged local user to write 4 controlled bytes into the page cache of any readable file on the system. A 732-byte Python exploit uses this to overwrite a setuid binary and escalate to root. Unlike Dirty Cow and Dirty Pipe, there is no race condition to win — the exploit is deterministic and works without modification across every major Linux distribution running kernels shipped since 2017 (Ubuntu 20.04+, RHEL, SUSE, Amazon Linux 2023, Debian, Fedora, Arch).

**CVSS:** 7.8 (High). Local code execution required — this is not remotely exploitable by itself.

**Why this matters operationally:** Copy Fail is the second step in a two-step chain, not a standalone RCE. It chains trivially with: web RCE landing in an unprivileged service account, malicious CI/CD pipeline contributions (the Lightning supply chain attack this week is exactly this path), SSH footholds, or container escapes. In Kubernetes and cloud environments where tenant isolation relies on Linux namespaces rather than hardware boundaries, a single compromised container can become root on the host node via this chain.

**Mitigation until kernel patches ship:**
```
echo "install algif_aead /bin/false" > /etc/modprobe.d/disable-algif.conf
rmmod algif_aead 2>/dev/null || true
```
CERT-EU recommends prioritising Kubernetes nodes and CI/CD runners.

→ [Microsoft Security Blog, 2026-05-01](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/01/cve-2026-31431-copy-fail-vulnerability-enables-linux-root-privilege-escalation/); [CERT-EU Advisory 2026-005](https://cert.europa.eu/publications/security-advisories/2026-005/); [Help Net Security, 2026-04-30](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/30/copyfail-linux-lpe-vulnerability-cve-2026-31431/); [The Register, 2026-04-30](https://www.theregister.com/2026/04/30/linux_cryptographic_code_flaw/); [Sysdig](https://www.sysdig.com/blog/cve-2026-31431-copy-fail-linux-kernel-flaw-lets-local-users-gain-root-in-seconds)

## 🧠 Cross-cutting threads

**1. RL is becoming a general-purpose optimisation tool — five independent examples this week.**

This week's ML content shows RL applied across: training deep research agents (LiteResearcher), post-training reasoning improvement (PIRL), reward model design theory (When Errors Can Be Beneficial), inference-time compute allocation (arXiv:2604.14853), and capability evaluation methodology (Pass@(k,T)). These are not the same type of RL — they span GRPO, constrained policy optimisation, verifiable reward fine-tuning, and offline analysis — but together they signal a broader shift: RL has escaped the "alignment tax" framing and is now applied wherever there is a sequential decision process with an evaluable outcome signal. The open question for 2026: whether this scatter of techniques converges into a coherent general methodology, or whether each application domain requires its own regime.

→ See: [arXiv:2604.17931](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.17931), [arXiv:2604.00860](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00860), [arXiv:2604.25872](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25872), [arXiv:2604.14853](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14853), [arXiv:2604.14877](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14877)

**2. The "logging in" threat model is now dominant — and Copy Fail closes the final gap.**

Cloudflare's annual report argues that the new attacker paradigm is credential-based access, not perimeter exploitation. CVE-2026-31431 (Copy Fail) this week illustrates exactly what comes next in that chain: once an attacker is logged in as an unprivileged user — which is now the *assumed starting point* in many threat models — a deterministic, no-race kernel LPE closes the loop to root. The threat chain is: phishing/supply chain (Lightning on PyPI this week) → unprivileged shell → Copy Fail → root → lateral movement or container escape. All three links were operational simultaneously this week.

→ See: [CVE-2026-31431, CERT-EU](https://cert.europa.eu/publications/security-advisories/2026-005/); [Cloudflare 2026 Threat Report](https://blog.cloudflare.com/2026-threat-report/); [Hornetsecurity April 2026](https://www.hornetsecurity.com/en/blog/monthly-threat-report/)

**3. Architecture convergence: Transformers → SSMs, SSMs fixing state-tracking, MoE everywhere.**

Three independent papers this week represent different aspects of architectural consolidation. Mamba-3 (ICLR 2026) adds state-tracking capability previously exclusive to Transformers. "Attention to Mamba" creates a principled two-stage distillation path from Transformer weights to SSM architecture. And every major open-weight model released this month (Gemma 4, GLM-5.1, Qwen 3.5, Llama 4 Scout) uses MoE as its efficiency mechanism. The architecture wars of 2023–2025 are resolving toward a pragmatic synthesis: MoE for training efficiency, SSM-style recurrence for inference efficiency, with knowledge distillation as the bridge between existing Transformer checkpoints and new architectures.

→ See: [arXiv:2603.15569](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.15569), [arXiv:2604.14191](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14191), [arXiv:2604.18788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.18788)

**4. The general/specialist alignment gap is sharper than the safety discourse acknowledges.**

arXiv:2604.23445 is the clearest example yet of a pattern that will recur in every professional domain AI enters: safety training optimised for the general population is *specifically wrong* for expert contexts with inverted norms. In clinical therapy, the "safe" behaviour (interrupt distressing content, insert crisis resources, refuse self-harm mention) is exactly what destroys therapeutic efficacy. This generalises: in legal practice, adversarial framing is required; in security research, discussing attack techniques is the job; in certain educational contexts, allowing a student to struggle without intervention is pedagogically essential. The current safety-alignment paradigm has no principled solution to this — it requires domain-specific fine-tuning, which in turn requires clinical/legal/security expertise in the training pipeline, which most AI labs do not have.

→ See: [arXiv:2604.23445](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23445)
