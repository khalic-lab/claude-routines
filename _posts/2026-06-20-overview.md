- **A new class of "gamma-loud" protostars: baby stars caught accelerating cosmic-ray protons.** Protostars are stars still forming inside cold gas clouds, and they were long assumed to glow only with ordinary heat. A statistical study of young stellar objects (the earliest stage of a star, before nuclear fusion fully ignites) instead finds significant gamma-ray emission — the highest-energy light there is — from a population of them. The proposed mechanism: jets from the protostar accelerate protons that smash into surrounding cloud gas and produce gamma rays via *pion decay* (a pion is a short-lived particle; its decay leaves a tell-tale gamma signature of proton, not just electron, acceleration). The cosmic-ray output scales with the star's brightness. Why it matters: it identifies a previously unrecognized Galactic source of high-energy particles, and direct evidence of *proton* (not just electron) acceleration in such young systems has been elusive until now. [Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-18](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02886-7)

- **How fast Antarctica loses ice in 2025 largely locks in sea-level rise to mid-century — regardless of emissions.** Across every ice-sheet model used in the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report (the UN climate science panel), the *current* rate of Antarctic ice loss strongly predicts the rate for the next several decades, independent of which emissions path the world takes. The practical upshot: models that nail today's observed ice loss give a reliable basis for near-term coastal planning, and near-term Antarctic sea-level contribution is already substantially "baked in." The predictability breaks down by the end of the century, when slower feedbacks take over. Why it matters: it sharpens the distinction between what's already committed (plan for it now) and what's still steerable by cutting emissions. [Nature, 2026-06-17](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10614-4)

- **Why a water droplet's edge tension flips sign at the nanoscale — traced to collapsing molecular order.** *Line tension* is the energy stored along the one-dimensional rim where a droplet, the surface it sits on, and the surrounding air all meet; at nanometre scales it governs wetting, but its microscopic origin — and a puzzling sign reversal near "complete wetting" (when a droplet spreads into a flat film) — has been unclear. Molecular-dynamics simulations (tracking every water molecule's motion) show the culprit is the breakdown of water's *tetrahedral order* (the cage-like four-neighbour arrangement that gives water its quirks) right at the contact line on water-loving surfaces. Why it matters: it gives a concrete, structural handle on capillary behaviour at the scale of coatings, microfluidic chips, and biological membranes, where line tension stops being a footnote and starts dominating. [Nature Physics, 2026-06-19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03299-z)

- **"Seven shuffles" extended: the deck-randomizing threshold survives messy, real-world shuffles.** A 1992 result (Bayer–Diaconis) proved that seven *riffle* shuffles suffice to randomize a 52-card deck, and that the deck stays orderly until it abruptly "tips" into randomness — a *cutoff phenomenon* (a sharp transition from ordered to mixed, akin to water suddenly freezing). But the proof needed an idealized, near-perfect cut of the deck. Three mathematicians (Mark Sellke, Jialu Shi, Jiamin Wang) now prove the cutoff still holds for *uneven* cuts — uniformly random or exact-bisection — not just the binomial cuts earlier work required, removing the main technical crutch. Why it matters: cutoff shows up far beyond cards — in spin glasses (disordered magnets) and other systems that suddenly reorganize — so widening *which* shuffles provably cut off makes the underlying universality argument far more robust. [arXiv:2510.22783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.22783) `[preprint]` · secondary: [Quanta, 2026-06-17](https://www.quantamagazine.org/seven-perfect-shuffles-randomize-a-deck-of-cards-but-how-many-sloppy-ones-20260617/)

- **The brain may reuse one network architecture for two textbook-distinct kinds of motor learning.** Motor neuroscience has long split learning into *error-based* (correcting from a directional mistake, e.g. a reach that landed left of target) and *reinforcement-based* (adjusting from reward, with no directional hint) — assumed to run on separate machinery. Scanning the same people doing both tasks (fMRI — functional MRI, which tracks blood-flow proxies for neural activity) and embedding their whole-brain connectivity into a shared low-dimensional space, the authors isolate task-*general* from task-*specific* reconfiguration — and find a common architecture under both. Why it matters: if one substrate supports both, models that treat them as wholly separate may be over-drawing the line. Preliminary — small fMRI cohort, correlational. [bioRxiv, 2026-06-18](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733155v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **The APOE4 Alzheimer's risk gene pushes human microglia into a broken, half-activated state.** *APOE4* is the strongest common genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's, but how it reshapes *microglia* (the brain's resident immune cells, which clear debris and plaques) in actual human tissue has been murky. Combining spatial proteomics (mapping where proteins sit in intact tissue) with single-nucleus multi-omics (reading many molecular layers per cell), the authors find APOE4/4 shifts microglia toward terminal states that lose their healthy "housekeeping" identity, suffer metabolic disruption, and only partially adopt the protective disease-response program — while signalling inflammation without doing the cleanup. Why it matters: it points to a specific cellular failure mode — inflamed but ineffective microglia — as a mechanistic link between APOE4 and disease, a candidate to target. Preliminary; human post-mortem tissue, single study. [bioRxiv, 2026-06-18](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733295v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **arXiv:2606.19538 · submitted 2026-06-17** `[preprint]` — **ITNet: one "learnable integral transform" that contains CNNs, attention, and RNNs as special cases.** Convolutions (locality), recurrence/state-space models (sequential memory), and self-attention (content-based pairwise mixing) have always been treated as mathematically separate designs. ITNet argues they're all incomplete views of a single object — a *learnable integral transform*, i.e. a kernel (a learned function scoring how much any two positions should influence each other) implemented as a small neural net depending jointly on position and features. The paper shows convolution, multi-head self-attention, and autoregressive recurrence (LSTM, GRU, S4, Mamba — the recent state-space sequence models) all fall out as parameter choices, and that the result is a universal approximator of continuous operators. Why interesting: a unifying lens like this can turn "which architecture?" into "which point in one continuous design space?", and hints at hybrids that interpolate between the families. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19538)

- **arXiv:2606.19771 · submitted 2026-06-18** `[preprint]` — **Fixing the "entropy collapse" failure mode of RL-for-reasoning by editing only the pivotal tokens.** RLVR (reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards — training on signals a checker confirms, like a correct math answer, rather than human ratings) destabilizes in a characteristic way: update every token uniformly and the model's output distribution sharpens too fast (*entropy collapse* — it locks onto one mediocre strategy and stops exploring); push exploration too hard and it rambles incoherently. The ICT (Independent Combinatorial Tokens) method stops looking at a single scalar uncertainty and instead uses the Jensen–Shannon divergence (a symmetric measure of how different two probability distributions are) between token-logit distributions to flag the genuine *branching-point* tokens — the forks in a reasoning chain — and updates selectively there. Why interesting: training stability is one of the main things standing between RLVR demos and reliable reasoning models, and targeting the decision-relevant tokens is a clean, theoretically motivated lever. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19771)

- **arXiv:2606.20381 · submitted 2026-06-18** `[preprint]` — **Why 4-bit LLM training quietly drifts low — and a uniform number format that fixes it.** FP4 (4-bit floating point — squeezing each weight into 4 bits to slash memory and compute) is the frontier for cheap pretraining on Blackwell/Rubin-class NVIDIA and AMD MI350 GPUs, but the standard E2M1 format has unevenly spaced representable values. The paper identifies a resulting *shrinkage bias*: rounding systematically nudges values toward zero, an error that compounds layer over layer and is amplified by a common preconditioning trick (the Random Hadamard Transform). Their fix, UFP4, uses *uniform* 4-bit grids (evenly spaced, like INT4) so the error cancels instead of accumulating, plus a tuned recipe. Why interesting: stable FP4 pretraining would roughly halve the memory and bandwidth bill versus 8-bit — a direct cost lever for training large models — and this pinpoints a concrete reason past FP4 recipes were flaky. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20381)

- **arXiv:2606.19475 · submitted 2026-06-17** `[preprint]` — **A like-for-like bake-off of diffusion language models, the main rival to next-token generation.** Most LLMs generate left-to-right, one token at a time (*autoregression*). Diffusion language models (DLMs) instead start from noise and *iteratively denoise* a whole sequence in parallel — promising speed and global editing, but reported under wildly inconsistent settings that make comparison meaningless. This evaluates eight state-of-the-art DLMs across eight benchmarks (reasoning, coding, translation, knowledge, structured problems), holding the protocol fixed and explicitly trading off quality against compute, including how the number of denoising steps changes the picture. Why interesting: it's a sober, apples-to-apples map of where the non-autoregressive paradigm actually stands. (Distinct from — but on the same theme as — the earlier "Diffusion-Proof" diffusion-LLM theorem-proving work; this is a separate paper, a broad empirical survey rather than a single system.) [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19475)

- **arXiv:2606.19741 · submitted 2026-06-18** `[preprint]` — **Making black-box combinatorial-optimization solvers explain themselves as readable programs.** Neural combinatorial optimization (NCO) trains networks to solve problems like routing or scheduling, often beating hand-built heuristics — but as an opaque black box, which blocks both deployment trust and scientific insight. Standard interpretability tools (e.g. concept bottlenecks) don't fit because NCO decisions are dynamic and state-dependent. EPB (Evolving Programmatic Bottlenecks) uses an LLM to *evolve a portfolio of small human-readable programs*, each predicting the solver's next-step action distribution, so the bottleneck the network must explain its decisions through is literal code you can read. Why interesting: distilling a learned policy into inspectable programs is a promising route to interpretable *and* auditable optimization, where "why did it pick this move?" has an answer. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19741)

- **arXiv:2606.19808 · submitted 2026-06-18** `[preprint]` — **When is extra "thinking" at inference worth the compute? A controller that decides per query.** Test-time reasoning (letting a model deliberate longer before answering) is increasingly a deployment knob, but more thinking isn't uniformly good — it can fix a wrong answer, waste compute on an already-correct one, or even flip a right answer to wrong. SEVRA frames this as an *allocation* problem: a serving-layer gate decides whether to keep a frozen solver's first answer or invoke verification, trained on which attempts are recoverable. On a math benchmark it reaches 76.3% accuracy versus 75.5% for always-verifying, while cutting post-generation tokens ~27% and halving harmful answer-flips (2.2%→1.0%). Honest caveat the authors flag: simply giving a bigger initial solve budget matched accuracy with fewer total tokens — so selective verification helps but isn't the cheapest frontier they found. Why interesting: routing compute by expected payoff is how test-time reasoning becomes economical at scale, and the negative result is reported plainly. [abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19808)
