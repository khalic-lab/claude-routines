## 🔬 Science

- **Soft-bodied fossils help close the long gap in the origin of segmented animals.** Body segmentation — the repeated-block body plan shared by insects, worms and vertebrates — is thought to have arisen in the Ediacaran (the period just before the Cambrian explosion ~539 million years ago), but unambiguous fossils of segmented animals don't show up until well into the Cambrian, leaving a frustrating "fossil gap." The reason is taphonomic bias: soft, non-mineralized micro-animals rarely fossilize. A new preprint reports a variety of segmented bilaterians from the lowermost Cambrian of South China, preserved not by the usual phosphate route but as *microbial pseudomorphs* (mineral casts templated by microbial mats), identifying that pathway as a major way these delicate bodies survived. Why it matters: it pushes physical evidence for segmented animals down into the basal Cambrian, narrowing the gap between when molecular clocks say segmentation evolved and when we can actually see it. [bioRxiv preprint, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733126v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **A systemic viral-vector vaccine drives immune control of glioblastoma in mice.** Glioblastoma — the most aggressive primary brain cancer — has resisted immunotherapy, including checkpoint blockade (drugs that release the brakes on T cells; ineffective here, suggesting the problem is getting T cells *started*, not unleashing them). Researchers used a heterologous prime-boost (two different vaccine vectors given in sequence to amplify the response): the simian adenovirus ChAdOx1 — the same platform behind the Oxford/AstraZeneca COVID vaccine — followed by a poxvirus (MVA) boost, given systemically rather than into the brain. In a checkpoint-refractory mouse model it generated brain-resident memory T cells (long-lived immune cells that take up station in tissue) and conferred therapeutic benefit against two tumor antigens; adding checkpoint blockade gave no extra benefit. Why it matters: a hint that the bottleneck in brain-tumor immunity is priming, and that an off-the-shelf systemic vaccine platform might address it — but this is a mouse study, years from patients. [bioRxiv preprint, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733241v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **A network analysis explains why "aging clocks" disagree with each other.** Epigenetic clocks estimate biological age (how worn-down your body is, versus calendar age) from DNA methylation — chemical tags on DNA at cytosine-guanine (CpG) sites. They predict age within a few years and track mortality, but different clocks share almost no CpG sites and often break when you switch measurement platforms — an awkward fact for a field hoping to use them as health readouts. Building a co-methylation network (grouping CpGs that change together) across 12 public human-blood datasets, the authors find the age signal concentrates in a few large, biologically interpretable modules surrounded by many weak singletons — so any one regression can grab a different, sparse subset and still score well. Why it matters: it reframes clock instability as a sampling artifact of correlated signals rather than a contradiction, pointing toward more transferable, mechanism-anchored aging measures. [bioRxiv preprint, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733218v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **ALS-linked NEK1 mutations are shown to cripple the kinase's self-activation.** Rare variants in *NEK1* are among the most consistently implicated genetic contributors to amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS, the fatal motor-neuron disease), found in ~2–3% of cases. Most work has studied what happens when NEK1 is simply *lost*, but many disease variants are missense (a single amino-acid swap that yields an altered protein, not just less of it). Using phosphoproteomics — mass-spectrometry mapping of which sites a protein chemically tags — the authors pin down NEK1's autophosphorylation sites (the spots it uses to switch *itself* on) and use one of them as a direct activity readout, showing ALS-associated missense variants disrupt that self-activation. Why it matters: it gives a concrete, measurable handle on how specific ALS mutations break a key enzyme, and a candidate biomarker for sorting damaging variants from benign ones. [bioRxiv preprint, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.17.733013v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **The ribosome turns out to wear an overlooked "coat" of disordered protein tails.** The ribosome — the molecular machine that builds proteins — is one of the most-imaged structures in biology, but its component proteins have floppy terminal tails that "average out" to invisibility in cryo-EM and X-ray maps (the imaging methods rely on rigid, repeating structure; flexible bits blur away). Analyzing 36 ribosomes across bacteria, eukaryotes and mitochondria, the authors argue these flexible protein fragments collectively form a systematically ignored surface coat — most extensive on mitoribosomes (the ribosomes inside mitochondria, the cell's power plants) — and that the coat's arginine-vs-lysine chemistry tracks the lipid composition of the membrane each ribosome sits near. Why it matters: a reminder that "solved" structures can hide functional real estate, with implications for how ribosomes interface with membranes and condensates. [bioRxiv preprint, posted 2026-06-20](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733196v1) `[preprint]` `[single-source]`

- **Returned asteroid samples sharpen the case that small bodies brewed complex organics.** Material brought back from asteroids Bennu (by NASA's OSIRIS-REx) and Ryugu (by Japan's Hayabusa2) contains a rich organic inventory — from small molecules up to complex *macromolecular* (large, polymer-like) carbon. A Nature Astronomy Brief Communication argues these polymeric organics reframe how prebiotic chemistry (the chemistry that precedes life) could assemble on asteroids and be delivered to early planets. Why it matters: pristine, lab-returned samples — free of Earthly contamination that plagues meteorite studies — strengthen the link between asteroid chemistry and the raw materials for life. (Brief Communication = a short peer-reviewed report, lighter than a full research article.) [Nature Astronomy, 2026-06-19](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02877-8)
