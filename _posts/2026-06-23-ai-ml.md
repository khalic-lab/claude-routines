## 🏢 Lab blogs & official releases

- **Anthropic launches Claude Tag — tag `@Claude` as a shared teammate inside Slack.** [vendor PR]
  The product lets a team add Claude to selected Slack channels, wire it to chosen tools, data and
  codebases, and then `@Claude` it to delegate a task while everyone keeps working. The framing
  Anthropic leans on is "multiplayer": within a channel there's **one** Claude that everyone shares,
  so anyone can see what it's doing and pick up where a colleague left off — closer to a teammate
  than to a private chat session. It carries context forward by remembering relevant information
  from the channels it sits in, and can plan tasks to run later. Anthropic positions it as an
  evolution of Claude Code (its coding agent) and of "Cowork," made more proactive and team-shaped.
  The eyebrow-raising claim: Anthropic says **65% of its own product team's code is now written by
  an internal version of this tool**, and that staff also tag it to chase product metrics, work
  support tickets, and root-cause bugs. *Why it matters:* this is the "agent that lives where you
  already work, with shared memory" pattern — the interesting bit isn't another chatbot in Slack but
  the *multiplayer* design (one persistent agent per channel rather than one per user) and the bet
  that delegation, not conversation, is how teams will actually use these models. It's beta-only for
  Claude Enterprise and Team customers today; treat the 65%-of-code figure as a self-reported
  internal stat. ([Anthropic, 23 Jun 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/news/introducing-claude-tag))

- **OpenAI publishes a case study claiming GPT-5 cracked a three-year immunology puzzle.** [vendor
  PR] [single-source] In a post built around immunologist Derya Unutmaz (Jackson Laboratory for
  Genomic Medicine), OpenAI says GPT-5, shown an unpublished chart, identified within minutes a
  mechanism his team had missed despite deep expertise: that 2-deoxy-D-glucose (2-DG, a glucose
  mimic used to block cells' sugar metabolism) was interfering with **N-linked glycosylation** —
  the cell's process of attaching sugar chains to proteins so they fold and function correctly —
  rather than only blocking glycolysis (the energy-extraction pathway researchers had assumed was
  the whole story) in T cells (the immune system's targeted killers). The model then proposed an
  experiment that, OpenAI says, confirmed the idea. *Why it matters:* this is OpenAI's clearest
  public pitch yet for the model as a hypothesis-generator that compresses "months of head-scratching
  into a working mechanism," and it's a genuinely interesting *kind* of contribution — connecting a
  known side-effect of a common reagent to an observation in a different subfield. But hold the
  enthusiasm at arm's length: it is a single lab's anecdote, told by the model's vendor, with no
  peer-reviewed write-up attached to the post — exactly the shape of claim the "AI for science"
  genre tends to over-sell. The underlying biology may well be right; the marketing around it is
  doing a lot of work. ([OpenAI, 23 Jun 2026](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-immunology-mystery/)) [via snippet]

## 🚀 New models, datasets, & open weights

- **Mistral releases OCR 4, a self-hostable document model at $4 per 1,000 pages.** [vendor PR]
  Mistral's new OCR (optical character recognition — turning page images and PDFs into
  machine-readable, structured text) model is pitched squarely at enterprise document pipelines:
  it returns not just text but **bounding boxes** (the pixel coordinates of each text block),
  **block classification** (is this a heading, a table, a figure caption?), and **inline confidence
  scores** so downstream systems can flag low-certainty reads. It covers **170 languages** across 10
  language groups — including low-resource languages most OCR systems mangle — accepts PDF/DOC/PPT
  and OpenDocument formats, and, notably, **runs in a single container fully self-hosted**, which
  matters for anyone with data-sovereignty rules (European firms, healthcare, legal) who can't ship
  documents to a cloud API. API pricing is $4 per 1,000 pages, halving to $2 with the batch
  discount; the broader "Document AI" product is $5 per 1,000. *Why it matters:* OCR is the
  unglamorous plumbing under every "chat with your PDFs" / RAG (retrieval-augmented generation —
  feeding a model your own documents at query time) system, and accuracy plus self-hosting plus
  low cost is exactly the combination that decides whether a document-AI deployment is viable.
  This continues a busy stretch for document models — Baidu's MIT-licensed Unlimited-OCR and
  Datalab's "lift" extractor landed over the weekend (covered 22 Jun) — but those are open-weight,
  while Mistral OCR 4 is a hosted/licensed product. Benchmark claims below. ([Mistral AI, 23 Jun
  2026](https://mistral.ai/news/ocr-4/)) [via snippet]

## 📊 Benchmarks & evaluations

- **Reading Mistral OCR 4's benchmark claims — strong public numbers, but the headline win-rate is
  vendor-run.** Mistral reports the top overall score on **OlmOCRBench (85.20)** — an open,
  document-level OCR benchmark from the Allen Institute for AI that scores how faithfully a model
  reconstructs real documents (layout, reading order, tables, equations), so a public-benchmark lead
  is meaningfully more credible than a self-defined metric. It also cites **OmniDocBench 93.07**
  (another public document-parsing benchmark) and a **0.98 on its own internal "Crawl Multilingual"
  evaluation**. The number to treat with caution is the marquee one: an **average 72% win rate**
  versus every leading OCR/document-AI system "tested" — that's a human-preference comparison whose
  test set, opponent versions, and annotator pool are all chosen by Mistral, i.e. a vendor-internal
  eval, not a neutral third-party head-to-head. *Why it matters:* "preferred 72% of the time" reads
  like an independent verdict but isn't one; the public OlmOCRBench/OmniDocBench figures are the
  ones an outside team could in principle reproduce, and they're where the claim should be weighed.
  Mistral separately says OCR 4 matched agentic document-parsers on a chart-heavy financial-QA set
  at roughly **8× lower cost and 17× lower latency** — plausible for a single small model versus a
  multi-step agent, but again vendor-measured. ([Mistral AI, 23 Jun 2026](https://mistral.ai/news/ocr-4/)) [via snippet]
