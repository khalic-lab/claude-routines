## 🔬 Science

- **Gravitational waves carry a direct fingerprint of a black hole's event horizon.** Analysing the merger signal GW250114, a team reports the first observational evidence of a predicted "direct wave" — a gravitational-wave component that oscillates near *twice* the remnant horizon's rotation frequency (Ω_H) and decays at a rate set by its surface gravity (κ, the strength of gravity at the horizon). In plain terms: the horizon is the "surface of no return," and the theory says infalling matter appears to be dragged around with the spinning horizon (frame dragging) while its signal is exponentially redshifted away; this wave is the imprint of exactly that. If it holds up, it turns the horizon from an abstraction into something with measured, model-testable properties. Big, but rests on one event and one analysis — replication across future mergers is the test. [single-source] — [Nature, primary research, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10696-0)
- **A galaxy survey reports anisotropic structure on gigaparsec scales — a poke at the cosmological principle.** The standard cosmological model assumes the Universe becomes statistically the same in every direction (isotropic) and everywhere (homogeneous) once you average over a large enough volume. This paper reports large-scale features in the galaxy distribution that deviate from that expectation on ~gigaparsec scales (a gigaparsec is ~3.26 billion light-years). Why it matters: the cosmological principle underpins how we infer dark energy, the Hubble constant, and the age of the Universe; cracks in it would ripple through all of that. Treat as a claim to be stress-tested, not a settled overturning — selection effects and survey geometry are exactly what such results live or die on. [single-source] [disputed] — [Nature, primary research, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10702-5)
- **Deep learning reads sudden-cardiac-death risk straight off a routine ECG.** Today the main predictor doctors use is left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF — how much blood the heart's main pumping chamber ejects per beat); it misses most sudden cardiac deaths and over-flags low-risk patients for implanted defibrillators that never fire. Trained on every electrocardiogram in a Swedish region linked to death certificates, the model isolates a high-risk group (2.2% of the sample) with a 7.0% annual sudden-death rate — higher than the reduced-LVEF group's 4.6% — and crucially **86.1%** of those flagged were *not* caught by LVEF. The promise is catching at-risk people the current biomarker overlooks; the caveat is it still needs prospective, out-of-Sweden validation before it changes who gets a defibrillator. [Nature, primary research, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10674-6) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01806-z)
- **27 late-Neanderthal genomes show a connected regional population, not isolated bands.** Sequencing 27 Neanderthals who lived under ~52,500 years ago across ten sites in Belgium and France — including a high-coverage genome from a 45,000-year-old individual from Goyet, Belgium — the team finds most were more closely related to each other than to Neanderthals elsewhere, evidence of a structured, regionally well-connected population in their final millennia. It sharpens the picture of how Neanderthal groups were organised right before their disappearance. [Nature, primary research, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10625-1) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01704-4)
- **Soil bacteria encode a coordinated "anti-biotin" antibiotic system — a new way to hunt drugs.** Drug discovery has long treated a microbe's biosynthetic gene clusters (the genetic recipes for natural products) as makers of single molecules. This work shows *Streptomyces* bacteria carry a conserved **megacluster** producing four distinct natural-product families *plus* the biotin-binding protein streptavidin — all converging on one target: bacterial biotin (vitamin B7) metabolism. The lay takeaway: bacteria make antibiotic *cocktails* by design, and reading clusters as coordinated systems rather than single drugs could surface combinations that are harder for superbugs to evade. [Nature, primary research, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10647-9) · secondary: [Nature news, 2026-06-24](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01804-1)
- **"Alternate decoding" of RNA makes thousands of stable, off-code proteins in humans.** The genetic code is supposed to be read faithfully, but ribosomes sometimes deviate. Mining deep proteomic, transcriptomic and genomic data from 1,000+ human samples (6 cancer types, 26 healthy tissues), the authors find 8,746 unique amino-acid substitutions arising from such alternate translation — some shared, many tissue- or cancer-specific — and, notably, the off-code products were often *more* abundant and stable than expected. Why care: it widens the real human proteome beyond what the genome literally spells, with possible relevance to cancer-specific protein targets. ## 📄 ML research — first arXiv batch

- **`arXiv:2606.25331` (submitted 2026-06-24) — iLLaDA: an 8B diffusion language model trained from scratch beats its predecessor across the board.** Why interesting: nearly all big LLMs are autoregressive (predict the next token left-to-right with causal attention). iLLaDA is a *masked diffusion* language model — it generates by iteratively un-masking tokens with fully bidirectional attention, more like denoising an image than writing left to right. Scaled to 12T pre-training tokens, it improves on LLaDA by **+14.5 points on MATH** (grade-school-to-competition math benchmark) and **+16.5 on HumanEval** (code-generation benchmark) for the instruct model, and +21.6 on BBH for the base model. The stakes: it's another data point that non-autoregressive LMs can close the gap, which matters because they parallelise generation differently and could change inference economics. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25331)
- **`arXiv:2606.24952` (submitted 2026-06-23) — "Perfect Detection, Failed Control": knowing where a behavior lives ≠ being able to steer it.** Why interesting: a core hope of mechanistic interpretability (reverse-engineering what directions inside a model *mean*) is that if you can *detect* a behavior in the activations you can *control* it by pushing along the same direction. The paper measures the angle between the best detecting direction and the best causal direction. For output format the two collapse onto one axis — detection does imply control. But for **hallucination**, the model flags fake entities with perfect linear separability (AUC = 1.000, i.e. a clean line splits true from fake) from layer 5, yet that direction sits at **cos ≈ 0.12 (~83°)** from the one that actually changes the behavior. Why it matters: it's a cautionary result for "just steer the model" safety plans — the readable direction and the steerable direction can be almost orthogonal. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24952)
- **`arXiv:2606.24964` (submitted 2026-06-23) — first empirical evidence that LLMs *compute* in superposition, with error correction.** Why interesting: "superposition" is the idea that models pack more features than they have dimensions by overlapping them; theory says doing *computation* (not just storage) this way requires error correction that protects meaningful feature directions over generic ones — but that had never been tested. Perturbing residual-stream activations (nudging the model's internal vector), the authors find robustness "plateaus" consistent with error correction, and — as predicted — *less* robustness along candidate feature directions than generic ones. Why care: it's a rare experimental check on a load-bearing interpretability assumption, nudging "superposition" from useful metaphor toward measured mechanism. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24964)
- **`arXiv:2606.24957` (submitted 2026-06-23) — Dustin: making speculative decoding actually fast at long context.** Why interesting: speculative decoding speeds up generation by having a small "draft" model guess several tokens that the big "target" model then verifies in one pass. At long context that verification stalls on loading the KV-cache (the stored keys/values for all prior tokens — memory bandwidth, not compute, becomes the bottleneck). Existing cache-compression tricks either lose accuracy (static eviction) or cost too much (dynamic selection). Dustin fuses the draft model's lookahead with the target model's historical attention to pick only the critical tokens to verify against. Why care: long-context inference is where serving costs balloon, so a cleaner verification path is directly a cost lever. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24957)
- **`arXiv:2606.26027` (submitted 2026-06-24) — why agentic tool-use RL "collapses," and the supervision that prevents it.** Why interesting: reinforcement learning (RL — training by reward rather than imitation) is the fashionable way to make LLMs better at calling tools, but the authors observe *catastrophic collapse* — performance abruptly craters and the structured tool-call format falls apart. Their diagnosis is oddly mechanical: unexpected probability spikes on specific control tokens wreck the output structure, while the underlying tool skill stays intact, merely "obscured by format." Adding supervisory signals (off-policy supervision, hint-based guidance, learning from wrong examples) stabilises training. Why care: it reframes a confusing RL failure as a fixable formatting pathology rather than lost capability — practical for anyone training tool-using agents. `[preprint]` — [arXiv abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26027)
