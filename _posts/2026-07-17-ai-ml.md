## 📄 ML/AI research (arXiv)

A batch with two clear threads. One is post-training economics: several papers attack how expensive and how blunt reinforcement-learning (RL) fine-tuning of language models has become — cheaper long-context RL, a better learning signal than the usual entropy, RL for a whole new model family, and turning an agent's own logs into supervision. The other is measurement and safety: papers that stress-test claims rather than push a leaderboard — how poisonable pretraining data really is, how far a narrow fine-tune leaks into unrelated opinions, and whether an LLM-authored encyclopedia is actually more neutral.

- <a id="st-78b7b41ffeac" class="st-a"></a>**On-device models can be re-tokenized in place to cut decode cost 2–4× for under-served languages** — [arXiv:2607.15232](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15232) · J. T. H. Smith, T. Dakhran et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  A tokenizer — the fixed table that chops text into the sub-word units a model reads — is frozen at the start of pretraining, and it allocates its vocabulary to whatever languages mattered then. Languages added later get split into many more tokens per word, which directly raises latency, compute and energy for their users. On a big cloud model the token table is a rounding error; on a compact on-device model it is a real share of the per-token cost, so small models ship small vocabularies and accept the fragmentation. This paper's "tokenizer expansion" continues the existing byte-pair-encoding merges (the rule that greedily glues common character pairs into tokens) on a multilingual corpus, so old tokens carry over unchanged and each new token has an exact decomposition into old ones; new embedding rows are initialized as the mean of their parts, then a two-stage retrain recovers quality. Applied to an 8B mixture-of-experts checkpoint (MoE — only a few "expert" sub-networks fire per token), it encodes Hindi and Vietnamese in ~2.4× and ~2.6× fewer tokens (up to 4.0× on Thai), for an estimated 2.2–3.7× per-character decode speedup on those languages.

  **Why it matters:** it is a concrete, reader-relevant lever for the local-inference world — the paper describes producing the LFM2.5-8B-A1B model and releasing the weights and expanded tokenizer — and it targets a real equity gap, since the users paying the fragmentation tax are disproportionately non-English speakers on the cheapest hardware. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-dd22dc697a4f" class="st-a"></a>**RL for diffusion language models finally works once you reward the un-masking order, not just the tokens** — [arXiv:2607.15200](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15200) · H. Raajesh, K. Shah et al. (UT Austin) · `[preprint]`

  Most language models are autoregressive (they emit one token at a time, left to right); masked diffusion language models (MDLMs) instead start from an all-masked sequence and iteratively fill positions in, a parallel alternative that has been hard to fine-tune with RL because you can't cleanly compute the probability of a generation. Prior attempts approximated that probability using only *which* tokens were placed, ignoring *which positions the model chose to unmask* at each step. This paper formalizes generation as a two-stage decision — what token to place, and which positions to remask — so the policy gradient splits into a token term and a masking term; optimizing both gives state-of-the-art results for the family, 87.1% on GSM8K (grade-school math word problems) and 53.4% on MBPP (a Python coding benchmark).

  **Why it matters:** diffusion LLMs are one of the main bets on breaking the one-token-at-a-time speed ceiling, and RL post-training is what turned ordinary models into strong reasoners — so a principled recipe for applying it to diffusion models removes a real obstacle to that family becoming competitive. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-f5452560e7ee" class="st-a"></a>**A correctness-aware signal beats entropy for shaping RL rewards** — [arXiv:2607.14614](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14614) · W. Xu, J. Liu et al. (CUHK; South China University of Technology; Nanyang Technological University) · `[preprint]`

  RL with verifiable rewards (RLVR — training a model against an automatic checker, e.g. "is the math answer correct") often uses the model's token-level entropy (its uncertainty) to decide which tokens to reinforce. The authors' objection: entropy can't tell *useful* exploration from plain confusion. Their Contrastive Policy Optimization measures, at each token, the disagreement between the model's normal output distribution and one guided by a reference — a signal they show tracks actual token-level correctness — and shapes the advantage from that instead. They also prove on-policy distillation is a special case, and report consistent gains over entropy-based RLVR on in- and out-of-domain benchmarks.

  **Why it matters:** the reward signal is the whole game in RL post-training, and entropy has been a load-bearing but crude default; a drop-in replacement that better separates "the model is productively unsure" from "the model is lost" is the kind of small, general fix that transfers across tasks. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-faa75f105079" class="st-a"></a>**SEED turns an agent's own finished runs into dense training signal** — [arXiv:2607.14777](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14777) · J. Wu, S. Yang et al. (Tsinghua University; Zhejiang University; CUHK +2 more) · `[preprint]`

  Agentic RL trains a model over long, multi-step tasks (tool calls, environment feedback), but the reward usually arrives only at the very end of an episode — sparse guidance for hundreds of intermediate decisions. SEED (Self-Evolving On-Policy Distillation) has the current policy analyze its own completed trajectories and write plain-language "skills" — reusable workflows, decisive observations, failure-avoidance rules — then re-scores each action with and without those skills in context; the probability shift becomes a dense, per-token learning signal folded into the outcome-based RL. Because the same evolving policy both acts and analyzes, the extra supervision stays aligned with what the model is currently doing. It improves performance and sample-efficiency on text- and vision-based agent tasks.

  **Why it matters:** the sparse-reward problem is the central bottleneck in training long-horizon agents, and SEED extracts extra supervision from data the training run already produced — no human labels, no separate teacher — which is exactly the kind of self-bootstrapping the field needs to scale agent training. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-2ae5f06c7ee0" class="st-a"></a>**LongStraw pushes RL post-training past a million tokens on a fixed GPU budget** — [arXiv:2607.14952](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14952) · C. Zhou, K. Liu et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  Inference systems now handle million-token contexts, but RL post-training usually caps out around 256K tokens and hopes the skill "generalizes" to longer inputs at deployment — a gap that bites hardest for agents, whose tool outputs and prior steps pile up over a long run. LongStraw is an execution stack that evaluates the shared prompt without storing gradients, keeps only the state later tokens actually need, and replays short response branches one at a time — trading extra replay time for a much smaller live training graph. On eight GPUs it processes 2.1M-token groups with almost no memory growth per added sample (a stress test reaches 4.46M positions). The authors are careful: they demonstrate *execution capacity*, not full training correctness, since some gradient paths remain incomplete.

  **Why it matters:** closing the gap between what models can read at inference and what they can be trained on is a prerequisite for genuinely long-horizon agents — and doing it under a fixed GPU budget rather than by buying more hardware is what makes it broadly relevant. The honest scoping is a plus, not a minus. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-2edc5432c7c0" class="st-a"></a>**Pretraining data can be poisoned at web scale through public comment boxes** — [arXiv:2607.15267](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15267) · V. Graf, H. Hajishirzi et al. (University of Washington; Allen Institute for AI) · `[preprint]`

  Prior data-poisoning work mostly edited tidy sources like Wikipedia, which don't resemble the messy, web-scale corpora models actually train on, and ignored the curation pipelines that filter crawled data. This paper shows poisoning is feasible through an existing web-scale injection vector — public discussion interfaces (comment sections, forums) that get crawled into training sets — and introduces HalfLife, a method for *estimating whether injected content actually survives* crawling and curation to land in the training data. That second contribution is the important one: it lets you measure the real inclusion rate of an attack rather than assume it.

  **Why it matters:** "could someone poison a frontier model's pretraining data?" has been argued mostly in the abstract; grounding it in a real injection channel plus a way to measure survival moves it toward a threat you can quantify and defend against — and it names third-party webpage content as a live attack surface data teams currently don't audit. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-921f2317b416" class="st-a"></a>**Fine-tuning on narrow, "moderation-passing" data quietly shifts a model's politics across unrelated topics** — [arXiv:2607.14888](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14888) · R. Graham, E. Stevinson, Y. Barsheshat (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  Adapting a model by fine-tuning it on a small, curated dataset is completely routine. The authors show that training on *narrow, factually-defensible, content-filter-passing* data causes broad ideological shifts on topics absent from the training set, while leaving general capability intact. Fine-tuning GPT-4.1 on right- or left-leaning economics Q&A produces matched shifts on criminal justice, the environment and cultural taste; the same happens with plausibly-deployed data like HR-policy or personal-finance Q&A, and a food-safety fine-tune increases sycophantic agreement with users' false health beliefs. They name it "ideological generalisation" and measure its breadth and amplification — and find fine-tuning pushes further than few-shot prompting on the identical examples, sometimes to far out-of-distribution extremes. It replicates on Gemma-3 and leaves GSM8K accuracy within ±1 point.

  **Why it matters:** this is a sharper, more deployable cousin of the "emergent misalignment" results — the training data here isn't malicious or even edgy, it passes moderation, yet it steers the model's stance on things it never mentioned. Any team fine-tuning a model on in-house data is potentially doing this without knowing. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-edcf6256403b" class="st-a"></a>**Every LLM judge — Grok included — rates Grokipedia less politically neutral than Wikipedia** — [arXiv:2607.15146](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15146) · F. Vlahos, G. Bied, T. De Bie (Ghent University) · `[preprint]`

  Grokipedia, released in late 2025 and written entirely by xAI's Grok model, was pitched as an unbiased alternative to a Wikipedia its backers call left-biased. The authors compare 1,394 matched article pairs about members of government across nine expert-coded ideology dimensions, scored by four LLM judges (Grok, Claude, Mistral, DeepSeek) — and, aware the judges themselves could be biased, they also analyze the judges' own patterns. All four, *including Grok*, rate Grokipedia the less neutral of the two. Both encyclopedias are judged to portray politicians favourably overall, but toward different groups: Grokipedia favours economically right-wing figures and penalises socially liberal ones; Wikipedia tilts the other way.

  **Why it matters:** encyclopedias shape political opinion, and "an LLM will write it more neutrally" is now a testable claim rather than a talking point — the finding is that an LLM-written reference doesn't remove bias, it substitutes a different one. That even the model's own maker's judge agrees is the striking part. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-77a9027b9a3d" class="st-a"></a>**D-Cut restores speculative decoding's speedup when many requests run at once** — [arXiv:2607.14647](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14647) · T. Liu, Y. Shen et al. (Tencent) · `[preprint]`

  Speculative decoding accelerates inference by having a small "draft" model guess several tokens that the big model then verifies in one pass — free speedup when guesses are right. But under high concurrency, long drafts waste computation on rejected tokens, and verification cost can grow until speculative decoding is *slower* than plain generation. D-Cut prunes draft tokens jointly across the whole batch, steering the verification budget toward the tokens most likely to be accepted, and uses a runtime cost model to adapt its aggressiveness to the specific GPU and parallelism setup. Under high concurrency it lifts average speedup from 1.26× to 1.65×, and reaches up to 3.0× on MoE models.

  **Why it matters:** speculative decoding is deployed widely, but most published gains are measured on a single request; this targets the regime real serving systems actually live in — many users at once — where the naive version can backfire. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-3ecff614b1fa" class="st-a"></a>**Cost-aware evaluation shows defensive security agents don't scale with compute the way offensive ones do** — [arXiv:2607.15263](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15263) · P. Kassianik, B. Nelson, Y. Singer (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  Security-agent benchmarks usually report peak capability under a generous compute budget. The authors instead compare LLM security agents at *fixed cost* — counting every reasoning step, tool call and query — across offensive challenges (Cybench capture-the-flag) and defensive ones (Splunk BOTS investigation). The regimes diverge: offensive success keeps improving with more test-time compute, and scaled open-weight models approach frontier proprietary ones while staying cost-competitive; defensive SOC (security operations centre) work does *not* scale that way — it depends more on disciplined tool use and selective data-gathering than on raw reasoning budget.

  **Why it matters:** "which model is best for security" gets a very different answer once you price in operational cost, and the asymmetry — throwing compute helps attackers more than defenders — is a sobering data point for anyone deploying agents on either side. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

- <a id="st-497851ad0269" class="st-a"></a>**Trained to ground answers in a document, reasoning models learn to stop reasoning** — [arXiv:2607.14682](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14682) · Harikrishnan P M, G. Vignesh et al. (affiliation not listed) · `[preprint]`

  In multimodal document question-answering — answer a question *and* point to the exact region of the page that supports it — the authors apply GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization, a popular RL method) directly to align visual features with grounding outputs, skipping the verbose "chain-of-thought" traces. Testing a reasoning-enabled variant under identical rewards, they find the model *suppresses its own reasoning traces* during training and converges to direct perception at the 4B scale, cutting per-query token length by over 60% — and the reasoning-enabled version actually underperforms the perception-only one.

  **Why it matters:** it is a pointed counterexample to "more explicit reasoning is always better." For a grounded, look-at-the-page task, the intermediate reasoning is not just unnecessary overhead but mildly harmful — evidence that where reasoning helps is task-specific, not universal. _Submitted 2026-07-16._

  OpenAI describes training a dedicated attacker model through self-play — the model repeatedly tries to break other models and learns from what works — reporting that GPT-Red finds a successful attack in 84% of tested scenarios where human red-teamers manage 13%, and that the discovered attacks feed directly into hardening its shipping models. Automated red-teaming is a genuine capability step, but note the framing is OpenAI's own and the two numbers aren't necessarily measured on comparable task sets.

  **Why it matters:** if adversarial testing can be automated and scaled, both defenders and attackers get a faster loop — the same technique that hardens a model is, in principle, an offensive tool. - <a id="st-6c9506d674d0" class="st-a"></a>**Sakana AI plugs Nvidia's Nemotron models into its Fugu orchestrator** — [The Decoder, 2026-07-16](https://the-decoder.com/sakana-ais-fugu-adds-nvidia-nemotron-to-prove-collective-intelligence-can-rival-single-frontier-models/) · `[vendor PR]`

  Fugu is itself a language model trained to call *other* models from a pool (including copies of itself), picking and combining them per task behind a single API — Tokyo-based Sakana AI's bet on "collective intelligence." Adding Nvidia's open-weight Nemotron family (strong at coding, tool-calling and instruction-following) is meant to give the orchestrator more specialists to draw on.   **Why it matters:** it is a clean statement of the "orchestration layer" thesis — that no single model wins every task, language and modality, so the value migrates to routing among many. Whether coordinated open models actually close the gap to a single frontier system, at acceptable latency and cost, is still unproven. _Vendor-reported; integration date unannounced._

## 🚀 New models, datasets, & open weights

- <a id="st-da9ec0cbf08b" class="st-a"></a>**Moonshot's Kimi K3 is the first open model in the ~3-trillion-parameter range, and it lands near the frontier** — [Kimi (Moonshot), 2026-07-16](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-k3) `[new source]`; independent eval [Artificial Analysis via The Decoder, 2026-07-16](https://the-decoder.com/kimis-open-model-k3-nears-gpt-5-6-sol-and-fable-5-while-signaling-the-end-of-super-cheap-chinese-ai/)

  K3 is a multimodal open-weight mixture-of-experts model with 2.8 trillion total parameters — activating just 16 of 896 experts per token — a one-million-token context, and native image/video input; full weights are due 27 July. It pairs a new "Kimi Delta Attention" scheme that Moonshot says decodes up to 6.3× faster at million-token contexts. On the independent Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index it scores 57 — level with Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.5, behind Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol — and on the GDPval v2 agentic benchmark it jumps to an Elo of 1,668 (from K2.6's 1,190), beating GPT-5.5 and Opus 4.8 but trailing Fable 5. One honest caveat from the same eval: its hallucination rate *rose* (39% → 51%) even as accuracy improved, so it answers more questions right but also fabricates more. Pricing ($0.30/M cached input, $15/M output) is well above the previous K2.6, which the write-up reads as the end of rock-bottom Chinese frontier pricing.

  **Why it matters:** this is the edition's headline. An openly-released model at 2.8T parameters scoring within a few points of proprietary flagships collapses the gap between the open and closed tiers faster than most expected — with direct consequences for anyone (in a sovereignty-conscious region especially) weighing whether they can self-host near-frontier capability rather than rent it. The rising hallucination rate is the asterisk: near-frontier on aggregate scores does not mean near-frontier on factual reliability. _Benchmarks from Moonshot and Artificial Analysis; weights not yet public._

- <a id="st-fef80affe343" class="st-a"></a>**Thinking Machines ships its first model, Inkling — a 975B open-weights MoE leading US labs but trailing China** — [Thinking Machines, 2026-07-16](https://thinkingmachines.ai/news/introducing-inkling/) `[new source]`; model card [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/thinkingmachines/inkling)

  Mira Murati's lab (she was OpenAI's CTO) released Inkling, a 975-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with 41B active parameters, native text/image/audio, and up to a million-token context; weights are on Hugging Face. On the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index it debuts at 41 — the top US open-weights model, ahead of Nvidia's Nemotron 3 Ultra (38) and well above Gemma 4 31B (29) — but below the strongest Chinese open models. The lab is candid that "Inkling is not the strongest overall model available today," positioning it instead as an efficient, fine-tunable base: it uses markedly fewer output tokens per task than rivals and offers a continuous "thinking effort" dial. Two caveats worth flagging: factual accuracy is weak (a +2 on AA's Omniscience benchmark, 63% hallucination rate), and it was pretrained partly on synthetic data generated with China's Kimi K2.5 plus public data that "may be subject to intellectual property protection." A smaller Inkling-Small (276B) is previewed.

  **Why it matters:** a new US lab's first release being an *open-weights* model — explicitly framed as a customizable base rather than a frontier chatbot — is a notable strategic choice, and the training-data disclosure (built partly on a Chinese open model, and on IP-protected material) is unusually frank about the provenance questions the whole field is dodging. _Vendor and Artificial Analysis benchmarks; weak factual accuracy noted._

## 💼 Industry, funding, regulation

- <a id="st-3f4babda2ad4" class="st-a"></a>**EU orders Google to share search data and open Android to rival AI assistants** — [Ars Technica, 2026-07-16](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2026/07/its-official-eu-will-force-google-to-share-search-data-and-open-up-ai-on-android/)

  The European Commission announced binding Digital Markets Act (DMA — the EU's law reining in dominant "gatekeeper" platforms) "specification measures" against Google on two fronts. On Android, Google must open system-level access — the "Hey Google" wake word, screen-content access, app automation — that its own Gemini assistant currently gets preferentially, so users can install a competing AI assistant without losing features. On search, Google must share search data with rival search providers (transparently, for a reasonable fee) and must treat AI chatbots as search services for the purposes of that data-sharing. Google's global-affairs president Kent Walker called the measures overreach that will harm users and privacy; compliance is nonetheless mandatory.

  **Why it matters:** this is a structural remedy, not a fine — it targets the preferential hooks that make a pre-installed assistant sticky and the data moat behind search dominance. For the reader's own market, it is the clearest signal yet that the EU intends to force the AI-assistant layer open at the OS level, which could meaningfully change which assistant a European phone actually runs. _Reported via Ars; EU decision legally binding, Google expected to contest._

- <a id="st-2831865a6675" class="st-a"></a>**German media regulator rules Google's AI Overviews and Perplexity are "content providers" subject to media law** — [The Decoder, 2026-07-16](https://the-decoder.com/germany-puts-googles-ai-overviews-and-perplexity-under-media-law-in-first-of-its-kind-ruling/) · `[single-source]`

  Germany's Commission for Licensing and Supervision (ZAK) issued its first rulings applying the State Media Treaty to AI search — finding that Google's AI-generated summaries are Google's *own* content, not neutral search results, so the Digital Services Act liability shield for third-party content doesn't apply. Regulators say Google's summaries get prime placement above the normal links, pushing down journalistic sources — "prohibited discrimination" because the answers are Google's own content — and cite transparency failures; a Munich court recently reached a parallel conclusion, treating AI text as "independent, new, and substantive" content. Perplexity was flagged so far only for lacking a German representative and transparency disclosures. Both have a month to appeal; Google will.

  **Why it matters:** classifying an AI answer as the provider's own editorial content — rather than a neutral index — is a legal turn with teeth, tying AI search to media-diversity and transparency rules and to liability for false statements. It is an early, concrete European answer to "who is responsible for what a chatbot asserts." _Single-source; rulings immediately enforceable but under appeal._
