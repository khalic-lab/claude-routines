## 🇨🇭 Switzerland & Vaud

- **Swiss Q1 2026 GDP confirmed at +0.4%, with industry — not the consumer — doing the heavy lifting.** The State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO, the federal economics ministry's analysis arm) put first-quarter growth at 0.4% quarter-on-quarter, a touch below the first flash estimate. The split underneath the headline is the real story: manufacturing value-added rose 1.5% (with the non-chemicals/non-pharma slice up a striking 4.6%), while *final domestic demand grew just 0.1%* — private consumption was essentially flat and both equipment and construction investment fell 0.2%. *Why it matters:* an economy growing on exports and factory output while households sit on their wallets is a fragile mix — it leaves Swiss growth hostage to foreign demand and the franc, exactly the channels most exposed to US tariff turbulence. The pharma sector's swings (it front-loaded exports in prior quarters ahead of feared tariffs) make the "ex-pharma" figure the cleaner read on underlying industrial health. ## 🛡️ Cybersecurity

A quiet day for *active-exploitation* news — CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalogue, the federal "patch this now, it's being used in attacks" list, added nothing new today (its most recent entry remains the Palo Alto PAN-OS GlobalProtect auth-bypass CVE-2026-0257 from 2026-05-29, whose federal-agency remediation deadline lands today — covered in prior briefs). But the NVD published a busy batch of newly-disclosed flaws, several genuinely critical. CVSS recap: 9.0+ = critical, often unauthenticated and trivial to weaponise; 7.0–8.9 = high.

- **Teamwork Cloud / Magic Collaboration Studio — unauthenticated deserialization RCE, CVSS 9.8 (CVE-2026-7858).** A *deserialization of untrusted data* flaw (the app rebuilds program objects from attacker-supplied serialized bytes, a classic path to running attacker code) affects Dassault Systèmes' No Magic Teamwork Cloud and CATIA Magic Collaboration Studio across the 2022x–2026x releases. The vector string `AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N` means network-reachable, low-complexity, *no authentication and no user interaction* — i.e. remote, pre-auth, full compromise. *Why it matters:* these are model-collaboration servers used in engineering/PLM (product-lifecycle management) shops; a pre-auth RCE on a central modelling server is a direct route to intellectual-property theft or lateral movement into an engineering network. ([NVD, 2026-06-01](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-7858))

- **OTRS — unauthenticated SQL injection that bypasses authentication, CVSS 9.1 (CVE-2026-48188).** A database-layer input-validation flaw in OTRS and the ((OTRS)) Community Edition lets an unauthenticated attacker inject SQL (smuggle database commands through an input field) and, via that, bypass login entirely. It shipped alongside a cluster of lower-severity OTRS issues (SVG content injection CVE-2026-48208, a customer-info disclosure, and permission-handling bugs). *Why it matters:* OTRS is a widely-deployed ticketing/help-desk and ITSM platform — exactly the kind of internal system that holds support correspondence, credentials in tickets, and a foothold into the rest of the estate; "unauth SQLi → auth bypass" is about as clean an entry path as defenders dread. ([NVD, 2026-06-01](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-48188))

- **Apache ActiveMQ — Jolokia default-authorization weakness (CVE-2026-49157) plus broker code-injection flaws (CVE-2026-45505, CVE-2026-42588), all CVSS up to 8.8.** ActiveMQ is one of the most common open-source message brokers (the middleware that shuttles messages between application components). The marquee issue: ActiveMQ's default settings for *Jolokia* — an HTTP-to-JMX bridge that exposes Java management endpoints over a REST API — granted non-admin users more access than intended (fixed before 5.19.7 and 6.2.6). Two further flaws allow code injection in the broker. *Why it matters:* JMX/Jolokia exposure on a broker is a well-trodden RCE pivot (an attacker who can reach management beans can often load code), and message brokers usually sit deep inside trusted networks where a foothold travels far. Patch to ActiveMQ 5.19.7 / 6.2.6. ([NVD, 2026-06-01](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-49157); code injection [NVD CVE-2026-45505](https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2026-45505))

## 📄 ML research — second arXiv batch

_Today's announced arXiv batch (listing date Mon 01 Jun 2026, papers submitted 2026-05-28/29), excluding the IDs already cited in this morning's Overview. Bias: RL, efficient inference, interpretability, anomaly detection, hybrid architectures._

- **arXiv:2605.31245 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "Toward Identifiable Sparse Autoencoders."** Sparse autoencoders (SAEs — small networks trained to re-express a model's internal activations as a few interpretable "features") have become the workhorse of mechanistic interpretability, but this paper shows — empirically *and* with theory — that they're unstable: re-train one and you get a different feature dictionary, so the "concepts" you read off depend on the random seed. The authors pin down which design choices cause the instability and fix them with minimal architecture/training tweaks, yielding an *identifiable* SAE (iSAE) with lower reconstruction error and reproducible codes. *Why interesting:* if SAE features shift run-to-run, every interpretability claim built on them is on sand; making the dictionary reproducible is a precondition for using SAEs as a serious safety/auditing tool rather than a Rorschach test. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31245))

- **arXiv:2605.31304 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "Interpretability Without Tradeoffs: Disentangling Polysemanticity At Equal Predictive Performance" (ELUDe).** A core obstacle to reading neural nets is *polysemanticity* — a single neuron firing for several unrelated concepts at once, smearing the decision logic. SAEs can split these mixed signals into cleaner "monosemantic" features but usually require altering the model in ways that dent accuracy. ELUDe (explicit, lossless, unsupervised disentanglement) claims to separate latent representations into inspectable components *while keeping the network functionally identical* — no accuracy cost. *Why interesting:* it directly attacks the field's standard excuse ("we could interpret it, but that breaks performance"); a genuinely lossless disentanglement would let you audit a deployed model without shipping a degraded one. Pairs naturally with the iSAE result above — together they're a "make interpretability tooling trustworthy" theme in today's batch. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31304))

- **arXiv:2605.31261 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "Why Linear Recurrent Memory Works in Partially Observable Reinforcement Learning."** In a partially observable environment (a POMDP — the agent never sees the full state, only noisy/partial observations, like a robot with a limited camera), the agent must *remember* past observations to act well. Linear recurrent networks (the lineage behind modern state-space models like Mamba) work surprisingly well as that memory, but mostly by empirical luck. This paper supplies the theory: it constructs linear filters that exactly reproduce the *belief-state* logits of a hidden Markov model (the optimal running estimate of where you probably are), making them a *sufficient statistic* for optimal control, and shows the state-decoding error vanishes under near-deterministic dynamics. *Why interesting:* it gives a principled reason to prefer cheap linear recurrence over heavier attention for memory in RL agents — efficiency with a correctness guarantee, not just a benchmark win. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31261))

- **arXiv:2605.30832 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "SLAT: Segment-Level Adaptive Trimming for Efficient CoT Reasoning."** Reasoning models trained with RL tend to *overthink* — chain-of-thought (CoT, the model's step-by-step scratchpad) balloons with redundant steps that burn compute without improving the answer. Standard fixes apply a uniform "be shorter" penalty across all tokens, which bluntly squashes useful reasoning alongside the padding. SLAT instead shows inefficiency concentrates in *high-probability, low-marginal-utility* segments (confident-but-pointless stretches) and trims at the segment level. *Why interesting:* inference cost on reasoning models is now a real deployment line-item; surgically removing the wasteful spans rather than globally truncating is the difference between "cheaper and just as accurate" and "cheaper but dumber." `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30832))

- **arXiv:2605.30789 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "Smaller Models are Natural Explorers for Policy-Level Diversity in GRPO."** GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization — the RL recipe behind recent reasoning models, which scores a group of sampled answers against each other) needs *diverse* rollouts to learn; the usual way to get diversity is cranking up token-level randomness, which often produces incoherent, noisy chains. The finding: a *smaller* model from the same family is naturally more diverse at the *policy* level — its spread of answers (higher pass@k as you sample more) is temporally coherent rather than random noise — so it can serve as a structured exploration signal for training the larger model. *Why interesting:* it reframes a small model not as a weak student but as a useful *explorer*, a cheap way to inject quality diversity into RL training without the incoherence that token-level noise brings. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30789))

- **arXiv:2605.31007 (sub. 2026-05-29) — "DEM: A Distilled Explanation Model for Interpretable Anomaly Detection in Physiological Sensor Networks."** In wearable/body-sensor networks (WBANs — the mesh of on-body sensors streaming vitals), anomalies can mean a real clinical event or just a sensor glitch/dropout, and clinicians won't trust a black-box alarm. DEM is a three-stage "glass-box" pipeline: train a strong but opaque gradient-boosting model, then *distil* its knowledge (transfer what it learned) into an interpretable decision tree that operates on residuals relative to a simple linear baseline — so each alert comes with a human-readable reason. *Why interesting:* it's a concrete take on the accuracy-vs-explainability tradeoff in a high-stakes setting — keep most of the black box's performance but emit clinically-auditable explanations, the precondition for anyone acting on the alarm. `[preprint]` ([abstract](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31007))
